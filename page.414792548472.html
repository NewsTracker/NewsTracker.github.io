Schools are using STUDENTS to help interview for teachers?

Bali Nine's Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran to be moved to Nusakambangan island this week, authorities say

1Students as young as thirteen are interviewing prospective teachers for jobs in schools.
2While Victorian schools have been praising the unusual hiring process, the Australian Education Union believes it isn't an appropriate place for the student voice to be heard and the responsibility of the employment of staff should be left to the principal.
3Peter Hutton, Principal of Templestowe College, has been running with the alternative hiring system for the past four years.
4He told Daily Mail Australia that he thinks the process is 'fantastic' and up to 70 percent of the staff at the school have been employed using the unusual hiring method.
5'Not only do they interview teaching staff but they also interview support staff and even assistant principals,' Mr Hutton said.
6The students range in ages and Mr Hutton said in the past there have been Year 7 students involved in the selection process.
7'The students take it very very seriously.
8There have been very few occasions where we haven't agreed as a panel,' he said.
9'To be honest if somebody wasn't prepared to be interviewed by students then they wouldn't be a good fit for us anyway.
10Speaking to Daily Mail Australia Australian Education Union Victorian branch president Meredith Peace said she strongly does not believe students should decide who is employed at Victorian schools.
11'We don't support students being involved in the formal selection process of hiring staff,' Ms Peace said.
12Ms Peace said that the students may have the best intentions but they do not possess the skills or expertise necessary to able to make such a judgement.
13'Students through no fault of their own haven't developed the skills and knowledge needed.
14The selection process Is pretty rigorous and it requires people with an understanding of how that works,' she said.
15Principal Hutton said the prospective employee is firstly taken into a room with three adults members of the panel.
16The person is interviewed and then sent into another room to face a selection of students.
17The panel of students is chaired by a senior student, who has had experience in interviewing.
18He said the interviewees are told before they arrive that they will be questioned by students at the school during their interview.
19The school has trained nearly 50 students to be interviewers.
20Firstly the students explore exactly what qualities they would like in a teacher.
21Then they divide up into small groups and work out some appropriate questions that will give them an estimation as to how suitable the applicant will be for the job.
22The students' interview with the prospective employee usually last about 15 minutes.
23'I never fail to be impressed by their honesty,' Mr Hutton said.
24'Some of the comments that I've heard expressed today by members of the public (about the hiring method) just shatter me at the lack of faith that they have in students.
25Tess Shacklock from the Victorian Student Representative Council sits on a selection panel that is part of the hiring process at Templestowe College.
26She believes the process helps to build a strong student-teacher relationship as makes it easier for students to develop a connection with the new member of staff.
27She said a normal interview technique would be to ask the interviewee a scenario question.
28'Asking questions like that we'd receive a more open-ended and extended answer, which gives you a better insight into how that individual would teach,' Ms Shacklock said.
29The 17-year-old who is in Year 12 at the school said she can't understand why people would think students should not be involved in the hiring process.
30'We are just as capable as a teacher is to interview a fellow teacher.
31We have all the experience of being in a classroom and observing what it's like to teach and what's it like to be taught, so why wouldn't we be on the panel that decides who will be our teacher,' she said.
3213-year-old Kyle Meredith-Peck, who is also on the selection panel at Templestowe College, believes that students gain more respect for their teachers after becoming involved in selecting them for employment.
33'The best thing is to get a balance between respect, kindness and knowledge.
34I think if you can balance that out you're going to get really good teachers,' he said.
35Both Tess and Kyle agree that the top traits they look for in a prospective teacher are for them to be; approachable, knowledgeable, kindness and respect.
36An Education Department of NSW spokesperson told Daily Mail Australia that the practice of involving students in employment processes does not exist in public schools in the state.
1Two Australian Bali Nine members on death row in Indonesia will be transferred this week to the most notorious prison on Nusakambangan island, known as Indonesia's Alcatraz.
2The head of the Bali prosecutors' office confirmed on Monday afternoon that Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran will be taken to the maximum security Batu prison on the penal island.
3The name of the jail, which was built in 1925, means stone prison.
4Ten officers from the paramilitary force Brimob will escort the two men to Nusakambangan.
5Momock Bambang Samiarso said the transfer would not take place on Monday - and was unlikely to happen on Tuesday - but would definitely happen this week.
6Mr Samiarso said he would notify the families as soon as possible.
7Prime Minister Tony Abbott revealed on Monday he made a further plea for Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran's lives to Indonesian President Joko Widodo.
8And a judge involved in handing down the death sentence to the two drug smugglers has denied allegations there was political intervention or bribery in the case.
9Attorney-General spokesman Tony Spontana said earlier that condemned drug felons from Bali's infamous Kerobokan jail, where Chan and Sukumaran are on death row, will be the first transferred.
10They would be later joined by felons from prisons in Madiun and Yogyakarta.
11"Only after everybody is gathered at Nusakambangan will the D-day be decided," Mr Spontana said.
12Eleven prisoners on death row for drug and murder charges are expected to be killed in the second round of executions in Indonesia this year.
13Of these, seven drug felons are foreigners from Australia, the Philippines, France, Brazil, Nigeria and Ghana.
14Mr Abbott said he felt "sick in the pit of my stomach" when he thought about what was happening to Chan and Sukumaran.
15Mr Abbott would not provide further information on his overtures to Mr Joko because he did not want it to come down to a test of strength.
16"If we do turn this into a test of strength, I think we are much more likely to back the Indonesians into a corner than to get the result we want," he said.
17Chan and Sukumaran's legal team have written to the Indonesian judicial committee requesting an investigation into allegations of political interference and bribery when the death sentence was imposed.
18Foreign Affairs Minister Julie Bishop said these were very serious allegations and she understood the lawyers would continue to raise these matters.
19However a judge involved in the case, Wayan Yasa Abadi, denied there had been political interference or bribes.
20He said he would respond if summonsed by the judicial committee.
21Meanwhile, a new Lowy Institute poll has found that 62 per cent of Australians oppose the execution of Chan and Sukumaran.
22This is in contrast to an earlier Roy Morgan poll - seized upon by the Indonesian government to justify the executions - which found that 52 per cent supported the death penalty for Australian drug traffickers.
23The earlier poll was criticised for being crude and misleading due to the rubbery nature of the questions asked.
24According to the Lowy poll, 69 per cent of the population do not believe the death penalty should be applied for drug trafficking.
25Executive director of the Lowy Institute Michael Fullilove said Australian public and political opposition was crystallising as the date of the execution drew closer.
