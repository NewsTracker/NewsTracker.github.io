Dozens feared dead in Vanuatu after cyclone Pam leaves trail of destruction

1More than 40 people are reported to have been killed so far after winds reaching 200mph destroyed homes, roads, power supplies and harbours.
2The death toll is expected to rise well beyond that figure.
3Some reports said entire villages had been destroyed in remote areas.
4"Homes have been blown to pieces, and even evacuation shelters, where people had sought refuge, have been flooded and left exposed to the cyclone," said Charlie Damon of Care International.
5In some places, islanders crowded into caves and other makeshift shelters for safety.
6"I can say that for anybody who wasn't in a secure shelter last night, it would have been a very, very tough time," said Chloe Morrison of World Vision, who is based in the capital, Port Vila.
7UN secretary general Ban Ki-moon said the impact of the disaster was not yet clear, but he feared the damage and destruction were widespread.
8The UN was preparing to deploy emergency response units.
9Britain pledged up to 2m towards relief efforts - the Department for International Development said up to 1m would be available immediately with the same sum from the UK's rapid response facility.
10Alex Mathieson, Oxfam's former director of Vanuatu, who lived there for four years and now works for the charity in Melbourne, Australia, said a British woman is among a number of his friends he is anxiously waiting to hear from.
11"We're concerned for her and a couple of other friends we haven't been able to get hold of," he said.
12"We're hoping for the best from those we haven't heard from. Me and my colleagues have watched with a sense of horror and helplessness."
13The Foreign Office said it is working to establish whether any British nationals have been affected by the disaster.
14Cyclone Pam had not been forecast to hit Vanuatu directly, but a westward change of course put the islands in its path early on Saturday.
15Unicef spokeswoman Alice Clements said the cyclone was "15 to 30 minutes of absolute terror" for "everybody in this country".
16Save the Children's Vanuatu director, Tom Skirrow, described its aftermath as a scene of complete devastation: "Houses are destroyed, trees are down, roads are blocked and people are wandering the streets looking for help."
17New Zealand pledged NZ$1m (about 500,000) to help with relief efforts, while Australia said it was preparing to send a crisis response team if needed.
18"There are destructive winds, rain, flooding, landslides, sea surges and very rough seas, and the storm is exceedingly destructive there," said the foreign minister, Julie Bishop.
19"We stand ready to assist."
20The UN said there were unconfirmed reports of 44 deaths in Vanuatu's north-eastern islands after Pam veered from its expected track.
21However, that number is expected to rise significantly as reports come in from outlying areas.
22Vanuatu has a population of about 267,000, spread over 83 islands, with some 47,000 people living in Port Vila.
23"We anticipate that there will be higher numbers [of casualties] on remote islands, because they have less sophisticated shelter," said Oxfam's Vanuatu director, Colin Collett van Rooyen.
24"But we have no indication of what's happening there because we have no communications to the islands there has been significant destruction of homes in some areas. We have no power or running water and are still not able to move around freely," he added.
25"The scale of this disaster is unprecedented in this country, and the people of Vanuatu are going to need a lot of help to rebuild their homes and their lives."
26Vanuatu's president, Baldwin Lonsdale, who had been attending a conference on disaster risk in Japan, issued an emotional appeal for aid.
27"I stand to appeal on behalf of the government and the people of Vanuatu that the global community give a lending hand in responding to these very current calamities that have struck us," he said.
28The island nation, which lies about a quarter of the way from Australia to Hawaii, has repeatedly warned that it is suffering devastating effects from climate change as its coastal areas are washed away, forcing people to resettle on higher ground.
29Port Vila is considered to be the city most exposed to natural disasters in the world: as well as cyclones, it faces risks of earthquakes, tsunamis and floods.
30Weather forecasters said cyclone Pam had started to weaken as it moved away from Vanuatu and would pass between Fiji and New Caledonia before brushing the North Island of New Zealand.
31A UN report this week found that economic losses over the past 45 years in the Asia Pacific from natural disasters totalled $1.15tn, with a loss of more than two million lives.
32Vanuatu was the country most at risk in the world from natural disasters, ahead of Tonga, the Philippines, Japan and Costa Rica, the document said.
