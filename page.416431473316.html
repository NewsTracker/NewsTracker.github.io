Morrison's 'God Help the Child' excavates characters' self-image with a touch of magical realism

1In 1970 Toni Morrison detonated a beautiful atom bomb of a debut novel called"The Bluest Eye."
2In telling the story of Pecola Breedlove, a little Ohio girl taught to hate her black skin, Morrison laid bare society's poisonous obsession with racial appearance.
3She also introduced a theme that would run throughout her body of work, including her latest short novel,"God Help the Child": the combustive combination of love and hate so integral to African-American self-image.
4Morrison is now 84, and apparently drinking from the same fountain of youth that sustained her only rival for the title of Greatest Living American Writer, Philip Roth.
5"God Help the Child," her 11th novel, bristles with the high colloquial style that infused her first several novels, before her work took a decidedly modernist (and often challenging) turn.
6It reads more like"Sula"and"Song of Solomon"than"Jazz"or"Paradise"(or her crowning masterpiece,"Beloved").
7Like"Bluest Eye"'sPecola,"Child"'sBride is made to feel ashamed of her pitch-black skin.
8Bride's mother, Sweetness, sounds the alarm on the novel's first page: "She was so black she scared me. Midnight black, Sudanese black ... tar is the closest I can think of yet her hair don't go with the skin. It's different - straight but curly like those naked tribes in Australia."
9Sweetness is one of five first-person narrators (plus a third-person omniscient voice) guiding this vessel, each of them identified at the top of each chapter.
10Morrison knows her Faulkner and her"As I Lay Dying."
11Bride, however, flips the"Bluest Eye"script.
12A rising manager for a boutique cosmetics company, she wears white to set off the beauty of her blackness.
13"Every feature," thinks her lover, "the ledge of her cheekbones, her invitational mouth, her nose, her forehead, chin, as well as those eyes - was more aesthetically pleasing because of her obsidian-midnight skin."
14Bride still has her crosses to bear, chiefly a lie she told as a little girl that destroyed a woman's life.
15And she still suffers from an inability to love healthily, a theme that haunts other characters here and in Morrison's body of work.
16It's as hard as ever to pick nits in Morrison's writing.
17Every page contains at least one passage of breathtaking prose, a lyrical flow accentuated by stark imagery and laden with poetic contrasts.
18Here's Bride's lover, the peripatetic Booker, identifying the remains of his brother as a child: "Filthy, rat-gnawed, with a single open eye socket. The maggots, overfed and bursting with glee, had gone home leaving fastidiously clean bones under the strips of his mud-caked yellow T-shirt."
19Morrison has always had the command to render the most horrific scenes with uncompromising splendor.
20To her, it's all part of life and death.
21You'll also find a touch of what could be called Morrison's magical realism, a term that has always felt a little trite.
22There's no reincarnation, as in"Beloved," or humans taking "Song of Solomonflight."
23Instead, as Bride finds herself reckoning with her past, she physically reverts to childhood.
24Morrison remains deeply imaginative in depicting the historical ravaging of the black body.
25In her work this is never merely a physical matter, but a symptom and reminder of the country's bloodstained past.
26The protest novel, of course, has never been Morrison's thing; she's more interested in excavating her characters' interior lives, and their ancestral connections to the worlds of history and myth.
27Her heroes and villains, separated by a thin membrane, follow Joan Didion's dictum.
28They tell themselves stories in order to live, to make better sense of past pains, and their urgency gives their tales a sense of brave desperation undercut with shame.
29In Morrison's universe, shame is never far away.
30My only complaint with"God Help the Child"?
31I wish there were more of it.
32Like her previous novel,"Home"in 2013,"God Help the Child"checks in under 200 pages.
33If it were anyone else we'd just call it a novella, but the word seems to belittle Morrison's stature and gift.
34The writing is crisp, the narrative economical, and a new Morrison book is always cause for celebration.
35But some of the late-breaking plot developments feel orphaned by the novel's brevity and eagerness to bring Bride's denouement.
36Then again, the last word, which goes to Sweetness, will ring in your ears.
37It carries echoes of"Beloved"and the potent brew of burden, fear and pride that goes into motherhood, and bringing another being into this most imperfect of worlds.
38God help the child, indeed.
39By all accounts Morrison is in fine health, and the words are clearly still there.
40Yet we know she's closer to the end than the beginning, and"God Help the Child"feels a bit like the closing of a circle begun with Pecola and that first novel 45 years ago.
41This time the sadness and unflagging wisdom is cut with hope - not the promise that anything will be easy, but the suggestion that it will at least be all right.
