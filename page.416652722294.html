The Latest on Nepal: British Gurkhas return to aid recovery

1A Nepalese couple rides past while carrying a sack of wheat flour through earthquake damaged buildings in Kathmandu, Nepal, Friday, May 1, 2015.
2A strong magnitude earthquake shook Nepal on Saturday devastating the region and leaving some thousands shell-shocked and displaced.
3Members of the British army Gurkha engineers are back in their native Nepal to help restore water supply in the capital.
4One of them, the 34-year-old Cpl.
5Besh Gurung, says he has been away from Nepal for 13 years and now is glad "I could serve my countrymen when they really needed something so necessary like clean drinking water."
6Nearly 2,000 people in tented camps and the Nepalese soldiers stationed in the former royal palace area are the immediate beneficiaries.
7Gurkhas have been part of the British military for over two centuries, and have fought in the Falklands, Iraq and Afghanistan.
8Thousands of young men from the poverty-stricken Himalayan hills still apply to join the force every year.
9Nepal's government is giving out 100,000 rupees ($1,000) to families of each of those killed in Saturday's earthquake, and another 40,000 rupees ($400) for funeral costs, according to the state-run Radio Nepal.
10Meanwhile, Nepal Rastriya Bank, the central bank, has ordered all private banks to open for at least a few hours Friday as well as over the weekend to meet the demand.
11The latest death toll figure stands at 6,198 in Nepal alone, plus 61 elsewhere in the region.
12Most of the people who were sleeping outside their homes for fear of aftershocks have moved back indoors.
13There were only a few dozen tents left at the Tudikhel grounds in the heart of Kathmandu, with less than a thousand people still there.
14The area used to be crowded with more than 30,000 people in the first days after the quake hit.
15Almost all the gas stations are open Friday in Kathmandu and there are no more lines.
16The popular Hermann's bakery at Lalitpur, a Kathmandu suburb, is selling fresh bread and croissants, which were quickly snapped up.
17Krishna Maharjan, who brought green onions and cauliflower from his farm on a bicycle, says he and his fellow farmers on the outskirts of Kathmandu are now delivering produce to the city.
18"We are trying to get as much fresh food to the people as possible. I feel it is our small contribution, but that's what we can do and every little bit helps," he says.
19Farmers who miss the planting season that is expected to start late May onwards will be unable to harvest rice - the country's staple food - again until late 2016.
20The U.N. Food and Agriculture Organization says this, together with likely losses of food stocks and wheat and maize harvests, would severely limit food supplies and incomes in the South Asian country, where around two-thirds of people rely on agriculture for their livelihood.
21The U.N. humanitarian chief, Valerie Amos, is in Nepal for a three-day visit to meet victims and local leaders.
22She plans to visit areas outside the Kathmandu Valley, according to the deputy spokesman for the U.N. secretary-general, Farhan Haq.
23The U.N. humanitarian office says about 24,000 people are living in 13 camps in Kathmandu.
24More than 130,000 houses are reportedly destroyed.
25The main challenges are the "inaccessibility of some remote areas, the lack of helicopters, poor communication and security concerns."
26Police say a woman in her 20s has been rescued five days after Nepal's massive earthquake.
27A police official identified the woman as Krishna Devi Khadka and said she was rescued Thursday evening in an area near Kathmandu's main bus terminal.
28The official asked not to be identified because he wasn't authorized to speak to the media.
29Earlier Thursday, a 15-year-old boy, Pemba Tamang, was pulled, dazed and dusty, from the wreckage of a seven-story building elsewhere in Kathmandu.
