Official: Mexico arrests Zetas leader Omar Trevino Morales

1Cartel leader Omar Trevino Morales, alias "Z-42," had a $2 million reward for his capture.
2MEXICO CITY (AP) - Mexican police and soldiers on Wednesday arrested the man widely considered to be the most important leader of the Zetas drug cartel, Omar Trevino Morales, a federal official said.
3The official, who was not authorized to be quoted by name due to government policy, said the man known as "Z-42" was arrested in a pre-dawn raid in San Pedro Garza Garcia, a wealthy suburb of the northern city of Monterrey.
4The Mexican government had offered a 30-million peso ($2 million) reward for his capture on weapons and organized crime charges.
5The U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration had offered a $5 million reward for his capture, saying he was wanted on drug-trafficking charges, but listed "Omar" as an alias and his given name as Alejandro.
6The suspect is the brother of Miguel Angel Trevino Morales, described as the most bloodthirsty leader of Mexico's most violent cartel.
7Miguel Angel was arrested in July 2013, almost a year after marines killed the Zetas' other biggest leader, Heriberto Lazcano "El Lazca."
8The Zetas carved a path of brutality, bloodshed and mutilated bodies across northern Mexico during their turf battles with the rival Gulf cartel.
9Much of the violence along Mexico's northeast border, however, is now due to internal battles among factions of the Gulf cartel.
10The capture of Omar Trevino Morales comes just a few days after Friday's arrest of another big cartel leader, Servando Gomez, alias "La Tuta."
11Gomez allegedly led the Knights Templar, a pseudo-religious drug gang that built up control of many sectors of the economy in the western state of Michoacan.
12Like Gomez, Omar Trevino Morales was captured without any shots being fired.
13The Trevino Morales brothers took proceeds from drug sales in the United States and laundered them by purchasing American quarter horses.
14That scheme was led by Jose Trevino Morales, a third brother.
15A jury in Texas found Morales guilty in May of investing $16 million of drug money in the buying, training and racing of horses across the Southwest.
