Apple will replace AT&T in the Dow Jones industrial average

1NEW YORK (AP)?
2Apple is in.
3AT&T is out.
4In another milestone for the popular and profitable iPhone giant, Apple will replace AT&T in the venerable Dow Jones industrial average on March 19, the manager of the index announced Friday.
5The move isn't likely to impact the 30-stock index much, and will have no effect on the fortunes of the two companies.
6But market experts say it does have symbolic importance, sort of like getting an Oscar at the Academy Awards?
7or at least a nomination.
8The change cements Apple as "the gold standard of technology," says Daniel Ives, a financial analyst at FBR Research.
9"They've really become the modern-day Wright Brothers."
10The reshuffling of the 119-year-old Dow, a barometer of market fortune and folly once dominated by railroads, also reflects a changed business world.
11"It underscores that technology continues to be a critical driver of the overall economy," says Edward Jones analyst Bill Kreher.
12Apple is the world's most valuable company.
13Its market value on the stock exchange, or what it would take to buy all its shares, closed last month above $700 billion, a first for any company.
14Apple won't get top billing in the Dow, though.
15Thanks to a quirk in the way the index is calculated, that honor will go to a company a little over a tenth as valuable: Goldman Sachs.
16The Dow weights companies by how much it costs to buy a single share, not all of them.
17On Friday, a Goldman share fetched $186.91 versus $126.60 for Apple.
18For all the symbolic importance, the trigger for the move is less colorful.
19The manager of the index, the S&P Dow Jones Indices, said it's making the change in response to a planned stock split for Visa, another Dow member.
20After its four-to-one split, Visa will wind up with a lower price.
21S&P said that would reduce the weight of the information technology sector in the Dow because Visa, a credit-card and payment-processing giant, counts as a tech stock.
22Adding Apple will help balance out this reduction.
23S&P Dow Jones Indices said the decision to fold in Apple won't alter the overall level of the index, which stood at 17,856 Friday.
24S&P Dow Jones is casting the move as a sort of a housekeeping maneuver, a way to ensure that the index better reflects the U.S. economy and markets.
25The switch is not a reflection of its view of Apple.
26"This doesn't mean we like the stock, or don't like the stock, or something like that," says David Blitzer, chairman of the index committee at S&P Dow Jones.
27While the Dow change wasn't triggered by anything AT&T did, it comes at a challenging time for the phone giant.
28Its stock has risen just 3.5 percent in the past 12 months.
29That compares with a 10.4 percent gain in the Standard & Poor's 500.
30Apple, meanwhile, has jumped 67 percent.
31To keep growing, AT&T has had to look beyond cellphones?
32to tablets and connected cars, for example.
33Adding a tablet to a phone plan gets AT&T another $10 in monthly service fees.
34The Dallas-based company is also trying to wean customers off equipment subsidies and shift them toward installment plans in which they ultimately pay full price for a phone.
35In its most recent quarter, AT&T booked a loss due to one-time expenses.
36But its revenue rose 4 percent as it added 1.9 million subscribers, double the year-ago quarterly increase.
37AT&T has bounced in and out of the blue chip average over the Dow's long history.
38It first entered in 1916 as American Telephone & Telegraph, joining Central Leather, Studebaker and other industrial giants in an elite club of 20 companies.
39Much later, in 2004, AT&T was kicked out only to return the following year when it merged with SBC Communications.
40Created in 1896, the Dow is one of the oldest gauges of stocks.
41Grover Cleveland was U.S. president that year.
42Companies like the Pacific Mail Steamship were counted among its ranks.
43The index tracks only 30 stocks.
44The Standard & Poor's 500 reflects the moves of 500.
45The S&P 500 also ranks companies differently, assigning weights based on the value of all a company's shares, not just one.
46That gives Apple more influence on daily moves in the index than any other stock.
47It accounts for nearly 4 percent of the index.
48Professional investors focus more on the S&P 500 because they think it reflects the stock market better.
49They tend to use that index, not the Dow, to judge the performance of their own portfolios.
50Ordinary investors also prefer the S&P 500, judging from the money they've put into index funds that mimic its performance.
51Investors have $4 trillion in 1,261 funds that track the S&P 500, according to Morningstar.
52That compares with just $13.6 billion in 12 Dow-based index funds.
53Still, the Dow can't be dismissed as a relic.
54It continues to be much cited and isn't seen as wildly distorted.
55One reason is that, for all its flaws, the Dow has largely mirrored the ups and downs of the much larger S&P 500.
56In the past 12 months, for instance, the Dow has risen 8.7 percent versus the S&P 500's 10.4 percent.
57The last big Dow shake-up came in September 2013, when Goldman Sachs, Nike and Visa knocked out Alcoa, Bank of America and Hewlett-Packard.
