Greek store closures spike as austerity returns

1ATHENS, Greece - Running a business in Koukaki is becoming a struggle.
2Shop-owners in the central Athens neighborhood, one of the capital city's most financially diverse, are finding it a lot more difficult to get by.
3They could be cutting hair or selling extra-large shirts - it makes no difference.
4Their tales of hardship can be repeated up and down the country of nearly 11 million people.
5Empty storefronts are again a feature of Greece's towns and cities amid a crisis that put Greece's future in the euro in doubt.
6The downturn worsened after the late-June decision by the Greek government to impose a series of strict controls on the free flow of money, with a paltry 60-euro ($66) a day limit on daily withdrawals from ATMs.
7Though banks reopened this week for the first time in more than three weeks, the ATM withdrawal limit is unchanged and cash is becoming scarce.
8For an economy where cash payments are the norm, that's a problem.
9In Koukaki, about 2 kilometers (1.2 miles) south of downtown Athens, 65-year-old mechanic Giorgos Prasinoudis is angry.
10His wife and 11-year-old daughter have already moved to Germany - the country that's ironically blamed for many of the economic and social problems afflicting Greece.
11On Wednesday, he sat drinking coffee on the sidewalk outside his motorcycle repair shop, with posters of bikes and children's drawings pinned to the wall.
12He's closed the store after 32 years.
13A "For Sale" sign is taped to the window.
14"It's over for Greece. We won't recover for another 50 years," he said.
15"The country borrowed so much money, those who benefited left the country, and ordinary people have been handed the bill ... I hope my daughter learns German and doesn't come back. Not even for a holiday."
16Prasinoudis is one of the countless victims of Greece's economic crisis.
17Locked out of international bond markets in the spring of 2010, the country has relied on foreign rescue money to pay its debts - on condition that tough austerity measures, such as cuts to spending and increases in taxes were imposed.
18The cost has been huge.
19A million jobs, mostly in the private sector, have been lost since then - around a fifth of the country's workforce.
20But after appearing to stabilize last year, the Greek economy has gone into reverse but unemployment remains high.
21At last count, unemployment was still over 25 percent and more than 50 percent for the under-25s.
22Alongside the capital controls, the government imposed a new round of austerity, raising sales taxes and levies on businesses, while maintaining emergency taxes on households that have eaten up disposable incomes.
23Early Thursday, parliament approved a second round of measures demanded by rescue creditors for a new bailout.
24Retail associations fear a return to the peak levels of unemployment around 2012 when they were hit by a surge of business failures.
25The main commercial street in Koukaki is beginning to look a lot it did back then: Vanishing behind whitewashed front windows are a bakery, grocer, barber, and clothes shops.
26Two blocks away from the repair shop, hardware store owner Costas Kitsos says sales have plummeted since capital controls were imposed on June 29.
27"People come into the shop but they ask about the prices and don't buy anything," he said.
28Five years after the first bailout by Greece's partners in the euro currency and the International Monetary Fund, some 85,000 retailers have gone out of business and the sector has lost 45 percent of its income, according to the National Confederation of Greece Commerce.
29Constantine Michalos, head of the Athens Chamber of Commerce, wrote a letter to the finance ministry this week urging the government to take urgent measures to help businesses stay open, particularly those struggling to import goods from abroad.
30"In essence the bank holiday has not ended," he wrote.
31"The vast majority of Greek companies are half a step from going out of business."
