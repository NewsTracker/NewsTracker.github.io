Rainbow Facebook photos: Armchair activism or shifting tide?

1Rainbow Facebook photos: Armchair activism or shifting tide?
2You may have noticed your Facebook friends getting considerably more colorful.
3More than 26 million Facebook profile photos have taken on a rainbow hue in the days since the U.S. Supreme Court ruled on Friday that marriage is a right guaranteed under the Constitution regardless of a person's sexual orientation.
4People have been covering their profile photos with the Facebook-supplied overlay that uses the best-known symbol of the lesbian, gay, bisexual and transgender rights movement: the rainbow.
5Call it armchair activism.
6Call it a mark of a shifting tide in public opinion.
7The rainbows are the latest sign of the important place social media has taken in our lives, when it comes to self-expression, politics and privacy.
8Rainbow-tinted celebrities have popped up all around, and not just Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg.
9Arnold Schwarzenegger, Leonardo DiCaprio and "Fifty Shades of Grey" author E.L. James are among those that have used the filter.
10Leslie Gabel-Brett, director of education and public affairs at Lambda Legal, a national nonprofit that focuses on legal issues affecting the LGBT community, said the overlay is "fun" and "effective."
11But she said it's also important for people know there are other ways to show support.
12While the people who've used the overlay is a fraction of Facebook's 1.4 billion users worldwide, the number is far bigger than the last mass profile photo change on the site.
13In 2013, some 3 million Facebook users changed their photos to show a pink-on-red equal sign in support of gay marriage.
14Four years earlier, in what might have been the first large-scale profile-photo activism, Twitter users turned their photo green to support pro-democracy protesters in Iran.
15Michelle Zubiate Ferchaw, a mother of two who lives in Anaheim Hills, California, found out about the Supreme Court decision on Facebook, she "cried tears of relief and of joy."
16Many of her "equally joyous" Facebook friends were turning rainbow, so she did the same.
17"It was a great opportunity to join the celebration," she wrote in a Facebook message.
18To get the Facebook-suppled filter, users click on someone else's rainbow picture.
19Or they go to the "Celebrate Pride" page Facebook set up.
20The rainbows didn't come without controversy.
21Not long after the rainbows appeared, so did questions about whether Facebook was tracking people who changed their photos and what it was going to do with the information.
22Facebook quickly shot those down.
23"We haven't experimented with anything, and other than counting how people used it, we aren't using the data for anything else," wrote Alex Schultz, a vice president at Facebook, in a post.
24Facebook also said it is not using the filters for ad targeting nor does it plan to do so.
25Facebook said the fact that the filter popped up on the same day as the Supreme Court decision was not planned any more than the Supreme Court planned to issue its ruling the Friday before gay pride parades in San Francisco, New York and elsewhere.
26The filter was created last week by two Facebook summer interns, Austin Freel, 21, and Scott Buckfelder, 33.
27They said they wanted to help other Facebook employees "show their spirit" for Pride Week, which fell on the last week of June in the San Francisco Bay Area.
28The filter was popular among employees, so Facebook rolled it out to regular users.
29Since then, others also have turned to rainbow as well.
30Ride-hailing app Uber, for example, added tiny rainbows to the cars on its map.
31And on Wednesday, Beyonce posted a video of herself dancing in various rainbow-colored outfits with the hashtag "lovewins."
32Counter-filters also have popped up, with some users in Russia overlaying their profiles with the country's flag instead of a rainbow.
33In the U.S., the website Right Wing News created a tool that lets people place a filter of the American flag over their profile photos.
