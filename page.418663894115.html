Greek banks open, but transaction restrictions remain; prices on many goods and services rise

1ATHENS, Greece - Greeks will finally see their banks reopen at 8 a.m. local (0500 GMT) Monday morning, but many restrictions on transactions, including cash withdrawals, will remain.
2Also, many goods and services will become more expensive as a result of a rise in Value Added Tax approved by Parliament last Thursday, among the first batch of austerity measures demanded by Greece's creditors.
3The parliament also agreed to deep reforms in the pension system including a gradual phasing out of all early retirement options.
4In a decree published Saturday, the Greek government kept the daily cash withdrawal limit at 60 euros ($65) but added a weekly limit.
5For example, a depositor who doesn't withdraw cash on Monday can withdraw 120 euros ($130) on Tuesday, and so on, up to 420 euros ($455) a week.
6Bank customers will still not be able to cash checks, only deposit them into their accounts, and will not be able to get cash abroad with their credit or cash cards, only make purchases.
7There are also restrictions on opening new accounts or activating dormant ones.
8The decree also pushes back by a month, to August 26, the deadline for filing income tax returns.
9The decree came on the same day as Greece's coalition government swore in its new, reshuffled cabinet.
10Five prominent dissidents from the radical left Syriza party, the senior coalition party, were replaced.
11Four of them had voted against the agreement with Greece's creditors Thursday and the fifth had resigned before the vote.
12The most urgent business for the reshuffled government is to pass another batch of austerity measures by Wednesday or early Thursday.
13Among the goods that Greeks will find more expensive Monday morning are some meats, coffee, tea, cooking oils other than olive oil, cocoa, vinegar, salt, flowers, firewood, fertilizer, insecticides, sanitary towels and condoms.
14All these will see the VAT rise from 13% to 23%.
15Services whose VAT also goes up from 13% to 23% include restaurants and cafes, funeral parlors, taxis, cramming and tutorial schools - very popular with Greek students who want to make up for the deficiencies of the school system - language institutes and computer learning centers.
16Public transport fares are expected to rise early next month.
17Greece closed its banks beginning June 29 to prevent a bank run after the European Central Bank did not increase emergency funding as Greece's second bailout expired.
18After the Greek Parliament passed an agreement Thursday to seek a third bailout, the ECB raised its emergency funding to the cash-strapped Greek banks.
19Also, on Friday, the European Union decided to release a short-term loan of 7.16 billion ($7.75 billion) to help Greece pay back a loan due Monday to the ECB plus arrears owed to the International Monetary Fund.
