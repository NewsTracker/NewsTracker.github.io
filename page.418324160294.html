US stocks fall after Greek 'no' vote; European markets sink

1NEW YORK (AP) - Stocks in the U.S. fell in afternoon trading Monday following sharper drops in Europe and Asia as Greeks overwhelmingly voted to reject terms of the country's latest bailout package.
2U.S. government bond prices rose as investors sought safe places to park money.
3Oil drillers and other energy companies fell as the price of oil dropped 6 percent.
4The market declines were not as bad as many had feared, something analysts are crediting to the resignation of the Greek finance minister, which might help bailout talks resume.
5The International Monetary Fund said on Monday that it will help the country if asked.
6In Sunday's referendum on creditor proposals, 61 percent of Greeks voted "no," a much higher proportion than anticipated.
7Many in the markets fear that the decision has pushed Greece one step closer to leaving the euro.
8Greece's banks may soon run out of money and the country could be forced to issue its own currency.
9A so-called "Grexit" from the euro is considered one of the biggest risks facing the global economy.
10The Dow Jones industrial average fell 88 points, or 0.5 percent, to 17,641 as of 1:34 p.m. Eastern time.
11The Standard & Poor's 500 index gave up 11 points, or 0.5 percent, to 2,065.
12The Nasdaq composite fell 27 points, or 0.5 percent, to 4,982.
13The U.S. market is coming off its sharpest weekly decline in three months.
14In Europe, Germany's DAX fell 1.5 percent while the CAC-40 in France fell 2 percent.
15The FTSE 100 index of leading British shares was 0.8 percent lower.
16Sung Won Sohn, an economist at California State University, said that he expects a Greek exit to push up the value of the dollar as investors scramble for safety, making U.S. exports more expensive.
17He also thinks the European economy will slow.
18"Our economic growth will be slower, and in Europe, whose economy is the most important for the U.S., growth will slow," he said.
19Russ Koesterich, chief strategist at giant money manager BlackRock, wrote in note to clients that he doesn't think the Greece crisis poses a "longer-term" threat to the global economy or financial markets.
20Oil prices took a big hit.
21The benchmark U.S. contract tumbled $3.64 to $53.30 a barrel in New York.
22Bond prices rose.
23The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 2.31 percent from 2.39 percent late Thursday.
24U.S. markets were close Friday for Independence Day.
25With Greek banks still shuttered and the European Central Bank under pressure to stop its emergency liquidity measures, Greece may not have long to secure a deal with creditors.
26A meeting of the eurozone's 19 leaders has been called for Tuesday.
27Some hopes for progress in the talks grew Monday after Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis quit.
28His replacement may help unblock discussions with peers in the eurozone.
29Finance ministers also meet on Tuesday.
30Over months of negotiations, Varoufakis' relations with his peers in the eurozone had deteriorated significantly.
31"The fact that Varoufakis has resigned hints that the Greek government may at least be offering an olive branch given his reputation for using aggressive terms such as 'water-boarding' to describe the creditors' actions," said Jane Foley, a senior currency analyst at Rabobank International.
32The euro fell 0.4 percent to $1.1084.
33The dollar slipped 0.1 percent to 122.44 Japanese yen.
34The eurozone has taken steps after after years of trouble with Greece to limit damage from a default and euro exit.
35Banks in Europe no longer hold many Greek government bonds and the European Central Bank has pledged to pump liquidity into its financial system should fear spread through Europe.
36Still, investors are on edge.
37When the Greek government announced June 29 plans to hold a referendum and the closure of the country's banks, markets plunged around the world.
38In the U.S., the S&P 500 fell 2.1 percent, its biggest drop since the start of the year.
39On Monday, investors braced for a repeat as Asian markets opened sharply lower.
40By the end of their trading days, Japan's Nikkei 225 and South Korea's Kospi each dropped more than 2 percent.
41Hong Kong's Hang Seng sank 3.2 percent.
42But China bucked the trend.
43China's benchmark index, the Shanghai Composite climbed 2.4 percent after regulators and the securities industry intervened to prop up prices that had been falling in recent weeks.
44Among U.S. stocks making big moves, Aetna sank $9.3to $116.28, a 7 percent loss.
45That was the biggest slide in the S&P 500.
46The company agreed last week to buy rival health insurer Humana for $35 billion.
