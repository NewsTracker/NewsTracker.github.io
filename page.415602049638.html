Pope's drug war riffs offend 2nd biggest Catholic nation

1Then he says 'the devil is punishing Mexico.' Que pasa, Francisco?
2MEXICO CITY - Almost everyone seemed to love what Pope Francis had to say, until he opened his mouth about Mexico.
3He won the applause of millions of gay Catholics when he asked, "Who am I to judge them?"
4He was cheered for telling women they had a greater role in the church.
5He was named Time's Person of the Year.
6He even helped the United States and Cuba restore diplomatic relations.
7But a recent spat with Mexico, home to the world's second-biggest Catholic population, signaled the honeymoon could be over, at least in these parts.
8Francis first ruffled the feathers of the Mexican government in an e-mail he sent to a lawmaker in his native Argentina.
9"I hope we are in time to stop Mexicanization," the pope reportedly wrote Feb. 21, commenting how his home country has suffered from a rise in drug violence.
10"I was talking to Mexican bishops, and it is a question of terror."
11For the pope, Mexico had become the reference point for drug war mayhem.
12When the mail became public, Mexican Foreign Secretary Jose Antonio Meade sent a letter to the Vatican, complaining it was "stigmatizing" Mexico.
13"We express our sadness and concern regarding the comments apparently made in a letter from Pope Francis," Meade told a news conference.
14When the Vatican sent a reply that it meant no offense and Mexico's top diplomat said things were "completely settled," the matter seemed to be over.
15Until Pope Francis gave a follow-up interview to Mexico's biggest TV network and made an even more provocative comment.
16Drug violence, he told Televisa on March 12, was the devil punishing Mexico; Satan was angry because Mexicans are such devoted Catholics.
17"I think that the devil is punishing Mexico with great fury," Francis said.
18"The devil does not forgive Mexico because she (the Virgin Mary) showed her son there. That is my interpretation."
19As the statement was plastered across front pages, it sparked furious debate, and some anger, in a country that has a special relationship with the Holy See.
20Outside Mexico City's elegant metropolitan cathedral, several devotees coming from prayer this week said they were disappointed with the pontiff.
21"I believe in the church. But I think that saying that people have died because of some spiritual punishment is not very respectful to the suffering families," said Hector Cruz, 52, an engineer.
22Since 2007, cartel-related violence has killed more than 83,000 people, according to a government count.
23Many innocent people, including students and children, are among the victims.
24Several columnists questioned the wisdom of the pope's words.
25"Evil exists, without doubt, and its representation is Satan, the devil," Raul Rodriguez wrote in El Grafico.
26"But in this case, it is necessary to point the finger at corruption, criminals infiltrating the government, poverty, hunger, ideological confrontation and inequality as some of the causes of the explosion (of violence)."
27Eighty-two percent of Mexico's 120 million people are Catholic, according to the 2010 census, but the country has secular laws, keeping the church out of politics.
28The number of Catholics has fallen from 98% in 1950 as some Mexicans have turned to Protestantism or atheism.
29Religious anthropologist Elio Masferrer said the pope's message was more complicated than the scandalous-sounding headlines.
30In the interview, Francis talked about poverty and American drug consumption driving violence.
31"The pope said many more things than this comment. You have to analyze these messages carefully. He uses a lot of symbolic language," said Masferrer, president of the secular Latin American Religious Studies Association.
32"Mexicans are mixed in their feelings about this. While some may be offended, others agree with what Pope Francis says."
33Masferrer pointed out that Francis, who took his papal name after the saint of the poor, has supported victims of violence.
34In December, the Vatican's envoy in Mexico gave Mass to the families of disappeared students in the bloodstained state of Guerrero.
35The Rev.
36Hugo Valdemar, spokesman for the archdiocese of Mexico City, said the pope's comments simply reflect the age-old Catholic position.
37"Scripture states clearly that the devil is behind evil acts. This is in line with Catholic thinking, and one shouldn't make a scandal out of it," Valdemar told GlobalPost.
38Mexican Catholics have felt a special link to the Vatican since it supported them in the face of religious persecution after the Mexican Revolution, Valdemar said.
39They had a particular devotion to Pope John Paul II, who visited the nation five times, attracting crowds of millions.
40His successor, Pope Benedict XVI, visited Mexico in 2012 and drew more modest crowds before stepping down because of ill health.
41"There was a deep affection in Mexico for John Paul II, and those are hard shoes to fill," Valdemar said.
42"But Pope Francis can still win the hearts of the Mexican people."
