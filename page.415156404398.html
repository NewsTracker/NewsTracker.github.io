AIDS was passed to humans from gorillas as well as chimpanzees

UN warns against complacency as Ebola fight enters new phase

1Two of the four known groups of viruses that cause the disease have been traced back to the great apes that share about 98 per cent of their DNA with humans.
2It had been known simian immunodeficiency virus (SIV) - the monkey equivalent of HIV - had jumped from chimpanzees into humans, but now it seems gorillas in Cameroon also played a vital role.
3Scientists say understanding the disease's origins will help predict the future risk of an outbreak.
4The connection was uncovered after researchers analysed faecal samples collected in remote forests.
5They found HIV-1 groups known as 'O' and 'P' originated in western lowland gorillas.
6HIV-1 - the virus that causes AIDS - has jumped species to infect humans on at least four separate occasions.
7The other lineages, 'M' and 'N', are known to have started in geographically distinct chimpanzee communities in southern Cameroon.
8Group 'M' gave rise to the AIDS pandemic - infecting over 40 million people worldwide by spreading across Africa and throughout the rest of the world.
9Scientists found HIV-1 groups known as 'O' and 'P' originated in western lowland gorillas.
10Groups 'N' and 'P', at the other extreme, have only been found in a few individuals from Cameroon.
11But 'O' - although not as widespread and prevalent as 'M' - has nonetheless infected about 100,000 people in west central Africa.
12The team screened fecal samples from western lowland gorillas, eastern lowland gorillas, and mountain gorillas in Cameroon, Gabon, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and Uganda for evidence of SIV strains - the pre-cursors of HIV.
13HOW DID THE AIDS EPIDEMIC BEGIN?
14A new book claims the AIDS epidemic began in a rainforest in southeastern Cameroon in 1908 and not more than 70 years later when the virus started to be recognized in the early 1980s.
15For the book, which is subtitled 'How AIDS emerged from an African forest', David Quammen traced the history of AIDS by examining genetic samples from humans and chimps.
16This led him to believe the birthplace of the epidemic was the southeastern edge of Cameroon sometime around 1908.
17Quammen theorizes that a hunter in the rainforest was infected with a immunodeficiency virus similar to HIV by a chimp he killed and butchered.
18The hunter likely infected at least one other person through sex and the virus continued to make its way down the Sangha River in that matter until it reached the city of Leopoldville (now Kinshasa) in the Congo.
19After reaching the Congo, the virus spread even faster via the continued re-use of hypodermic syringes at health clinics, Mr Quammen said.
20They identified it in four communities of western lowland gorillas in southern Cameroon - and dubbed it 'SIVgor'.
21Professor Beatrice Hahn, of Pennsylvania University, said: 'Viral sequencing revealed a high degree of genetic diversity among the different gorilla samples.
22'Two of the gorilla virus lineages were particularly closely related to HIV-1 groups O and P.
23Dr Martine Peeters, of Montpelier University in France, said the study has implications for understanding emerging diseases and gauging future human infection risks.
24The origin of HIV has baffled scientists since 1984 when it was first shown it causes AIDS.
25Evidence soon pointed to Africa and a similar virus in chimpanzees.
26Many scientists suspect that the chimp virus jumped into humans who hunt and butcher these great apes.
27But gorillas are now emerging as having played a supporting role in the AIDS epidemic - possibly by humans hunting them.

2But U.N. Special Envoy on Ebola David Nabarro said the fight has reached a "second phase, getting to zero, and it really is the hardest part."
3Ebola has infected nearly 24,000 people and killed more than 9,600, according to World Health Organization statistics.
4Nabarro said eradicating the disease requires treating all those who are ill and tracking everyone they come in contact with.
5"It's a really difficult, painstaking task," he said in Brussels, on the eve of a major international conference on Ebola expected to draw around 600 delegates, including the leaders of Guinea, Liberia and Sierra Leone.
6According to U.N. figures, the number of new cases has dropped from some 800-900 per week at the height of the outbreak to around 100 per week.
7"We are reaching the most difficult phase, where there is a fatigue among the governments among the population, certainly among the donors, and we are not yet there," he said.
8The outbreak is focused in an area of West Africa about the size of France, with a population of about 20 million people, and where infrastructure is limited.
9Ebola has cost the three countries an estimated 12 percent of their gross domestic product, shifted resources causing other health care problems, and hurt business as people became scared to leave their homes or go to markets.
