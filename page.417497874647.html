CORRECTED-WRAPUP 7-FBI extends FIFA scrutiny to World Cup host bids of Russia, Qatar

1NEW YORK/ZURICH, June 3 (Reuters) - The FBI's investigation of bribery and corruption at FIFA includes scrutiny of how soccer's governing body awarded World Cup hosting rights to Russia and Qatar, a U.S. law enforcement official said.
2Russia and Qatar have denied wrongdoing in the conduct of their bids for the 2018 and 2022 tournaments, which were not the subject of charges announced by U.S. prosecutors a week ago against FIFA officials that stunned world soccer.
3The U.S. law enforcement official, who spoke to Reuters on condition of anonymity, said the review of the bids would be part of a probe that goes beyond the indictments.
4Among issues the FBI is examining is the stewardship of FIFA by longtime president Sepp Blatter, who unexpectedly announced on Tuesday he was resigning shortly before it emerged that he too was under investigation by U.S. law enforcement.
5Authorities said last week that they were investigating a case of $150 million paid in bribes over two decades while Swiss prosecutors announced their own criminal inquiry into the 2018 and 2022 bids.
6On Wednesday, the partially blacked out transcript of the November 2013 guilty plea of Charles Blazer, a U.S. citizen and FIFA executive committee member from 1997 to 2013, showed that Blazer and others in FIFA agreed to accept bribes in bidding for the 1998 World Cup in France and 2010 in South Africa.
7"Among other things, I agreed with other persons in or around 1992 to facilitate the acceptance of a bribe in conjunction with the selection of the host nation for the 1998 World Cup," Blazer told a federal judge in New York, according to the transcript.
8Blazer went on to say that from 2004 and through 2011 "I and others on the FIFA executive committee agreed to accept bribes in conjunction with the selection of South Africa as the host nation for the 2010 World Cup."
9In the case of Qatar, there was some surprise that the tournament was awarded to a small desert country with no real soccer tradition and where daytime summer temperatures can top 40 degrees Celsius (104F).
10Qatar's Foreign Minister Khaled al-Attiyah said there was no way Qatar would be stripped of its right to host the World Cup because it had had the best bid.
11"It is very difficult for some to digest that an Arab Islamic country has this tournament, as if this right can't be for an Arab state," he told Reuters in an interview in Paris.
12"I believe it is because of prejudice and racism that we have this bashing campaign against Qatar."
13For its part, Russia dismissed concerns it might lose the right to host the cup.
14"Cooperation with FIFA is going on and, most importantly, Russia is continuing preparations for the 2018 World Cup," President Vladimir Putin's spokesman, Dmitry Peskov, said.
15U.S. authorities said last week that their announcement was the beginning and not the end of the investigation.
16Attorney General Loretta Lynch said the Department of Justice looked forward to continuing to work with other countries.
17A source close to FIFA said it was Blatter's advisers who had told him he must quit.
18Critics pointed to the widening criminal probe, disquiet among sponsors, and pressure from European soccer body UEFA as possible reasons.
19The international police organisation Interpol put two former top FIFA officials on its wanted list at the request of U.S. authorities.
20Interpol issued wanted person alerts for Jack Warner, a former president of CONCACAF, which governs soccer in North and Central America and the Caribbean, and Nicolas Leoz, the ex-head of South America's soccer federation.
21The others subject to the "red notices" - which are not arrest warrants - are Alejandro Burzaco, Hugo and Mariano Jinkis, and Jose Margulies, a Brazilian who headed two companies involved in broadcasting soccer matches.
22They are among FIFA officials and sports media and promotion executives named in the U.S. indictment.
23FIFA has denied that another senior official, Secretary General Jerome Valcke, was involved in a $10 million payment approved by the South African Football Association that lies at the heart of the U.S. investigation.
24At a news conference in Johannesburg, sports minister Fikile Mbalula confirmed the payment to Warner during South Africa's successful bid for the 2010 World Cup but denied it was a bribe.
25Mbalula said the cash was intended for football development in the Caribbean, Warner's home region.
26Valcke said on Wednesday he was not guilty of corrupt practice relating to the payment and he saw no reason to resign.
27Blatter announced his decision to step down six days after police raided a hotel in Zurich and arrested several FIFA officials, and four days after he was re-elected to a fifth term as president.
28Blatter has not been charged and FIFA did not respond to a request for comment on his being under investigation.
29An election to choose a new president will probably not take place until at least December.
30Blatter, meanwhile, remains in his position.
31FIFA executive committee member Kozo Tashima of Japan told Japanese media that Blatter should go at once.
32Former England captain and Manchester United and Real Madrid midfielder David Beckham, who was a major figure in England's failed bid to host the 2018 World Cup, joined the chorus of calls for change at FIFA, the Federation Internationale de Football.
33"Some of the things that we now know happened were despicable, unacceptable and awful for the game that we love so much," Beckham told Sky Sports.
34Among potential candidates to lead FIFA, UEFA chief Michel Platini, a former French international soccer star, is the favourite.
35Jordan's Prince Ali Bin Al Hussein, who withdrew from last week's presidential election after winning 73 votes to Blatter's 133 in the first round, stopped short of confirming he would run again.
36Asked if there should be a fresh start at FIFA, he told Britain's Channel 4 News: "I'm willing to help."
37Chung Mong-joon, billionaire scion of South Korea's Hyundai conglomerate, said he would consider running.
38Possible candidates also include Domenico Scala, the independent chairman of FIFA's audit and compliance committee.
39Others could include former Brazil international Zico, Argentine soccer legend Diego Maradona, Jerome Champagne, a former French diplomat and FIFA deputy secretary general, and German Wolfgang Niersbach, an ex-media chief at FIFA.
