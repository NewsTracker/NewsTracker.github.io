11Forty years ago, Norman Kirk, who was then the Prime Minister of New Zealand, tried to define what people want from life and, by extension, what they seek in a political leader.
12We don't want much, he said, just 'someone to love, somewhere to live, somewhere to work and something to hope for'.
13He was talking not so much about politics as about fundamental human needs, but he intended it to carry political meaning.

29Across Europe, in Spain and Italy, for example, minority parties are making great gains by appealing to disillusioned voters.
30In Greece, as we have just seen, the threat of such an upstart party seizing control has become reality.
31When the leader of Syriza, the charismatic young Alexis Tsipras, made his acceptance speech, he chose one word to explain why the people of Greece had turned away from the old to risk the new.
32It was 'elpida', meaning 'hope'.
33In Britain, too, people desperately want to vote for someone who will give them hope - that their voice will be listened to at last.
34The new challenge for the old establishment is to find an emotional tone of voice to appeal to those people.
35For in the real world away from Westminster, countless thousands of votes will be cast that are motivated not by the small print of party manifestos, but by something deeper and perhaps less well understood.
36Which takes us back to the former PM of New Zealand Norman Kirk, and the reason why I have been spending so much time recently in Lowestoft filming for the BBC1 Panorama programme.
37In the final month before the start of the campaign proper, Panorama is putting aside its usual diet of hard-edged investigation and, using Kirk's four themes, is trying to paint a picture of Britain that is as much emotional as factual.
38Lowestoft may not be the first town you'd choose to look for hope.
39Politically it's fascinating, because it's one of those bellwether constituencies much loved by political nerds.
40For 30 years, the party that has won Lowestoft has won Westminster.
41But, if the polls are to be believed, this election might be the one that breaks the mould.
42From Clapton to Cleethorpes, the seaside towns of the East Coast appear to be looking for a new saviour.
43And that saviour may well be clad in Ukip colours.
44Such is the disillusion at the three traditional parties that in the Lowestoft district in last May's European elections, Ukip polled nearly 50 per cent more votes than the Tories, with Labour and others even further behind.
45Why?
46Fishing - the industry that sustained the town over the generations - is dying, if not dead.
47One of the most haunting sights on the outskirts of the Suffolk town is what locals call The Nets: an acre of land on the seashore covered with frames built from sturdy timbers where the huge drift nets were hung to dry.
48No nets hang there today.
49A century ago, there were more than 500 trawlers and drift netters registered to operate out of here.
50Now, just ten boats remain: small inshore vessels no more than ten metres long.
51Their skippers fish the way men have fished for millennia: lines slung over the sides of the boat with baited hooks.
52This is a hard way to earn a living.
53I went out with Steve Wightman, whose family have been working these waters for at least 200 years.
54A vicious north-east wind made the sea too rough to catch anything, and I was mightily relieved when it was decided to return to port, but Steve wasn't complaining.
55As he said, real fishermen don't complain about the weather; they live with it.
56But I heard many complaints at the weekly fish auction on the quayside - all summed up in a few words: Europe's fishing regulations, and the limited quotas they impose on each fisherman in terms of what species they can catch and how much of it.
57Steve's wife, Emma, told me: 'You can cope with the weather and you can cope with the tides and getting up at two in the morning in the freezing cold, but you can't cope with throwing half your day's catch back into the sea when you've got bills to pay and children to feed.
58Do they believe the Coalition is fighting tooth and nail on their behalf against Brussels?
59The dramatic show of support here for Ukip's anti-EU message is the clearest possible answer.

61Lowestoft has another reason for its anger.
62A bridge is the perfect metaphor for hope: it overcomes obstacles.
63But several times a day, the town grinds to a halt because the only bridge across the harbour that joins the two halves of the town has to be raised to allow ships through.
64Everyone agrees there is a desperate need for a new one, but politicians from across the spectrum and all the way to Westminster have been arguing about it for 40 years, and still nothing happens.
65The disenchantment such issues provokes is what should worry members of the old political establishment, if they ever manage to spend longer than ten minutes on flying visits to towns such as Lowestoft - towns that are geographically isolated and struggling to rebuild their futures on the ruins of dying industries.
66Not one of the people I spoke to in Lowestoft had anything but disdain for the traditional parties.
67Maybe the people here will get new jobs one day, but there is a powerful sense that their problems will be solved in spite of politicians rather than because of them.
68There is also suspicion that too many politicians subscribe to what has become a fashionable view in some metropolitan circles: that towns like theirs represent a Britain that's going nowhere and thus should be ignored.
69Add to that the belief in many corners of the country that the political establishment is happier scoring points in the House of Commons and patronising the electorate with meaningless slogans - such as calling them 'everyday' folk - and you end up with a pretty toxic brew.
70For our ruling classes to remind themselves how much hope matters might prove a useful antidote.
71They could, at least, start by digging out Norman Kirk's old speech.
