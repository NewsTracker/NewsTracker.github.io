Game of chicken over Greece risks slipping out of control

1Greece's Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis arrives for a cabinet meeting at Greek Prime Minister's office in Athens, Greece, on Sunday, June 21, 2015.
2Greece is struggling to reach a deal with its creditors for new loans that it needs to avoid defaulting on debt payments at the end of the month.
3Without the bailout, Greece could be headed for bankruptcy or an exit from the 19-nation eurozone.
4French Finance Minister Michel Sapin, left, speaks with Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis during a round table meeting of EU finance ministers at the European Council building in Luxembourg on Friday, June 19, 2015.
5Europe was scrambling Friday to pick up the pieces after another failed meeting over Greece's bailout that reinforced fears that the country was heading for bankruptcy and a possible euro exit.
6Greece's Defense Minister Panos Kamenos arrives for a cabinet meeting at Greek Prime Minister's office in Athens, Greece, on Sunday, June 21, 2015.
7Greece's Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis enters the Greek Prime Minister's office to attend a cabinet meeting in Athens, Greece, on Sunday, June 21, 2015.
8In the game of chicken between Greece and its creditors over whether the troubled country gets more bailout cash, each side has played its part, refusing to back down.
9Now time is running out and the politicians behind the opposing steering wheels are dangerously close to getting what neither side wants: a head-on collision.
10Greece's bailout program expires in 11 days and money is draining from the country's banks.
11The resulting financial blowup could slip out of control and lead to Greece's departure from the 19-nation eurozone.
12While it would be devastating economically for Greece, some predict it would be manageable for Europe and the global economy.
13The truth is nobody really knows.
14Whether that's a risk worth taking will be the question facing the government leaders from the 19 countries that use the shared euro currency when they hold yet another emergency summit on Monday.
15Greece's eurozone partners - led by Germany - are willing to lend it more money.
16But only under conditions, including Greek cuts on spending and tax increases.
17Greece's radical left-wing government says such austerity policies are smothering the economy and wants Greece's debts owed to other eurozone countries partly forgiven.
18One crunch date is June 30.
19That's when Greece's bailout program expires and the last 7.2 billion euros ($8.1 billion) left in it will no longer be available.
20On the same day, Greece has to pay the International Monetary Fund 1.6 billion euros ($1.8 billion) and it doesn't have the money to do so.
21If it doesn't pay, it won't be immediately declared in default by ratings agencies.
22But the IMF also says it wouldn't be able to lend Greece new funds until the arrears are taken care of.
23As the days go by and Greece and its creditors bet the other side will fold, the risk is that the politicians will be overtaken by events.
24The threat many economists cite as the major one: the possibility of a run on the banks in Greece.
25The Greek government could try to stem that by imposing limits on withdrawals.
26If the banks are seen as failing and the government is defaulting on its obligations, the European Central Bank would eventually face a decision on whether to end the emergency credit it allows Greeks banks to draw on to survive.
27The ECB, which represents all 19 eurozone countries, would be risking losing central bank money on a failing banking system.
28Out of euros, the Greek government might have to print a new currency to rescue the banks and to pay its bills.
29So far, Greeks have been holding on.
30There has been no bank run, more like a slower "bank jog," with no big lines in front of cash machines.
31Still, the outflow of money from Greek banks appears to have picked up this week.
32But pulling the plug on Greece is the last thing the ECB wants to do.
33It wants the politicians to choose, not unelected central bankers, whether Greece is in or out of the euro.
34Cutting off banking support would be "a profoundly political decision," says Commerzbank chief economist Joerg Kraemer.
35"The ECB will therefore delay its decision for as long as possible."
36So what Greece could be heading for, within just a few days, is an unsettling twilight zone where it is running out of cash, remains cut off from borrowing on financial markets, has imposed limits on bank withdrawals and is missing debt payments.
37Kraemer says that all depends on the big political question: "whether the creditors actually want to keep Greece in the monetary union."
