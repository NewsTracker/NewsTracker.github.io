Long odds for insanity defense in Colorado movie theater massacre trial - VIDEO: Opening arguments to begin in trial - VIDEO: Insanity defense in Holmes trial puts burden to prosecutors

1June 4, 2013: Aurora theater shooting suspect James Holmes is seated in court in Centennial, Colo.
2Holmes faces trial starting on April 27, 2015, in the mass shooting in an Aurora, Colo., movie theater that left 12 dead and 70 wounded.
3CENTENNIAL, Colo.
4- The key to the death penalty trial of a man who methodically shot at moviegoers at a Batman movie premiere will be what was going on inside his mind as he threw smoke canisters and then marched up and down the aisles, firing at anyone who tried to flee.
5James Holmes acknowledges killing 12 people and wounding 70 more inside the packed theater, but has pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity.
6His lawyers will argue that he was too addled by mental illness to tell right from wrong.
7And unlike most other states, Colorado puts the burden on prosecutors in insanity cases: They must convince jurors beyond a reasonable doubt that Holmes was sane.
8It adds another obstacle for a state that has already spent millions to manage an outsized number of victims, hundreds of witnesses and more than 85,000 pages of evidence.
9Even so, experts say Holmes faces long odds.
10Insanity defenses are successful in only 25 percent of felony trials nationally, even less so in homicides.
11"Lay people tend to think of people with mental illness as extremely dangerous, and that also influences jurors, especially if someone has killed someone," said Christopher Slobogin, a professor of law and psychiatry at Vanderbilt Law School.
12"Usually there's evidence of intent and planning that seems to be counterintuitive to the lay view of mental illness."
13Winning a trial on mental-health grounds is rare, but then again, so is a jury trial for a mass shooter, many of whom are killed by police, kill themselves or plead guilty.
14A review of 160 mass shootings found killers went to trial 74 times, and just three were found insane, according to Grant Duwe, a Minnesota corrections official who wrote the book "Mass Murder in the United States: A History."
15Just one mass shooter has won a mental-health case in the last two decades, Duwe said: Michael Hayes, who shot nine people, killing four, in North Carolina in 1988.
16Based on that, Holmes "faces some pretty long odds," he said.
17Holmes was arrested almost immediately, while stripping off his body armor in the parking lot outside the Century 16 movie theater.
18That he was the shooter who replaced Hollywood violence with real human carnage has never been in doubt.
19The victims include a 6-year-old girl, two active-duty servicemen, a single mom and an aspiring broadcaster who had survived a mall shooting in Toronto.
20Several died shielding friends or loved ones.
21Nearly three years have passed as trial preparations were stalled by complicated legal debates over capital punishment and insanity pleas.
22Prosecutors will argue that the once-promising doctoral candidate in neuroscience plotted and planned for months, amassing guns, ammunition, tear gas grenades and enough chemicals to turn his dingy apartment into a potentially lethal booby trap that could have caused even more carnage.
23They'll ask jurors to find him guilty, and if so, sentence him to death rather than life without parole.
24If jurors decide instead that Holmes was insane at the time of the shooting, he would be committed indefinitely to a state psychiatric hospital.
25Dueling mental health evaluations could factor in their deliberations.
26Judge Carlos A. Samour Jr. ordered a second exam after prosecutors said the first was biased.
27Defense attorneys have objected to the results of the second one, suggesting it might not help them.
28Like many other details, the results of both exams have been kept from public view.
29Also secret is the list of people expected to testify.
30Holmes faces 166 counts, including first-degree murder, attempted murder and an explosives offense.
31The judge read each charge, naming each victim, out loud.
32It took nearly two hours.
33Holmes' trial could take at least four months or more and is sure to be emotionally wrenching.
34Jury selection alone took nearly three months as attorneys and the judge settled on 12 jurors and 12 alternates from a pool of 9,000.
35Experts said the jury selection was among the largest and most complex in history, in part because it was so difficult to find people who weren't personally affected by the shooting.
36Holmes' parents begged prosecutors to consider a plea deal sparing his life and avoiding a drawn-out trial.
37But some survivors want Holmes executed, even if that means reliving horrific details.
38"My intent to go down there is to see that that guy gets convicted of all 166 counts that were against him," said Tom Sullivan, whose son, Alex, was killed while celebrating his 27th birthday and wedding anniversary.
39David Hoover wants Holmes executed to avenge the death of his 18-year-old nephew, A.J. Boik.
