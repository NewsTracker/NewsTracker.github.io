Apple Pay launches in UK as major hold-out signs up

1Apple introduced its mobile payments service to Britain on Tuesday as Barclays confirmed its participation in the U.S. tech giant's move to capitalize on the increasing number of consumers who are comfortable making tap-and-go purchases.
2A quarter of a million outlets will offer Apple Pay, from London Underground stations to coffee shops, retailers and travel businesses, making it more widely available than when it first launched in the United States last year.
3Tap-and-go payments have surged in recent years, with growing acceptance among banks, retailers, card issuers and consumers, but the real tipping point in Britain came with the London Underground's introduction of "contactless" payment methods.
4The Apple Pay logo is displayed in a mobile kiosk sponsored by Visa and Wells Fargo to demonstrate the new Apple Pay mobile payment system.
5"We actually think that the U.K. can be our leading market for Apple Pay, given the unique characteristics (of the market)," said Jennifer Bailey, vice president of Apple Pay.

8Convenience, as ever, is key and Apple says its new service is even quicker than rival contactless payment options.
9Set-up is via an Apple app on an iPhone 6 or late-model iPad.
10Customers use the built-in camera to scan an image of their credit or debit cards and then confirm their details by text message, email or a phone call from their bank.
11To make payments, customers hold their phone, tablet or Apple Watch near a merchant's existing contactless terminal while touching the mobile device's fingerprint ID button or double-tapping the watch face.
12The beauty of the scheme for Apple is that it offers another way to bind customers more tightly to its phones, tablets and watches, while also taking a small slice of the bank transaction fees on each payment made.
13Apple Pay will eventually be supported by all major British banks.
14The last hold-out, Barclays, confirmed on Tuesday that its customers will be to use Apple Pay in the future.
15HSBC Holdings will offer Apple Pay later this month and other major banks have said they will introduce it in the autumn.
16In the meantime, 70 percent of all credit and debit cards will accept Apple Pay, including cards offered by MasterCard, Visa Europe and American Express.
17That figure will top 82 percent once Barclaycard is ready, Bailey said.
18"We have been surprised that so many banks have signed up to support Apple Pay," said Benjamin Ensor, an analyst at Forrester Research.
19"It's worth asking what Apple Pay brings that the banks can't do themselves."
20His answer is that Apple has managed to win over banks, retailers and even telecoms network operators that had refused to cooperate with one another for similar services.
21Ensor, however, has yet to be convinced that Apple Pay will win both the hearts and minds of customers in the way the company's other products have.
22"Are people crying out for a really different method of making payments? I think that's not yet clear," he said.
23"Consumers just want to buy stuff."
24Tony Prestedge, chief operating officer of Nationwide, discusses Apply Pay, which comes to the U.K. in July, and why banks are supporting it.
25Apple has played its part in that respect, winning support from London Underground and retailers including pharmacy chain Boots, Costa Coffee and supermarket chains Marks and Spencer and Waitrose.
26The U.S. company had learnt from Apple Pay's launch in its home market last October, when a number of major chains pushed back because the service did not work with the customer loyalty cards that many retailers rely on to collect customer data.

28Last month Apple said it would begin accepting private-label credit cards and that retail chains can work with the company to upgrade their loyalty programs to work with Apple Pay.
29"We are actively working with U.K. retailers on loyalty programs," Bailey said.
30Analysts said that while contactless payments have caught on in London, adoption has been slower across the country.
31However, Apple said it was working with retailers and e-commerce sites to support take-up.
32No credit card information is stored within Apple Pay, reducing vulnerability to hackers.
33It also relies on higher levels of user identification, such as fingerprints.
34"With multiple levels of security, it's designed from the beginning to be more secure than a credit card," Bailey said.
35But for all its efforts in the United States and Britain, the speed with which Apple has expanded in consumer electronics through international product launches will be hard to match with Apple Pay.
36The service does not benefit from a unified global payments market and will have to launch country by country.
