Nepal sets up post-quake camps for sexual minorities

1"As soon as my sexual orientation became known to others in the camp, they turned their back on me," said K.C., sitting on a plastic mat in a tent, arranging her long black hair in a bun.
2The 7.8 magnitude quake, which rattled the Himalayan nation on April 25, killed more than 8,000 people, and left around two million more in urgent need of food, water and shelter.
3Many survivors live in hard-to-reach mountainous regions where aid delivery is difficult.
4But even though aid is more accessible in the capital Kathmandu, problems including discrimination against lesbian, gay, bisexual and transgender (LGBT) people have prompted charities to set up 15 relief camps for sexual minorities.
5"They are more vulnerable than others in terms of their access to relief and safety after the earthquake," said Dibya Raj Paudel from the Nepal Red Cross Society, which is providing tarpaulin sheets, water purification tablets and other items to LGBT people in camps like the one K.C. sought refuge in.
6"We have supported the needs of this group as many of them don't get any help from their families and are left alone after the disaster," Paudel added.
7Nepal has become increasingly progressive regarding LGBT rights since a decade-long Maoist civil war ended in 2006, and a feudal monarchy was abolished two years later.
8But there is still no clear legislation on the rights of the estimated 350,000 LGBT people, many of whom still face harassment and discrimination.
9In 2007, Nepal's Supreme Court ordered the government to end discrimination against sexual minorities, and make laws to guarantee their equal rights.
10Lauded by activists, the judgment emboldened many LGBT people to come out and assert their identities.
11Same-sex marriages have also taken place in public - though they are still not recognised in law - and gay pride parades and beauty contests or "Pink Pageants" have been held in Kathmandu.
12But there is still no law in place because politicians are still writing Nepal's first post-monarchy constitution which is expected to ensure the rights of underprivileged and marginalised groups including the LGBT community.
13"A lot has been done to ensure the rights of sexual minorities," said Laxmi Ghalan, a lesbian.
14"But people's thinking and mindset are very slow to change. Even politicians use us for votes but do little in terms of our rights," she said as her friends sat on a patch of grass and prepared boiled lentils, pumpkin and rice for dinner in a camp.
15Some transgender people, who made their living as sex workers, were left with no business after the earthquake, while others cannot seek support from their families who have shunned them due to their orientation.
16There are no toilets established in camps for those who consider themselves the third gender, and they face ostracism by other survivors.
17They also find it hard to rent alternative accommodation due to discrimination by landlords.
18LGBT activists say the authorities, which are carrying out assessments of the damage done to houses and properties, are insensitive to the needs of sexual minorities, and are concerned that they will get little support to rebuild their lives.
19In a post disaster situation, sexual minorities have their own specific needs, they say, which must be included the government's Post Disaster Needs Assessment (PDNA).
20"They urgently need food, blankets, clothes and medicines," said Manisha Dhakal, executive director of the Blue Diamond Society, which is collecting details of the loss of life and property faced by the LGBT members in the quake's aftermath.
21"We must have a reach, our voice and views must be included in the PDNA."
