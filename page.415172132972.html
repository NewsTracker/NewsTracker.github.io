Courting Disaster: 'Obamacare Came Just In Time For Us'

Iraqi forces try to seal off Islamic State around Tikrit

1The Supreme Court will hear arguments Wednesday over a lawsuit that would eliminate health insurance subsidies in more than 30 states.
2When Jay Joshi first heard about the Supreme Court case that could take away the health insurance subsidies that enabled her family to get covered, "it came as a total shock," she said.
3Joshi's husband, Kaye, has diabetes and was uninsured before the Affordable Care Act went into effect.
4The family was paying full price for his insulin at the pharmacy or stocking up on cheaper medicines during their visits every two years to India, the country they left more than three decades ago for life in the United States.
5"He's 62 and I'm 60, so this is the age when I think health issues start cropping up. So we really needed some health insurance, and Obamacare came just in time for us," said Joshi, who lives in Richardson, Texas, outside Dallas.
6The couple and their two sons got subsidized coverage for less than $300 a month on the state's insurance exchange, which is run by the federal government.
7The lawsuit before the Supreme Court alleges only exchanges created by states themselves are permitted to provide these tax credits.
8Things have been tougher since Jay Joshi's career as a travel agent dried up and the family's laundromat went out of business during the economic downturn.
9Kaye Joshi has only ever run small businesses and has no experience doing anything else, but the family doesn't have the money to open a new one.
10Jay Joshi has been working part-time as an after-school teacher, but they've had to spend down their savings.
11Having a pre-existing condition kept her husband without health coverage even when things were going better, Joshi said.
12"It was very, very difficult -- almost impossible -- to get him insurance, and my greatest fear was if something was to happen to him and he had to be hospitalized, that would be a big expense for us," she said.
13Health care already ate up a quarter of the family's budget even without a catastrophe, she said.
14When they got Obamacare, it was "a sigh of relief for us," Joshi said.
15The "nervous edge" she felt about the financial toll of an unseen health emergency dissipated.
16Losing their health insurance subsidies now, however, would put the Joshi family back at square one.
17"I've just been keeping my fingers crossed that Obamacare is not taken away, the subsidy is not taken away," Joshi said.
18"Because I believe, looking at my invoice that I have, it's something that I don't think I can afford without the subsidy."

2His presence on the frontline highlights neighboring Iran's influence over the Shi'ite fighters who have been key to containing the militants in Iraq.
3In contrast, the U.S.-led air coalition which has been attacking Islamic State across Iraq and Syria has not played a role in Tikrit.
4U.S. Defense Secretary Ash Carter acknowledged to Congress that he was concerned about the risk that the operation could inflame sectarian tensions.
5"Sectarianism is what brought us to the point where we are. So I do look at it with concern," Carter said.
6Iraqi military officials said security forces backed by the Shi'ite militia known as Hashid Shaabi (Popular Mobilization) were advancing gradually, their progress slowed by roadside bombs and snipers.
7They have yet to enter Tikrit, the hometown of executed former president Saddam Hussein, or the nearby Tigris river town of al-Dour, which officials describe as a major center for the Islamic State fighters.
8On the southern flank of the offensive, army and police officials said government forces had surrounded and sealed off al-Dour, but had not yet launched an assault on the town, a source in military operations command said.
9To the north, they captured a village close to Tikrit, the army said.
10Soleimani, head of the Iranian Revolutionary Guards Quds Force, was directing operations on the eastern flank from a village about 55 km (35 miles) from Tikrit called Albu Rayash, captured from Islamic State two days ago.
11With him were two Iraqi Shi'ite paramilitary leaders: the leader of the Hashid Shaabi, Abu Mahdi al-Mohandis, and Hadi al-Amiri who leads the Badr Organization, a powerful Shi'ite militia.
12"(Soleimani) was standing on top of a hill pointing with his hands toward the areas where Islamic State are still operating," said a witness who was accompanying security forces near Albu Rayash.
13U.S. WATCHING REMOTELYU.S. General Lloyd Austin, who oversees U.S. forces in the Middle East, said his military was not coordinating with Tehran and was monitoring Iran's activities through intelligence.
14"We have very good intelligence services and we have good overhead imagery ... So the activity in Tikrit was no surprise," Austin, commander of the U.S. military's Central Command, told a hearing in Congress.
15Austin also emphasized the impact of U.S.-led strikes, estimating that more than 8,500 Islamic State fighters had been killed since the start of U.S.-led coalition bombings in Iraq in August, which were later expanded into neighboring Syria.
16Islamic State fighters have staged several suicide bomb attacks against the army and militia in recent days.
17Twitter accounts linked to Islamic State supporters named one as Abu Daoud al-Amriki (American), suggesting he was a U.S. citizen, saying he had detonated a vehicle packed with explosives.
18The offensive is the biggest in the Salahuddin region north of Baghdad since last summer, when Islamic State killed hundreds of Iraqi army soldiers who had abandoned their base at Camp Speicher outside Tikrit.
19Several Shi'ite Hashid Shaabi fighters have described this week's campaign - which has been given the title "Here I am, Messenger of God" - as revenge for the Speicher killings.
20Prime Minister Haider al-Abadi has urged them to protect civilians in Salahuddin, a mainly Sunni Muslim province.
21The drive follows several failed attempts to push the militants out of Tikrit.
22Since Islamic State declared a caliphate last year in territories under its control in Iraq and Syria, Iraqi forces have not managed to recapture and control a single city.
23But months of the U.S.-led air strikes, backed up by the Shi'ite militias, Kurdish peshmerga fighters and Iraqi soldiers, have contained Islamic State in Iraq and pushed it back from around Baghdad, the Kurdish north, and the eastern province of Diyala.
24The Tikrit battle will have a major impact on plans to move further north and recapture Mosul, the largest city under Islamic State rule.
25If the offensive stalls, it will complicate and delay a move on Mosul.
26A quick victory would give Baghdad momentum, but any retribution against local Sunnis would imperil efforts to win over Mosul's mainly Sunni population.
