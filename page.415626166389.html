Vanuatu residents begin to rebuild after Cyclone Pam

1EFATE -- The roof from what was once the home of principal Melizabeth Uhi and her staff is now folded over branches high in the trees that border her school.
2She's now living with two other staff members in the only classroom that's still habitable.
3And she's running low on the rice she had before Cyclone Pam tore through Vanuatu a week ago, upending her life.
4But Uhi is moving forward.
5She's helped organize a community cleanup of the Manua Centre School grounds on the main island of Efate on Monday, and is determined that her 314 students will return to school as soon as possible.
6A week after the cyclone tore through the South Pacific archipelago with winds of 270 kilometres per hour, people are focused on the task of rebuilding.
7The signs of progress are everywhere.
8The road that rings the main island is now accessible to all vehicles following temporary repairs to bridges, and the cell network that's vital to keeping people connected is gradually being restored on Efate and some other islands.
9On the sides of roads, crews have been repairing electricity lines and residents cutting tree trunks into fire-sized slices.
10The task remains enormous.
11Many of the outlying islands still don't have any way to communicate with the outside world, while tourism, vital to the economy, has taken a huge hit, with many operators fielding call after call to cancel bookings.
12Throughout Vanuatu, many people remain worried about the most basic necessities: water, shelter and food.
13Around 65,000 people across Vanuatu are in need of temporary emergency shelter, according to the U.N. Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs, which raised the death toll from the cyclone to 16 on Saturday, after confirming three more casualties.
14Vanuatu and other small island nations face unique challenges in recovery due to their remote location and scattered populations, said Osnat Lubrani, the United Nations' Humanitarian Coordinator for Vanuatu.
15Delivering services even when there isn't a crisis is hard and expensive, she said.
16"So certainly in a time of disaster, that makes things very difficult," she said.
17"On the positive side, I think people are extremely resilient."
18Lubrani said the total price tag for the rebuild is still unknown.
19Neighbours New Zealand and Australia quickly committed millions of dollars toward the relief effort.
20There's also been millions more committed by other countries, including France, the U.S., the United Arab Emirates and South Korea.
21Relief agencies are also moving enormous resources into the region.
22But for people like Uhi, the school principal, the response can't come quick enough.
23She's been given some tents by World Vision, but needs much more help, she said.
24Mothers have been coming by every day to ask when their children can return.
25"There's a little bit of heartbreak," she said.
26"I really want the children to be able to go back to school."
27The government has cancelled all classes across the nation until March 30, although there's no guarantee the Manua school will be ready to open then.
28For many people, small improvements are noticeable each day, whether in their living situation or their health.
29At the main hospital in Port Vila, the capital, George Palap has been watching the progress of his 1-year-old nephew, Moses Sam.
30The family was on Tanna island when the cyclone struck in the early morning of March 14 and tore apart their homes.
31They ran for some nearby caves.
32Palap's sister Sampat, who was holding her infant, stumbled in the ferocious winds, he said, and the boy's head smacked into the ground.
33The family made it to the caves, but Moses's wound was serious.
34Palap said his head swelled to twice its normal size and became infected.
35The Red Cross arrived by helicopter and took the boy to Tanna's airport, where he was flown to Port Vila, his uncle said.
36The swelling has now gone down and Moses is doing much better, Palap said.
37There doesn't appear to be any permanent damage and the family hopes he can leave the hospital next week, he said.
38For others, the cyclone has delivered an opportunity to make changes.
39Lisau Manses was hanging laundry inside her house Saturday.
40The interior looked relatively normal -- books on the shelves and crockery stacked in a cabinet.
41Missing entirely, however, was the roof, allowing the sun to shine in and dry her clothes.
42Manses said she and her husband didn't think they would try to rebuild their coastal home, with its particular exposure to extreme weather.
43Instead, they are considering building a new home farther inland.
44Village chief Philip Kasamu poses on a smashed hut destroyed by Cyclone Pam on Tanna Island, Vanuatu on Thursday, March 19, 2015.
