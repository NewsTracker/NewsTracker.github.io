Latest on police-custody death: Curfew

1A protestor faces police enforcing a curfew Tuesday, April 28, 2015, in Baltimore.
2A line of police behind riot shields hurled smoke grenades and fired pepper balls at dozens of protesters to enforce a citywide curfew.
3President Barack Obama says the Baltimore riots show that police departments need to build more trust in black communities.
4In an interview broadcast Wednesday on "The Steve Harvey Morning Show," Obama says his heart goes out to the Baltimore officers who were injured by rioters.
5He says there's no excuse for that kind of violence and that Baltimore police showed "appropriate restraint."
6But he is calling on police departments "to hold accountable people when they do something wrong."
7He says Attorney General Loretta Lynch is reaching out to mayors to let them know what resources are available for retraining police and providing body cameras to hold them accountable.
8And Obama says problems will continue if the response is only to retrain police without dealing with underlying social issues such as poor education, drugs and limited job opportunities.
9He says tackling those problems will require a broader movement.
10A citywide curfew in Baltimore ended at 5 a.m. and the morning rush is getting underway with traffic flowing on most streets downtown.
11There are still a few road and lane closures around police headquarters and around Pennsylvania and North avenues, where demonstrators have been congregating and a hotspot for rioting Monday night.
12Local television showed a large police presence at that intersection and the CVS pharmacy in that neighborhood that burned in Monday's riots being boarded up on Wednesday morning.
13Also, schools are set to reopen Wednesday morning after they were closed in the wake of Monday's riots.
14In a letter to the city school community on Tuesday, schools CEO Gregory Thornton thanked the students who avoided violence and law-breaking on Monday.
15But he also condemned students who participated in the riots, saying they will be held accountable.
16For the people arrested in Baltimore under the state of emergency, there could be a longer wait than usual to see a District Court official.
17Normally, state law requires that people arrested without warrants appear before a court official within 24 hours of their arrests.
18But as part of the state of emergency declared Monday by Republican Gov. Larry Hogan following unrest in the city, the governor extended the period to no later than 47 hours.
19That's according to a letter he sent Tuesday to Judge Barbara Baer Waxman, the administrative judge for the Baltimore District Court.
20"This exercise of my authority is necessary to protect the public safety and to address the more than 200 arrests that were made by Baltimore Police Department and other law enforcement officials," Hogan wrote in the letter, a copy of which was obtained by The Associated Press.
21At midnight Tuesday, Baltimore police arrested one man wearing a Wu-Tang Clan T-shirt for violating the 10 p.m. curfew near the scene of Tuesday night's demonstration.
22Police placed him in plastic handcuffs and arrested him without incident.
23The man, who declined to give his name, said while he was being arrested that he was out at that hour because he had car problems.
24He said no animosity toward the officers.
25"They're doing their job," he said.
26Officers placed him in a prisoner transport van and told him they were taking him about 2 miles to Central Booking.
27Baltimore Police Commissioner Anthony Batts says a citywide curfew seems to be working.
28Batts told a news conference shortly before midnight Tuesday that only 10 people had been arrested following the 10 p.m. curfew, including seven for violating the curfew.
29He said two people were arrested for looting and one for disorderly conduct.
30Batts said he was pleased with the efforts of dozens of community organizers, clergy and neighborhood activists who urged residents to remain calm.
31"The curfew is, in fact, working," Batts said.
32"Citizens are safe. The city is stable. We hope to maintain it that way."
