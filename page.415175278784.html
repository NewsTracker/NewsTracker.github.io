7LONDON (AP) - Barely a year after emerging from a savage recession comparable in its intensity to the Great Depression in the U.S., Greece's economy is shrinking again.
8The uncertainty generated by January's general election and the drama over extending Greece's financial lifeline is helping to push the economy back toward recession despite encouraging signs from the tourism sector, the country's biggest money earner.
9The uncertainty, which started toward the end of 2014 when early elections were called, has damaged confidence among investors, consumers and business owners.
10Though few official economic figures for the first months of 2015 are available, surveys suggest the manufacturing sector is contracting as both foreign and domestic clients are more hesitant to make new orders.
11And the financial system has been shaken by an apparent increase in deposit withdrawals by worried savers, which is seen to have hurt investment.
12When figures last fall showed the Greek economy finally growing again after a recession that saw it shrink by a quarter and unemployment and poverty levels swell dramatically, then Prime Minister Antonis Samaras said "Greece is back."
13The return has been brief - three quarters in fact.
14In the last three months of 2014, the Greek economy shrank by a quarterly rate of 0.4 percent, double the initial estimate.
15If the first quarter of 2015 is negative, Greece will officially be back in recession - not something most economic forecasters were predicting just a few months back.
16"Following the first contraction in activity in a year during the fourth quarter of 2014, the economy is anticipated to enter recession," said IHS senior economist Diego Iscaro.
17The main reason behind his assessment is the uncertainty generated by the early elections and the bailout renegotiation between the new left-wing Syriza government and Greece's international creditors.
18As the negotiations dragged on, fears of a Greek exit from the euro - so-called "Grexit" - ratcheted higher.
19That was most evident in the banking sector, where there were signs of savers pulling money out in droves.
20After weeks of intense toing and froing between Greece and its creditors in the 19-country eurozone, the sides agreed to a four-month extension to the country's bailout to June.
21In return, Greece has committed to operate making a series of economic reforms.
22Phil Smith, an economist at financial information company Markit, which compiles a monthly survey into the manufacturing sector, said Greece's economy continues to "struggle under the weight of uncertainty."
23Markit's February survey found manufacturing output falling at its fastest rate for 16 months and its key gauge of business activity is in recession territory.
24When the current bailout ends, Greece will most likely need further financial support to make ends meet.
25There's no guarantee it will get more help and its future in the euro could hang in the balance again.
26Vassilis Korkidis, head of Greece's National Confederation of Commerce, said the recent deal with creditors gave Greece's economy a "last-minute kiss of life and avoided the so-called sudden death," but cautioned that any respite was "temporary."
27Whatever growth Greece ekes out in 2015 is likely to be markedly lower than expected just a few weeks ago, a sign of the damage wrought by the bailout deliberations.
28As recently as early February, the European Union was predicting that Greece would be one of the fastest-growing eurozone economies in 2015, with growth of 2.5 percent.
29Douglas Renwick, a senior director at Fitch Ratings, said the period of uncertainty has damaged investor, consumer, and depositor confidence and increased "the downside risks to growth and Greece's incipient economic recovery."
30Repairing the damage done from the recent bout of brinkmanship "could take time," he added, noting the experience of 2012 when Greece was last near the Grexit door.
31Renwick said Fitch was likely to further reduce its 2015 growth forecast for Greece from the current 1.5 percent.
32Even though recession looms, the scale of the retreat is nothing like what Greece experienced in the years after it got its first bailout in 2010.
33The fact that tourism, which accounts for around a fifth of Greek economic output, appears to be heading for another strong year helps.
34Optimism has been buoyed by the euro's slide against many other currencies.
35The fall makes the country a cheaper destination for those coming from outside the eurozone.
36The Association of Greek Tourist Enterprises expects 1 million more arrivals this year, bringing the total to 25 million, while TUI, one of the world's biggest travel companies, with extensive operations in Germany and the U.K., is finding strong growth in bookings.
37Noel Josephides, chairman of London-based Greece holiday specialists Sunvil Holidays, conceded there have been some instances recently where potential holidaymakers have said "they'd rather wait" before making their bookings.
38Overall, though, he said the company's regular clientele are not changing their plans and that the current situation is nothing like 2012, when travelers were put off by a spate of violent street protests against austerity, particularly in Athens.
39Though the number of people heading for Greece's beaches and nightclubs appears to be holding up, the financial uncertainty does have an impact on the industry.
40Andreas Andreadis, head of the Association of Greek Tourist Enterprises, said spending on new infrastructure and upgrading hotels was frozen due to the uncertainty.
41"It is very important, for tourism as well, for Greece to reach a final agreement with its partners and to stabilize its economy, which will restart investment," he said.
42Nicholas Paphitis in Athens contributed to this report.
