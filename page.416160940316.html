7CAIRO (AP)?
8An Egyptian criminal court sentenced a dual U.S.-Egyptian citizen on a monthslong hunger strike to life in prison Saturday on charges of financing an anti-government sit-in and spreading false news.
9Mohammed Soltan, the son of a prominent member of the now-outlawed Muslim Brotherhood, was arrested in August 2013, when security forces came looking for his father at his house.
10They didn't find the father at the time, but arrested him instead, Soltan's family said.
11His father, Salah, was detained later.
12Mohammed, a 27-year-old Ohio State University graduate and former Barack Obama campaigner, has been on a hunger strike over his detention for more than 14 months, and his health is rapidly deteriorating, his family said.
13The court also upheld death sentences for 14 people, including Brotherhood leader Mohammed Badie and Salah Soltan, and sentenced 36 others to life in prison, including three Egyptian journalists.
14Soltan's brother Omar said in a phone interview that Mohammed is being held alone in a prison hospital where even the guards are not allowed to speak with him.
15He only receives outside news during court sessions, when he sees his father in the defendant cage.
16Mohammed has been refusing medical checks since security forces moved his father to a higher security prison two months ago.
17The father, a diabetic with blood pressure issues, has been denied any medication since then, Omar said.
18"Egyptian authorities are using my father to pressure Mohammed into ending his hunger strike, but he continues to refuse," said Omar, adding that the family plans to appeal the sentences.
19The case is rooted in the violence that swept Egypt after the military-led ouster in July 2013 of Islamist President Mohammed Morsi, a veteran Brotherhood leader, whose supporters set up protest camps in Cairo.
20Security forces violently dispersed the sit-ins in August 2013, killing hundreds.
21In retaliation, many police stations and churches came under attack.
22President Abdel-Fattah el-Sissi, who as army chief had overthrown Morsi amid mass protests against the Islamist leader, issued a decree in November that allows him to deport foreign defendants convicted or accused of crimes.
23"We're hoping that Mohammed will be deported from Egypt under that new law," said Omar.
24The government has given no indication that such a move is under consideration.
25None of the defendants were present in the courtroom as Judge Mohammed Nagi Shehata read out his ruling.
26Shehata has a reputation for issuing harsh sentences against perceived government critics.
27Last year he sentenced three journalists from Al-Jazeera English to jail terms ranging from seven to 10 years, a verdict that exposed Egypt to widespread allegations of suppressing media freedom.
28The State Department said the U.S. government is "deeply disappointed" by the court's decision.
29"We remain deeply concerned about Mr. Soltan's health and detention," spokeswoman Marie Harf said in a statement.
30"The United States reiterates our call for the release of Mr. Soltan on humanitarian grounds, and we urge the Government of Egypt to redress the verdict."
31Since Morsi's ouster, Egypt has faced international criticism for conducting mass trials with judges rapidly issuing severe sentences.
32Mohamed Elmessiry, the Egypt researcher for rights group Amnesty International, said in a phone interview that Saturday's verdicts were "politically motivated."
33"Egypt's criminal justice system has proved since the ousting of Morsi that it's not independent," he said.
34Courts have issued hundreds of life sentences and death sentences, said Elmessiry, but "on the other side we haven't seen any security officer held accountable for the killing of protesters or torturing in custody or death in custody."
