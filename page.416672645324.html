A week after earthquake, Nepal still needs tents and food

1A Nepalese man puts up a barricade to stop locals entering into the damaged Durbar Square in Kathmandu, Nepal, Friday, May 1, 2015.
2The strong magnitude earthquake shook Nepal on Saturday devastating the region and leaving some thousands shell-shocked and displaced.
3A Nepalese man carries sacks of onions and potatoes on his bicycle as he walks past buildings damaged in an earthquake in Kathmandu, Nepal, Friday, May 1, 2015.
4An injured Nepalese boy is held by a woman as they are evacuated in an Indian Air Force chopper from Melamchi at Sindhupalchowk district, in Nepal, Friday, May 1, 2015.
5The U.N. said the quake affected 8.1 million people, more than a fourth of Nepal's population of 27.8 million, and that more than 1.4 million needed food assistance.
6A man prays at a damaged temple in Kathmandu, Nepal, Friday, May 1, 2015.
7The structural engineer strides through Kathmandu's old city, past buildings reduced to rubble, buildings whose facades are cracked in dozens of places, like the fractured shell of a hardboiled egg.
8But it's the many buildings that made it unscathed through the earthquake that amaze Kit Miyamoto.
9"It could have been so much worse," says Miyamoto, head of a global earthquake and structural engineering firm, who flew to Nepal soon after he heard about last weekend's 7.8 magnitude quake.
10Nepalese soldiers dig for a rumored missing person in the rubble of a collapsed house in the Manakamana village known for its spiritual Hindu temple, near the epicenter of last week's massive earthquake, in the Gorkha District of Nepal, Friday, May 1, 2015.
11Nepal's government renewed its appeal to international donors to send tents, tarpaulins and basic food supplies in the wake of last weekend's devastating earthquake, saying some of the items being sent are of little use.
12The government also asked donors to send money to help with relief efforts if they cannot send things that are immediately necessary.
13"We have received things like tuna fish and mayonnaise. What good are those things for us? We need grains, salt and sugar," Finance Minister Ram Sharan Mahat told reporters Friday.
14Mahat said the government had decided to exempt taxes on tents and tarpaulins.
15Information Minister Minendra Rijal said Nepal would immediately need 400,000 tents and so far has been able to provide only 29,000 to the people who need them.
16A week after the massive earthquake killed more than 6,600 people and collapsed buildings, temples and homes, remote villages remain cut off from help.
17Aid workers still face "immense logistical challenges," U.N. humanitarian chief Valerie Amos said Friday, noting that the scale of the devastation in Nepal would be an obstacle for any government.
18The U.N. has estimated the magnitude-7.8 quake that struck April 25 affected 8.1 million people - more than a fourth of Nepal's population of 27.8 million.
19In Kathmandu, rescue workers in orange jumpsuits continued to search through collapsed buildings, but fewer tents were standing in a central part of the capital that had been packed with people in the first few days.
20Some residents who had been fearful of aftershocks have left the city or moved elsewhere.
21The government announced it was giving the equivalent of $1,000 to families of each person killed and another $400 for funeral costs, state-run Nepal Radio said Friday.
22More than 130,000 houses were destroyed in the quake, according to the U.N. humanitarian office.
23Near the epicenter, north of Kathmandu, whole villages were in ruins, and residents were in desperate need of temporary shelters against the rain and cold.
24Isolated hamlets are still cut off because scarce helicopters can't land in some mountainous areas, and roads have often been destroyed, said Amos, who flew over some of those places during a three-day visit.
25"Of course we are worried that it is taking so long to get to people who desperately need aid. Some of those villages are virtually flattened. But it's very, very hard to see how we're going to get to them," she said.
26Some villagers might be able to walk to a place where the aid helicopters could land, but that would be of little help to those who are injured, she added.
27Tents and tarpaulins are the most urgent need, but there's also a demand for water, food, health care and better sanitation.
28Debris must be removed, bodies recovered, and officials need to determine which buildings are too dangerous to enter, Amos said.
29The U.N. Children's Fund, or UNICEF, said Friday that in the past 48 hours, it had delivered nearly 30 metric tons of supplies, including tents, water purification tablets and first aid and hygiene kits.
30Krishna Maharjan, a farmer on the outskirts of Kathmandu, brought green onions and cauliflower on his bicycle into the city.
31"We are trying to get as much fresh food to the people as possible," he said.
32"I feel it is our small contribution. But that's what we can. do and every little bit helps."
33The death toll from the quake has climbed to 6,624, police said Saturday.
34A European Union official said about 1,000 people from Europe still were unaccounted for and had not contacted their embassies since the quake struck.
35EU Ambassador to Nepal Rensje Teerink said that "doesn't mean they are dead. It just means they haven't reported back."
36Most were tourists and trekkers, and many did not register with their embassies.
37While the U.N. humanitarian office said about 24,000 people are living in 13 camps in Kathmandu, a sense emerged that life was starting to return to normal in the capital in some semblance.
38Only a few dozen tents remained at the Tudikhel grounds in the heart of the city, with less than 1,000 people still there.
39Earlier in the week, the area had been crowded with more than 30,000 people.
40Almost all the city's gas stations were open Friday, and they had no lines.
41A popular suburban bakery even was selling fresh bread and croissants.
42And at a field hospital set up by the Israeli military, a baby girl was born Friday to a young Nepalese couple.
43Lata Chand, 19, and her husband, Hariender, had rushed out of their home when the quake hit.
44While their house was undamaged, the hospital where she was to give birth was forced to close.
45"When the quake struck, I was thinking 'Will we survive?' because most of the pregnant women miscarried their babies. I was scared it would happen to us," Hariender Chand said.
46"Now we're safe, it's good."
47Both the mother and the still-unnamed child were doing well.
48Beaming midwife Dganit Gery said she hoped the birth would show all Nepalese women that there is hope for the future.
