100Christopher Holtz scheduled a trip from his native Austria to Palo Alto, Calif., so he could be one of the first to buy the watch on opening day, only to discover it was sold out.
101"It's a bummer," he said.
102"I won't get to go home with the watch."
103The sale of the Apple Watch, the first significant new product under CEO Tim Cook, has veered from Apple's past product launches.
104These have become part cultural institution, part tech-fan meet-ups that attract festive crowds outside Apple stores on the first day of sale.
105Not so with the watch.
106Apple has encouraged customers to try out the smartwatch - which starts at $350 and goes as high as $17,000 - at its stores but make the actual purchase online.
107Lines and fanfare have been modest to non-existent.
108The one exception was at luxury retailers.
109Under Apple retail chief Angela Ahrendts, the company has sought to position the watch, which includes a lower priced Sport and the high-end Apple Watch Edition, as an item for the Rolex crowd.
110Friday, it was possible to try, purchase and walk away with a watch at tony stores including Galeries Lafayette in Paris, Selfridges in London and Isetan in Tokyo.
111James McQuivey, an analyst with Forrester Research, says it makes sense for Apple to pursue the luxury market.
112"Consumer tech is cheap compared to luxury watches," he says.
113"This puts Apple in a class they've never been."
114By reaching out to luxury retailers for the first time, Apple can nab "Saudi princes, businessmen and some celebrities who have to have the watch now, who will tweet about it and tell their friends."
115McQuivey expects the luxury stores to be sold out of their inventory by Saturday, and no new watches will be available again until June.
116The select crowd sporting the watch in the months before they reached retail shoppers, such as musicians Pharrell and Beyonce, were typically showing off the high-end edition.
117Ultimate demand for the device will rest with a broader group of consumer tech buyers that have made Apple the most valuable company in the world.
118The Apple Watch accepts phone calls, notifies you of incoming texts, offers mapping information and has thousands of apps for boarding airplanes, reading one-sentence news articles and monitoring exercise.
119It has the potential of turning the fragmented wearables market on its head.
120Gene Munster, an analyst with Piper Jaffray, estimates Apple shipped 1 million watches this week and has orders for 2 million that will be fulfilled by June.
121He forecasts Apple will sell an additional 5 million watches this year, mostly in the fourth quarter.
122"What they've done is sell first to tech enthusiasts," he says.
123"Now, they go out and evangelize and build up the demand."
124By the fall, production will have caught up with demand, he says.
125Apple may release details of demand when it reports quarterly earnings Monday.
