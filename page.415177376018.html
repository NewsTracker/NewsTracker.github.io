DOJ: Ex-CIA chief admits mishandling classified information

1RALEIGH, N.C. (AP) - Former CIA Director David Petraeus, whose career was destroyed by an affair with his biographer, has agreed to plead guilty to charges he gave her classified material - including information on war strategy and identities of covert operatives - while she was working on the book.
2The plea agreement carries a possible sentence of up to a year in prison and represents another blow to the reputation of the retired four-star Army general who led U.S. forces in Iraq and Afghanistan and was perhaps the most admired military leader of his generation.
3Petraeus, 62, will plead guilty to a misdemeanor count of unauthorized removal and retention of classified material.
4The agreement was filed in federal court Tuesday in Charlotte, where Paula Broadwell, the general's biographer and former mistress, lives with her husband and children.
5In court papers, prosecutors recommended two years of probation and a $40,000 fine.
6But the judge who hears the plea is not bound by that and could still impose a prison sentence.
7No immediate date was set for Petraeus to enter the plea.
8As part of the deal, Petraeus agreed not to contest the set of facts laid out by the government.
9Prosecutors said that while Broadwell was writing her book in Washington in 2011, Petraeus gave her eight binders of classified material he had improperly kept from his time as the top military commander in Afghanistan.
10Days later, he took the binders back to his house.
11Among the secret materials contained in the "black books" was information on identities of covert operatives, the coalition war strategy and notes about Petraeus' discussions with President Barack Obama and the National Security Council, prosecutors said.
12Those binders were later seized by the FBI in a search of Petraeus' Arlington, Virginia, home, where he had kept them in the unlocked drawer of a desk in a ground-floor study.
13Prosecutors said that after resigning from the CIA, Petraeus signed a form falsely attesting he had no classified material.
14He also lied to FBI agents in denying he supplied the information to Broadwell, according to court documents.
15Petraeus' lawyer declined to comment.
16A telephone message left for Broadwell was not immediately returned.
17Her lawyer said he had no comment.
18Petraeus admitted to the affair when he resigned as CIA director in November 2012.
19Both he and Broadwell have publicly apologized and said their romantic relationship began only after he had retired from the military.
20Broadwell's admiring biography of him, "All In: The Education of General David Petraeus," came out in 2012, before the affair was exposed.
21Petraeus held the CIA post less than a year, not long enough to leave a significant mark on the spy agency.
22Petraeus presided over the "surge" of American forces in Iraq and a plan to pay Sunni militias to fight al-Qaida in Iraq.
23He was then promoted to commander of U.S. Central Command, which has authority over the Middle East.
24When Gen. Stanley McChrystal was fired in 2010 by Obama as commander in Afghanistan after his staff made impolitic remarks to a Rolling Stone reporter, Petraeus was brought in to replace him.
25Since his resignation as CIA director, Petraeus has slowly taken steps to re-enter public life, going on the speaking circuit, becoming a scholar at Harvard's Kennedy School of Government, and taking a position at a private equity firm.
26If he manages to avoid prison, Petraeus will receive far more lenient punishment than that meted out to others convicted of leaking secrets.
27In 2012, former CIA officer John Kiriakou pleaded guilty to intentionally disclosing the identity of a covert agent to a reporter and was sentenced to 2 1/2 years in prison.
28Petraeus, then CIA director, hailed the conviction.
29"Oaths do matter, and there are indeed consequences for those who believe they are above the laws that protect our fellow officers and enable American intelligence agencies to operate with the requisite degree of secrecy," he said at the time.
30David Deitch, a former federal prosecutor who handled counterterrorism and national security issues, said those deciding Petraeus' fate probably weighed his decades of service to the nation when considering his punishment.
31Also, a public trial might have revealed classified material the government would rather keep secret.
32"What is achieved by sending David Petraeus to jail?" asked Deitch, now in private practice in Washington.
33"What will be achieved in terms of deterrence, in terms of punishment, in terms of rehabilitation? The conclusion is probably not much."
34Sen. John McCain, a longtime supporter of Petraeus, said it is time to consider the issues raised by the ex-general's extramarital affair closed.
35"At a time of grave security challenges around the world, I hope that Gen. Petraeus will continue to provide his outstanding service and leadership to our nation, as he has throughout his distinguished career," the Arizona Republican said.
