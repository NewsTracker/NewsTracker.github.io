A dozen arrested as Freddie Gray protests turn violent

1Thousands of protesters took to the streets Saturday in the largest Freddie Gray rally yet, and after hours of peaceful demonstrations, pockets of protesters smashed out police car windows and storefronts.
2Two people were hurt in the mayhem and at least a dozen were arrested.
3The problems happened near Camden Yards, where the Baltimore Orioles game against the Boston Red Sox went on as scheduled, only fans were told toward the end of the game to stay in the stadium because of public safety worries.
4Before the game, demonstrators fought with fans at a bar.
5Gray died April 19 after suffering a fatal spinal injury while in police custody.
6Authorities have not explained how or when Gray's spine was injured.
7Police have said Gray should have received medical attention at the spot where he was arrested - before he was put inside a police transport van handcuffed and without a seat belt, a violation of the department's policy.
8In her first public comments since Gray's death, his twin sister, Fredricka Gray, appealed for calm as she appeared with the mayor at a news conference.
9"My family wants to say, can you all please, please stop the violence?" she said.
10"Freddie Gray would not want this. Freddie's father and mother did not want nobody ... Violence does not get justice."
11There have been near-daily protests since Gray's death.
12On Saturday, a small group threw cans and plastic bottles in the direction of police officers.
13One protester broke out the window of a police cruiser, grabbed a police hat inside and wore it while standing on top of the cruiser with several other protesters.
14From inside the stadium, fans watched the protesters gather.
15Before the protest turned tense and violent, demonstrators filled two city blocks and marched 2 miles to City Hall, where the crowd overtook the grassy plaza adjacent from the building.
16Tanya Peacher, a 36-year-old Baltimore resident, said she'd never attended a protest in the city before, but watching a video of Gray's arrest motivated her.
17Residents young and old, from Baltimore and beyond, voiced their anger at how the department and the city's officials are handling the investigation into Gray's death.
18At one point, the crowd paused for a moment of silence in front of Shock Trauma, the hospital where Gray died.
19The marchers then migrated to Camden Yards.
20At a downtown intersection, a dozen marchers laid down in the street during an impromptu "die-in."
21Wearing a sign around his neck that said "I am Freddie Gray," 33-year-old Dante Acree joined thousands of others outside City Hall.
22Acree said he came out to the protest because "it could have been one of my kids."

24The drawing shows Gray being hoisted from a police van to heaven by two angels.
25"I'm here to do what I can. Police brutality is as old as the 1950s, the 1960s. It's still here," he said.
