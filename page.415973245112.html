Secular writers in Bangladesh fearful after 2nd blogger slain by suspected Islamists

1What happened earlier this week, when the second secularist blogger in less than a month was hacked to death in the streets of the capital, made it clear he wasn't safe.
2"Anytime they can hit me or my like-minded friends," said Ananya Azad, a 25-year-old blogger who has written pieces that were critical of Islamic fundamentalism and politics driven by religion.
3He quit his job as a newspaper columnist and stopped writing blogs in recent months after receiving numerous threats, but still posts critical comments on Facebook.
4Ananya says he's thinking about fleeing the country and spends much of his time indoors these days.
5"They don't hesitate to kill in the name of their beliefs," he said.
6"I'm an easy target for the fanatics."
7Bangladesh, a majority Muslim nation long seen as insulated from the most fervent strains of militant Islam, has seen that reputation crack amid an increasingly bloody divide between secular bloggers and conservative Islamist groups.
8In many ways, the divide is clear: the bloggers want authorities to ban religion-based politics while the Islamists are pressing for blasphemy laws so that nobody can undermine Islam's holy book, the prophet or basic pillars of being a Muslim.
9In a crowded nation of 160 million, whose recent political history has been dominated by a bitter power struggle that regularly spills into street violence, many fear that religion could further destabilize the situation.
10Islam is Bangladesh's state religion but the country is governed by secular laws based on British common law.
11Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina has repeatedly said she will not give in to religious extremism.
12Yet over the last decade or so, extreme interpretations of Islam have steadily gained ground here.
13"These attacks are not stray incidents," said Abdur Rashid, a retired army general and expert on national security.
14"They are well planned and strings are being pulled in some quarters eager to control the future of Bangladesh."
15He believes Islamist political parties are orchestrating the attacks to further polarize country and expand their influence.
16"Some political parties, which have a distant desire to come to power in Bangladesh, are either directly or indirectly connected with this radicalization process," he said.
17The past weeks have seen a spike in radical attacks.
18First, a prominent Bangladeshi-American blogger and writer, Avijit Roy, was hacked to death by unidentified attackers in late February when he was walking with his wife.
19Roy bled to death while his wife, also a blogger, was critically injured.
20Roy was an atheist who promoted secularism through his blog, books and newspaper articles.
21A previously unknown Muslim militant group, Ansar Bangla 7, claimed responsibility for the attack.
22Then, on Monday, 27-year-old Oyasiqur Rahman Babu, was attacked in daylight as he left his house.
23Unlike Roy, who had been on the radar of radical Islamists and had regularly received death threats, Babu was a low-profile online activist.
24Still, his Facebook page includes a cartoon depicting the Prophet Muhammad from the French satire magazine Charlie Hebdo, and he had openly questioned writings in the Quran.
25Two of the three men believed to have attacked Babu were caught by passers-by and handed over to the police.
26A third man fled the scene.
27The two captured men are students at local madrasas.
28They told police they neither knew Babu nor were familiar with his writings.
29Instead, they said a fourth man had showed them Babu's photographs and some of his writings, and then asked them to kill him.
30They followed the instructions, they said, because they believed it was their duty as Muslims, according to police.
31Islamist political parties have denied any involvement in the killings.
32Writing has never been a particularly safe profession here.
33Ananya's father was attacked a decade ago for his writings against Muslim extremism, and journalists are often targeted by political thugs.
34Bangladesh ranks 146 out of 180 countries on the press freedom index of the group Reporters Without Borders.
35The attacks come as Bangladesh grapples with an ongoing political standoff over last year's contentious elections boycotted by the main opposition Bangladesh Nationalist Party and its allies.
36The opposition has staged violent protests and shut down roads with an often-bloody transportation strike.
37For more than a decade, Bangladeshi politics have been dominated by battles for power between Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina and her archrival, former Prime Minister Khaleda Zia, who heads the Bangladesh Nationalist Party.
38Media reports say radical Muslim groups have compiled lists of bloggers and writers they view as anti-Islam.
39Ananya, who regularly meets with others concerned about the rise of fundamentalism, says his name is on some of those lists.
40"Fundamentalists glorify Allah but they think nothing about lying. When I try to stand for truth they want to kill me," he said on a March 12 Facebook post.
41Many of those under threat, including Ananya, demand the banning of Jamaat-e-Islaami, a key ally of the Nationalist Party.
42Ananya is also among those who called for a senior Jamaa-e-Islaami leader to be executed for his role in war crimes during Bangladesh's bloody war of independence in 1971.
43"I can't rely on the system for my protection," Ananya said.
44"We are frustrated, my friends don't see a liberal Bangladesh where free thought will be encouraged and protected."
