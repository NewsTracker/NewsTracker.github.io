With Greece deal elusive, risk rises of financial lockdown

1NICOSIA, Cyprus (AP) " The longer it takes to secure a deal to keep Greece in the euro, the greater the risk the country will have to put its financial system in lockdown to prevent worried savers from pulling money out in droves.
2Banking industry figures suggest Greeks have been taking money out ahead of the country's emergency talks with creditors on Friday.
3If that trend picks up, the banks could become so weak that the government decides to impose capital controls " limits on what people can transfer out of the country or even withdraw from cash machines.
4For now, the European Central Bank is propping up the Greek banks with emergency cash, keeping alive hopes that the lenders will not collapse.
5But the ECB support " called Emergency Liquidity Assistance, or ELA " could easily be turned off if Greece's bailout negotiations fail to yield a deal.
6The ECB would be under intense pressure to stop pumping money into a banking system that is collapsing anyway.
7To avoid that scenario, the Greek government could, if needed, resort to capital controls.
828, when the country's European bailout program expires.
9Going into a meeting on the issue Friday, the two sides remain far apart.
10Without a deal soon, analysts say Greeks and investors could pull their money out of the banks at a faster pace.
11By last count, in December, deposits in Greece were at 207.9 billion euros ($235 billion), the lowest level since June 2012, when there were last fears that Greece might fall out of the euro.
12Though the central bank of Greece has yet to publish figures for January and February, separate reports show the ECB increased the amount of financial support for the country's banks, a sign the lenders need to fill a hole left by deposit outflows.
13The Bank of Greece declined to comment on the issue of capital controls.
14"If there's no deal (on Friday) then there could be substantial further flight (of money) as we head into the last week of the deal," said Simon Derrick, a senior analyst at Bank of New York Mellon.
15Though capital controls are in theory against the rules of the euro, there is a precedent.
16They were used in Cyprus in 2013 when the country was negotiating a bailout deal.
17The population of Cyprus was stunned when eurozone ministers at a late Friday meeting in March 2013 decided to raid bank deposits as part of a rescue of the country's banking system.
18Following signs of a run on those banks, the government decided to shutter them all.
19Banks were left closed for days before authorities imposed harsh restrictions on money withdrawals and transfers abroad.
20Initially, people could only take out a maximum 300 euros ($340) cash from their deposits and couldn't transfer money out of the country.
21Large business transactions involving money transfers abroad had to be strictly vetted.
22It took about two years before almost all restrictions were lifted.
23WOULD IT WORK IN GREECE?
24It worked in Cyprus, though at a high cost.
25While deposits there are down 35 percent, they are high enough for the banking system to survive.
26But it was a blunt instrument to say the least.
27The Cypriot economy plunged into recession as an ensuing liquidity crunch stunted growth and confidence in the banks took a hit.
28In Greece, capital controls could be insufficient if the country does not secure a bailout deal with its European lenders.
29In case of no deal, the ECB would have to consider whether to pull the plug on Greek banks.
30The Greek government would then have to take over from the ECB the burden of providing financing to the Greek banks.
31But because it has no money, it could do so only by dropping out of the euro and printing its own money.
32"As soon as it got out that Greece was likely to leave (the euro), market disruption and huge capital outflows from Greece would threaten to make an exit very messy," analysts at Capital Economics said in a research paper.
33"An important initial step would almost certainly be the imposition of capital controls, while Greek banks would be closed for a period."
