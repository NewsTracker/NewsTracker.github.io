13Like the families of other victims, most of whom were Chinese or Malaysian, Mr. Wattrelos has spent the past year suspended in a limbo of acceptance, grief, anger and hope fanned by a lack of answers.
14Distressed by the shrillness of the early news media attention, when cameras besieged his home and the faces of his wife, son and daughter would haunt him from television screens and magazine covers, he now speaks of something that to him is far worse: silence.
15"The world has moved on," Mr. Wattrelos said in a recent interview.
16"But I can't. Not until I know what happened."
17When the status of Flight 370 changed from "delayed" to "missing" on flight-tracking sites in the early afternoon of March 8, 2014, Mr. Wattrelos was 28,000 feet in the air.
18He was on a flight from Paris to Beijing to meet his family for the second half of their vacation.
19They had been living in Beijing for six years and were about to move back to France.
20His plane landed nine hours after Flight 370 was scheduled to arrive.
21When he turned on his cellphone, a text message from a colleague appeared on his screen: "I am so terribly sorry about your family."
22At the gate, a hostess was waiting for him.
23She took him to a private room with the French consul, an acquaintance from years of working in China.
24The consul grabbed his shoulders.
25"The plane is missing," he said.
26"Your family has died," using the French word "disparu."
27It had never occurred to him what an irony it was that in French the word can mean "dead," though more often "disappeared."
28But in those early hours, there was no ambiguity.
29He had to call his oldest son, who had stayed behind, studying in Paris.
30"It was the hardest thing I've ever done," he said.

32Mr. Wattrelos remembers them all.

55"How dare they say it was an accident, just like that?" he said.
56"They would not be offering us death certificates if they didn't know that my wife and children are dead," he said.
57"That's the part I believe. But if they know that much, what else do they know? And why aren't they telling us?"
58Together with an American teacher, Sarah Bajc, whose partner was on the plane, and a handful of other family members of victims, Mr. Wattrelos wants to motivate a whistle-blower to speak up.
59Last year, they made a video and raised just over $100,000, which was quickly spent on private investigators.
60They want to try again.
61"If we offer a million, maybe someone will speak up," he said.
62He does not have a single theory of what happened.

67"It's too big," he said.
