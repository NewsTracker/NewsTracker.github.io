Ukraine wants global court to investigate crimes in Crimea and east

1Ukraine has already given the global court the authority to investigate crimes on its territory from November 21, 2013, to February 22, 2014, the period leading up to the fall of Ukraine's former president Viktor Yanukovich.
2Russia annexed Crimea soon after, and later in 2014 fighting erupted in eastern Ukraine between Kiev's forces and pro-Russian separatists.
3Kiev accuses Russia of sending arms and troops to aid the rebels, which Moscow denies.
4"We are quite optimistic about more, definitely more, engagement of the ICC," Pavlo Klimkin told Reuters before meeting the court's president and prosecutor on Friday.
5An ICC referral would cover "everything under the (ICC) mandate, including crimes against humanity", he said.
6Klimkin named the attack on the strategic port of Mariupol in eastern Ukraine which killed 30 in January as an example of a crime against humanity.
7"The shelling killed, in seconds, more than 30 people and heavily wounded 100 people," Klimkin said.
8"If you deliberately shell and, I stress, deliberately shell cities, killing civilians, it's a completely different situation (from military operations) and we have to engage the ICC."
9The wider probe proposed by Klimkin would for the first time consider allegations of direct Russian involvement.
10Ukraine is also examining the possibility of launching a case against Russia at another court in The Hague, the International Court of Justice, the highest U.N. court for territorial disputes, Klimkin said.
11There was no immediate reaction from Russian authorities to Klimkin's comments.
12ICC prosecutors began their preliminary inquiry last April after the government of Ukraine asked them to look into allegations that Yanukovich's troops had killed over 100 protesters in Kiev and other cities.
13The referral period runs up to just before Russia annexed Crimea, so the investigation so far excludes any crimes that might have been committed by Russian-backed troops.
14Alex Whiting, a former senior ICC prosecutor, said a new referral could force an ICC investigation of the wider Ukraine conflict.
15"The prosecutors have been kind of sitting on it because it's more complicated whether there have been war crimes," he said.
16"But here (Crimea and eastern Ukraine) there have been widespread allegations of war crimes and even crimes against humanity, so it will be much harder to ignore."
17Klimkin said that an amnesty granted to suspects in the conflict by the Minsk ceasefire agreement would not apply to crimes that fell under the ICC's statute.
18Set up in 2002 to try serious crimes where national authorities are unable or unwilling to intervene, the ICC has been criticized for its failure to act in some of the world's most serious conflicts.
