Surprise ending at end of Colorado theater shooting trial: James Holmes gets life

1- James Holmes will spend the rest of his life in prison after at least one juror balked at the possibility of sentencing him to death for the massacre that claimed 12 lives and spawned a gut-wrenching four-month trial.
2Because the 12 jurors failed to unanimously agree that Holmes should be executed, he will be sentenced to life in prison without parole for the chilling 2012 attack on a midnight screening of a Batman movie that also left 70 injured.
3Nine jurors wanted to execute Holmes, but one was steadfastly opposed and two others wavering, a juror told reporters after the verdict was announced.
4"Mental illness played into the decision more than anything else," said the woman, who would not give her name.
5"All the jurors feel so much empathy for the victims. It's a tragedy."
6The verdict shocked the courtroom.
7Holmes' mother, Arlene, who had begged jurors to spare his life, leaned her head against her husband's shoulder and began sobbing.
8In the back, Aurora police officers who responded to the bloody scene of Holmes' attacks began crying.
9Sandy Phillips, whose daughter Jessica Ghawi was killed by Holmes, shook her head no and then held it in her hands.
10Ashley Moser, whose 6-year-old daughter died in the attack and who was herself paralyzed by Holmes' bullets, also shook her head and then slowly leaned it against the wheelchair of another paralyzed victim, Caleb Medley.
11Families of victims began to leave the courtroom as Judge Carlos Samour Jr. continued reading the verdict.
12Their wails were audible through the closed courtroom doors.
13As in previous proceedings, Holmes, who is on anti-psychotic medication that dulls his responses, showed no reaction.
14His attorneys left court without commenting.
15The verdict was also a setback for District Attorney George Brauchler, who two years ago rejected a plea deal from Holmes' attorney that would have ended the case with life in prison, the same result.
16Brauchler said the defense refused to let Holmes be examined by a state psychiatrist or release a notebook in which he detailed his reasons for the attack.
17Holmes' subsequent videotaped psychiatric exams were played at trial and the notebook entered into evidence.
18"Because of that decision," Brauchler said, "the community now knows everything about this case."
19Brauchler added that he was disappointed with the verdict, but praised the jury for doing "a hell of a job" during the grueling, 4-month-long trial.
20Defense attorneys argued Holmes' schizophrenia led to a psychotic break, and powerful delusions drove him to carry out one of the nation's deadliest mass shootings.
21Victims were devastated by the outcome.
22"We just have to deal with it and accept it," said Robert Sullivan, whose 6-year-old granddaughter Veronia was Holmes' youngest victim.
23"He's living. He's breathing and our loved ones are gone. The gaping void we have for our granddaughter has been replaced with a new abscess of him living."
24The verdict is the latest blow to the death penalty in Colorado, which has executed only one person since the U.S. Supreme Court reinstated the penalty in 1977.
25Gov. John Hickenlooper in 2013 said he would not carry out the scheduled execution of a man convicted of killing four at a Chuck E. Cheese in 1993.
26There was never any question during the trial as to whether Holmes was the killer.
27He meekly surrendered outside the theater, where police found him clad head-to-toe in combat gear.
28The trial hinged instead on the question of whether a mentally ill person should be held legally and morally culpable for an act of unspeakable violence.
29It took jurors only about 12 hours of deliberations to decide the first part - they rejected his insanity defense and found him guilty of 165 felony counts.
30The defense then conceded his guilt, but insisted during the sentencing phase that his crimes were caused by the psychotic breakdown of a mentally ill young man, reducing his moral culpability and making a life sentence appropriate.
31The case could have ended the same way more than two years ago, when Holmes offered to plead guilty if he could avoid the death penalty.
32Prosecutors rejected the offer.
33The trial provided a rare look inside the mind of a mass shooter.
34Most are killed by police, kill themselves or plead guilty.
35By pleading insanity, he dropped his privacy rights and agreed to be examined by court-ordered psychiatrists.
36Holmes told one that he had been secretly obsessed with thoughts of killing since he was 10.
37His parents testified he seemed a normal, affectionate child who withdrew socially in adolescence and became fascinated with science but did not seem abnormal.
38Holmes studied neuroscience hoping to understand what was happening to his mind.
39But it was when he moved from San Diego to Colorado to attend graduate school that his meltdown accelerated.
40Holmes flunked out of his prestigious doctoral program at the University of Colorado and broke up with a fellow graduate student, the only girlfriend he'd ever had.
41He began to buy guns and thousands of rounds of ammunition and scouted out The Century 16 theater complex to learn which auditorium would offer the highest number of victims.
42He kept his mounting homicidal thoughts from a university psychiatrist, instead describing them in the notebook he mailed to her hours before the shooting.
