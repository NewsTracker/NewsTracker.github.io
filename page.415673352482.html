Tsipras in debt talks with Merkel

1In his first visit to Germany since coming to power in January, Alexis Tsipras sounded a conciliatory note, though he stopped short of promising anything concrete on reforms that creditors such as Germany want to see before they pay more money.
2Greece urgently needs more funds as it faces a cash crunch within weeks.
3Backtracking on some of his previous rhetoric blaming Greece's problems on German insistence on budget austerity measures, Mr Tsipras said he wanted to "break the stereotypes that have grown in the past five years: The Greeks are not lazy and the Germans are not to blame for everything".
4He said Angela Merkel invited him to Berlin for talks as the tensions between the two countries increased, telling him "it is better to talk with one another than about one another".
5"I did not come here to ask for financial help," Mr Tsipras said after meeting Mrs Merkel behind closed doors for more than an hour.
6"I came for an exchange of our thoughts and opinions, to see where there is common ground and where there is disagreement."
7He said the talks were "positive", adding that he found Mrs Merkel "listens and wants to be constructive in the exchange of opinions".
8She was careful to point out Germany was only one of the eurozone nations that would be responsible for deciding whether Greece's reforms are sufficient, and said no decisions had been made in her talks with Mr Tsipras.
9"Today we can only talk about things," she said, adding that the meetings were being held in "a spirit of trust".
10Mr Tsipras' first weeks in office have been marked by tension between the two governments' contrasting approaches to Greece's debt crisis and over Athens' revival of calls for Second World War reparations from Berlin.
11He brought reparations up as he spoke alongside Mrs Merkel, but said "there is no linkage at all with the financial crisis and the eurozone crisis - it is clearly a bilateral issue".
12"This is primarily a moral issue and I believe that we have to work together, our two countries, to address this moral issue," he said.
13Mrs Merkel stuck to her government's contention that "the question of reparations has been politically and legally settled" with a 1960 accord with Greece and a payment at that time.
14But she noted Germany has earmarked a million euro ( 732,000) annually for a "German-Greek future foundation" meant to fund remembrance and historical research projects and said "in this spirit we will talk with the Greek government further".
15Mr Tsipras' left-wing Syriza party won general elections with the campaign promise to end the spending cuts favoured by Germany in exchange for 240 billion euro ( 175 billion) in international bailout money.
16His new government agreed a month ago to push through reforms in exchange for keeping European Union aid flowing, but has delayed submitting the measures for approval.
17A full reform list should be presented "by early next week", Greek government spokesman Gavriil Sakellaridis told Mega television.
18He insisted that "the Greek government will not take any recessionary measures" that will weigh down the country's troubled economy.
19German officials have complained about their Greek counterparts making commitments and then publicly casting doubt on them, but also insist the debt spat is not a bilateral matter.
