7CHICAGO (AP) - What will drive Hillary Rodham Clinton's second bid for the presidency?
8To start, strengthening economic security for the middle class and expanding opportunities for working families.
9Those are issues her campaign says will be promoted by a results-oriented "tenacious fighter."
10The former first lady, U.S. senator and secretary of state was expected to make her 2016 effort official Sunday with an online video, followed by small events with residents of early-voting states over the days ahead.
11The campaign's opening strategy was described ahead of the announcement by two senior advisers who requested anonymity to discuss her plans.
12Clinton's strategy sounds familiar.
13In 2012, President Barack Obama framed the choice for voters this way: Democrats focused on the middle class versus Republicans wanting to protect the wealthy and return to policies that led to the recession.
14Clinton intends to sell herself as being able to work with Congress, businesses and world leaders, the advisers said Saturday.
15That approach could be perceived as a critique of Obama, who has largely been unable to fulfill his pledge to end Washington's intense partisanship and found much of his presidency stymied by gridlock with Congress.
16Ahead of the expected announcement, Republicans tried to link Clinton to Obama, a regular focus of GOP criticism.
17"We must do better than the Obama-Clinton foreign policy that has damaged relationships with our allies and emboldened our enemies," said former Florida Gov. Jeb Bush, a potential Republican candidate, in a video Sunday.
18Kentucky Sen. Rand Paul, who launched his presidential campaign last week, pointed to the Clinton family's foundation, saying it was hypocritical for the Clintons to accept from Saudi Arabia, which places public restrictions on female movement and activity.
19"I would expect Hillary Clinton if she believes in women's rights, she should be calling for a boycott of Saudi Arabia," Paul said on NBC's "Meet the Press."
20Clinton was not expected to roll out detailed policy positions in the first weeks of her campaign.
21Advisers said she planned to talk about ways families can increase take-home pay, the importance of expanding early childhood education and making higher education more affordable.
22It's not clear whether that would include a noticeable break with Obama on economic policy.
23The GOP has hammered Obama's approach as anti-business and insufficient in the wake of the recession.
24The White House says the economy has improved significantly in recent years.
25The unemployment rate fell to 5.5 percent in March, but manufacturing and new home construction slowed, cheaper gas has yet to ignite consumer spending and participation in the labor force remains sluggish.
26Clinton is seen as the overwhelming favorite for her party's nomination.
27Still, her team has said her early strategy is designed to avoid appearing to take that nomination for granted.
28The early events were expected to include discussions at colleges, day care centers and private homes, and stops at coffee shops and diners.
29After about a month of such events, Clinton planned to give more specifics about her rationale for running.
30Clinton's husband, Bill, and daughter, Chelsea, are unlikely to appear at her early events.
31Bill Clinton, the former two-term president, said recently that he wanted to play a role as a "backstage adviser" in his wife's campaign.
32Advisers said Bill Clinton has been engaged with his wife in some of the policy discussions leading up to this weekend's rollout.
33To prepare for the campaign, Clinton has spent months meeting with economic policy experts, including Heather Boushey, whose research focuses on inequality, and Teresa Ghilarducci, a labor economist and retirement policy expert.
34The policy development process has been overseen by aides Jake Sullivan and Dan Schwerin.
35Clinton's growing team of staffers began working Friday out of a new campaign headquarters in Brooklyn.
36They gathered Saturday to hear from campaign manager-in-waiting Robby Mook, who told them the campaign would value teamwork, respect, diversity, discipline and humility.
37A memo distributed by Mook, "We are Hillary for America," said the campaign "is not about Hillary Clinton and not about us - it's about the everyday Americans who are trying to build a better life for themselves and their families."
38A Democratic official in attendance described the meeting on condition of anonymity because it was a private strategy session.
39The memo was first reported by Politico.
