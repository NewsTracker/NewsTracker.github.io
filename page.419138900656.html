Deadline passes for Croat held by IS militants in Egypt

1Police searches and diplomatic efforts intensified over the fate of a Croatian hostage held in Egypt by Islamic State extremists, who threatened to kill him Friday if the government did not release imprisoned Islamist women.
2CAIRO: Police searches and diplomatic efforts intensified over the fate of a Croatian hostage held in Egypt by Islamic State extremists, who threatened to kill him Friday if the government did not release imprisoned Islamist women.
3The group's Egyptian affiliate said Wednesday that it would kill 30-year-old Tomislav Salopek in 48 hours if the Egyptian government did not release jailed "Muslim women'', a reference to those detained in the government's crackdown on supporters of the Muslim Brotherhood and other Islamists.
4As extremist sympathizers noted the passage of the deadline on social media, Croatia's foreign minister, Vesna Pusic, met with her Egyptian counterpart in Cairo to press efforts.
5Foreign Minister Sameh Shourki's office pledged in statement that Egypt ``will spare no effort'' in the search for Salopek.
6No other information was immediately available about the fate of the hostage, a married father of two.
7In previous kidnapping situations involving the Islamic State group, deadlines have passed and videos of killings have appeared later.
8Salopek, a surveyor in the oil and gas industry working with France's CGG Ardiseis, was abducted in Cairo last month.
9The extremists' videotaped demand, entitled ``A Message to the Egyptian Government,'' was shot in the style of previous Islamic State propaganda videos in which they threaten and behead hostages.
10It was the first time such a video had featured a foreign hostage in Egypt.
11In Croatia, friends gathered in support at Salopek's house in the village of Vrpolje.
12"We used to take our children together to school and then sit in the bar across and drink coffee,'' said Franjo Raspudic, a friend of Salopek. "
13He never used to talk much about his trips, just that he went places, like Syria or Egypt, and that was the end of it.
14He is a quiet guy, closed to himself.
15Straight ace, I would say.''
16In Egypt, the sister of a woman jailed for alleged ties to the outlawed Brotherhood urged the extremists to spare Salopek.
17Doaa el-Taweel, sister of jailed photographer and activist Esraa el-Taweel, said that her sister is innocent and that ``her release should not come at the expense of another innocent person.''
18Esraa el-Taweel disappeared in June for weeks before the Interior Ministry announced that they arrested her on charges of belonging to the Brotherhood, which has been declared a terrorist organization, and for spreading false information to tarnish the country's image.
19She is now awaiting trial.
20Croatia's President Kolinda Grabar-Kitarovic said Friday she would talk to Egyptian President Abdel-Fattah el-Sissi about the kidnapping, and that Croatian authorities were doing all in their power to resolve it.
21She said she could not go into specifics because of concerns for Salopek's safety and that of "all Croatian citizens who work in places where there is a danger of kidnapping.''
22A day earlier, Croatian state television said that Salopek's wife, Natasha, flew to Cairo along with the foreign minister.
23An Egyptian security official said that security forces were searching for Salopek across the country, focusing on the western provinces of Matrouh and Wadi Gedid, which border Libya, as well as Beheira in the Nile Delta and Giza next to Cairo.
24The official said Salopek's driver, who the kidnappers left behind, said that the gunmen who seized the Croatian off a western Cairo highway had Bedouin accents.
25That suggests they could have come from a variety of isolated places in Egypt, including the restive eastern Sinai Peninsula, where Egypt's Islamic State affiliate is based, or the vast western desert that is the gateway to volatile and lawless Libya, home to its own Islamic State branch.
26The security official spoke on condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to speak to journalists.
27Last December, IS's Egyptian affiliate claimed responsibility for the killing of an American oil worker with Texas-based energy company Apache Corp. Apache said that one of its supervisors had been killed in an apparent carjacking in the Western Desert, part of Egypt's mainland, though no video was released of the slaying.
28The mother of two other jailed female Islamists also called for Salopek's life to be spared.
29"My girls should be released, but not with the blood of innocent people,'' said Fatma Ahmed, whose daughters, Hind and Rasha Mounir, are appealing life sentences for participating in protests.
30Egypt has seen an increase in violence since the 2013 military ouster of Islamist President Mohammed Morsi, with attacks by suspected Islamic extremists in both Sinai and the mainland, focusing primarily on security forces.
31In Sinai, the militants have launched increasingly sophisticated attacks in recent months that have killed dozens of Egyptian soldiers and police.
32Government forces have been carrying out an intensified hunt for them in several northern towns in the peninsula.
33Foreign interests have also been targeted, including the Italian Consulate, which was hit with a car bomb last month.
34But this is the first time the local Islamic State affiliate released a video showing a kidnapped foreigner in Egypt, a major escalation as the country tries to rebuild its crucial tourism industry after years of unrest following the 2011 revolt against autocrat Hosni Mubarak.
