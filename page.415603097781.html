Tunisian official: 2 gunmen trained in Libya before attack

1Tunisian official: 2 gunmen trained in Libya before attack The two extremist gunmen who killed 21 people at a museum in Tunis trained in neighboring Libya before carrying out the deadly attack, a top Tunisian security official said.
2The Islamic State group has issued a statement claiming responsibility for the deadly attack on Tunisia's national museum that killed 23 people, mostly tourists.
3TUNIS, Tunisia (AP) - The two extremist gunmen who killed 21 people at a museum in Tunis trained in neighboring Libya before carrying out the deadly attack, a top Tunisian security official said.
4Wednesday's attack at the National Bardo Museum killed 21 people - 17 of them cruise ship tourists - before the two gunmen were killed in a firefight with security forces.
5The attack of such magnitude in Tunisia - the only country to emerge from the 2011 Arab Spring uprisings with a functioning democracy - raised concerns about the spread of extremism to the rest of North Africa.
6Rafik Chelli, the Interior Ministry's top security official, said the attackers had slipped out of Tunisia in December and received weapons training in Libya before returning home.
7He told the El Hiwar El Tounsi TV channel that authorities did not have further details about where or with which group they had trained.
8The Islamic State group, based in Iraq and Syria, has claimed responsibility for the Bardo attack.
9Several well-armed groups in Libya, which borders Tunisia, have pledged their allegiance to Islamic State.
10Authorities identified the gunmen as Yassine Laabidi and Hatem Khachnaoui.
11Laabidi had been flagged to intelligence but not for anything specific, Prime Minister Habib Essid said.
12Laabidi hailed from the working-class Tunis suburb of Ibn Khaldun, and Khachnaoui was from the western town of Sbeitla, an interior ministry official told The Associated Press.
13He spoke on condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to speak publicly about the matter.
14Police in Tunisia have arrested five people described as directly tied to the two gunmen, including Khachnaoui's father and a sister, as well as four others in central Tunisia said to be supporters of their cell.
15Tunisians on Thursday stepped around trails of blood and broken glass outside the museum to rally in solidarity with the 21 victims and with the country's fledgling democracy.
16Marchers carried signs saying, "No to terrorism," and "Tunisia is bloodied but still standing."
17At Tunis' Charles Nicolle hospital, victims' families continued to arrive Friday to help identify the dead and recover their bodies.
18Tunisian Health Minister Samar Samoud told The Associated Press on Friday the latest tally of victims included four Italians, three Japanese and three French, two Spanish and two Colombians and one citizen each from Britain, Poland and Belgium.
19The nationalities of three victims remain unconfirmed.
20Two of the cruise ships that had passengers killed or wounded in the Tunis attack sailed into Spanish ports on Friday, with disembarking passengers telling reporters chilling tales of how they just missed being victims.
21In Palma, Spanish cruise ship passenger Catalina Llinas told reporters she and her husband luckily chose a day trip Wednesday to the Roman ruins of Carthage near Tunis instead of the museum excursion.
22The couple's tour bus, she said, passed by the Bardo museum just 10 minutes before the attacks.
23Essid announced new security measures Thursday, including a crackdown on websites seen as promoting terrorism.
24French Interior Minister Bernard Cazeneuve was travelling to Tunis on a pre-scheduled visit Friday coinciding with the country's Independence Day holiday.
25In claiming responsibility for the attack, the Islamic State group issued a statement and audio on jihadi websites applauding the dead gunmen as "knights" for their "blessed invasion of one of the dens of infidels and vice in Muslim Tunisia."
26Analysts cautioned against seeing every such attack as evidence of a well-organized, centrally controlled entity spanning the Middle East, saying instead that small groups could merely be taking inspiration from the high-profile militant group.
27"I think (the Islamic State) is probably taking credit for something it may not have played a role in," said Geoff Porter, a security analyst for North Africa.
28Confronted with a poor economy, young Tunisians have disproportionately gone abroad to fight with extremist groups in Libya, Syria and Iraq, including some affiliated with the Islamic State.
29Tunisian authorities have estimated that of the 3,000 young people who left the country to fight with radical groups, about 500 have returned.
30President Barack Obama spoke with Tunisian President Beji Caid Essebsi by phone to offer his condolences, sympathy and support.
31The White House says Obama offered to keep providing assistance to Tunisia as the investigation proceeds.
32The deaths of so many foreigners will damage Tunisia's tourism industry, which draws thousands of foreigners to its Mediterranean beaches, desert oases and ancient Roman ruins.
33The industry had just started to recover after years of decline.
34The two cruise ship lines who had passengers killed in Tunis on Wednesday announced they were dropping Tunis from their itineraries for now.
35Culture Minister Latifa Lakhdar gave a defiant news conference Thursday at the museum, where blood still stained the floor amid the Roman-era mosaics.
