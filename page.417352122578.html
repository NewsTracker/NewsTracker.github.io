Baltimore gets bloodier as arrests drop post-Freddie Gray

1FILE - In this May 24, 2015, file photo, police pick up a pair of shoes after a double shooting in Baltimore.
2Residents in West Baltimore say they've been victims of excessive policing in this neighborhood where street crime and drug-dealing have been endemic.
3Now, one month after riots erupted in the wake of the death of Freddie Gray in police custody, homicides and shootings are up and those same residents feel like they've been abandoned and left to fend for themselves.
4A Baltimore Police officer speaks to a family member, where a young boy and a 31-year-old woman were shot and killed, Thursday, May 28, 2015, in Baltimore.
5In the month since Freddie Gray died and the city erupted in civil unrest, Baltimore has seen its murder rate skyrocket.
6There have been 36 murders in May alone.
7A Baltimore Police officer follows a man, who screamed "Let me in! I'm going in!" before crossing the yellow tape and walking into the crime scene on the 100 block of Upmanor Road, in Baltimore, where a young boy and a 31-year-old woman were shot and killed, Thursday, May 28, 2015.
8BALTIMORE (AP) - A 31-year-old woman and a young boy were shot in the head Thursday, becoming Baltimore's 37th and 38th homicide victims so far this month, the city's deadliest in 15 years.
9The most recent killings claimed the lives of Jennifer Jeffrey and her 7-year-old son, Kester Anthony Browne.
10They were identified by Jeffrey's sister, Danielle Wilder.
11Jeffrey and her son were found dead early Thursday, each from gunshot wounds to the head.
12As family members cried and held each other on the quiet, leafy block in Southwest Baltimore where they lived, Wilder said she felt as if "my heart has been ripped out."
13Wilder said a neighbor called their other sister early Thursday, concerned that she hadn't heard any noise coming from Jeffrey's house: no footsteps, Wilder said, no voices, and no gunshots.
14But when her brother let himself into the house to check on the mother and son, he discovered their bodies.
15"She was in the living room," Wilder said.
16"The baby was upstairs, in the bed."
17Wilder said police told her there were no signs of forced entry, and that whoever killed Jeffrey and Browne were let into the house sometime yesterday.
18Wilder said Jeffrey also lived with her niece and grand-niece.
19Wilder said she believed that whoever killed Jeffrey wanted to catch her alone, and that the boy was collateral damage.
20Thursday's deaths continue a grisly and dramatic uptick in homicides across Baltimore that has so far claimed the lives of 38 people.
21Meanwhile, arrests have plunged: Police are booking fewer than half the number of people they pulled off the streets last year.
22Arrests were already declining before Freddie Gray died on April 19 of injuries he suffered in police custody, but they dropped sharply thereafter, as his death unleashed protests, riots, the criminal indictment of six officers and a full-on civil rights investigation by the U.S. Justice Department that has officers working under close scrutiny.
23"I'm afraid to go outside," said Antoinette Perrine, whose brother was shot down three weeks ago on a basketball court near her home in the Harlem Park neighborhood of West Baltimore.
24Ever since, she has barricaded her door and added metal slabs inside her windows to deflect gunfire.
25"It's so bad, people are afraid to let their kids outside," Perrine said.
26"People wake up with shots through their windows. Police used to sit on every corner, on the top of the block. These days? They're nowhere."
27West Baltimore residents worry they've been abandoned by the officers they once accused of harassing them, leaving some neighborhoods like the Wild West without a lawman around.
28"Before it was over-policing. Now there's no police," said Donnail "Dreads" Lee, 34, who lives in the Gilmor Homes, the public housing complex where Gray, 25, was chased down.
29"People feel as though they can do things and get away with it. I see people walking with guns almost every single day, because they know the police aren't pulling them up like they used to."
30Police Commissioner Anthony Batts said his officers "are not holding back," despite encountering dangerous hostility in the Western District.
31"Our officers tell me that when officers pull up, they have 30 to 50 people surrounding them at any time," Batts said.
32Batts provided more details at a City Council meeting Wednesday night, saying officers now fear getting arrested for making mistakes.
33"What is happening, there is a lot of levels of confusion in the police organization. There are people who have pain, there are people who are hurt, there are people who are frustrated, there are people who are angry," Batts said.
34"There are people, and they've said this to me, 'If I get out of my car and make a stop for a reasonable suspicion that leads to probable cause but I make a mistake on it, will I be arrested?' They pull up to a scene and another officer has done something that they don't know, it may be illegal, will they be arrested for it? Those are things they are asking."
35The Baltimore Fraternal Order of Police Lodge 3 on Thursday posted a statement from President Gene Ryan on social media saying that the police are "under siege."
36"The criminals are taking advantage of the situation in Baltimore since the unrest," Ryan wrote.
37"(Police) are more afraid of going to jail for doing their jobs properly than they are of getting shot on duty."
38Protesters said Gray's death is emblematic of a pattern of police violence and brutality against impoverished African-Americans in Baltimore.
39In October, Batts and Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake invited the Justice Department to participate in a collaborative review of police policies.
40The fallout from Gray's death prompted the mayor to ramp that up, and U.S. Attorney General Loretta Lynch agreed to a more intensive probe into whether the department employs discriminatory policing, excessive force and unconstitutional searches and arrests.
41Baltimore was seeing a slight rise in homicides this year even before Gray's death April 19.
42But the 38 homicides so far in May is a major spike, after 22 in April, 15 in March, 13 in February and 23 in January.
43With one weekend still to go, May 2015 is already the deadliest month in 15 years, surpassing the November 1999 total of 36.
44Ten of May's homicides happened in the Western District, which has had as many homicides in the first five months of this year as it did all of last year.
45Non-fatal shootings are spiking as well - 91 so far in May, 58 of them in the Western District.
46The mayor said her office is "examining" the relationship between the homicide spike and the dwindling arrest rate.
47Even before Gray's death, police were making between 25 and 28 percent fewer arrests each month than they made in the same month last year.
48But so far in May, arrests are down roughly 56 percent.
49Police booked just 1,045 people in the first 19 days of May, an average of 55 a day.
50In the same time period last year, police arrested 2,396 people, an average of 126 a day.
51In fact, police did not make any arrests in the triple digits between April 22 and May 19, except on two occasions: On April 27, when protests gave way to rioting, police arrested 246 people.
52On May 2, the last day of a city-wide curfew, police booked 140 people.
53At a news conference Wednesday, Rawlings-Blake said there are "a lot of reasons why we're having a surge in violence."
54"Other cities that have experienced police officers accused or indicted of crimes, there's a lot of distrust and a community breakdown," Rawlings-Blake said.
55"The result is routinely increased violence."
56"It's clear that the relationship between the commissioner and the rank-and-file is strained," she added.
57"He's working very hard to repair that relationship."
58Emergency response specialist Michael Greenberger cautions against directly blaming police.
59The founder and director of the University of Maryland Center for Health and Homeland Security, the spike in homicides is more likely a response to Gray's death and the rioting.
60"We went through a period of such intense anger that the murder rate got out of control. I think it's been really hard for the police to keep on top of that," he said.
61Lee disagrees.
62He says rival gang members are taking advantage of the police reticence to settle old scores.
63"There was a shooting down the street, and the man was standing in the middle of the street with a gun, just shooting," Lee added.
64"Usually, you can't walk up and down the street drinking or smoking weed. Now, people are everywhere smoking weed, and police just ride by, look at you, and keep going. There used to be police on every corner. I don't think they'll be back this summer."
65Batts acknowledged that "the service we're giving is off-target with the community as a whole" and he promised to pay special attention to the Western District.
66Veronica Edmonds, a 26-year-old mother of seven in the Gilmor Homes, said she wishes the police would return, and focus on violent crime rather than minor drug offenses.
67"If they focused more on criminals and left the petty stuff alone, the community would have more respect for police officers," she said.
