Ferguson mayor: Changes already being made

1Ferguson mayor: Changes already being made ST. LOUIS - Ferguson Mayor James Knowles kept his comments brief and on script as he addressed a roomful of reporters Wednesday evening.
2Ferguson Mayor James Knowles addresses the most recent Department of Justice report.
3He is the first city official from Ferguson to speak since the Department of Justice released its findings following an investigation into the Ferguson Police Department.
4That review found evidence a pattern of racial bias in policing, as well as a city and court system more focused on collecting revenue than on preserving public safety.
5The Department of Justice report included a list of 26 recommendations to improve the police department and municipal court system in Ferguson.
6Standing behind Knowles in a room at the Ferguson Community Center were a few other city officials: City Manager John Shaw, City Councilman Keith Kallstrom and City Councilman Mark Byrne.
7Only Knowles spoke, and he refused to answer any questions.
8Noticeably absent from the press conference was Ferguson Police Chief Tom Jackson.
9City spokesperson Jeff Small said he was not sure why Jackson did not attend.
10In his remarks, Knowles said Ferguson has cooperated with the Department of Justice, and has already implemented some changes.
11After learning on Tuesday about racial bias in e-mails between three employees of the Ferguson Police Department, Knowles said those three people were immediately placed on administrative leave pending an investigation.
12One person has already been terminated, and the other two are still under investigation.
13"Let me be clear: This type of behavior will not be tolerated in the Ferguson Police Department, or in any department in the city of Ferguson," Knowles said.
14"These actions taken by these individuals are in no way representative of the employees of the city of Ferguson."
15Ferguson, Mo., Mayor James Knowles leaves a news conference after reading a statement March 4, 2015.
16The Justice Department cleared a white former Ferguson police officer in the fatal shooting of an unarmed black 18-year-old, but also issued a scathing report calling for sweeping changes in city law enforcement practices it called discriminatory and unconstitutional.
17Next, Knowles outlined changes already underway in the police department.
18Those include several new hires since Aug. 9, 2014, including a female African-American corrections officer and two female African-American assistant court clerks.
19Knowles said FPD plans to fill three police officer positions.
20Knowles convened a task force to create a Civilian Review Board for FPD.
21The city hired a consultant who came recommended by the DOJ to conduct a study which will focus on "patrol staffing and deployment."
22The city council repealed several provisions of the municipal code related to administrative fees in September 2014.
23The Municipal Court Judge established a special docket for people who struggle to afford fines, which will allow defendants to request payment plans or alternative sentencing.
24Ferguson, Missouri, Mayor James Knowles reads a prepared statement to reporters in Ferguson,Missouri on March 4, 2015.
25Knowles was responding to a Justice Department report that was released on the problems in Ferguson following the riots of 2014.
26Ferguson, Missouri Mayor James Knowles reads a prepared statement to reporters in Ferguson,Missouri on March 4, 2015.
27"These are just some of the initiatives the City of Ferguson has taken and will continue to take moving forward, to hopefully move this city, its residents, and our entire community forward," Knowles said.
28City leaders will review the recommendations from the DOJ, and have limited options how to proceed.
29Ferguson officials can agree to the recommendations in full and enter into a "consent decree" with the DOJ.
30That gives federal authorities oversight as changes are implemented.
31If Ferguson refused to comply with the recommendations, the Department of Justice could sue the city.
32Nobody from the city would answer any questions related to Ferguson's legal plans.
