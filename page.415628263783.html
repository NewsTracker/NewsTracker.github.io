For Liberal Israelis, Netanyahu's Win Is a Reality Check

1Israeli liberals woke up after national elections with a demoralizing feeling: Most of the country, in a deep and possibly irreversible way, does not think like they do.
2There had been a sense of urgency among moderate Israelis, and even an ounce of hope, that widespread frustration with Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's six straight years in office would lead voters to pull Israel away from what they perceive as its rightward march toward international isolation, economic inequality and a dead end for peace with the Palestinians.
3But as the results trickled in on Wednesday, they showed Likud with a shocking lead that has all but guaranteed Netanyahu a third consecutive term.
4Netanyahu called it a victory "against all odds."
5The liberals' optimism has been replaced with despair - and an infuriating belief that the masses may never understand that logic shows the current path is suicidal.
6"Drink cyanide, bloody Neanderthals. You won," award-winning Israeli author and actress Alona Kimhi wrote on her Facebook page, before erasing it as her comments became the talk of the town.
7"Only death will save you from yourselves."
8Such rage rippled through liberal Israel this week.
9Social media was full of embittered Israelis accusing Netanyahu's supporters of racism, and some vowed to stop donating charity to the underprivileged whom they perceived as being automatic supporters of the right.
10The prime minister's main rival denounced such attacks.
11"Attempts to divide, vilify and spread hate in Israeli society disgust me, and it doesn't matter whether it comes from the right or the left," wrote Zionist Union leader Isaac Herzog on Facebook.
12The anger was about far more than the election, reflecting a larger and more dramatic battle for the heart of the country.
13Israel's founding fathers were Jews of Ashkenazi, or eastern European, descent and the ideological predecessors of the Labor party, the main faction in the rebranded Zionist Union.
14The left led the country for its first three decades until Likud - heavily backed by working class Jews of Mizrahi, or Middle Eastern, descent - gained power in 1977.
15The Labor Party returned to power in the 1990s, leading the first efforts at peace with the Palestinians.
16But the Palestinian uprising in the early 2000s saw the return of hawkish rule, which in one form or another has lasted until today.
17The divisions between right and left largely revolve around the question of what do with territories captured by Israel in the 1967 Mideast war - and the millions of Palestinians who live there.
18Parties on the left would trade the land for peace and allow the creation of a Palestinian state.
19They also argue that the lands are a liability, since incorporating the Palestinians as citizens would destroy Israel as a Jewish-majority state.
20The right emphasizes the lands' strategic value and biblical symbolism and pushes constantly for settling them with Jews.
21Its success in this endeavor has, paradoxically, put the country on a path toward being a place where Jews may no longer be a strong majority.
