Fugitive ex-NSA contractor Edward Snowden in talks on returning home: lawyer

Boko Haram's new video shifts from speeches to beheadings

1Moscow: A Russian lawyer for Edward Snowden said on Tuesday the fugitive former US spy agency contractor who leaked details of the government's mass surveillance programmes was working with American and German lawyers to return home.
2Anatoly Kucherena, who has links to the Kremlin, was speaking at a news conference to present a book he has written about his client.
3Moscow granted Mr Snowden asylum in 2013, straining already tense ties with Washington.
4"I won't keep it secret that he... wants to return back home. And we are doing everything possible now to solve this issue. There is a group of US lawyers, there is also a group of German lawyers and I'm dealing with it on the Russian side."
5The United States wants Mr Snowden to stand trial for leaking extensive secrets of electronic surveillance programmes by the National Security Agency (NSA).
6Russia has repeatedly refused to extradite him.
7Mr Snowden has said in the past he would like to return home if he was assured he would be given a fair trial.
8A deeply divisive figure, Mr Snowden is praised by some as a civil rights campaigner and whistleblower and condemned by others as a traitor who had compromised US security.
9Mr Kucherena said last August Mr Snowden had been granted a three-year residence permit in Russia.
1The latest video from Nigerian militant group Boko Haram, which shows the beheaded bodies of two men accused of spying, does not offer the same slick cinematography that the Islamic State uses to shock the West and lure recruits.
2But while more amateurish, the video titled "Harvest of Spies" is a sharp contrast with Boko Haram's past crude YouTube efforts.
3Those mainly included rambling speeches by its leader, Abubakar Shekau, most infamously boasting about the kidnapping of nearly 300 schoolgirls.
4Boko Haram has come under increasing pressure in recent weeks by a regional force that includes troops from Chad, Cameroon, Niger and Nigeria, and on Monday lost control of the northeastern Nigeria town of Dikwa.
5As it has been driven from towns and villages, it has issued several videos both threatening neighboring countries and vowing to attack voters in Nigerian elections due later this month.
6A 12-minute video last month was typical, showing Shekau seated near a Kalashnikov, declaring that elections were un-Islamic because men could only be led by God, not politicians.
7The elections wouldn't go ahead, he vowed, "even if we are dead."
8But with its latest video, posted online Monday, Boko Haram appears to have borrowed at least some elements from the Islamic State's recent postings.
9The masked terrorists are clad in black or camouflage, one of them wielding a long dagger.
10They stand behind their victims, who kneel in a dusty glade, hands tied behind their backs, in what might be a nod to the recent Islamic State footage of 21 Coptic Christians seized in Libya and beheaded on a beach by masked men in black.
11While some analysts speculated Boko Haram may be increasingly inspired by Islamic State, Shekau did not mention his fellow terrorists in last month's video address.
12Throughout more than a decade-long insurgency, Boko Haram's focus has been more local than that of Islamic State or Al Qaeda.
13Its objective has always been to establish an Islamic state in Nigeria, and it has rarely threatened global targets.
14In recent years, Boko Haram has decapitated hundreds of prisoners and has reportedly filmed the confessions of alleged spies and their beheadings.
15But the posting of such videos on social media is a more recent development.
16The new video shows a man identified as a farmer from the northeastern town of Baga, Dawood Muhammad, who confesses to being paid about $25 by a Nigerian policeman named Saeed to spy.
17Muhammad, questioned by a fighter in camouflage holding a long dagger, says a man offered to be "partner on a job that could earn us both a lot of money."
18The two went to meet a third man who introduced them to the police officer, Muhammad says.
19He said there were three men in a spy ring.
20"Our mission is to give them information about the residents who live in here," he says, appearing to speak under duress.
21"They said that by God's will, I will become rich and will never go back to being a farmer again."
22The group's black flag is shown behind the prisoners.
23The video ends with the decapitated heads of the two men, pictured on their chests.
24It authenticity couldn't be independently confirmed.
25In recent weeks, Nigeria's military has claimed to have killed hundreds of militants and recaptured dozens of towns, recovering ammunition, weapons, tanks and vehicles.
26However, troops from Chad, more than from Nigeria, have been instrumental in the victories.
27Chadian forces Monday reclaimed control of Dikwa, east of Maiduguri, the capital of Borno state, according to Nigeria's Premium Times.
28Boko Haram had killed hundreds of people in the town, Associated Press reported.
29Nigerian military officials said Tuesday that their troops killed 73 Boko Haram fighters disguised as cattle herders who attacked the town of Konduga.
30The militants were driven back, the military said.
31Nigeria last month postponed until March its presidential election, which was originally planned for Feb. 14, citing the insecurity in the country's northeast.
32With President Goodluck Jonathan facing a strong challenge from former military dictator Muhammadu Buhari, the election delay bought him time to turn around the fight against Boko Haram.
33Jonathan boasted last month that Shekau would be captured before the elections were held, while military officials insist the war is nearly over.
