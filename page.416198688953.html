Clinton's 2016 White House launch contrasts with her 2008 bid

1Clinton, who begins the 2016 presidential race as the commanding Democratic front-runner, entered the fray with a flurry of video, email and social media announcements that indicated she had absorbed some of the lessons of her painful 2008 loss and would not take anything for granted this time.
2When she lost the Democratic nominating battle to Barack Obama, her campaign was heavily criticized for conveying a sense of arrogance and entitlement, and for being out of touch with the party's progressive wing.
3This time, the video launching her campaign portrayed her as a warmer, more empathetic figure and laid the groundwork for a more populist economic agenda.
4Eight years ago, her launch message was "I'm in it to win."
5On Sunday, she shifted the attention to voters, declaring on her new website, "Everyday Americans need a champion. I want to be that champion."
6Her roll-out included a sophisticated use of social media, including Twitter, Facebook and YouTube - a contrast to her last campaign that was seen as less adept than Obama's at using technology to convey messages.
7In a tweet on Sunday night, Clinton said: "Road trip! Loaded the van and set off for IA."
8A campaign aide said Clinton left her home in Chappaqua, New York, for the drive to Iowa, where she will attend her first campaign events this week.
9In the tweet from a stop in Pennsylvania, Clinton said: "Met a great family when we stopped this afternoon. Many more to come."
10But showing a more down-to-earth side while connecting with ordinary voters will be a challenge for Clinton, one of the most famous figures in the United States after decades as the wife of former President Bill Clinton, a U.S. senator and secretary of state.
11Indeed, her launch drew praise from French and German government ministers.
12While Clinton enters the race as prohibitive favorite to be her party's presidential nominee, a crowd of potential candidates are vying for the Republican nomination.
13Clinton's campaign will be based around her plans to address economic inequality and will tout the historic nature of her effort to become the first woman U.S. president, aides said.
14In announcing her presidential bid in 2007, Clinton spoke to the camera alone while sitting on a couch and asked voters to join her later for a series of Web chats.
15This time, her video featured a mix of Americans talking about their futures and their economic troubles, along with images of Clinton in listening mode and only a small snippet of her speaking.
16Her announcements featured strong words but no specific policy proposals about the struggles of working Americans and the need for economic equality.
17That included a shot at executive salaries that reflected populist rhetoric to a degree that could raise alarm among her Wall Street backers.
18"Families have fought their way back from tough economic times. But it's not enough - not when the average CEO makes about 300 times what the average worker makes," Clinton said in an email to supporters.
19One analyst noted the picture of Clinton on her launch website, holding a paper coffee cup and talking to a gray-haired man and woman, showed her appreciation for one set of voters.
20Aides have said Clinton's campaign schedule will feature plenty of smaller events where she can listen to voters.
21Her visit to Iowa, which holds the kickoff contest in the nominating process early in 2016, will be a "listening tour."
22It will include a roundtable discussion with students and educators on Tuesday and small-business owners on Wednesday.
23"I'm going to work my heart out to earn every single vote, because I know it's your time," Clinton said in her email to supporters.
24She mentioned her father's small business, her mother's "tough childhood" and her baby granddaughter.
25Opinion polls show Clinton has a huge lead over potential Democratic rivals, and few are expected to enter the race.
26A Reuters-Ipsos tracking poll shows Clinton backed by more than 60 percent of Democrats.
27Massachusetts Senator Elizabeth Warren, a favorite of the party's liberal wing who says she is not running, is a distant second at 18 percent.
28So far, only former Senator Jim Webb of Virginia and former Maryland Governor Martin O'Malley, who both languish in single digits in polls, are the only Democrats to make moves toward a candidacy.
29Even before Clinton entered the race, potential Republican opponents took swings at her.
30Former Florida Governor Jeb Bush criticized her guidance of U.S. foreign policy as secretary of state.
31"We must do better than the Obama-Clinton foreign policy that has damaged relationships with our allies and emboldened our enemies," Bush said in a video released by the political action committee Right to Rise.
32Many Democrats have been waiting for Clinton to get back into the White House fight since the day in June 2008 when she pulled out of her primary battle against Obama with an expression of regret that she could not crack "that highest and hardest glass ceiling this time."
33But Clinton still has to convince some liberals that she is the best candidate to tackle issues like income inequality and the power of Wall Street banks.
34Some liberal groups are pushing Warren, who has vocally criticized some Wall Street practices, to challenge Clinton.
35The Clinton campaign's finance chair, Dennis Cheng, emailed donors and bundlers on Sunday telling them to expect an email message from Clinton herself explaining her vision for the campaign and the presidency.
