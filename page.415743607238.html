US, UN Press Europe to Make Strong Return to Peacekeeping

1Russia's aggressiveness in the east.
2The Islamic State group's sudden rise in the south.
3As Europe finds new threats close to home, it's now being asked by the United States to make a strong return to peacekeeping around the world as well.
4The U.N. on Friday convenes an unprecedented meeting of defense officials from more than 100 countries to seek support for the largest peacekeeping effort ever deployed, with nearly 130,000 personnel in 16 missions from Congo to the Golan Heights.
5They face growing threats from groups like the Islamic State that have little regard for traditional ideas of war and peace.
6Following up in September, President Barack Obama will chair a meeting on the sidelines of the annual U.N. General Assembly of world leaders, with the goal of collecting countries' peacekeeping pledges after Friday's pitch by Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon and other top officials.
7Obama's envoy to the U.N., Ambassador Samantha Power, this month made it clear that Europe is expected to step up and is needed "more than ever."
8Two decades ago, European nations contributed more than 40 percent of U.N. peacekeepers, she said in a speech in Brussels.
9It's less than 7 percent now.
10This call from the United States, easily the biggest contributor to the U.N.'s peacekeeping budget at $2.5 billion, comes as tens of thousands of troops from European and other countries emerge from years of war in Iraq and Afghanistan.
11In her speech, Power envisioned the German and Dane helicopter pilots from Afghanistan doing similar work in Darfur, or Romanian and Czech patrols guarding bases in South Sudan.
12If the U.N. had asked for this kind of help earlier, it would have been seen as competing with the U.S.-led military effort of the past decade, said Jean-Marie Guehenno, a former U.N. peacekeeping chief and the current president of the International Crisis Group.
13"Now, when the U.S. says, 'You're our allies, and one of the best ways to show friendship is to contribute to the U.N.,' that's quite a signal," he told The Associated Press.
14Europe may need some convincing.
15The image many still have of U.N. peacekeeping is of twin failures two decades ago.
16In the Rwandan genocide, an existing peacekeeping force was swiftly reduced from 2,000 to 270 after 10 Belgian peacekeepers were killed.
17In Bosnia, Dutch peacekeepers were overwhelmed by Bosnian Serb forces who went on to massacre 8,000 Muslim men at Srebrenica.
18The U.N., which has no standing army, now heavily relies on troops from South Asia and Africa, whose countries make up the top 10 contributors.
19Bangladesh led the way with 9,446 peacekeepers as of the end of February.
20The United States had 119, less than any other permanent member of the Security Council except Russia, which had 72.
21One problem in today's peacekeeping, officials and observers say, is that sheer numbers are not enough against groups like al-Qaeda that blur borders.
22"Who of you would have thought a few years ago we would not see gruesome videos of hostages being beheaded on Mediterranean beaches?"
23Dutch Defense Chief Gen. Tom Middendorp told an international military conference this week, referring to the Islamic State group's emergence this year on Libya's shore.
24Beyond manpower, the U.N. now needs peacekeepers who are skilled in, and equipped for, tasks such as intelligence work, strategic airlifts, medical evacuations and the growing use of drones.
