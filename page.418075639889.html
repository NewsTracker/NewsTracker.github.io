U.S. top court backs Obamacare, president says it's here to stay

1WASHINGTON The U.S. Supreme Court on Thursday rejected a conservative legal challenge that could have doomed President Barack Obama's healthcare law, upholding nationwide tax subsidies crucial to his signature domestic policy achievement.
2Obama strode into the White House Rose Garden after the ruling to declare that the law known as Obamacare is working, helping millions of Americans afford health insurance who otherwise would have none, and that it is "here to stay."
3Chief Justice John Roberts, a conservative appointed by Republican President George W.
4Bush, wrote in the 6-3 ruling that Congress clearly intended for the tax subsidies that help millions of low- and moderate-income people afford private health insurance to be available in all 50 states.
5The court decided that the law did not restrict the subsidies to states that establish their own online health insurance exchanges, as the challengers in the case contended.
6"Congress passed the Affordable Care Act to improve health insurance markets, not to destroy them," Roberts wrote, adding nationwide availability of the credits is required to "avoid the type of calamitous result that Congress plainly meant to avoid."
7Roberts was joined by fellow conservative Justice Anthony Kennedy and the court's four liberal members in a ruling that may ensure Obamacare becomes a lasting element of the nation's social programs.
8The ruling means the current system will remain in place, with subsidies available nationwide.
9If the challengers had won, at least 6.4 million people in at least 34 states would have lost subsidies worth an average of $272 per month.
10It marked the second time in three years the high court ruled against a major challenge to the law brought by conservatives.
11Both rulings were written by Roberts.
12Unlike the 2012 case, in which the court was split 5-4, Kennedy joined Roberts in the majority this time.
13The law was passed by Obama's fellow Democrats in Congress in 2010 over the unified opposition of Republicans, who have fought it since its inception.
14Republicans will keep attacking Obamacare in Congress and on the 2016 presidential election campaign trail to energize right-wing voters and raise money, but little chance exists of the law being rolled back before the end of Obama's presidency in January 2017, political analysts said.
15Obama said the law has been "woven into the fabric of America."
16"After more than 50 votes in Congress to repeal or weaken this law, after a presidential election based in part on preserving or repealing this law, after multiple challenges to this law before the Supreme Court, the Affordable Care Act is here to stay," Obama added.
17The question before the justices was whether a four-word phrase in the expansive law saying subsidies are available to those buying insurance on exchanges "established by the state" has been correctly interpreted by the administration to allow subsidies to be available nationwide.
18The exchanges are online marketplaces that allow consumers to shop among competing insurance plans.
19After Chief Justice Roberts announced the decision from the bench, Justice Antonin Scalia read for 11 minutes from his dissenting opinion inside the court's white marble and crimson-draped setting.
20Scalia said the statute's words were clear, that Congress wanted to limit the credits to the state exchanges.
21Scalia recalled the court's 2012 decision upholding the law, again over his dissent.
22"We really should start calling the law SCOTUScare," Scalia said.
23SCOTUS is the acronym for the Supreme Court of the United States.
24"This court has no free-floating power to rescue Congress from its drafting mistakes," Scalia added.
25Roberts, sitting next to him on the bench, sat stone-faced.
26He smiled slightly at the SCOTUScare line, but otherwise betrayed no emotion.
27Conservative Justices Clarence Thomas and Samuel Alito joined Scalia's dissent.
28The Obama administration said 16.4 million previously uninsured people have gained health insurance since the law was enacted.
29There are currently around 26 million Americans without health insurance, according to government figures.
30"This is not about the Affordable Care Act as legislation, or Obamacare as a political football. This is healthcare in America," Obama said.
31Congressional Republicans vowed to continue efforts to repeal Obamacare despite appeals from Democrats for them to stop.
32The top two congressional Republicans denounced the law on Thursday.
33Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnnell said it makes life "miserable" for many people it purports to help.
34House of Representatives Speaker John Boehner called it "fundamentally broken."
35The U.S. hospital industry breathed a collective sigh of relief on Thursday and investors cheered that the growing number of paying customers created by Obamacare would not disappear.
36The ruling sparked a broad rally in shares of health insurers with an especially heavy stampede into hospital operators, which were seen as being at particular risk of facing steep losses had the subsidies been struck down.
37Shares of hospital chain Tenet Healthcare surged 12.2 percent to rank as the biggest gainer for the day in the S&P 500.
38Four other hospital operators' shares hit lifetime highs: Community Health Systems, HCA Holdings, Universal Health Services and Lifepoint Health, with gains ranging from 7.6 percent to nearly 13 percent.
39Conservatives have called Obamacare a government overreach and "socialized medicine" and launched a series of legal challenges.
40The current case started as a long-shot by conservative lawyers opposed to law.
41Financed by a libertarian Washington group called the Competitive Enterprise Institute, the lawyers recruited four people from Virginia as plaintiffs.
42The lead plaintiff was a self-employed limousine driver named David King.
43The plaintiffs said they were "deeply disappointed" with the ruling.
44The law "unfairly restricts the health insurance choices of millions of people, and it threatens their jobs as well," they added.
45The case is King v.
