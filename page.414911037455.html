Not everyone is unhappy about the weather! Adorable red pandas frolic in the snow at Cincinnati Zoo

Leaders of Ebola Fight at U.N. Express Worry About Eradication

1The weather phenomenon dubbed the Siberian Express caused record-breaking low temperatures in almost every single area in the central and eastern US on Friday prompting many to stay inside.
2The freezing weather however is perfect for the red pandas at the Cincinnati Zoo and Botanical Garden who enjoy playing in the ice and snow.
3ABC reports that Lin, a two-year-old female panda, and Rover, a 9-year-old male panda are natives of the Himalayas and are accustomed to the cold weather.
4'We get snow about the same amount, or a little less, than in New York City,' Michelle Curley, the zoo's communications director, told ABC News.
5'The red pandas LOVE it and cold weather,' she added.
6The pandas adorably celebrate their love of the cold by splashing in the snow, climbing trees, and rolling around in the cutest way imaginable.
7According to The Weather Channel, the blasts of cold air this weekend meant that more snow, sleet and freezing rain were likely to continue from the Southern Plains to the Southeast into next week.
8If the weather stays as cold as its predicted, more adorable panda play is likely in the forecast too.
1The officials, Dr. Bruce Aylward of the World Health Organization and Dr. David Nabarro, the United Nations' special envoy for Ebola, said that the incidence of new cases was no longer plunging, and that the anti-Ebola effort was facing a $900 million shortfall for 2015.
2Their remarks, at a United Nations news conference, emphasized a sobering message they had delivered during meetings at the organization's headquarters this week.
3The Ebola crisis has ravaged Guinea, Liberia and Sierra Leone over the past year, leaving nearly 9,400 people dead.
4Asked about the goal set by the leaders of the three afflicted nations on Sunday to stop transmission of Ebola in two months by isolating all known cases, Dr. Aylward said it was theoretically possible but would be "extremely difficult."
5He said many people suspected of having been infected were reluctant to be included on so-called contact lists, which are a critical tool for health workers to track transmission of the disease and isolate the sick.
6Some potentially infected people, Dr. Aylward said, "go through extraordinary measures to not get on a contact list because of the stigma."
7While the first three to four weeks of 2015 were encouraging because of the rapid decline in new cases, he said, "the story of the last four weeks has been different."
8In that period, the decline flattened out, and health officials began recording 120 to 150 new cases per week.
9"This is not what you want to see with Ebola," Dr. Aylward said.
10Dr. Nabarro echoed those remarks, saying, "The slowdown that Dr. Aylward referred to in the journey toward zero is a worry, and it shows that we have to maintain constant vigilance and surveillance to get to zero."
11Dr. Nabarro also said that the fund for the United Nations-led effort to combat Ebola contained $600 million, but that the estimated cost of defeating the epidemic in 2015 was $1.5 billion.
12"We continue to need resources," he said.
13"Obviously, if we don't get the full amount, we'll do what we can. But for the moment, we're worried."
14Asked about a new diagnostic field test kit for Ebola that the W.H.O. approved on Friday, which can determine in 15 minutes if someone is likely to have been infected, Dr. Aylward said it would be helpful, but "I wouldn't say it's a game changer."
15The new test, announced on the W.H.O. website, is based on detection of an Ebola protein rather than the virus's genetic material.
16Genetic-material tests can take 12 to 24 hours and must be done in a laboratory, but they are more reliable than the protein test.
17The protein test would require backup testing to confirm that a patient has Ebola.
18"With Ebola, this is an incredibly unforgiving disease," Dr. Aylward said.
19"The whole thing started with one case."
20He expressed caution about placing hope in "any test that can't find every virus reliably every single time."
