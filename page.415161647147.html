Central Figure in the Atlanta Schools Cheating Scandal Dies

Bali nine: chief prosecutor 'hopeful' Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran will be transferred within 48 hours

1Though 12 lower-level educators are currently on trial in a racketeering case related to the cheating, the death of Dr. Hall, 68, means that the prosecutors' highest-profile target will never face her accusers - and that the long-running scandal, a major embarrassment for this city, is bound to end on an unresolved note.
2Dr. Hall had been deemed too ill to stand trial on racketeering and other charges, for which the sentence could have been up to 45 years in prison.
3After details of improbable gains in test scores began to emerge late in 2008, Dr. Hall, once celebrated as a savior of the school system, became to many the chief villain in the drama, especially as they learned that she had received more than $500,000 in performance bonuses.
4But Dr. Hall, who came to the school system in 1999, asserted her innocence until the end, a fact reiterated by a statement released by her legal team Monday afternoon.
5"Even after millions of dollars, hundreds of witnesses and interviews, and a review of thousands upon thousands of emails, not a single witness has said, nor a single email demonstrated, that Dr. Hall ordered, directed or participated in cheating," the statement said.
6But others accused her of fomenting a culture in which widespread cheating was allowed and whistle-blowers were often ignored or punished.
7In 2010, Sonny Perdue, then Georgia's governor, appointed a group of special investigators to look into allegations of cheating.
8The report they returned the next year found that teachers on some Atlanta school campuses had been coaching students during standardized tests, and in other cases erasing wrong answers and filling in the correct ones.
9It was also unsparing in its criticism of Dr. Hall, a native of Jamaica who had previously served as an educator and administrator in New York, and as the superintendent in Newark.
10Dr. Hall and top members of her staff, the report stated, "created a culture of fear, intimidation and retaliation" that "infested the district" and "allowed cheating - at all levels - to go unchecked for years."
11The report found that 178 teachers and principals had cheated.
12Dozens retired, resigned or were fired, and 21 of them pleaded guilty to crimes like obstruction and false statements.
13The criminal case against the dozen who maintain they are innocent opened in September, and closing statements are set to begin March 16.
14Jurors are under orders to ignore any media reports of the case, though Gerald Griggs, one of the defense lawyers, said it might be difficult for them to avoid the news of Dr. Hall's death.
15Mr. Griggs, echoing a number of legal experts here, said he was unsure how that might affect the outcome - "except," he said "that the person alleged to have been the ringleader will never stand trial."
