Obama says Kenya is at a crossroads; urges nation to ditch 'bad traditions'

1Photo: Barack Obama said his ancestral homeland faced "tough choices" ahead.
2United States president Barack Obama has urged Kenya to renounce corruption and tribalism in a rousing speech at the end of a landmark visit to the East African nation and birthplace of his father.
3Speaking to a raucous crowd at an indoor arena in the capital Nairobi, Mr Obama said Kenya needed to ditch "bad traditions".
4"Kenya is at a crossroads, a moment filled with enormous peril but also enormous promise," he said in an address to the nation that was televised live.
5Seeking to leverage his status as a "son of the soil" and his huge local popularity, Mr Obama said his ancestral homeland faced "tough choices" ahead.
6He urged Kenyans to end the "bad tradition" of failing to empower women and warned "a politics based only on tribe and ethnicity is a politics doomed to tear a country apart".
7"Treating women as second-class citizens ... those are bad traditions, they need to change, they are holding you back," he said.
8"Corruption is not unique to Kenya but the fact is, too often corruption is tolerated because that's how things have always been done.
9Much of Mr Obama's speech stressed his affinity with young Kenyans, a vital group in a country where 60 per cent of the population is aged under 24.
10"He gets us," his half-sister Auma said, introducing a man she described as "my brother, your brother, our son."
11Compounding that message, Mr Obama recalled details of pre-presidential trips to Kenya replete with the stuff of everyday life: broken down cars, traditional foods, lost luggage and reconnecting with his family.
12To a mixture of applause and laughter, he described being picked up at the airport on his first visit to Kenya in the 1980s by his sister in an old Volkswagen Beetle that often broke down.
13This time, he arrived on Air Force One and travelled in the president's armoured car, nicknamed The Beast.
14Barack Obama Sr was a pipe-smoking economist who walked out when the president was just two, and died in a car crash in Nairobi in 1982, aged 46.
15At a state dinner late Saturday, Mr Obama also joked that some of his critics in the United States no doubt believed he was in Kenya "to look for my birth certificate", a reference to some who mistakenly believe he was born abroad.
16Photo: The visit has seen the US increase its security assistance to Kenya.
17Throughout his two-day trip, Mr Obama tried to bridge two constituencies: Americans re-examining their stereotypes of Africa, and Africans hoping for a better future.
18But the friendly, aspirational message belied a hard-nosed security need.
19A young but impoverished population could be fertile ground for instability and the growth of groups like Somalia's Al-Qaeda-affiliated Shebab.
20The group have been at the top of the list of security concerns surrounding the president's stay.
21The visit has seen the United States increase its security assistance to Kenya, although Mr Obama also urged the country to respect its Muslim minority.
22The Kasarani stadium complex where he delivered the speech was used to hold hundreds of ethnic Somalis during controversial mass arrests following the 2013 Somali-led Shebab assault on the Westgate shopping mall in central Nairobi that killed 67 people.
23After the speech, Mr Obama met with members of Kenya's vibrant civil society.
24His visit to Kenya had been delayed while president Uhuru Kenyatta faced charges of crimes against humanity for his role in post-election violence seven years ago.
25The International Criminal Court has since dropped the case, citing a lack of evidence and accusing Kenya of bribing or intimidating witnesses, although the trial of deputy president William Ruto continues.
26On Saturday, Mr Obama also stressed the importance of protecting basic rights, comparing homophobia in Africa to racial discrimination he had encountered in the United States.
27"As an African-American in the United States I am painfully aware of what happens when people are treated differently under the law. I am unequivocal on this," Mr Obama said, openly disagreeing with Mr Kenyatta.
28Homophobia is on the rise in Africa, and espousing evangelical Christian values is a major vote-winner in many countries.
29Mr Kenyatta replied by repeating the view that gay rights were unacceptable to Kenyans and therefore "a non-issue".
30Mr Obama leaves later Sunday for the Ethiopian capital Addis Ababa, where he will also become the first United States leader to address the African Union.
