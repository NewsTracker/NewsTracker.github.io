Greek debt deal hangs in balance

1Greece's Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis declared he was ready to do "whatever it takes" to reach agreement over its bailout after the collapse of talks with EU finance ministers.
2Mr Varoufakis spoke after Greece rejected an EU offer to extend its current 240bn ( 178bn) bailout, a plan he called "absurd" and "unacceptable".
3He said he was prepared to agree a deal but under different conditions.
4But the Dutch finance minister said there were just days left for talks.
5Jeroen Dijsselbloem, who chairs the Eurogroup of finance ministers, said it was now "up to Greece" to decide if it wanted more funding or not.
6"My strong preference is and still is to get an extension of the programme, and I think it is still feasible," Mr Dijsselbloem told a news conference after the talks collapsed.
7Greece's current bailout expires on 28 February.
8Any new agreement would need to be approved by national governments, so time is running out to reach a compromise.
9Without a deal Greece is likely to run out of money.
10Mr Varoufakis said there was still "substantial disagreement" on whether the task ahead was to complete the current programme, which Greece's newly elected government has pledged to scrap.
11He dismissed the promise of "some flexibility" in the programme as "nebulous" and lacking in detail.
12Speaking at a news conference after Mr Dijsselbloem, he said he had been presented with a draft communique by Pierre Moscovici, the EU's economics commissioner, which he had been ready to sign.
13However, that draft had been withdrawn minutes before the meeting started, Mr Varoufakis said.
14Two pressing financial issues loom over Greece: whether the government can pay its bills and the stability of the banks.
15Greek officials have said the government could keep going for several months, but there are doubts.
16How long it takes depends to a great extent on Greek taxpayers.
17The banks have already seen money being withdrawn and increasingly need central bank loans.
18If there is no bailout programme, the European Central Bank could pull the plug on the banks.
19If it came to that, it really would mean a major financial crisis, with perhaps the imposition of extensive financial controls to prop up the banks and possibly even the re-introduction of a national currency.
20It's hard to nail down a date by which an agreement must be done to avert some sort of financial Armageddon, because it depends on the actions of taxpayers, bank customers and the ECB.
21But time is getting short.
22Does Greece have a plan?
23Before the meeting, German finance minister Wolfgang Schaeuble had already said he was not optimistic a deal would be reached.
24"The problem is that Greece has lived beyond its means for a long time and that nobody wants to give Greece money any more without guarantees," he said.
25But French Finance Minister Michel Sapin said European leaders needed to respect the political change in Athens.
26As he arrived in Brussels he urged the Greeks to extend their current deal to allow time for talks.
27Greece has proposed a new bailout programme that involves a bridging loan to keep the country going for six months and help it repay 7bn ( 5.2bn) of maturing bonds.
28The second part of the plan would see the county's debt refinanced.
29Part of this might be through "GDP bonds" - bonds carrying an interest rate linked to economic growth.
30Greece also wants to see a reduction in the primary surplus target - the surplus the government must generate (excluding interest payments on debt) - from 3% to 1.49% of GDP.
31In Greece last week, two opinion polls indicated that 79% of Greeks supported the government's policies, and 74% believed its negotiating strategy would succeed.
