Mexican drug lord's escape tunnel was deep and 'high-tech'

1The kind of tunnel that led Mexico's most powerful drug lord to freedom would have been more than a year in planning.
2The digging would have caused noise.
3The entrance would have to be in a place beyond the view of security cameras at Mexico's toughest prison.
4As authorities hunted Monday for any sign of Mexico's most powerful drug lord, it was clear that Joaquin "El Chapo" Guzman's escape must have involved inside help on a grand scale.
5Interior Secretary Miguel Angel Osorio Chong said as much Monday night.
6He announced that three prison officials had been fired, including Valentin Cardenas, director of the facility known as Altiplano, a maximum security prison 90 kilometres west of Mexico City.
7"They had something or a lot to do with what happened, and that's why we made that decision," Osorio Chong said.
8Still, he did not say who exactly is suspected of aiding the escape.
9The prison has the same high-security standards as those in the U.S. and Canada, he said, and Guzman was given extra surveillance, including a tracking bracelet, although it worked only inside the prison.
10Osorio Chong said the 1.5-kilometre tunnel had been dug 19 metres below the surface and called it a "high-tech" breach of the prison's extensive security measures, including 750 cameras and 26 security filters.
11The Mexican government announced that it is offering a $3.8 million reward for Guzman's recapture.
12An Interpol alert was sent to 10 countries and at least 49 people have been questioned by the government's organized crime unit, including 34 prison employees.
13A tunnel of such sophistication - with lights, air venting, and a customized motorcycle rigged up on a rail line - would normally take 18 months to two years to complete, said Jim Dinkins, former head of Immigration and Customs Enforcement's Homeland Security Investigations.
14"When it's for the boss, you probably put that on high speed," he said.
15Guzman is among the world's most wealthy and powerful drug traffickers.
16If anyone was capable of pulling off such a feat, it was Guzman, who is believed to have at least a quarter-century of experience in building large, sophisticated tunnels to smuggle drugs under the U.S.-Mexico border and to escape from hideouts as authorities closed in.
17His Sinaloa Cartel also has been most successful in coopting officials, said Edgardo Buscaglia, an organized crime expert at Colombia University.
18"By far they are the most infiltrated in Mexico's government institutions," he said.
19Experts expressed skepticism that such an engineering feat could go on undetected.
20Joe Garcia, who retired this year as interim special agent in charge of U.S. Homeland Security Investigations in San Diego, has extensive experience in tunnel investigations.
21He said the tunnel at Altiplano was longer than any passage ever found on the U.S.-Mexico border.
22To pull off such a feat, rescuers likely had intelligence on the prison even before Guzman was arrested, Dinkins said.
23Designers and workers would have needed access to sensitive information such as prison floor plans and alarm and camera systems.
24And just the noise alone as they bored the final 10-metre vertical shaft directly under the prison to reach Guzman's cell would have generated some attention.
25A policeman keeps watch at the site where the tunnel connected to the Altiplano Federal Penitentiary.
26"It's not just like someone took a couple tools, shovels and pickaxes. This is a very sophisticated operation," said Alonzo Pena, a former senior official at U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement.
27"How could they be there and not hear that construction was going on underneath? It's just impossible."
28The area around the prison is a sprawling construction site, with giant water pipes, trenches and excavation crisscrossing the landscape, and that would have made it easy for the tunnel work to go unnoticed, Garcia said.
29"It's the removal of dirt and debris that can cause attention," Dinkins said.
30An Associated Press reporter who viewed the partially built house where the tunnel ended observed that the dirt level in the yard surrounding the house was about one metre higher than the rest of the terrain, indicating workers may have just spread dirt around the property rather than haul it out.
31Osorio Chong also said much of the excavated dirt, which he described as fine and thin, may simply have been scattered or compacted at the site.
32By Monday afternoon, the search for Guzman in the area near the prison had died down dramatically.
33Road blocks were gone and Federal Police patrols had lightened.
34Reporters traveled the area unhampered.
35Flights at the nearby Toluca airport that had been suspended on Sunday were back to normal.
36The prison break is the second for Guzman, who infamously fled a different maximum-security facility in 2001.
