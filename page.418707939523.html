Facebook loses in clash with prosecutors over users' data

1NEW YORK -- Facebook can't block nearly 400 search warrants seeking users' postings for a criminal fraud investigation, an appeals court said Tuesday, but the judges said they understand the social networking site's unease about prosecutors' extensive request.
2The state Supreme Court Appellate Division ruling won't put any new information in prosecutors' hands.
3Facebook had lost earlier rulings and already turned the data over.
4But the case has been closely watched by social media companies, civil libertarians and prosecutors.
5Facebook said it was weighing its options for continuing the fight.
6FILE - This June 11, 2014 file photo shows Facebook's "like" symbol at the entrance to the company's campus in Menlo Park, Calif.
7"We continue to believe that overly broad search warrants -- granting the government the ability to keep hundreds of people's account information indefinitely -- are unconstitutional and raise important concerns about the privacy of people's online information," Facebook, which is based in Menlo Park, California, said in a statement.
8The Manhattan district attorney's office, meanwhile, noted the unanimous decision from the Appellate Division's First Department about the warrants, which a lower-court judge had approved at the outset.
9The 381 warrants helped build a massive disabilities benefits fraud case against police and fire department retirees, and 108 people have pleaded guilty.
10"In many cases, evidence on their Facebook accounts directly contradicted the lies the defendants told to the Social Security Administration" about being too psychologically devastated to work, district attorney's office spokeswoman Joan Vollero said Tuesday.
11Some defendants disclosed on Facebook that they flew helicopters, traveled overseas, did martial arts and otherwise led active, full lives.
12Tuesday's ruling centered on procedural questions about when a search warrant can be challenged.
13The court said Facebook wrongly argued it could legally stop prosecutors from gathering the information, as opposed to defendants arguing for keeping it out of court afterward.
14But "our holding today does not mean that we do not appreciate Facebook's concerns about the scope of the bulk warrants issued here or about the district attorney's alleged right to indefinitely retain the seized accounts of the uncharged Facebook users," the five-judge panel wrote.
15Facebook can contain more intimate personal information than someone's home, the judges noted, and only 62 of the people targeted in the 381 warrants have been charged.
16Prosecutors have said they gave the initial judge 93 pages of details on why all the accounts were targeted.
17A Manhattan judge OK'd the warrants in July 2013, saying law enforcement has authority to search massive amounts of material to seek evidence.
18Facebook said prosecutors cast too wide a net.
19Google, Twitter and other online players joined the case on Facebook's side, as did the American and New York civil liberties union.
20NYCLU staff attorney Mariko Hirose said the group was disappointed that Tuesday's ruling didn't address the privacy issues in the case more directly.
