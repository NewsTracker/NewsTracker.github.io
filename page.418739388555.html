Greek bailout: Leaders approve second set of changes

1The Greek parliament in session on Thursday.
2Athens: Under the threat of yet another deadline, the Greek Parliament approved a second package of policy changes early on Thursday that the country's creditors had said must be in place before the detailed negotiations for an 86 billion bailout (or more than $127 billion) could begin.
3The new measures, overhauling the banking and judicial systems, passed easily with significant support from opposition parties eager to do whatever it takes to keep Greece from leaving the eurozone.
4The vote was 230-63, with five abstentions and two absences.
5Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras, right, listens to Finance Minister Euclid Tsakalotos as Justice Minister Nikos Paraskevopoulos, centre, looks on during a parliamentary session in Athens on Thursday.
6Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras was able to win over only two additional members of his leftist Syriza party, compared with the first major vote last week on the bailout accord.
7Most of the party's hard-liners continued to rail against what they said was the creditors' "blackmail" and voted no, making it likely that new elections would be held in the autumn.
8Mr Tsipras spoke just before the vote, telling members of Parliament that the final terms of the deal with creditors were still under negotiation and that more would be done to protect the poorest members of Greek society.
9"We're not done here," he said, adding: "We are fighting to improve the final text of the deal. We will fight for counter-measures and for funds for society."
10The rift within Mr Tsipras' party has grown heated in recent days, with both sides taking shots at each other and the government's spokesman saying that a "divorce" might be inevitable.
11The vote showed that Mr Tsipras had gained support from one of his most vocal critics lately, his former finance minister, Yanis Varoufakis, who stepped down this month in an apparent gesture to European creditors who had found him difficult to deal with.
12Speaker of Parliament Zoe Konstantopoulou speaking during an emergency parliament session in Athens on Thursday.
13Mr Varoufakis, who voted no last week, voted yes this time.
14But 36 Syriza members remained opposed, deeply frustrated with Mr Tsipras' decision to bow to the creditors, in the face of closed banks and an economy on the verge of collapse.
15In Brussels, Pierre Moscovici, the European commissioner for economy, said at a daily news conference that he was pleased with the progress in Greece, that formal talks with Athens on granting a third bailout had begun, and that they could be completed during the second half of August.
16Asked about a decision by Athens to postpone legislation overhauling pensions and tax issues affecting farmers, Mr Moscovici suggested that those laws could wait.
17He said that passage by the Greek Parliament of other changes would be sufficient to keep bailout talks on track.
18Acting leader on main opposition New Democracy conservative party Vangelis Meimarakis addresses parliamentarians on Thursday.
19The measures approved early Thursday will streamline court procedures, which are notoriously slow here, and save billions of euros.
20They will also adopt some new European Union banking regulations that aim to shield taxpayers from having to bail out troubled lenders and were supposed to have been passed months ago.
21The new regulations impose losses on shareholders and depositors of ailing banks before any taxpayer money can be tapped to rescue a lender.
22The measures that were approved last week called for higher taxes and cuts in pensions.
23Until the last few days, the disagreement between Mr Tsipras and the hard-line faction of his party, the Left Platform, largely played out behind closed doors and without rancour.
24On Tuesday night, however, Mr Tsipras, perhaps recognising that he was making little headway in persuading the Left Platform to stand by him, let it be known that he believed the hard-liners were conveniently "hiding behind the safety of his signature".
25Alexis Tsipras, Greece's prime minister, before addressing his colleagues in the Greek parliament on Wednesday.
26They responded with a lengthy critique of Mr Tsipras' performance posted on Iskra.gr, the blog considered the voice of the Left Platform.
27On a morning radio news show, the new government spokeswoman, Olga Gerovasili, said that a split in the Syriza party might be unavoidable.
28Although there was never much question that the bills would be approved, that did not stop legislators from taking the floor to blame one another for the country's dire economic straits.
29Some members of Syriza objected to being given 1,000 pages of legislation to review in only two days.
30Zoi Konstantopoulou, Syriza's outspoken speaker of the Parliament, who voted against the measures, said it was impossible for the members to really know what was in the documents.
31She said they would take an average person 32 hours to read.
32"Maybe we should issue a stamp that says 'Parliament of Greece' and give it" to the country's creditors, Ms Konstantopoulou said, so they can "ratify whatever they want".
