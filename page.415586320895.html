Marvin Gaye family seeks to stop 'Blurred Lines'

Cyclone Nathan makes landfall in Australia, losses strength

1Los Angeles (AFP) - The family of Motown legend Marvin Gaye has asked a court to block further sales of smash hit "Blurred Lines" after a jury concluded that the song was plagiarized.
2Gaye's estate on March 10 was awarded more than $7 million after convincing a jury that Robin Thicke and Pharrell Williams' "Blurred Lines," the biggest hit of 2013, plagiarized the late singer's 1977 track "Got to Give It Up."
3In a request for an injunction, the Gaye family asked a federal court in Los Angeles to halt all distribution of "Blurred Lines" and to bar Thicke and Williams from performing it in light of the landmark decision.
4"A permanent injunction is justified because there is continued infringement of 'Got to Give It Up, " said the motion submitted Tuesday.
5"The jury's award only applies to past sales of the infringing work and does not provide the Gayes a percentage of future sales and profit."
6The family said that it was not seeking to kill the song permanently but rather to negotiate terms for Gaye to share credit and revenue.
7The family rejected arguments that the verdict -- which looked, due to copyright law, at the sheet music for the two songs -- did not apply to the recording, saying that without the song, all label Interscope "would be distributing is a picture of Robin Thicke and a CD containing silence."
8Thicke himself in media interviews before the court case had said that he had been inspired by Gaye.
9But the court decision has triggered an angry response from many songwriters, who note that there were major differences between the two songs, including the melodies and lyrics.
10Williams, a popular songwriter who had another smash hit with "Happy," said in an interview published Thursday that all creative people had inspirations.
11"The verdict handicaps any creator out there who is making something that might be inspired by something else," Williams told the Financial Times.
1SYDNEY - A powerful tropical cyclone was losing strength after crossing Australia's north-east coast on Friday, with no immediate reports of injuries.
2Cyclone Nathan made landfall shortly before dawn as a category four system, the second highest intensity rating, and was expected to continue its westward movement inland while weakening, according to Australia's Bureau of Meteorology.
3The storm was downgraded by the bureau to a category three system at about 2200 GMT.
4At its peak, Nathan was packing sustained winds near the centre of 130 km (85 miles) per hour with wind gusts to 189 km per hour.
5Queensland Premier Annastacia Palaszczuk told reporters there have been no reports of injury.
6The cyclone is expected to lose much of its punch over land, but meteorologists warned it could reform again if it reaches the warm waters of the Gulf of Carpentaria, 250 km (155 miles) from the coast.
7The archipelago nation of Vanuatu was devastated last weekend by Cyclone Pam, one of the strongest storms ever to hit the South Pacific.
