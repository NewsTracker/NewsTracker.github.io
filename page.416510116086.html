Each death in Baltimore makes mistrust harder to overcome

1Baltimore Police Department Commissioner Anthony Batts speaks about the investigation into Freddie Gray's death at a news conference, Friday, April 24, 2015, in Baltimore.
2Gray died from spinal injuries about a week after he was arrested and transported in a police van.
3BALTIMORE (AP) - Baltimore's mayor and police commissioner came in making big promises to the inner-city residents and police who spent decades staring each other down in neighborhoods ravaged by crack and heroin.
4But with each death of a black man in custody, their efforts to overcome mistrust have hit hard walls of skepticism and outrage.
5Two and a half years into his job leading the city's police department, Commissioner Anthony Batts is frustrated that the people he was appointed to serve have lost their faith in justice.
6"They don't believe what I say," Batts said in an interview Thursday with The Associated Press.
7Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake hired Batts in September 2012 after a previous round of police scandals involving excessive use of force, and she was still supporting her commissioner ahead of more protests Saturday.
8Batts also rejected demands by several African-American ministers that he resign.
9"That's not going to happen," he announced Friday.
10He promised to be as transparent as possible without compromising an investigation that could lead to criminal charges.
11Six officers have been suspended with pay since Freddie Gray died of a spinal injury he suffered during an arrest Batts characterized as questionable.
12Both Batts and the mayor, who took office in 2010, are African-American and no strangers to communities like Sandtown, a set of public housing projects not far from Baltimore's Inner Harbor and the Orioles' baseball stadium where Gray tried to outrun three bicycle cops who spotted him on a street corner on April 12.
13Gray, 25, was pinned to the ground and already injured when he was handcuffed and hoisted into a van, police acknowledged Friday.
14He repeatedly begged for medical help, but officers instead added leg irons and locked him inside again.
15Gray was eventually hospitalized in critical condition, and died a week later.
16Batts, who spent decades policing in Long Beach and Oakland, California, before taking this job, insists he is part of the solution in Baltimore.
17He said he's fired 50 police employees and reduced officer-involved shootings, and the use-of-force reports police must file dropped from 598 in 2012 to 435 in 2014.
18"I have been a reform commissioner. I have taken an organization that has many challenges and faced them head-on," he insisted, even as he acknowledged that some cases have "tarnished this badge and the reputation of the department."
19With each death in custody and each police brutality settlement, the trust erodes even more.
20A Baltimore Sun investigation revealed last year that the city has paid roughly $5.7 million in brutality settlements since 2011, involving 102 instances of excessive force.
21And taxpayers keep paying: Just days after Gray's death, the city's Board of Estimates approved $80,000 for 37-year-old Timothy Ashe, whose teeth were punched out by police officer after parking illegally.
22A week earlier, $175,000 was awarded to the family of Michael Wudtee, who was fatally shot by an officer at a gas station in July 2012.
23- Tyrone West, 44, died in July 2013; he stopped breathing after he was tackled and handcuffed by officers during a traffic stop.
24The autopsy blamed a heart condition aggravated by the struggle and hot temperatures.
25An internal review board said the officers were justified, but said officers "made several tactical errors."
26West's family is suing.
27- Anthony Anderson, 46, died in September 2012 after police said they broke up his drug deal.
28Officers said he fought with them and was slammed to the ground.
29His death, of internal bleeding from blunt force injuries, was ruled a homicide, but an internal review board ruled officers were justified, and prosecutors declined to charge them.
