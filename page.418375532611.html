Activist: Boko Haram offering swap for captive girls

1LAGOS, Nigeria -- Nigeria's Boko Haram extremists are offering to free more than 200 young women and girls kidnapped from a boarding school in the town of Chibok in exchange for the release of militant leaders held by the government, a human rights activist has told The Associated Press.
2The activist said Boko Haram's current offer is limited to the girls from the school in northeastern Nigeria whose mass abduction in April 2014 ignited worldwide outrage and a campaign to "Bring Back Our Girls" that stretched to the White House.
3The new initiative reopens an offer made last year to the government of former President Goodluck Jonathan to release the 219 students in exchange for 16 Boko Haram detainees, the activist said.
4The man, who was involved in negotiations with Boko Haram last year and is close to current negotiators, spoke on condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to talk to reporters on this sensitive issue.
5Fred Eno, an apolitical Nigerian who has been negotiating with Boko Haram for more than a year, told the AP that "another window of opportunity opened" in the last few days, though he could not discuss details.
6He said the recent slew of Boko Haram bloodletting - some 350 people killed in the past nine days - is consistent with past ratcheting up of violence as the militants seek a stronger negotiating position.
7Presidential adviser Femi Adesina said on Saturday that Nigeria's government "will not be averse" to talks with Boko Haram.
8"Most wars, however furious or vicious, often end around the negotiation table," he said.
9Eno said the 5-week-old administration of President Muhammadu Buhari offers "a clean slate" to bring the militants back to negotiations that had become poisoned by the different security agencies and their advice to Jonathan.
10Two months of talks last year led government representatives and Eno to travel in September to a northeastern town where the prisoner exchange was to take place, only to be stymied by the Department for State Service intelligence agency, the activist said.
11At the last minute, the agency said it was holding only four of the militants sought by Boko Haram, the activist said.
12It is not known how many Boko Haram suspects are detained by Nigeria's intelligence agency, whose chief Buhari fired last week.
13The activist said the agency continues to hold suspects illegally because it does not have enough evidence for a conviction, and any court would free them.
14Nigerian law requires charges be brought after 48 hours.
15Thousands of suspects have died in custody, and some detainees wanted by Boko Haram may be among them.
16Amnesty International alleges that 8,000 detainees have died in military custody - some have been shot, some have died from untreated injuries due to torture, and some have died from starvation and other harsh treatment.
17In May, about 300 women, girls and children being held captive by Boko Haram were rescued by Nigeria's military, but none were from Chibok.
18It is believed that the militants view the Chibok girls as a last-resort bargaining chip.
19In that infamous abduction, 274 mostly Christian girls preparing to write science exams were seized from the school by Islamic militants in the early hours of April 15, 2014.
20Dozens escaped on their own in the first few days, but 219 remain missing.
21Boko Haram has not shown them since a May 2014 video in which its leader, Abubakar Shekau warned: "You won't see the girls again unless you release our brothers you have captured."
22In the video, nearly 100 of the girls, who have been identified by their parents, were shown wearing Islamic hijab and reciting the Quran.
23One of them said they had converted to Islam.
24International indignation at Nigeria's failure to rescue the girls was joined by U.S. first lady Michelle Obama.
25In a radio address in May 2014, she said she and President Barack Obama are "outraged and heartbroken" over the abduction.
26Jonathan's government initially denied there had been any mass abduction and delays of a rescue that might have brought the girls home became a hallmark of his other failures.
27He steadfastly refused to meet with the Bring Back Our Girls campaigners, charging they were politicizing the issue.
28On Wednesday, President Buhari welcomed those campaigners at the presidential villa in Abuja and pleaded "We only ask for your patience."
29He said "The delay and conflicting reaction by the former government and its agencies is very unfortunate."
30Campaign leader Oby Ezekwesili said, "The rescue of our Chibok girls is the strongest statement that this government could make to showg respect for the sanctity and dignity of every Nigerian life."
31There have been unconfirmed reports that some of the girls have been taken to neighboring countries, and that some have been radicalized and trained as fighters.
32At least three were reported to have died - one from dysentery, one from malaria and one from a snake bite.
33Last year, Shekau said the girls were an "old story," and that he had married them off to his fighters.
34Lawan Zanna, whose daughter is among the captives, said this week that 14 Chibok parents have died since the mass kidnapping, many from stress-related illnesses blamed on the ordeal.
35Some of the Chibok girls who managed to escape have been rejected by their community and now live with family friends, tired of hearing taunts like "Boko Haram wives."
36The assumption that all girls and women held by the group have been raped is a difficult stigma to overcome in Nigeria's highly religious and conservative society.
37Shekau had threatened in 2013 to kidnap women and girls if Nigeria's military did not release detained Boko Haram wives and children.
38The government freed them in May of that year as a goodwill gesture ahead of failed peace talks.
39Boko Haram has kidnapped hundreds more - girls, boys, women and young men.
40Some have become sex slaves, while others are used as fighters, according to former captives.
41Nigerian opinion on negotiating with the extremists is mixed.
42Some say the group's crimes are too heinous to be forgiven: The 6-year-old Islamic uprising has killed more than 13,000 people and forced about 1.5 million from their homes.
43"A lot of people take a hard-line stance that you must never negotiate with a terrorist," said Sen. Chris Anyanwu.
44She called it a "very complex" issue, balancing the lives of more than 200 girls against the dangers of freeing extremists.
45The militants last year seized a large swath of northeast Nigeria and declared an Islamic caliphate.
46Nigeria and its neighbors deployed a multinational army that forced them out of towns and villages this year, but the bloodshed has risen at a fierce rate since Buhari's May 29 inauguration amid pledges to crush the insurgency.
