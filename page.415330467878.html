Chad, Niger troops push Boko Haram out of two Nigerian towns

British military bases in Cyprus are failing to stop illegal traps for migrating songbirds

1Troops from Chad and Niger have freed two towns in northern Nigeria previously held by Boko Haram, the first gains made in a joint offensive launched at the weekend, military sources said on Monday.
2About 30 Nigerien and Chadian soldiers were injured in fighting over Malam Fatouri and Damasak, a day after thousands of troops crossed the border to retake areas held by the feared armed group, whose insurgency has forced Nigeria to delay an election and neighbors to mobilize their armies.
3A Chadian officer, who asked not to be named, said about 10 Chadian soldiers were killed and 20 wounded in fighting for the towns.
4There was no official comment from Chad's army.
5"We have kicked the enemy out of these areas and they are now under our control," one of the military sources said.
6Damasak, the town furthest into Nigeria, is 6 miles south of the Niger border, where Niger and Chadian troops have been massing in recent weeks ahead of the offensive.
7The source said that hundreds of Boko Haram fighters had been killed in combat.
8There was no official confirmation of the toll and it was not possible to verify the figure.
9"We had permission from Nigeria for this action," the source said.
10There was no immediate comment from Nigeria, which has launched its own offensive against the group, whose gains forced Nigeria to delay elections that were due in February.
11Boko Haram's six-year insurgency, which aims at carving out a separate state in Nigeria's northeast, has killed thousands.
12The group has pledged allegiance to Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant (ISIL), which rules a self-declared caliphate in parts of Iraq and Syria, according to an audio clip posted online on Saturday.
13Cameroon, Chad, Niger and Benin have this year mobilized forces to help Nigeria defeat the group after it seized swathes of territory and mounting cross-border attacks.
14Nigeria and its neighbors have been working to pull together plans and rules of engagement for a regional force of 8,700 troops but cooperation between the region's armies has been strained at times.

1More than two million migratory birds were deliberately killed in Cyprus last autumn, according to new data.
2The birds, which cost 80 euros ( 86) for a dozen, are a local delicacy known as ambelopoulia.
3The illegal activity has now caused a drastic drop in the numbers of blackcaps, robins and songbirds and has been dubbed 'out of control'.
4A spokesman for the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds (RSPB), a partner of Birdlife Cyprus, described the issue as 'trapping on an industrial level'.
5He said: 'We are hoping that we can begin to get a grip and would like the British base of Dhekelia to help clear the acacia, which trappers plant to lure in birds.
6'It is so obvious when it is taking place.
7It is easy to detect and easy to see where it has been planted.
8We accept it is a difficult job but we would like to see some commitment to a timetable.
9'Unfortunately last year the action the British military took was too late because it was after the trapping season.
10'At the moment the Ministry of Defence are arguing with our figures but everybody needs to work together.
11The birds are caught by trappers using netting or limesticks - twigs covered in sticky lime or glue.
12Nearly ten miles of net supports and more than 6,000 limesticks were discovered between September and October last year, according to Birdlife Cyprus.
13Trappers, who like to use the area around Dhekelia, also play birdsong through loudspeakers hidden in the bushes to lure more in and throw gravel to force birds towards their nets.
14Nearly 4,000 poachers are believed to be involved in the autumn trapping season - much higher than in summer because the birds are plumper.
15Clairie Papazoglou, Birdlife Cyprus' chief, said poaching was a 'serious, persistent and growing problem'.
16She added that it 'has been the worst year with the highest trapping levels since the start of the monitoring programme in 2002'.
17Birdlife Cyprus has also called for tougher sentencing but admitted attitudes towards killing and eating migratory birds would have to change to make a difference.
18Poachers that are caught can currently receive penalties of up to three years in jail and fines as high as 17,000 euros ( 12,215).
19A Ministry of Defence spokeswoman said: 'We do not accept the report's unverified claims about loss of bird life during this period, which was based upon data collected from a very short period.
20'We are committed to tackling poaching which is why we arrested nearly 50 poachers and seized 450 nets and 286 piece of poaching equipment during the last migration period.
21When we catch poachers we can fine them 17,000, or send them to prison for up to 3 years.
