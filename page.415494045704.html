Pakistan mourns for victims of deadly Taliban church attacks, Christian protesters block roads

1Sunday's twin bombings struck in quick succession in the Christian neighborhood of Youhanabad during morning services.
2The bombers detonated their suicide vests outside the two churches, about 600 meters (650 yards) apart.
3At least 70 people were wounded in the attacks, the latest assault on religious minorities in this increasingly fractured country.
4After the attacks, angry Christian mobs blocked the highway, ransacked bus terminals and burned two people to death who they suspected of being involved in the bombings.
5Christian demonstrators also blocked roads Monday in the eastern cities of Faisalabad, Sargodha and Gujranwala.
6In Lahore, they blocked a major highway that runs near the two targeted churches and forced public transport services to shut down.
7They later pelted the police with stones and smashed police cars and other vehicles, according to Rana Sanaullah, an official with the ruling PML-N party.
8He said officers were trying to calm down the situation.
9Lahore's Police Deputy Inspector General Haider Ashraf told reporters that his men were "showing restraint" but had to fire tear gas canisters when the situation deteriorated.
10The city Commissioner Abdullah Sumbal Khan said Pakistani paramilitary troops had been called in to restore order.
11A least one protester died after being hit by a car, Ashraf said.
12Local television stations aired footage showing the car speeding away from the scene after hitting several protesters.
13Ashraf put the total death toll from Sunday's bombings at 17, after two of wounded later died in the hospital.
14Christian schools remained closed as prayer services and funerals for the bombing victims took place, said Shipping and Ports Minister Kamran Michael, a prominent Christian community leader.
15On Monday, U.N. Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon condemned the attacks "in the strongest possible terms" and noted "with grave concern that the Pakistani Taliban has claimed responsibility for the attacks while threatening to carry out more such acts in the future," U.N. deputy spokesman Farhan Haq said.
16Ban called on Pakistan's government to swiftly bring the perpetrators to justice "and to do its utmost to prevent attacks against places of worship and protect religious minorities," he said.
17Also Monday, Interior Minister Chaudhry Nisar Ali Khan denounced the church attacks as an "inhuman act of terrorism" and said it reflected the militants' "frustration" in the face of a stepped-up military operation against them.
18Last June, Pakistan launched a major operation to rout militants from their strongholds.
19"We have shrunk the space for them to operate," Khan said Monday.
20Life in Pakistan is increasingly dangerous for religious minorities.
21They have been targeted by extremist Sunni Muslim militants and are also discriminated against in the wider society, where they are often limited to menial jobs like garbage collection.
22Much of the country is already on edge after years of militant violence, including an attack on a Peshawar school in December that killed 150 people - mostly students.
23The Pakistani Taliban group, which claimed responsibility for the church attacks, has been waging an insurgency for over a decade, seeking to overthrow the government and install their own brand of fundamentalist Islamic rule.
24Thousands of Pakistanis have been killed in the attacks.
25Associated Press writers Ishtiaq Mahsud in Tank, Pakistan and Zarar Khan and Asif Shahzad in Islamabad contributed to this report.
