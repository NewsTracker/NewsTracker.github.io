How a former spy managed to survive two years in prison after leaking CIA secrets

1ARLINGTON, Va.
2- John Kiriakou claims to have achieved an exceedingly rare double-distinction for a federal inmate upon his incarceration: being greeting warmly by black nationalists from the Nation of Islam, and invited to dinner by white supremacists.
3A former spy has described the debate within the CIA over the arrest, rendition and torture of Canadian Maher Arar, saying multiple colleagues warned against it because they were convinced they were punishing an innocent man.
4It's a rare peek into discussions within the U.S. Central Intelligence Agency - whose role in the 2002-03 events has never been publicly examined, having remained off-limits in Canada's inquiry.
5He was never a normal inmate.
6Kiriakou arrived in prison with countless state secrets, had participated in the manhunt for Osama bin Laden, led post-9-11 counterterrorism arrests in Pakistan, and had learned Arabic for his old job.
7His old job was being a spy.
8He was jailed for telling journalists a bit too much about his former employer: the CIA.
9He insists he was punished for blowing the whistle on the use of torture in 2007, not because he tipped off journalists to the identity of a couple of former spy colleagues, which is why he was charged.
10"I'm 100 per cent positive," Kiriakou says in an interview at home, where he's completing his sentence under house arrest after two years in jail.
11He's adamant that he's being singled out.
12Lots of names leak out of the agency without consequences, he says.
13Also, he accuses the FBI of trying to entrap him several times and failing.
14To avoid a return trip to prison, there are limits to what he'll say in interviews.
15He will describe how former CIA colleagues protested the arrest, transfer, and torture in Syria of Canadian Maher Arar - but he absolutely won't reveal the name of a woman in CIA middle-management who he says insisted on Arar's arrest.
16He'll gladly discuss his latest book.
17He'd already published one about his CIA career.
18This forthcoming one was written by hand, in prison.
19It's titled, "Doing Time Like A Spy - How the CIA Taught Me To Survive And Thrive In Prison."
20It offers 20 life-lessons learned in the CIA, and used in the slammer.
21He describes tricking two particularly repellent inmates.
22One had raped numerous prostitutes.
23Another ordered a hit on his business partner, then ratted out the hitman.
24Kiriakou told one that the other had called him a rat, one of the worst prison insults.
25A fight ensued.
26One was moved to another prison, the other to solitary: "I thought, this is a way to get rid of both of them," Kiriakou said.
27Fights were actually rare.
28Most were about TV.
29A near-scuffle also broke out at the Mafia Christmas dinner - a guy from one Mob family didn't save a seat for a guy from a rival clan.
30He spent plenty of time with the Italians, eating with them during his second year in jail.
31He'd dined with white supremacists in Year One.
32The cafeteria sections were segregated, mostly by race: African-American, Latino, the Aryan Brotherhood, the Mafia, and the least coveted tables were reserved for the those on the lowest rung of the prison hierarchy - child molesters and informants.
33In the ecosystem of the minimum-security prison cafeteria there was, alas, no obvious spot for the former CIA head of Pakistan counterterrorism operations.
34So he first wound up with white supremacists.
35He says burly Aryans wandered into his bunk area asking if he was a homosexual, a child molester, or a rat.
36He answered, "No," to all three - and was invited to join their table.
37He says he was greeted warmly by Black Muslims because their leader, Louis Farrakhan, had said nice things about him.
38He made a cafeteria switch a year later.
39"I said, 'OK, thanks.' So I sat with the Italians the rest of the time& I was much, much more comfortable with the Italians."
40In fact, he made friends - including drug dealers from Philadelphia and Detroit.
41He describes men who'd made mistakes - in some cases fleeting ones - that ruined their lives.
42He says people with mental illness went to prison, and wound up worse.
43He expresses much less sympathy for prison guards.
44He describes the prison system as a make-work program for the unemployable, and plans to incorporate justice reform into his future work.
45"I'll tell you what motivated me: The sick bastards who run the place."
46Kiriakou describes how guards made fun of inmates' names; instigated fights; and punished with cruelty.
47He says the only required qualification seemed to be a high-school diploma.
48He recalls them struggling to read simple names, like "Jones," when distributing mail.
49"Now all of a sudden they can control people - and more than that, they can control people who can't talk back."
50Prisoners obeyed, to avoid solitary confinement.
51Kiriakou never got solitary.
52He says it helped that journalists and actor John Cusack were tweeting about his case.
53"What are you (guards) going to do - put me in solitary? It's gonna be a lot harder on you than it is on me, because you're gonna have to deal with CNN in the parking lot."
54To his detractors, Kiriakou's a self-promoting storyteller who imperilled former colleagues by blabbing to the media.
55They note that some of his stories have shifted, over time.
56To supporters, he's a whistle-blowing martyr for a noble cause.
57In their view, he's the only person ever punished for torture within the CIA - just for speaking out.
58He lost a lucrative post-CIA consulting career, his agency pension, and is now renting a smaller, more affordable house with his family while renting out their own.
59He hopes for a presidential pardon.
60It would help him retrieve the $800,000 CIA pension he says he lost.
61He expects a long pardon list in President Barack Obama's last months in office: "I really hope I'm on it."
