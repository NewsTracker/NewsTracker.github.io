Iraqi militias train young teens to face the threat of Islamic State war

1BAGHDAD - In the steamy Baghdad night, sweat poured down the faces of the Iraqi teens as they marched around a school courtyard, training for battle against the Islamic State group.
2This is summer camp in Iraq, set up by the country's largest paramilitary force after Iraq's top Shiite cleric issued an edict calling on students as young as middle-school age to use their summer vacations to prepare to fight the Sunni extremists.
3These young fighters could have serious implications for the U.S.-led coalition, which provides billions of dollars in military and economic aid to the Iraqi government.
4The Child Soldiers Prevention Act of 2008 says the United States cannot provide certain forms of military support, including foreign military financing and direct commercial sales to governments that recruit and use child soldiers or support paramilitaries or militias that do.
5Hundreds of students have gone through training at the dozens of such camps run by the Popular Mobilization Forces, the government-sanctioned umbrella group of mostly Shiite militias.
6It is impossible to say how many went on to fight IS, since those who do so go independently.
7But this summer, The Associated Press saw over a dozen armed boys on the front line in western Anbar province, including some as young as 10.
8Of around 200 cadets in a training class visited by the AP this month, about half were under the age of 18, with some as young as 15.
9Several said they intended to join their fathers and older brothers on the front lines.
10Dressed in military fatigues, 15-year-old Asam Riad was among dozens of youths doing high-knee marches at the school, his chest puffed out to try to appear as tall as the older cadets.
11"We've been called to defend the nation," the scrawny boy asserted, his voice cracking as he vowed to join the PMF.
12"I am not scared because my brothers are fighting alongside me."
13Another 15-year-old in the class, Jaafar Osama, said he used to want to be an engineer when he grows up, but now he wants to be a fighter.
14His father is a volunteer fighting alongside the Shiite militias in Anbar and his older brother is fighting in Beiji, north of Baghdad.
15"God willing, when I complete my training I will join them, even if it means sacrificing my life to keep Iraq safe," he said.
16It's yet another way minors are being dragged into Iraq's brutal war as the military, Shiite militias, Sunni tribes and Kurdish fighters battle to take back territory from Islamic State militants who seized much of the country's north and west last year.
17The Sunni extremists have aggressively enlisted children as young as 10 for combat, as suicide bombers and as executioners in their horrifying videos.
18This month, Human Rights Watch said that Syrian Kurdish militias fighting the militants continue to deploy underage fighters.
19The U.S. does not work directly with the Popular Mobilization Forces and has distanced itself from the Iranian-backed militias which are among the fighters under its umbrella.
20When informed of the AP findings, the U.S. Embassy in Baghdad issued a statement saying the U.S. is "very concerned by the allegations on the use of child soldiers in Iraq among some Popular Mobilization forces in the fight against ISIL," using an acronym for the militant group.
21"We have strongly condemned this practice around the world and will continue to do so."
22For Iraq's Shiite majority, the war against the Islamic State group - which views them as heretics to be killed - is a life-or-death fight for which the entire community has mobilized.
23Last year, when IS took over the northern city of Mosul, stormed to the doorstep of Baghdad and threatened to destroy Shiite holy sites, Iraq's top Shiite cleric, Ayatollah Ali al-Sistani, called on the public to volunteer to fight.
24So great was his influence that hundreds of thousands of men came forward to join the hastily-established Popular Mobilization Forces along with some of the long-established Shiite militias, many of which receive support from Iran.
25Then, on June 9, as schools let out, al-Sistani issued a new fatwa urging young people in college, high school and even middle school to use their summer vacations to "contribute to (the country's) preservation by training to take up arms and prepare to fend off risk if this is required."
26In response, the Popular Mobilization Forces set up summer camps in predominantly Shiite neighborhoods from Baghdad to Basra.
27A spokesman for the group, Kareem al-Nouri, said the camps give "lessons in self-defense" and underage volunteers are expected to return to school by September, not go to the battle front.
28A spokesman for the Iraqi prime minister's office echoed that.
29There may be "some isolated incidents" of underage fighters joining combat on their own, Saad al-Harithi told the AP.
30"But there has been no instruction by the Marjaiyah (the top Shiite religious authority) or the Popular Mobilization Forces for children to join the battle."
31"We are a government that frowns upon children going to war," he said.
32But the line between combat training and actually joining combat is blurry, and it is weakly enforced by the Popular Mobilization Forces.
33Multiple militias operate under its umbrella, with fighters loyal to different leaders who often act independently.
34At the training camp in a middle-class Shiite neighborhood of western Baghdad earlier this month, the young cadets spoke openly of joining battle in front of their trainers, who did nothing to contradict them.
35Neighborhood youths spent their evenings in training every night during the holy month of Ramadan, which ended in mid-July, with mock exercises held every few days since then for those who wish to continue.
36The boys ran through the streets practicing urban warfare techniques, since the toughest battles with the Islamic State group are likely to involve street fighting.
37They were taught to hold, control and aim light weapons, though they didn't fire them.
38They also took part in public service activities like holding blood drives and collecting food and clothing.
39Earlier this summer, at one of the hottest front lines, near the IS-held city of Fallujah in western Anbar province, the AP spoke to a number of young boys, some heavily armed, among the Shiite militiamen.
40Baghdad natives Hussein Ali, 12, and his cousin Ali Ahsan, 14, said they joined their fathers on the battlefield after they finished their final exams.
41Carrying AK-47's, they paced around the Anbar desert, boasting of their resolve to liberate the predominantly Sunni province from IS militants.
42"It's our honor to serve our country," Hussein Ali said, adding that some of his schoolmates were also fighting.
43When asked if he was afraid, he smiled and said no.
44The fight they are engaged in has been brutal.
45IS atrocities are the most notorious and egregious, including mass killings of captured soldiers and civilians.
46But Shiite militias are said to have committed abuses as well.
47In February, Human Rights Watch accused individual Shiite militias under the Popular Mobilization Forces umbrella of "possible war crimes," including forcing Sunni civilians from their home and abducting and summarily executing them.
48In June, the United Nations Children's Fund called for "urgent measures" to be taken by the Iraqi government to protect children, including criminalizing the recruitment of children and "the association of children with the Popular Mobilization Forces."
49The U.S. State Department released its annual Trafficking in Persons report Monday in which it lists foreign governments identified over the past year as having armed forces or government-supported armed groups that recruit and use child soldiers.
50Those governments are subject to restrictions in the following fiscal year on certain security assistance and commercial licensing of military equipment.
51The report lists Syria, but not Iraq.
52Donatella Rovera, Amnesty International's senior crisis response adviser, said that if the Shiite militias are using children as fighters, "then the countries that are supporting them are in violation of the U.N. Convention" on the Rights of the Child.
53"If you are supporting the Iraqi army, then by extension, you are supporting the PMF," she said.
54Iraq has a long history of training underage fighters.
55Under Saddam Hussein, boys 12 through 17 known as "Saddam's lion cubs" would attend monthlong training during summer breaks with the goal of eventually merging them into the Fadayeen - a paramilitary force loyal to Saddam's Baathist regime.
56The Iraqi army restricts the age of its recruits to between 18 and 35, a policy that rights groups say is enforced.
57But there is no law governing the Popular Mobilization Forces.
58A draft law for the national guard, a force geared toward empowering Sunni tribes to police their own communities, purposely omits any age restrictions, lawmakers saying they want to open it to qualified fighters over age 35.
59The U.N. convention does not ban giving military training to minors.
60But Jo Becker, the advocacy director of the children's rights division at Human Rights Watch, said that it puts children at risk.
61"Governments like to say, 'Of course, we can recruit without putting children in harm's way,' but in a place of conflict, those landscapes blur very quickly," she said.
62Once in a combat situation, children are plunged into the horrors of war, she said.
63"They don't have a mature sense of right and wrong and they may commit atrocities more easily than adults."
