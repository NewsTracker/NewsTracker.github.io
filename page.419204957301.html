Greece hopes to wrap up bailout negotiations

1Greek ministers and representatives of European institutions and the International Monetary Fund resumed talks on financial assistance to Greece on Monday.
2Greece laid out the hope Monday that intensive talks with international creditors over another financial lifeline for the country could be concluded this week, earlier than expected.
3Greek ministers and representatives of European institutions and the International Monetary Fund resumed talks on Monday after a marathon Sunday session that ended in the predawn hours.
4The discussions, which started late-July, have by all accounts made progress, though officials from the European Union were less optimistic than their counterparts in Athens about the likelihood of a deal being concluded this week.
5Germany, for one, has stressed the importance of being thorough in the complicated negotiations.
6The Greek government needs the deal to be sorted out by Aug. 20, when the country has a debt repayment of a little more than 3 billion euros to make to the European Central Bank (ECB).
7Greece won't be able to make that payment without funds emerging from the country's third bailout in just over five years.
8Annika Breidthardt, European Commission spokeswoman on economics and financial affairs, said experts are "working day and night" to finalize the text of the formal agreement and an additional list of prior actions which the Greek authorities could legislate shortly.
9"Progress has indeed been made and we expect further progress throughout the day and beyond as talks continue to resolve the remaining issues," she told a press briefing.
10"A deal ... can be reached in the month of August, preferably before the 20th of August."
11Greece has relied on international bailouts worth a total $263 billion since it lost market confidence and was unable to borrow from anyone else in 2010.
12To secure funds from the bailouts, successive governments have had to implement a series of spending cuts, tax hikes and reforms.
13While the austerity has reduced budget overspending, the measures compounded a deep recession and fueled record high unemployment.
14Though the radical left-led government was elected on a staunchly anti-austerity platform in January, it has been forced into a policy U-turn after bailout talks came close to collapse last month.
15While Greece's parliament ratified further tax hikes and reforms, a rebellion by hardline left-wingers has left Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras' Syriza party with only a nominal parliamentary majority and dependent on opposition backing to pass key creditor-demanded legislation.
16That has stoked talk that Tsipras will call early elections soon after the bailout deal is signed.
17Tsipras still retains strong personal support in opinion polls, which show Syriza heading for a potentially big victory.
18A Greek government official said the hope is that a deal that "covers everything" will be concluded as soon as possible and indicated that one could be imminent by Friday, when he said a meeting of the Eurozone's 19 finance ministers could take place.
19The official, who was not authorized to speak on the record, said any talk of elections must follow the agreement, with whatever provisions it may contain on lightening the country's crushing debt load.
20No Eurogroup meeting has been officially scheduled, and it remains unclear whether the ministers would hold a conference call or meet in Brussels should one take place.
21Greek banks could get an initial capital injection soon after a bailout deal is clinched, as much as 10 billion euros, even before the ECB completes a stress test, a eurozone official familiar with the issue said on Monday.
22In Berlin, German government spokesman Steffen Seibert was cautious on the timing of the bailout deal.
23"A swift conclusion of the negotiations would of course be desirable," he told reporters.
24A senior lawmaker with Chancellor Angela Merkel's Christian Democrats raised the possibility of an interim agreement - strongly opposed by Athens - that would tide Greece over the ECB payment, while leaving time for more thorough negotiations on the overall deal.
25"If there is no sustainable solution by Aug. 20 but we're on the right track in the negotiations, then there is still the possibility of further bridge financing," Ralph Brinkhaus told Deutschlandfunk radio.
26"That is always better than rushing into a bad agreement."
