Apple Watch stylish but not for everyone, reviewers say

1"After using it, I had no question that the Apple Watch is the most advanced piece of wearable technology you can buy today," Bloomberg's Josh Topolsky wrote.
2"The Apple Watch is cool, it's beautiful, it's powerful and it's easy to use. But it's not essential. Not yet."
3One issue is that it's relatively expensive: The Apple Watch, essentially a companion to the iPhone, is priced starting at $449 for the Watch Sport model up to breathtaking $22,000 for the 18-karat gold version.
4The devices are slated to start shipping April 24, with preorders starting April 10.
5Apple has lined up several partners -- including ESPN, CNN, Facebook, Twitter, MLB.com, the New York Times and Fandango -- whose apps provide text-based alerts to wearers of the Apple Watch.
6But, according to New York Times tech columnist Farhad Manjoo, while he generally liked the Apple Watch after a "steep" learning curve, the third-party apps available for the device are "mostly useless right now."
7"The Uber app didn't load for me, the Twitter app is confusing and the app for Starwood hotels mysteriously deleted itself and then hung up on loading when I reinstalled it," he wrote.
8As Manjoo concluded: "The first Apple Watch may not be for you -- but someday soon, it will change your world."
9The Wall Street Journal's Geoffrey Fowler found the Apple Watch handy for tasks like dictating a text message and using Apple Pay at participating retailers.
10But, he said, "the Apple Watch isn't quite the gatekeeper to my digital life that I wanted. Take app alerts -- there's a fine line between being in the know and having your wrist jiggle all day."
11Setting up alerts for the smartwatch is a "tedious -- and unfortunately ongoing -- chore," he added.
12USA Today's Ed Baig, who said he's eager to buy an Apple Watch, called it an "elegant combination of style and purpose."
13Even so, he acknowledged drawbacks, including that it requires an iPhone 5 or later and is initially confusing to operate.
14"As with most first-time products, it isn't flawless or essential," Baig wrote.
15"But among smartwatches, Apple Watch is second to none. I want one."
16Reviewers found Apple Watch's estimated 18-hour battery life lived up to the promises.
17Still, that means users need to regularly recharge it, unlike a conventional wristwatch.
18Fowler said the battery was often nearly drained by the end of the day, especially if used during exercise, while CNET's Scott Stein said recharge time was slow.
19Re/Code's Lauren Goode said that while the watch's battery life was not nearly as long-lasting as some other wearable devices, it was better than she had expected.
20The fact that the Apple Watch relies on an iPhone being in the close proximity for most of its functions to work was also seen by some reviewers as a drawback.
21Paired with an iPhone, the watch allows users to check e-mail, listen to music and make phone calls.
22Apps will also allow users to track their health - for instance by tracking heartbeats and the number of steps a person takes.
23"...If you can tolerate single-day battery life, half-baked apps and inevitable obsolescence, you can now wear the future on your wrist," Fowler said.
24"For now, the Apple Watch is for pioneers," Fowler concluded.
25"I won't pay the (US)$1,000 it would cost for the model I tested, only to see a significant improvement roll in before too long."
26But CNET's Stein said Apple wants people to see the Apple Watch as "fine jewelry."
27"Maybe that's a stretch, but in terms of craftsmanship, there isn't a more elegantly made piece of wearable tech."
28Ultimately, it looks like the Apple Watch -- to be fair, like other smartwatches on the market -- is a cool-to-have gizmo, not a must-have breakthrough.
29And for Apple, that indicates that it's not going to be The Next Big Thing the company is hoping for on the order of its category-busting iPhone and iPad successes.
