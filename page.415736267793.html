With cockpit voice tape recovered, officials hope to unravel Germanwings crash mystery

1The development came as French President Francois Hollande, Germany's Angela Merkel and Spain's Mariano Rajoy traveled to the crash site in a remote French Alpine region to pay tribute to the 150 victims, mostly Germany and Spanish.
2However, while Hollande promised that authorities would not rest until the causes of the crash were known, France's BEA air incident investigation bureau said it was still far too early to draw meaningful conclusions on why the plane, operated by the Germanwings budget arm of Lufthansa, went down.
3"We have just been able to extract a useable audio data file," BEA director Remi Jouty told a news conference at its headquarters outside Paris.
4"We have not yet been able to study and to establish an exact timing for all the sounds and words heard on this file," he said.
5He expected initial analysis in "a matter of days" but that rough read-out could be subject to errors and that more work would be needed for a full interpretation.
6Although he said "words" had been heard on the tape, Jouty would not confirm whether that meant the Airbus A320's pilots were conscious and he gave no details of the recordings.
7Interior Minister Bernard Cazeneuve earlier said a terror attack was not the main hypothesis for the crash.
8Jouty said, however, that no theory could be excluded at this point.
9While stressing it was too early to form a clear picture, he ruled out a mid-air explosion having taken place and said the crash scenario did not appear to be linked to depressurisation.
10Jouty said the second black box had not yet been found but could not confirm Hollande's remark that its casing had.
11Among the new details, he said the airliner had flown in a straight line directly into the mountain - but would not say whether that suggested at the hand of a pilot or auto-pilot.
12Germanwings said 72 Germans were killed in Tuesday's crash, the first major air passenger disaster on French soil since the 2000 Concorde accident just outside Paris.
13Spanish officials said 51 Spaniards were among the victims.
14As well as Germans and Spaniards, victims included three Americans, a Moroccan and citizens of Britain, Argentina, Australia, Belgium, Colombia, Denmark, Israel, Japan, Mexico, Iran and the Netherlands, officials said.
15However DNA checks to identify them could take weeks, the French government said.
16Lufthansa CEO Carsten Spohr described the crash as incomprehensible and said Lufthansa has never lost a plane during the cruise stage of flight.
17"This represents the darkest hours in the 60 year history of our Lufthansa group. We are still in a state of shock," he said.
18Hollande, Merkel and Rajoy thanked search teams and met residents in the village of Seyne-les-Alpes, where the salvaging operation has been set up.
19"Dear Angela, dear Mariano, rest assured ... we will find out everything," Hollande told German Chancellor Angela Merkel and Spanish Prime Minister Mariano Rajoy, pledging to get to the bottom of what caused the crash.
20"France stands by you."
21Merkel replied: "It feels good that in a difficult hour like this that we're standing so closely together in friendship. Dear Francois, I'd like to say to you a heartfelt 'thank you' in the name of millions of Germans who are deeply appreciative of this German-Franco friendship."
22A tribute ceremony took place on a site with a view in the distance of the mountain against which the Airbus crashed.
23Earlier, Lufthansa said it could not explain why the Airbus run by its low-cost Germanwings unit crashed.
24"It is inexplicable this could happen to a plane free of technical problems and with an experienced, Lufthansa-trained pilot," its CEO Carsten Spohr told reporters in Frankfurt.
25Lufthansa said the 24-year-old plane had just on Monday had repairs to the hatch through which the nose wheel descends for landing.
26A spokeswoman said that was not a safety issue but that repairs had been done to reduce noise.
27Police and forensic teams on foot and in helicopters investigated the site about 100 km (65 miles) north of Nice where the airliner slammed into mountains in what aviation officials was a sharp descent but not freefall.
28"When we go to a crash site we expect to find part of the fuselage. But here we see nothing at all," pilot Xavier Roy, coordinating air operations, said of the confetti of debris.
29Roy said teams of investigators had been dropped by helicopter onto the site and were working roped together at altitudes of around 2,000 meters (6,000 feet).
30Germanwings said it canceled one flight on Wednesday and was using 11 planes from other carriers for about 40 flights after some of its crew members had refused to fly.
31Employees laid candles and flowers by Germanwings headquarters at Cologne/Bonn airport, while Lufthansa and Germanwings staff worldwide held a moment of silence at 10:53 a.m. local (0953 GMT) - the moment the plane went missing.
32Among the victims were 16 teenagers and two teachers from the Joseph-Koenig-Gymnasium high school in the town of Haltern am See in northwest Germany.
33They were on their way home after a Spanish exchange visit near Barcelona.
34"On Tuesday last week we sent off 16 happy young people, with two happy young colleagues, on a journey and what was meant to be a happy trip ... has ended in tragedy," headmaster Ulrich Wessel told reporters.
35Barcelona's Liceu opera house said two singers, Kazakhstan-born Oleg Bryjak and German Maria Radner, died while returning to Duesseldorf after performing in Wagner's "Siegfried".
36Germanwings said on Tuesday the plane started descending a minute after reaching cruising height and lost altitude for eight minutes.
37Experts said that while the Airbus had descended rapidly, it did not seem to have simply fallen out of the sky.
38The A320 is one of the world's most used passenger jets and has a good safety record.
39At 24-years-old, the plane was older than many others at Lufthansa, where the average for its fleet is 11-and-a-half years.
40It was delivered to Lufthansa in 1991 and had clocked up around 58,000 flight hours over 46,000 flights, Airbus said.
