Pasco, Washington Police Shooting Sparks Mexican Anger

Fighting rages in run-up to Ukraine ceasefire

1In this still frame taken from a cell phone video provided by Dario Infante and taken on Tuesday, Antonio Zambrano-Montes, left, turns to face police officers while running from them in Pasco, Wash.
2Moments later, Zambrano-Montes was shot and killed.
3Pasco police said he threw multiple rocks, hitting two officers, and refused to put down other stones.
4PASCO, Wash.
5(AP) - Officers killed a man accused of hurling rocks in the fourth fatal police shooting since last summer, a death that is shaking this agricultural city of 68,000 in southeastern Washington and drawing criticism from as far away as Mexico.
6The killing Tuesday of orchard worker Antonio Zambrano-Montes sparked protests after witnesses said he was running away when he was shot in a busy intersection in Pasco, a city about 215 miles southeast of Seattle.
7Mexico's Foreign Relations Department on Thursday condemned the shooting of the man raised in that country.
8Washington Gov. Jay Inslee said he was monitoring the situation.
9Franklin County Coroner Dan Blasdel, whose office was conducting an autopsy on Zambrano-Montes, said he was considering convening an inquest jury to look into the death.
10"We don't want another Ferguson here in Pasco," Blasdel told The Seattle Times, referring to the unrest that followed the Aug. 9 killing of an unarmed black man in Ferguson, Missouri, and a grand jury's decision not to indict the white officer who shot him.
11But the coroner says he won't decide until the investigation wraps up.
12"This was really not a racial issue," Pasco Police Chief Bob Metzger told KING-TV of Seattle.
13The chief met for two hours with Zambrano-Montes' relatives.
14"Three police officers against one man throwing a rock?" an aunt, Angela Zambrano, said to The Times.
15"This was murder in cold blood."
16Police say Zambrano-Montes' threatening behavior led officers to open fire.
17The 35-year-old threw multiple rocks, hitting two officers, and refused to put down other stones.
18They say a stun gun failed to subdue him.
19He had a run-in with Pasco police early last year, having been arrested for assault after throwing objects at officers and trying to grab an officer's pistol, court records show.
20The shooting launched protests, and demonstrators say they will gather again Saturday.
21Meanwhile, a handful of people showed up at Pasco City Hall on Thursday to support police.
22"It's important for these officers to know the entire community is not out to get them," Chris Black, an Army veteran, told the Tri-City Herald.
23The police chief has appealed for patience during an investigation and an internal review.
24"The officers are ... on administrative leave - until they are reviewed and everything is done, they will not be back to work," he said.
25"It's important we get the right information."
26Some people who saw the shooting videotaped the confrontation.
27In one recording, five "pops" are audible shortly after the video begins, and the man can be seen running away, across a street and down a sidewalk, pursued by three officers.
28As the officers draw closer to the running man, he stops, turns around and faces them.
29Multiple "pops" are heard and the man falls to the ground.
30In three previous fatal police shootings in Pasco, prosecutors cleared officers with the Pasco Police Department and a sheriff's deputy who was working on a regional SWAT team.
31Zambrano-Montes was raised in Michoacan, Mexico, and has lived for about a decade in Pasco, where more than half the residents are Hispanic.
32In its statement, Mexico's Foreign Relations Department called the shooting one of the "events in which unwarranted use has been made of lethal force."
33The department said it was helping Zambrano-Montes' family "with the aim of ensuring that all available legal avenues are explored and taken to their fullest extent."
34Family members told the Tri-City Herald that Zambrano-Montes battled depression after being separated from his two teen daughters.
35"He was a kind person, family-oriented," his cousin, Blanca Zambrano, told the newspaper.
36"He was hard-working."

20NATO and the United States said the fighting ran counter to the spirit, if not the letter of the agreement and U.S. officials said further sanctions were still on the table.

30The deal calls for the withdrawal of heavy weapons from the front line when and if the ceasefire has taken hold, and constitutional reform to give eastern Ukraine more autonomy.
31The rebels have advanced far past an earlier ceasefire deal, agreed in September, and the new agreement appears to envisage them pulling their guns back around 75 km, to take them back behind it, while Ukrainian guns would move 25 km back.
32This would leave a buffer zone 50 km wide, a challenge for the monitors from the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe who are expected to patrol it.
33It also appears to take more territory outside Kiev's control.
34The White House, under pressure from Congress to provide arms to the stretched Ukrainian military, said the deal was "potentially significant" but urged Russia to withdraw soldiers and equipment, and give Ukraine back control over its border.
