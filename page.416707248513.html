Baltimore Unrest Puts Spotlight on O'Malley's Stint as Mayor

1Martin O'Malley often casts Baltimore as the comeback city that overcame the ravages of drugs and violence when he was mayor.
2Now, weeks before the former Maryland governor expects to enter the 2016 presidential race and challenge Hillary Rodham Clinton in the Democratic primaries, Baltimore's turnaround has been marred by the unrest after the police-custody death of Freddie Gray.
3The turmoil has placed new scrutiny on O'Malley's "zero tolerance" law enforcement policies as mayor from 1999 to 2006.
4The record shows that murders and violent crime overall declined in O'Malley's years as mayor.
5But it was when a grand jury concluded that too many arrests were being made in black neighborhoods without merit, and when the city settled a lawsuit from people who said they were wrongly arrested for minor offenses - the sort of concerns driving some of the anger in Baltimore today.
6David Rocah, a staff attorney with the ACLU of Maryland, said the O'Malley administration left a legacy of "hyperaggressive and militarized policing" that, in his view, contributed to the outrage behind the riots.
7"I think the idea that you can arrest your way to public safety has always been deeply misguided and counterproductive," Rocah said.
8But O'Malley says those judging him in hindsight should remember the crime and despair of the Baltimore he inherited as mayor.
9"I don't think that any of us want to go back to the days of 1999," O'Malley said.
10"Our city is undoubtedly a safer place, and our city is becoming a better place, but our city still has a lot of progress to make."
11He spoke outside the Dawson Safe Haven Center, an after-school refuge for children that was once a home for a family of seven killed in a 2002 firebombing by a drug dealer.
12O'Malley called that episode "our Alamo."
13O'Malley has tried to build a following in Iowa and New Hampshire as an alternative to Clinton, the dominant front-runner.
14O'Malley has backed tougher regulations on Wall Street, opposed the Trans-Pacific Partnership trade deal and addressed student debt - issues that resonate with liberals.
15Still relatively unknown, even among Democrats, O'Malley frequently points to his time as mayor as a key part of his biography.
16A 2013 video by his team, shown at a New Hampshire Democratic dinner where he appeared, described Baltimore in the late 1990s as a "cauldron of crime, drugs and profound despair" and credited O'Malley with "an assault on hopelessness. He didn't make a campaign promise to make the city safer, he made a pledge. And he kept it."
17In the 1990s, more than 300 people were murdered each year in Baltimore.
18O'Malley advocated "stop and frisk" practices, cracked down on lower-level crimes such as public drunkenness and disorderly conduct, and brought in two police commanders from New York steeped in such policing.
19The number of homicides fell to 253 in 2002 and stayed below 300 during his two terms, while never dropping to his goal of 175.
20But the approach did lead to many arrests.
21In 2005, a Baltimore grand jury found excessive arrests in black neighborhoods and recommended retraining so officers would use better judgment.
22Judge Joseph McCurdy Jr. had tasked the panel with determining "what can be done to address the lack of confidence that exists between many members of the public and law enforcement."
