No precedent for Netanyahu's contentious speech to Congress

D.W. Griffith?s The Birth of a Nation 100 Years Later: Still Great, Still Shameful

1In a speech that stirred political intrigue in two countries, Netanyahu told Congress that negotiations underway between Iran and the U.S. would "all but guarantee" that Tehran will get nuclear weapons, a step that the world must avoid at all costs.
2WASHINGTON (AP) - Given anywhere else, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's speech Tuesday wouldn't have caused such a ruckus.
3But a foreign leader denouncing President Barack Obama's policy from within the grand hall of American democracy upended nearly two centuries of tradition.
4A joint meeting of Congress, gathering senators and representatives together in the House chamber, is a ceremony typically bestowed on one or two friendly foreign leaders per year.
5It looks a lot like a presidential State of the Union address.
6The speaker embodies his or her nation; the audience of lawmakers represents all Americans.
7Unity and shared purpose are the standard themes.
8Standing ovations are a given.
9"It establishes there is a strong bond between the two nations," said Kathleen Hall Jamieson, director of the University of Pennsylvania's Annenberg Public Policy Center.
10"In that context, a speech that provokes controversy at home or abroad is problematic."
11So leaders speaking against the backdrop of the chamber's 8-foot-tall American flag tend to gloss over national differences and steer clear of internal politics.
12Some of these speeches are powerful - think Winston Churchill rallying Americans for a long, hard war or Nelson Mandela calling them to lead all humanity toward democracy and peace.
13Others, frankly, are so bland they're dull.
14It's not unusual for staffers and student pages to be sent in to fill the seats of lawmakers who didn't bother showing up.
15Netanyahu's appearance, in contrast, was so contentious that more than four dozen Democratic lawmakers announced they were staying away in protest, despite the nation's close ties to Israel.
16Still, demand for seats in the chamber and visitors' gallery was fierce, and Netanyahu was welcomed with enthusiastic ovations and cheers, especially from Republicans.
17Netanyahu said he was morally obliged to speak out in hopes of stopping what he called the "bad deal" the U.S. is in the midst of negotiating with Iran over its nuclear program.
18Before the address, the White House complained that House Speaker John Boehner, R-Ohio, had violated protocol by inviting Netanyahu without first consulting the White House.
19Boehner responded that Americans needed to hear Netanyahu's fears that Iran will get nuclear weapons.
20History offers scant precedent for Netanyahu's contentious speech.
21The tradition of inviting foreign dignitaries dates to the Marquis de Lafayette, the French hero of the American Revolution, who gave separate speeches to the House and Senate in 1824.
22It took another 50 years for the two bodies to come together to hear a foreigner - King Kalakaua, of what was then the Kingdom of Hawaii.
23Since World War II, these joint meetings - similar to the "joint sessions" that hear U.S. presidents - have become the favored venue for foreign leaders and dignitaries.
24Usually their appearances get more notice back home than among Americans, which made Netanyahu's address before a cheering Congress just two weeks before he's up for re-election in Israel even more controversial.
25Israel is one of a few allies that dominate the rostrum.
26With Netanyahu's return, it ties Britain and France at eight speeches apiece.
27In all, 110 foreigners and a handful of Americans - mostly early-Space Age astronauts - have addressed joint meetings.
28As Australian prime minister in 2011, Julia Gillard nailed the mutual-admiration genre.
29Her voice breaking, Gillard recalled being a small girl amazed that Americans had landed on the moon.
30"On that great day I believed Americans could do anything," she said.
31"I believe that still."
32The closest comparison to Netanyahu's latest speech to Congress may be Netanyahu's last speech to Congress.
1But David Wark Griffith must have realized that his film deserved a grander title.
2For its New York premiere on March 3, 1915, exactly a hundred years ago, he renamed it The Birth of a Nation.
3Released on the 50th anniversary of the last full month of the Civil War, Griffith's monument became a groundbreaking popular, technical and critical success.
4Produced for $100,000 and charging a top price of $2 (when tickets to most movies cost a dime), Birth was the seminal blockbuster of the silent-film period and the most widely seen of all motion pictures until it was eclipsed by another Civil War epic, Gone With the Wind, in 1939.
5Griffith's film is estimated to have earned $18 million in its first few years - the astounding equivalent of $1.8 billion today.
6In current dollars, only Avatar and Titanic have earned more worldwide.
7In its bold editing and composition of shots, in its contrast of intimate scenes with spectacular battles and a final thrilling chase, The Birth of a Nation was the culmination of six years of pioneering artistry by Griffith, the would-be novelist who at first thought he was slumming when he began working in the movies in 1908 but who established in the hundreds of one- and two-reelers he directed a cinematic textbook, a fully formed visual language, for the generations that followed.
8More than anyone else - more than all others combined - he invented the film art.
9He brought it to fruition in The Birth of a Nation, an enormous risk that he embarked on without a real script and using just one camera manned by his invaluable cinematographer, G.W. "Billy" Bitzer.
10Before he walked on the set, motion pictures had been, in actuality, static.
11At a respectful distance, the camera snapped a series of whole scenes, clustered in the groupings of the stage play.
12Griffith broke up the pose.
13He rammed his camera into the middle of the action.
14He took closeups, crosscuts, angle shots and dissolves.
15His camera was alive, picking off shots; then he built the shots into sequences, the sequences into tense, swift narrative.
16For the first time the movies had a man who realized that, while a theater audience listened, a movie audience watched.
17"Above all & I am trying to make you see," Griffith said.
18In The Birth of a Nation, Griffith made audiences see the Civil War through his eyes - the eyes of the son of a colonel in the Army of the Confederacy.
19The potent drama of the movie's subject and method stirred President Woodrow Wilson to say, "It is like writing history with lightning; my only regret is that it is all so terribly true."
20Or truly terrible.
21The most ambitious and powerful film of its time was also the most controversial, indeed notorious.
22The rhetorical fire it kindled makes recent arguments over the validity of such Oscar-nominated films as Selma and American Sniper seem like the most decorous debates in the Red Hat Society - for The Birth of a Nation not only was about the country's history, it changed it, unarguably for the worse.
23Like many popular films of the next 40 years, Birth took the side of the South in its depiction of the Civil War.
24It saw the antebellum South as a paradise of Anglo gentility and the Reconstruction Era as the crushing of that dream.
25At heart a Romeo and Juliet story extended to gargantuan proportions, the movie focuses on two families, the Northern Stonemans and the Southern Camerons, whose eldest sons fall in love with girls from the other family.
26Though the Civil War places the young men on opposing sides, they retain respect for their old friends - Ben Stoneman (Henry B. Walthall) stops mid-battle to comfort a wounded Cameron - and love for their ladies.
27So far, so predictable.
28The Birth of a Nation occupies a view of the South not far from Scarlett O'Hara's in Gone With the Wind, and modern audiences have to wrestle with that beloved movie's romanticizing of racism.
29But Griffith's film went further, lower.
30Taking its cue from Dixon, whom film historian Russell Merritt aptly describes as a "professional Southerner and white supremacist," Birth revels in the coarsest racial imagery: of crude Negroes (most of them played by white actors in blackface) who act like savages both in the Reconstruction Senate, as they deprive the white gentry of their rights, and in their sexual brutality toward Southern white women.
31It is romantic chivalry, Griffith insists, that led to Southerners' retaliations against Negroes.
32A rapacious black man stalks a young white woman until, to protect her virginity, she leaps off a cliff to her death.
33To avenge such indignities and defend the honor of white womanhood, Ben Stoneman and his noble fellows give birth to the Ku Klux Klan (who, in the film's climax, gallop to the rescue to the music of Wagner's "Ride of the Valkyries").
34That racist realm, not the restored United States, is the true Nation of the film's title: the land of lynchings, voter suppression and second-class citizenship for Southern blacks.
35The seductive artistry of Griffith's masterwork made his virulent, derisive depiction of blacks all the more toxic - one could say epidemic.
36This was not simply a racist film; it was one whose brilliant storytelling technique lent plausibility and poignancy to the notion of blacks as stupid, venal and brutal.
37Viewers could believe that what they saw was true historically and emotionally.
38Birth not only taught moviegoers how to react to film narrative but what to think about blacks and, in the climactic ride of hooded horsemen to avenge their honor, what to do to them.
39The movie provoked protests and riots in Northern cities with large black minorities.
40And by stirring bitter memories in the white South, it helped revive the dormant Ku Klux Klan, which for the next few decades went on a righteous spree of killing black men.
41In a 1930 conversation with actor Walter Huston (then starring in Griffith's first talking picture, Abraham Lincoln) for the re-release of The Birth of a Nation, the director argued that the Ku Klux Klan, riding like the cavalry to the rescue of the South from rapacious Negroes, "at that time was needed to serve the purpose."
42However myopic that sounds today, Griffith wasn't alone in his sentiments.
43He had Huston read a passage by Woodrow Wilson, positing that the purpose of Reconstruction was "to put the white South under the heel of the black South," under black officeholders "who knew none of the uses of authority except its insolences & The white men were roused by an instinct of mere self-preservation, until at last there had sprung into existence a great Ku Klux Klan, a veritable empire of the South to protect the Southern country."
44Bilge, all of it, and sadder still that it comes from a Southerner who was a noted historian and the head of Princeton University before becoming President.
45But it shows that a racist view of American history was near the norm in "civilized" society a hundred years ago, and for decades thereafter.
46Yet The Birth of a Nation is nearly as antiwar as it is antiblack.
47The Civil War scenes, which consume only 30 minutes of the extravaganza, emphasize not the national glory but the human cost of combat.
48"On the battlefield," announces one of the film's intertitles, "War claims its bitter, useless sacrifice."
49For all the spectacular panoramas of the battle footage, its explosions and ragged processions of soldiers, the most impressive and startling moments are the more intimate views of the battle's end.
50"War's peace," reads another intertitle, and we are shown a tableau of a half-dozen dead soldiers, as if taking a restorative rest after their fatal labor.
51These images have the impact of defiant art: Goya's Disasters of War or Picasso's Guernica.
52Griffith may have been a racist politically, but his refusal to find uplift in the South's war against the Union - and, implicitly, in any war at all - reveals him as a cinematic humanist.
53Stung by attacks on Birth, Griffith made an even more ambitious film, Intolerance.
54Cutting among four stories in four periods of world history, from Babylonian times to the present, Intolerance made a plea for universal brotherhood (not specifically including Americans of color).
55In 1919 he directed Broken Blossoms, an early interracial love story (but involving a man who was Chinese, not black).
56But he never could erase the stain that Birth left on the body politic.
57By the time talking pictures replaced the silents in 1930, Griffith the innovator was Griffith the anachronism.
58"Charlie Chaplin said, 'The whole industry owes its existence to him.' Yet of late years he could not find a job in the town he had invented. He clung to the shadows, a bald, eaglebeaked man, sardonic and alone. At parties, he sat drinking quietly, his sharp eyes panning the room for a glimpse of familiar faces, most of them long gone. David Wark Griffith had been The Master, and there was nobody quite like him afterwards."
