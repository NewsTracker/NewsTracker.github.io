Nepal earthquake moves Kathmandu but Everest height unchanged ? experts

1The earthquake that devastated Nepal and left thousands of people dead shifted the earth beneath Kathmandu by up to several metres south, but the height of Mount Everest likely stayed the same, experts said Tuesday.
2The massive 7.8-magnitude quake on Saturday was the Himalayan nation's deadliest disaster in more than 80 years, killing more than 4,300 people and causing massive destruction.
3According to seismological data, the ground beneath the capital Kathmandu may have moved about three metres (10 feet) southward, said University of Cambridge tectonics expert James Jackson.
4His analysis was similar to that of Sandy Steacy, head of physical sciences at the University of Adelaide.
5"It's likely that the earthquake occurred on the Himalayan Thrust fault, a plate boundary that separates the northern moving Indian sub-continent from Eurasia," Steacy said.
6"The fault dips about 10 degrees to the north-northeast. The relative movement across the fault zone was on the order of three metres at its greatest, just north of Kathmandu."
7The fault lies between two tectonic plates - one bearing India pushing northward into a plate carrying Europe and Asia at a rate of about two centimetres (0.8 inches) each year - the process that created the Himalayas.
8Mark Allen, from the department of earth sciences at the University of Durham in the UK, said that the plate on top of the fault moved southwards over the plate underneath it, causing an overall shortening of the Earth's crust in the region.
9"It would be simplistic to say that Kathmandu was relocated by three metres," he said.
10"There may have been three metres of slip on the fault at the earthquake nucleation point at 15 kilometres depth. But this dies out in all directions, including upwards to the surface."
11While Kathmandu may have moved, it is unlikely that the height of Mount Everest was affected as it is not directly above the faultline.
12"The main slip was west of Everest, the mountain was not directly above the fault plane," said Steacy.
13"In addition, the dip of the fault is very shallow so three metres in a horizontal direction doesn't mean much vertically."
14Ian Main, a professor of seismology and rock physics at the University of Edinburgh, said there may have been a small change in height but it was too soon to tell.
15"We will know soon if any detectable height change has occurred, when those working on satellite-based 'synthetic aperture radar' look at the difference in snapshots of the ground surface position before and after the event," he said.
16Everest stands at 8,848 metres (29,029 feet) above sea level.
17Saturday's earthquake triggering an avalanche on the mountain that killed at least 18 people at base camp.
18Experts warned that more aftershocks were likely, possibly for months to come.
19"The slip on the April 25th earthquake will have increased the stress on other segments of the same fault, or of adjacent faults, such that they are more likely to rupture themselves," Allen said.
20"There may be earthquakes for months in the region, which likely relate to the main event."
