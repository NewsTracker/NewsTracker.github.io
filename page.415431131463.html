Tucson begins evicting protesters from downtown homeless encampment

Ukraine's grinding war stains innocence of childhood

1- A large group of homeless campers is leaving a downtown Tucson park after the city issued removal notices.
2The protesters have until Friday evening to remove the nearly 40 wooden sleeping pods and several tents set up around downtown.
3Many have already begun leaving, and police are monitoring.
4The city issued notices Tuesday after a federal judge told city attorneys that an order he issued previously does not stop them from removing the pods and tents.
5The order stops the city from enforcing a rule that allows the homeless to have only three small belongings on city sidewalks.
6The campers say they are protesting unjust city ordinances that target the homeless.
7Business owners and city leaders say the camp unfairly takes space from pedestrians and visitors.
8They also say the camp risks public health.

17KHARTSYZK, Ukraine (AP) - Seryozha colors in his drawing of a tank, lost in thought.
18Like many 7-year-olds in eastern Ukraine, he has trouble recalling a time before the war.
19Yelena Nikulenko, the director of the children's home in the rebel-held town of Khartsyzk, says kids like Seryozha have been let down twice.
20First orphaned or abandoned by their parents, they were then dumped by their new families when the Ukrainian government stopped paying benefits to foster families in separatist-controlled areas.
21"On top of that, you have the war, the shelling, the fear," Nikulenko says.
22"It will be a scar for the rest of their lives, that's for sure."
23The conflict that erupted in Ukraine last year between government troops and Russian-backed separatists has claimed at least 6,000 lives and displaced nearly 1.8 million people.
24The United Nations Children's Fund estimates that 1.7 million children on both sides of the front line have been harmed through lack of proper shelter, nutrition, medicine or schooling.
25Children struggle to understand what is going on around them, and why.
26Fighting has abated drastically since a new cease-fire came into effect last month, but the suffering, loneliness and terror remain.
27In the government-held town of Popasna, 70 kilometers (45 miles) north of the children's home, only a few people walk along deserted streets between apartments gutted by rocket fire, a grim contrast to the days when the town bustled with 30,000 people.
28One of those destroyed homes belongs to Tatyana Belash, who has now taken shelter in a basement with her 3-year old daughter, Zlata.
29As the adults talk politics, Zlata, a shy girl with blonde pigtails, darts around the basement cluttered with battered mattresses.
30Asked about the shelling, Zlata shies away and seeks comfort in stroking her cat.
31"When we first came here, she kept saying: 'Let's go home!'" Belash says.
32"I couldn't explain to her that we couldn't go home because there was fighting going on."
33Children in the Ukraine-controlled village of Chermalyk play war and scuttle in and out of the craters made by falling Grad rockets.
34As 11-year old Tolik Tokar shimmies into one crater, his head disappears from view.
35Then he raises his head and pretends to shoot at the baddies: the separatists.
36The boys take aim with make-believe guns fashioned from sticks - but Tokar has seen shooting first-hand, not just in play.
37"When shelling was raging, we went to check it out, and then they opened fire on me," Tokar says, stammering as he tells his tale.
38"The bullets tore through the cloth on my shoulder here and flew past."
39A few dozen kilometers away on the rebel side, children play the same games - but with roles reversed.
40There, Ukrainian soldiers are the bogeymen, with "Nazi" one of the favorite slurs.
41The 22 children under Nikulenko's tutelage, in Khartsyzk, are some of the most vulnerable anywhere in the war-wracked region, and she asks that they be identified only by their first names.
42Before fighting began, the home served as a shelter for children rescued from the streets, or seeking respite from dysfunctional families.
43More have been abandoned in recent months.
44Veronika, a freckled and gap-toothed 6-year-old, smiles and pulls down on her red-checked dress as she recalls life before the war - the visits to the amusement park and zoo, her mother's home cooking.
45She even has fond memories of her father, who returned home after a stint in prison for slashing her mother's shoulder with a knife.
46Since then, her father enlisted with the rebel army and her mother left her at the children's home.
47Veronika relies on her own inner strength to ward off the terror brought on by war.
48"When they were doing boom-boom, it was so scary," she says, recalling a recent bout of shelling.
49"Once when they were shooting at night, I fell off the bed."
50Veronika's mother visits Khartsyzk from time to time.
51The young girl hopes to go home when summer comes.
52Nikulenko says children in her care have come under the spell of Russian television.
53They love watching programs that cast the rebels as valiant heroes of a popular uprising.
54Ukrainian government troops are treated as vicious occupiers.
55"It's very dangerous, this black-and-white perception," Nikulenko says.
56"These children get information only from one side. They see that (government troops) shoot at us and that their fathers and brothers take arms and go to protect us."
57As a group of girls huddles on a carpet in the games room, Yulya, a tall and unsmiling 12-year old, stands to one side.
58Before the war began, Yulya lived with her grandparents in Rusko-Orlivka, a village that changed hands several times as fighting raged last summer for the nearby town of Ilovaysk.
59Yulya's grandfather told her that when the rebel fighters captured Rusko-Orlivka, fighters found nine Ukrainian soldiers hiding out in a farm, marched them out to a forest and shot them.
60Yulya says she felt little pity.
61"I understand they are people too, but they kill other people," she says in a whisper.
62"I know that, because my grandfather told me so."
63For the very young, little is truly understood about who is fighting or why.
64Drawing his tank in the children's home, Seryozha, in a moment of confusion, gets it into his head to decorate it with a blue-and-yellow Ukrainian flag.
65"It was born in Ukraine. I was born in Ukraine, too," he says.
66Above the Ukrainian flag that sticks out from the side, he then draws the black, blue and red flag of the separatists.
67Seryozha's own past is a blur even to those around him.
68A scar on his back shows where he was shot with an air-powered pistol before the war.
69Nobody quite knows what happened, beyond that his parents died of tuberculosis, leaving his sister and two brothers orphaned.
70A bureaucratic oversight separated Seryozha from his siblings.
71As they were evacuated by Ukrainian authorities to a neighboring region, nobody remembered that Seryozha was lying in a hospital recovering from tuberculosis.
72He landed in the Khartsyzk home in January.
73Children may have a hazy understanding of the events around them, but forgiveness appears to come more easily than to the adults.
74Could Seryozha ever be friends with children from the other side?
75"Yes," he says simply.
76"But only if they behave and don't fight."
