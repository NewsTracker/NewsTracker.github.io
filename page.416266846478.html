Google fights EU abuse case

1GOOGLE has hit back after the European Commission accused the company of abusing its dominance of the internet search market.
2The US giant has been served with a charge sheet alleging it broke EU antitrust rules by "systematically favouring" its own comparison shopping product in its general search results pages, stifling competition and harming consumers.
3The Commission has also opened a separate probe into Google's Android mobile operating system, which will determine whether it has entered into anti-competitive agreements or abused its dominant position in services for smartphones.
4EU Commissioner in charge of competition policy Margrethe Vestager, right, said: "I am concerned that the company has given an unfair advantage to its own comparison shopping service.
5"Google now has the opportunity to convince the Commission to the contrary.
6On the Android probe, she added: "Smartphones, tablets and similar devices play an increasing role in many people's daily lives and I want to make sure the markets in this area can flourish without anticompetitive constraints imposed by any company."
7The verdict of a Commission investigation opened in 2010 was that the comparison service Google Shopping was given favourable treatment by being shown more prominently on screen.
8It added: "Users do not necessarily see the most relevant results in response to queries.
9The Commission can levy fines of up to 10 per cent of Google's global turnover.
10Google has been given 10 weeks to respond and can then seek a formal hearing.
11Google Search senior vice president Amit Singhal said: "We strongly disagree with the need to issue a statement of objections and look forward to making our case over the weeks ahead.
12While Google may be the most used search engine, people can now find and access information in numerous different ways.
