Islamic State Seizes Syria's Ancient Town Of Palmyra

1The Islamic State captured the ancient Syrian town of Palmyra after government defense lines there collapsed May 20, a stunning triumph for the group only days after it gained control of the strategic city of Ramadi in Iraq.
2It was unclear by nightfall how close to Palmyra's famed archaeological site the militants had advanced, activists said, adding that Syrian soldiers were seen fleeing the area.
3The ruins at Palmyra are one of the world's most renowned historic sites and there were fears the extremists would destroy them as they did major archaeological sites in Iraq.
4The UNESCO world heritage site is famous for its 2,000-year-old towering Roman-era colonnades and other ruins and priceless artifacts.
5Before the war, thousands of tourists a year visited the remote desert outpost.
6The fall of the town to the Islamic State group after a week of fighting was an enormous loss to the government, not only because of its cultural significance, but because it opens the way for the extremists to advance to key government-held areas, including Damascus and the Syrian coast to the south.
7Next to it are also important gas and oil fields in the country's central region.
8With the fall of Palmyra, IS now controls about 40 percent of Syria and almost all of the country's oil fields, which are a major source of revenue for the militant group.
9It was not immediately clear how close the militants were to the ruins, which are just southwest of the town.
10The militants entered from the north.
11"I am terrified," said Maamoun Abdulkarim, Syria's director-general of antiquities and museums.
12"This is a PR battle for Daesh, and they will insist on scoring victory against civilization by destroying" the ancient ruins, he said, using the Arabic acronym for the group.
13The fall of Palmyra just days after Islamic State fighters seized the strategic Iraqi city of Ramadi showed the extremists' ability to advance on multiple fronts at opposite ends of a sprawling battlefield that spans the two countries.
14It also erased any sense that recent IS losses in Tikrit and elsewhere had dealt a major blow to the militants.
15The Syrian Observatory for human rights reported that government forces collapsed in the face of IS attacks and withdrew from the town late May 20.
16Beibares Tellawi, an activist in Homs province, confirmed IS was in control of the town.
17He said the militants had reached the notorious Tadmur prison, where thousands of Syrian dissidents have been imprisoned and tortured over the years.
18The government evacuated what it considered its most important prisoners days agor, but the fate of the rest of the 1,000 or so prisoners was not immediately known.
19Syrian state TV acknowledged that pro-government forces had withdrawn from Palmyra, and the IS-affiliated Aaamaq News Agency reported the town was "under the complete control of the Islamic State fighters."
20IS fighters had also seized control of the Jazl oil field in the Homs countryside, the Aaamaq report said.
21Homs governor Talal Barazzi earlier reported that at least 19 people had died in fighting before the miltants assumed control of the city, including seven civilians and 12 pro-government militiament known as National Defense Forces.
22Abdulkarim said workers were able to save hundreds of statues and masterpieces from Palmyra that were transported to safe houses in Damascus.
23"But how do you save colonnades that weigh a ton? How do you save temples and cemeteries and, and, and. . .?" he asked.
24He appealed to the international community to declare "a red line" around Palmyra and called on the U.S.-led coalition to "at least prevent IS convoys from reaching it."
