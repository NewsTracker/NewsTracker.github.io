9The head of the Greek Orthodox Church in South America has had a privileged spot at Pope Francis' events this week.
10Metropolitan Tarasios is an old friend of the pope's from Buenos Aires, where he is based.
11The Vatican spokesman, the Rev.
12Federico Lombardi, said Sunday that Tarasios asked Francis if he might participate in the trip, and he traveled from Ecuador to Bolivia and to Paraguay on his own.
13On Sunday, he gave a greeting at Francis final Mass in Asuncion.
14Lombardi said his presence "gave an ecumenical dimension to the trip, which otherwise wasn't very evident."
15Indeed, Francis' pilgrimage has been notable for the absence of any meetings between the pope and leaders of other faiths - usually a mainstay of papal trips.
16Francis has very friendly relations with the Orthodox Church; he recently cited the works of Ecumenical Patriarch Bartholomew I in his encyclical on the environment.
17Pope Francis is starting to show the effects of his gruelling three-nation, week-long trip half-way across the world from Rome.
18The 78-year-old Francis appeared to doze off a bit at the end of Mass on Sunday when the archbishop of Asuncion, Paraguay delivered a lengthy speech of thanks for his visit.
19Federico Lombardi, said it's only natural that Francis "isn't in the condition in which he left Rome" given his packed schedule.
20Francis added to his official itinerary some impromptu visits on Saturday to a hospital and a Jesuit church and meetings with friends and family who came to Paraguay from Argentina.
21But Lombardi says Francis tends to rally, especially when he's surrounded by the kind of young people who will be the focus of his final event Sunday.
22The head of public relations for the Paraguayan police says that as many as 1 million people have attended Pope Francis' outdoor Mass on Sunday.
23Elisa Ledesma says the estimate comes from the agency's "experience and observation."
24Ledesma adds that police officials in Caacupe, where a papal Mass was held on Saturday, say that about 1 million people attended that event and the procession to the nearby sanctuary of the Virgin of Caacupe.
25Pope Francis is meeting with Paraguayan bishops in a closed-door session.
26One of the biggest issues in the Episcopal Conference relates to Francis' decision to oust a controversial bishop last year in Ciudad del Este, the country's second largest city that borders Brazil and Argentina.
27The Rev.
28Rogelio Livieres Plano was ousted in September.
29A member of the conservative Opus Dei movement, Livieres Plano claimed shortly after his removal that he was persecuted by opposing bishops and liberal parishioners.
30Francis has never commented on the decision and Vatican officials have also said very little.
31An Associated Press review found that during his decade as bishop, Livieres Plano made many questionable spending decisions.
32For example, he failed to use hundreds of thousands of dollars in donations for social works as they were intended.
33Since Pope Francis arrived in Paraguay there have been complaints that the popemobile has moved too fast for people to get a good look at the pontiff.
34There were also complaints about church authorities only permitting five people to ask questions of the pope Saturday evening.
35Now, residents of the Banado Norte slum are sounding off.
36Neighborhood association President Francisco Rodriguez says his wife and a neighbor were not invited to sit with the pope despite being co-founders of the chapel where the gathering was held.
37Rodriguez also said the two young people who described teenage pregnancy issues with the pope at the meeting were outsiders, just like some politicians who attended like Education Minister Marta Lafuente.
38When Lafuente walked onto the stage people heckled her in Guarani, shouting "Lying Minister."
39At the outdoor Mass in Asuncion, a young lector asked the faithful to pray for Paraguay's indigenous, for the poor and others.
40He also asked them to pray for a police officer who was kidnapped a year ago by leftist rebels of the EPP, or People's Paraguayan Army.
41The pope made reference to abducted officer Edelio Morinigo on Saturday evening at a meeting with civil society groups.
42But he didn't seem to have matters clear, mentioning someone "kidnapped by the army."
43At a news conference later, Paraguayan Catholic Church spokesman Mariano Mercado referred to the rebels as "criminals."
44The shadowy EPP as among the world's most hermetic guerrilla movements.
45It has operated since 2008 in the country's north, where it has attacked police posts, soldiers and ranches.
46It is blamed for at least 40 killings.
47While Pope Francis was telling people at a Mass six miles away to open their hearts to "the hungry, the infirm, the prisoners, lepers and the disabled" Pedro Fernandez was back at work picking up other people's trash.
48The father of eight was presente for the pope's visit earlier Sunday to Banado Norte, the slum by the Paraguay river that frequently flods.
49He told The Associated Press that the pope's visit may be good for the spirit, but he had to get out now and collect plastic bottles and cans on his motorcycle cart.
50If I don't work, said Fernandez, the household doesn't eat.
51He says his family is up at 3 a.m. looking for bottles and cans because he also has to pay on installments for the motorcycle cart.
52Until 2013, he used a horse.
53Pope Francis is celebrating the last Mass of his three-nation South America tour on a very special altar.
54It was made in honor of Paraguay's native Guarani and out of respect for Mother Earth.
55It is composed of 40,000 ears of corn, 200,000 coconuts and adorned with 1,000 squash gourds.
56The altar was created by the artist Koki Ruiz, who also included an image of St. Francis of Assisi, the patron saint of the natural world and the pope's namesake.
57Also pictured is St. Ignacio de Loyola, the founder of the Jesuit order the pontiff belongs to.
58Ruiz told The Associated Press last week that the corn, coconut and squash are subsistence products of Paraguay's native peoples.
59Hundreds of thousands have gathered on a huge swampy field called Nu Guazu (Nyew Gwa-ZOO) inside a military base awaiting the arrival of Pope Francis.
60Among them is Argentina's president, Cristina Fernandez.
61She's seated with Paraguayan President Horacio Cartes.
62At this very spot, Pope John Paull II in 1988 canonized Paraguay's first saint - Roque Gonzalez de Santa Cruz.
63The Jesuit priest was a missionary to the Guarani people.
64The residents want land titles and many wonder how they'll be affected when a planned highway is built alongside the river.
65Said one, Maria Garcia: "We built our neighborhoods piece by piece, we made them livable despite the difficulties of the terrain, the rising of the river and despite public authorities who either ignored us or were hostile to us."
66Among those waiting for the pope in Banado Norte at the St. John the Baptist chapel was 82-year-old widow Francisca de Chamorro.
67Her rudimentary wooden home sits right behind it.
68"Now I can die in peace," she said.
69Chamorro said that if the pope had visited this time last year he'd be wading through floodwater.
70People waited for the pope on a soccer pitch.
71The church had asked people not to hang banners, but big placards were on display demanding land titles.
72The locals want title to the land they live on so that when a highway is built along the river they will be compensated if they have to leave.
