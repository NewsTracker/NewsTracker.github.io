Tamir Rice cremated after he was killed by Cleveland police officer

1The body of 12-year-old Tamir Rice, who was shot dead by police at a Cleveland playground six months ago, has been cremated.
2Tamir Rice's mother made the decision last week, family attorney Walter Madison says.
3Tamir had an airsoft gun that shoots nonlethal plastic pellets when a rookie officer fatally shot him on November 22, 2014.
4Timothy Loehmann, the officer who shot Rice, and Frank Garmback, who was driving the police squad car, are white.
5Rice was black.
6The incident was one of a series of killings of black people by white police officers that fueled national outrage over the use of force by law enforcement officers.
7Rice, a sixth-grader, was playing with a replica handgun that mainly shoots plastic pellets when he was shot twice by police responding to a 911 emergency call about a man with a gun outside a recreation center.
8Rice died the next day.
9A security camera video of the shooting shows Loehmann shooting Rice less than two seconds after the two officers' squad car arrived.
10The officer had no way of knowing Tamir wasn't carrying a real firearm, a police union official says.
11Madison has sued the city, and says in a legal case Tamir's cause of death would be an issue and there was concern there may need to be independent examination of his body.
12Madison told The Daily Kos 'Samaria is a mother first.
13'Whether or in life or death, her instinct is to care for her child.
14'Due to an unfortunate turn of events she was faced with the unspeakable decision to finally put her son to final rest or endure this legal morass and its hardness.
15'After the tremendous amount of support from around the world, Samaria made the grief stricken decision to be a mother.
16The court filing also reportedly cited fears of missing evidence and said the lawsuit will not be stopped.
17The filing came after a city request the Rice family put the lawsuit on hold as long as the probe is going on, the Post reported.
18Cuyahoga County Sheriff Cliff Pinkney said Tuesday the investigation into Tamir's shooting is nearly complete.
19The brief statement to reporters from Pinkney on Rice's death in November appeared to be in response to a demand last week for answers from Rice's mother and family attorneys.
20Pinkney said that his office could not review a redacted version of the city's investigative file on the shooting until mid-February - more than a month after the sheriff was assigned the case.
21The redacted file lacked statements from police officers given as part of an internal review but which generally cannot be used in criminal prosecutions.
22Prosecutors received both redacted and unredacted files in January, Pinkney said.
23Pinkney said that while it would be 'politically expedient' to provide an arbitrary deadline to complete the investigation, he would not sacrifice the probe's integrity to do that.
24He declined to take questions.
25'All parties involved know that my department is conducting a fair, impartial and thorough investigation, one that leaves zero stones unturned,' said Pinkney.
26Cuyahoga County Prosecutor Timothy McGinty has said the shooting evidence will be presented to a grand jury when the sheriff completes his investigation.
27Pinkney said his investigators have reviewed thousands of pages of documents and conducted numerous interviews, but a few more witnesses need to be interviewed.
28Some family members shouted questions at Pinkney, including Tamir's cousin, Latonya Goldsby.
29'We want charges brought against these officers for their recklessness,' Goldsby later told reporters.
