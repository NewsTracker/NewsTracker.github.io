Iraq officials disagree on whether IS commander was killed in U.S.-led airstrikes

1In this Tuesday, Jan. 27, 2015 photo, a Kurdish Peshmerga soldier stands guard, overlooking the road between Mosul and Tal Afar at the frontline of Eski Mosul.
2The Iraqi Defence Ministry says a senior Islamic State commander has been killed in a U.S.-led airstrike in Tal Afar.
3BAGHDAD -- Iraq's Defence Ministry said Wednesday an airstrike by the U.S.-led coalition killed a senior Islamic State commander and others near the extremist-held city of Mosul, though the country's Interior Ministry later said it wasn't clear if he even was wounded.
4The Defence Ministry said the strike killed Abu Alaa al-Afari and others who were in a meeting inside a mosque in the northern city of Tal Afar, 72 kilometres west of Mosul.
5The ministry described al-Afari as a senior deputy to the Islamic State group's leader, Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi.
6It did not offer a time for the strike, nor any specific casualty figures.
7It did offer a black-and-white video clip of an airstrike hitting a building.
8A Defence Ministry official told The Associated Press that the airstrike happened late Tuesday.
9He spoke on condition of anonymity as he is not authorized to speak to journalists.
10But Interior Ministry spokesman Brig.
11Gen. Saad Maan Ibrahim contradicted the defence ministry statement, saying while al-Afari was present at the airstrike, it wasn't clear what happened to him.
12Pentagon spokesman Col.
13Steve Warren said the U.S. could not independently confirm the reports that al-Afari had been targeted.
14The U.S.-led coalition said earlier Wednesday it carried out a strike in the past day near Tal Afar, destroying "an ISIL fighting position and an ISIL heavy machine-gun," using a different acronym for the group.
15This wouldn't be the first time Iraqi authorities put out incorrect information in its war against the Islamic State group, which holds a third of the country and neighbouring Syria.
16In November, a Defence Ministry statement claiming that al-Baghdadi was wounded in an Iraqi airstrike was later retracted.
17Ibrahim said al-Afari was an alias for a wanted Islamic State group senior leader named Abd Al-Rahman Mustafa al-Qaduli.
18The U.S. Department of Treasury says al-Qaduli joined al-Qaida in Iraq in 2004 under the command of its slain leader, Abu Musab al-Zarqawi, and served as his deputy and the group's "emir," or senior leader in northern city of Mosul.
19The Treasury Department adds that in 2006, al-Qaduli travelled to Pakistan on behalf al-Zarqawi to conduct an interview, which was then to be provided to al-Qaida leaders in Afghanistan.
20The Treasury Department added al-Qaduli to the list of specially designated global terrorists in 2014 "for acting for or on behalf of ... the Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant," a former name for the Islamic State group.
21The State Department, which offers up to $7 million for al-Qaduli, says he was born in 1957 or 1959 in Mosul.
22American officials, who list a variety of aliases for al-Qaduli, have Abu Alaa as one, though not Abu Alaa al-Afari.
