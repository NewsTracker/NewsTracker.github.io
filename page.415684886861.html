Clashes rage in Ukrainian town, making mockery of truce

1The truce announced in mid-February has never taken here, so traveling by the main roads is too dangerous.
2Government and Russian-backed separatist forces face off in daily gun and artillery battles across an unseen line cutting through the town.
3The skirmishes are fierce, but contained - for now.
4Still, the enduring unrest arouses deep anxieties that a conflict which has already claimed more than 6,000 lives in eastern Ukraine could flare up again across the entire 450-kilometer (280-mile) front line.
5Fighters of the Azov Battalion cook food during a break in the town of Shyrokyne, eastern Ukraine, Sunday, March 22, 2015.
6Government and Russian-backed separatist forces face off against one another across an unseen line cutting through the town in daily gun and artillery battles.
7The truce announced in mid-February never made it here.
8Shyrokyne itself is not much of a prize.
9It is the industrial port city of Mariupol, 10 kilometers (6 miles) further west, that Ukrainian forces want to defend from the rebels at all costs.
10Residents and government troops alike believe the separatists' ultimate aim is to take Mariupol - and eventually create a land bridge between Russia and Crimea, which Russia annexed last March.
11Crimea has no physical link to Russian territory now and a bridge being discussed is years away from completion.
12Government forces in Shyrokyne are only truly at ease behind three defensive lines separating them from the heat of fighting in the center.
13At a makeshift garrison installed there, on the grounds of a restaurant near the shore, two tanks stood parked Sunday under a striped awning.
14As reports came that two enemy tanks had been spotted, Al's thoughts turned to the combat ahead.
15"It is all about to start," he said.
16To proceed closer to the area where the battle is fiercest, soldiers abandon their cars and race on foot toward a school, climbing through a hole in the fence.
17The asphalt on the road had been torn up by explosives, so only armored vehicles could get through with ease.
18The responsibility for defending Shyrokyne is shared between the Azov and Donbass battalions, who take weekly turns to serve in the town.
19Coordination is sometimes poor, however.
20As Azov troops jogged for cover behind the school Sunday, one soldier shouted: "What are you doing? Are you crazy running like that? There are booby traps there."
21Another soldier corrected him.
22"Nah, the booby traps are over there," he said, waving his hand vaguely to the left.
23"The Donbass guys put them there."
24Inside the school, children's drawings still decorated the walls.
25One man fried sausages and another chowed down on boiled oats as a mobile phone blared out music by a Russian death metal band.
26Underfoot, amid the spent bullet cartridges and shrapnel, students' art collages lay covered in fallen plaster.
27As the sound of mortars grew more intense, all the men ducked inside for cover.
28"There they go, they've started again," said an Azov spotter with the nickname Mathematician.
29A cease-fire between Ukrainian and rebel forces was forged after marathon negotiations between the leaders of Ukraine, Russia, Germany and France on Feb. 12.
30Under the truce, fighting was supposed to stop and heavy weapons were to be pulled back from the front line.
31Responsibility for verifying the cease-fire lies with monitors from the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe.
32Mathematician said the rebels always wait for the OSCE teams to leave before resuming their attacks.
33"As soon as the OSCE leaves, the firing starts," he said.
34Separatists accuse Ukrainian forces of similar deviousness, and it's hard to know definitely even at the front line who started any specific round of firing.
35The head of the OSCE monitoring mission on Monday demanded that his teams be granted secure access to Shyrokyne.
36"Both sides in this area continue to violate numerous provisions of the (cease-fire) agreements, including those related to cessation of fire, prohibition of attacking moves, withdrawal of heavy weapons, and deployment of (airborne drones)," said Ertugrul Apakan.
37In Washington, U.S. State Department spokeswoman Marie Harf said the disparity between what Russia and the separatists say and what they do threatens stability in the region.
38"Russia and the separatists claim to be honoring the ceasefire, but in reality, they are violating it on a regular basis, and are encroaching further beyond the ceasefire line," she said, and reiterated a call for unfettered access for OSCE monitors.
39Later on Sunday afternoon, sounds resembling outgoing mortars could be heard from a field near Shyrokyne.
40Soldiers refused to give an AP reporter access to the area.
41"We don't have any mortars," Mathematician said, smiling.
42"They only allow us to have small arms. But when they (rebels) get really brazen, we call in support and flatten them."
43Many in the Azov Battalion have unabashed Ukrainian nationalist sympathies, prompting rebels to label them neo-fascists.
44From time to time, Azov fighters in Shyrokyne greeted one another with ironic Roman salutes and then grinned at their own humor.
45That kind of idle larking and the battalion's flirtation with neo-Nazi symbolism is seized upon as confirmation of their critics' worst fears.
46Some embrace fervent Ukrainian nationalism as a repudiation of the heavily Russian-dominated Soviet legacy, all while serving with fighters from a wide array of political and ethnic backgrounds.
47Chit-chat switches casually from Ukrainian to Russian and back again.
48The best view of the skirmishes raging inside the village is from the House of Culture, a stolid building of a style popular across the Soviet Union during Josef Stalin's rule.
49From there, fighters unleashed salvoes from an automatic grenade launcher and 73 mm caliber anti-tank guns.
50Ukrainian forces hold the elevated sections of Shyrokyne, giving them a tactical advantage.
51Smoke could be seen billowing from houses in the lower-lying buffer zone.
52One shell apparently flying in from rebel positions landed by a church.
53All at once, the men on the House of Culture roof cried out in a jubilant chorus, pointing toward the village.
54"Did you see those flames? We hit a tank. Two direct hits," one government fighter said.
55By the day's end, the final Ukrainian tally was at least one enemy tank destroyed and two relatively light injuries among their ranks.
56Almost every day brings new casualties - on occasion, some fatal - but their determination to stop the rebel advance along the Azov Sea coast is intense.
57One Azov fighter calling himself Tantsor - Russian for dancer - said the rebels were clearly hoping to take Mariupol by stealth.
58"They are violating the cease-fire everywhere and using any chance they get to advance even by one centimeter toward peaceful Ukrainian towns," he said.
