Nepal quake toll tops 5,000 as aid reaches epicenter area

1PASLANG, Nepal (AP) - Aid reached a hilly district near the epicenter of Nepal's earthquake for the first time Wednesday, four days after the quake struck and as the death toll from the disaster passed the 5,000 mark.
2But it will still take time for the food and other supplies to reach survivors in remote communities who have been cut off by landslides, warned said Geoff Pinnock, a World Food Program emergencies officer.
3"It doesn't happen overnight," said Pinnock from the village of Majuwa, 20 kilometers (16 miles) downhill from Gorkha town, a staging area for relief efforts to areas worst-hit by Saturday's magnitude-7.8 earthquake.
4Houses on the higher reaches of mountains destroyed in s earthquake are seen from a helicopter near Dhadingbesti, in Nepal, Wednesday, April 29, 2015.
5The first aid shipments reached a hilly district near the epicenter of Nepal's earthquake, a U.N. food agency official said, and distribution of food and medicine would start Wednesday, five days after the quake struck.
6Nearby, five cargo trucks filled with rice, cooking oil and sugar stood on a grassy field fringed with banana and acacia trees beneath the soaring Himalayas, waiting for a helicopter carry the supplies to remote, quake-hit villages.
7Soon, the U.N. food agency was expected to deliver shipments of high-energy food biscuits to be sent out to areas without enough water for cooking, Pinnock said.
8The first aid shipments had reached Dhading district, just east of Gorhka, near the epicenter, about 80 kilometers (50 miles) northwest of Kathmandu.
9Nepalese police said Wednesday the death toll from the quake had reached 4,989.
10Another 18 were killed on the slopes of Mount Everest, while 61 died in neighboring India, and China's official Xinhua News Agency reported 25 dead in Tibet, putting the total over 5,000.
11The disaster also injured more than 10,000, police said, and rendered thousands more homeless.
12The U.N. says the disaster has affected 8.1 million people - more than a fourth of Nepal's population of 27.8 million - and that 1.4 million needed food assistance.
13"Under normal circumstances, a government would have the capacity to respond to maybe 10, or 20, or 30,000 people in need. But if you're looking at 8 million as we are here, you need a bit of time to scale everything up," Pinnock said.
14Planes carrying food and other supplies have been steadily arriving at Kathmandu's small airport, but the aid distribution process remains fairly chaotic, with Nepalese officials having difficulty directing the flow of emergency supplies.
15About 200 people blocked traffic in the capital Wednesday to protest the slow pace of aid delivery.
16The protesters faced off with police and there were minor scuffles but no arrests were made.
17Police arrested dozens of people on suspicion of looting abandoned homes as well as causing panic by spreading rumors of another big quake.
18Police official Bigyan Raj Sharma said 27 people were detained for stealing.
19But in a sign that life was inching back to normal, banks in Kathmandu opened for a few hours Wednesday and stuffed their ATMs with cash, giving people access to money.
20Thousands of people lined up at bus stations in the capital, hoping to reach their hometowns in rural areas.
21Some have had little news of family and loved ones since Saturday's quake.
22Others are scared of staying close to the epicenter, northwest of Kathmandu.
23"I am hoping to get on a bus, any bus heading out of Kathmandu. I am too scared to be staying in Kathmandu," said Raja Gurung, who wanted to get to his home in western Nepal.
24"The house near my rented apartment collapsed. It was horrible. I have not gone indoors in many days. I would rather leave than a live a life of fear in Kathmandu."
25In some heartening news, French rescuers freed a man from the ruins of a three-story Kathmandu hotel more than three days after the quake.
26Rishi Khanal, 27, said he drank his own urine to survive.
27Khanal had just finished lunch at a hotel last Saturday and had gone up to the second floor when everything suddenly started moving and falling.
28He was struck by falling masonry and trapped with his foot crushed under rubble.
29"I had some hope but by yesterday I'd given up. My nails went all white and my lips cracked ... I was sure no one was coming for me. I was certain I was going to die," he told The Associated Press from his hospital bed on Wednesday.
30Khanal said he was surrounded by dead people and a terrible smell.
31But he kept banging on the rubble all around him and eventually this brought a French rescue team that extracted him after being trapped for 82 hours.
32"I am thankful," he said.
