Tikrit victory boosts Iraq fight to defeat Islamic State

1Speaking from a newly recaptured area of central Tikrit after a month-long operation, Prime Minister Haider al-Abadi said the government was "determined to liberate every inch of Iraqi land."
2But Tikrit is far from secure, with various officials saying that Islamic State militants were still present in the city, while bomb-rigged houses and buildings also pose a major threat.
3Interior Minister Mohammed al-Ghaban told journalists in Tikrit that there were "a few pockets (of Islamic State fighters) remaining in some of the neighborhoods."
4And a commander from the Ketaeb Imam Ali militia, one of the paramilitary forces fighting alongside police and soldiers, reported clashes with Islamic State in the city's north in the morning.
5They "tried to advance on the university," Rasul al-Abadi told AFP, adding that there were "no more than 303 jihadis left in the city's large Qadisiya district.
6Ghaban said Islamic State fighters were trying to cross the Tigris River, east of the city, to escape.
7Supporting that assertion, a paramilitary commander said the jihadis launched an attack Wednesday from a mountain hideout northeast of Tikrit in an attempt to open a safe passage for fleeing militants.
8There has been concern that Iraq does not have enough specialized ordnance clearance teams to handle the large quantity of traps left by Islamic State fighters.
9Ghaban said that security forces had so far found 185 rigged houses and around 900 other bombs.
10Exploding bombs periodically sent clouds of dust rising over Tikrit on Wednesday, apparently detonated intentionally by forces working to clear the city.
11Columns of black smoke also rose from various burning houses and businesses.
12Some of the dozens of palaces in former dictator Saddam Hussein's massive complex on the eastern side of the city were smashed by shelling or airstrikes, but other areas of Tikrit were comparatively lightly damaged.
13Militiamen have quickly set about spray painting the names of their groups on walls and windows, and also stole items including clothes, shampoo and shaving cream from shops in central Tikrit.
14There is still evidence of Islamic State's almost 10-month presence in the city, including the group's black flag painted on walls and writing identifying some buildings as "properties" of the jihadis' self-declared state.
15On Tuesday, Abadi claimed the city was retaken but the U.S.-led coalition supporting Baghdad from the air said Islamic State still held parts of Tikrit.
16Defense Minister Khaled al-Obeidi vowed Wednesday to press on with offensive operations.
17"We are coming, Anbar. We are coming, Nineveh," he said in a recorded address, referring to the last two provinces still largely controled by Islamic State.
18The loss of Tikrit further isolates Mosul, the capital of Nineveh and the main Islamic State hub in Iraq, with Baghdad's forces now poised to push north while Kurdish forces close in from the three other directions.
19Zaid al-Ali, author of "The Struggle For Iraq's Future," said, however, that the fighting in Tikrit was made easier because the city was largely emptied of its population even before the operation began on March 2.
20"Mosul still has a large civilian population, which will make things very complicated," the analyst said.
21The government has provided no information on how many fighters were killed, wounded or captured in the fighting but Baghdad's forces are believed to have suffered significant casualties.
22Iraqi army and police forces, as well as pro-government paramilitary forces dominated by Iran-backed Shiite militias, completely surrounded Tikrit within two weeks of launching the operation, but the operation then stalled.
23Abadi requested coalition strikes, which began on March 25.
24But Iran was Baghdad's top foreign partner for much of the operation - involvement evidenced by graffiti in Persian in the city.
25The Iraqi government had tried and failed several times to retake Saddam's hometown, but the latest operation was much larger and better organized.
26A significant number of local Sunni tribal forces were involved in the battle to retake Salaheddin province, of which Tikrit is the capital, from Islamic State, in an effort to defuse sectarian resentment.
27Amid concerns over abuses committed by Shiite fighters against Sunnis in recaptured areas, the U.N.'s top envoy in Iraq has reiterated an appeal for civilians to be protected.
28But militia and police said civilians had left the city prior to its recapture.
29There have also been reports that pro-government forces were engaged in looting in the captured parts of the city.
