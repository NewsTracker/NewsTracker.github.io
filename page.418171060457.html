11ATHENS, Greece (AP) - Greece slipped deeper into its financial abyss on Tuesday, when the bailout program it has relied on for five years was set to expire and the country was due to become the first developed nation to miss a payment to the International Monetary Fund.
12After Greece made a last-ditch proposal to extend its bailout, the creditors decided in a teleconference that there was no way they could reach a deal.
13Greece's international bailout expires at midnight central European time, after which the country loses access to billions of euros in funds.
14At the same time, Greece has said it will not be able to make a payment of 1.6 billion euros to the IMF.
15An EU official said Greece's creditors would not agree Tuesday night on a new bailout deal or extension and would instead, starting Wednesday, focus on a new plan for the country.
16There was "clearly no agreement tonight," said the official, who spoke on condition of anonymity because the announcement was not official, adding the eurozone's finance ministers would reconvene Wednesday to discuss "a follow-up agreement."
17The brinkmanship that has characterized Greece's bailout negotiations with its European creditors and the IMF rose several notches over the weekend, when Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras announced he would put a deal proposal by creditors to a referendum on Sunday.
18He is advocating a "no" vote.
19The move increased fears the country could soon fall out of the euro currency bloc and Greeks rushed to pull money out of ATMs, leading the government to impose restrictions on banking transactions on Monday for at least a week.
20Greeks are now limited to cash withdrawals of 60 euros ($67) a day and cannot send money abroad or make international payments without special permission.
21European officials and Greek opposition parties have been adamant that a "no" vote in Sunday's referendum will mean Greece will leave the euro and possibly even the EU.
22The government says this is scaremongering, and that a rejection of creditor demands will mean the country is in a better negotiating position.
23Hopes for an 11th-hour deal before the deadline were raised when the Greek side announced it had submitted a new deal proposal Tuesday afternoon, and the eurozone's 19 finance ministers held a teleconference to discuss it.
24But those hopes were quickly dashed.
25Chancellor Angela Merkel said she ruled out further negotiations with Greece before Sunday's referendum on whether to accept creditors' demands for budget reforms.
26"Before the planned referendum is carried out, we will not negotiate over anything new," the dpa news agency quoted Merkel as telling members of her party.
27Earlier, she had said talks could continue after Tuesday night's deadlines expired.
28Greece's latest offer involves a proposal to tap Europe's bailout fund - the so-called European Stability Mechanism, a pot of money set up after Greece's rescue programs to help countries in need.
29Tsipras' office said the proposal was "for the full coverage of (Greece's) financing needs with the simultaneous restructuring of the debt."
30"Tonight we have sent a letter (to the eurogroup) that narrows the differences further," Deputy Prime Minister Yannis Dragasakis said in an interview on state television.
31"We are making an additional effort. There are six points where this effort can be made. I don't want to get into specifics. But it includes pensions and labour issues."
32Speaking about the latest Greek proposal, the EU official said the creditors insist that loans must come with conditions.
33Over the past five years of Greece's bailout, the creditors have insisted on budget cuts, tax increases and economic reforms.
34Both officials spoke on condition of anonymity because the talks were ongoing.
35In Athens, more than 10,000 "Yes" vote supporters gathered outside parliament despite a thunderstorm, chanting "Europe! Europe!"
36Most huddled under umbrellas, including Athens resident Sofia Matthaiou.
37"I don't know if we'll get a deal. But we have to press them to see reason," she said, referring to the government.
38"The creditors need to water down their positions, too."
39The protest came a day after a similar-sized government supporters advocating a "no" vote held a similar demonstration.
40On Monday, European Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker made a new offer to Greece.
41Under that proposal, Tsipras would need to accept the creditors' proposal which was on the table last weekend.
42He would also have to change his position on Sunday's referendum.
43Commission spokesman Margaritis Schinas said the offer would also involve unspecified discussions on Athens's massive debt load of over 300 billion euros, or around 180 percent of GDP.
44The Greek side has long called for debt relief, saying its mountainous debt is unsustainable.
45A Greek government official said Tsipras had spoken earlier in the day with Juncker, European Central Bank chief Mario Draghi and European Parliament president Martin Schulz.
46Meanwhile, missing the IMF payment will cut Greece off from new loans from the organization.
47And with its bailout program expiring, Greece will lose access to more than 16 billion euros ($18 billion) in financial support it had not yet tapped, officials said.
48They spoke on condition of anonymity because talks about the program were still ongoing at the time.
49On the streets of Athens, long queues formed again at ATM machines as Greeks struggled with the new restrictions on banking transactions.
50The elderly have been hit particularly hard, with tens of thousands of pensions unpaid as of Tuesday afternoon.
51Many also found themselves completely cut off from any cash as they do not have bank cards.
52The finance ministry said it would open about 1,000 bank branches across the country for three days from Wednesday to allow pensioners without bank cards to make withdrawals.
53But the limit would be set at 120 euros for the whole week.
