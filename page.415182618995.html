Netanyahu Urges U.S. To Hold Out For Better Nuclear Deal With Iran

1In a controversial address to Congress, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu criticized the Obama administration's negotiations on limiting the Iranian nuclear program.
2Israel's prime minister urged the U.S. Congress today to hold out for a better nuclear deal with Iran, but President Obama says Benjamin Netanyahu offered no viable alternative.
3We're going to begin this hour with reaction to Netanyahu's speech, including the view from Switzerland.
4That's where Secretary of State John Kerry negotiated with his Iranian counterpart today.
5First, here's NPR's Michele Kelemen on how Netanyahu's address was received in Washington.
6MICHELE KELEMEN, BYLINE: While Netanyahu began his speech with a nod to President Obama's support for Israel, he quickly turned to his major critique of U.S. foreign-policy.
7He says the U.S. is giving Iran too many concessions, negotiating a deal that would leave Iran with - in his words - a vast nuclear infrastructure and facing restrictions that would only last about a decade.
8PRIME MINISTER BENJAMIN NETANYAHU: It doesn't block Iran's path to the bomb.
9It paves Iran's path to the bomb.
10So why would anyone make this deal?
11KELEMEN: He says Iran's regime is, quote, "radical as ever."
12The Israeli prime minister says he thinks the greatest danger in the world today is the marriage of militant Islam with nuclear weapons.
13So he wants the U.S. to hold out for a deal that would prevent Iran from acquiring them and threatening Israel's very survival.
14NETANYAHU: A better deal that won't give Iran an easy path to the bomb, a better deal that Israel and its neighbors may not like, but with which we could live - literally.
15KELEMEN: The members of Congress who attended this speech gave him numerous standing ovations, but dozens of Democrats stayed away.
16REPRESENTATIVE JOHN YARMUTH: This speech was straight out of the Dick Cheney playbook.
17This was fear mongering at its ultimate.
18KELEMEN: That's Kentucky Congressman John Yarmuth, who says he resents the Israeli prime minister's attempts to tell the Obama administration how to negotiate.
19Illinois Democrat Jan Schakowsky adds, she thinks Netanyahu is trying to, quote, "stampede the U.S. into war once again."
20REPRESENTATIVE JAN SCHAKOWSKY: We should be able to continue to lock down and roll back in a verifiable way Iran's ability to achieve a nuclear weapon.
21That's the goal.
22We can't achieve it Bibi Netanyahu's way.
23KELEMEN: President Obama's national security adviser, Susan Rice, made a similar case to the pro-Israel lobby AIPAC last night, telling them, quote, "we cannot let a totally unachievable ideal stand in the way of a good agreement."
24SUSAN RICE: I know that some of you will be urging Congress to insist that Iran forgo its domestic enrichment capacity entirely.
25KELEMEN: That was clearly not meant as an applause line.
26Rice had to wait for the crowd to sit back down to finish her thought.
27RICE: But as desirable as that would be, it is neither realistic nor achievable.
28KELEMEN: No one, she says, can make Iran unlearn the expertise it already has.
29Netanyahu, in his speech to Congress, says it's the infrastructure that matters.
30NETANYAHU: Nuclear know how without nuclear infrastructure doesn't get you very much.
31A racecar driver without a car can't drive.
32A pilot without a plane can't fly.
33Without thousands of centrifuges, tons of enriched uranium or heavy water facilities, Iran can't make nuclear weapons.
34KELEMEN: President Obama says he didn't watch Netanyahu's speech, but read the transcript and didn't find anything new.
35PRESIDENT BARACK OBAMA: On the core issue, which is how do we prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon, which would make it far more dangerous and would give it scope for even greater action in the region, the prime minister didn't offer any viable alternatives.
36KELEMEN: He says Netanyahu made almost the same speech last year when the U.S. and other world powers reached a temporary agreement with Iran.
37That deal has held, Obama says, and he calls the current negotiations the best chance the U.S. and its partners have to prevent Iran from developing a nuclear bomb.
38Michele Kelemen, NPR News, Washington.
