Iraqi militia loyal to radical cleric joins Tikrit offensive

SXSW 2015: Of humans and robots in sci-fi chamber drama 'Ex Machina'

1Men from Iraq's Peace Brigade left to the Shiite holy city of Samarra, the launching point for operations to recapture Saddam Hussein's hometown.
2About a dozen heavily armed trucks packed with fighters left Baghdad earlier Sunday.
3"We are traveling to help the community of Muslims and to help the people of Iraq," said Ali al-Mousawi, a spokesman for the brigade.
4Iraqi troops and Iranian-backed Shiite militias launched a long-awaited offensive this month aimed at retaking Tikrit, a Sunni bastion on the Tigris River whose capture would pave the way for an assault on Mosul, which U.S. officials have said could come as soon as next month.
5Tikrit and Mosul have been under the control of the Islamic State group since it swept across northern Iraq in June.
6The extremists hold a third of Iraq and neighboring Syria.
7Iraqi officials say that at least 30,000 men - including soldiers, Shiite militiamen, Sunni tribes and police officers - are fighting to seize Tikrit.
8U.S. Gen. Martin Dempsey said Wednesday that at least 20,000 militiamen are taking part in the offensive.
9Earlier this month, New York-based Human Rights Watch called on the Iraqi government to protect civilians in Tikrit and allow them to flee combat zones.
10Its statement noted "numerous atrocities" against Sunni civilians by pro-government militias and security forces.
11Al-Sadr, who comes from a line of influential Shiite clerics, heads the militia previously known as the Mahdi Army, a paramilitary force he formed in 2003 in a show of resistance against the U.S.-led occupation of Iraq.
12Last month, he announced that he would withdraw the Peace Brigade from fighting in a "show of goodwill" following accusations that the militias were responsible for battlefield atrocities.
13Al-Mousawi said the Peace Brigade are looking to fight alongside Tikrit's Sunni tribes to liberate the city, saying: "It's them who should lead this fight, since Tikrit is where their homes and families are."
1"This might be one of the best films we've ever played at South by Southwest."
2High praise indeed coming from the SXSW festival's senior programmer, Jarod Neece, ahead of the Saturday night North American premiere of "Ex Machina."
3But the directing debut of longtime screenwriter Alex Garland more than lived up to it as a sci-fi chamber drama that manages to be tactile, cerebral and gripping, crammed with big ideas while also feeling like an engagingly twisting thriller.
4It is smart cinema, smartly done.
5Or as actor Oscar Isaac put in in the post-screening Q&A, "It's pretty cool when the action set pieces of a movie are two people torturing themselves with their brains."
6As the film opens, Caleb (Domhnall Gleeson), a coder at a large tech company, is summoned to the remote estate of the company's enigmatic CEO, Nathan (Isaac).
7There Caleb is introduced to Ava (Alicia Vikander), a robot designed and built by Nathan.
8Caleb is meant to give Ava an extended series of tests to see if her artificial intelligence can pass for human consciousness.
9This places the three of them in a battle of wits and wills.
10The film played very well to the room Saturday night, with frequent bursts of laughter and even a round of applause following a dance break by Isaac.
11After the screening, Garland, Isaac and Gleeson came out to huge applause from the audience.
12Perhaps because of the concurrent interactive conference occurring at SXSW and the general emphasis on technology at the festival, the film seemed to hit home with this audience in particular, which wanted to engage with the larger philosophical questions posed by the film and the minutia of how it was accomplished.
13"I'd say I'm on the robot's side in this movie," said Garland, whose previous screenplays, including "28 Days Later," "Sunshine," "Never Let Me Go" and "Dredd," have made him something of a go-to figure for dystopian speculative fiction.
14"My suspicions are with the humans, really," he added later.
15"I'm very sort of suspicious and worried about people. I don't feel that way about robots. I think they might be more reasonable than we are."
16Garland repeatedly downplayed any grand vision on his part in his first time out as director.
17Rather he spotlighted the work of collaborators who include cinematographer Rob Hardy, concept artist Jock and composers Geoff Barrow and Ben Salisbury.
18"This is a bunch of filmmakers," he said.
19"I promise you that's how I see it."
20(Barrow, a member of the group Portishead, and Salisbury also were DJs at the film's after-party, and their set was composed of film scores and songs from movies.)
21As for the seamless visual effects work involved in turning Vikander into a lithe robot with many exposed parts, Garland credited the effects team led by Andrew Whitehurst.
22He said they "connected to this film on a sort of emotional level quite early on, and they just said, we're going to make this work. Shoot it how you want it."
23Rob Hardy, the director of photography, "obviously shot it incredibly beautifully, and the VFX guys never got in the way, never slowed anything up," Garland said.
24The film opens in the U.S. next month.
25Though some may find unsettling the direction it points for the future of people, technology and artificial intelligence, Garland does not (and he included a slight dig at the recent "Interstellar" in saying so).
26"The reason I like the idea of A.I.'s is they're an extension of us," Garland said.
27"There's a long future attached to them, longer than ours. We have a very finite future. We're going to die on this rock. There's not going to be a wormhole off the side of Saturn. It's not going to be there. It sucks, but it's true."
