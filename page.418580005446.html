Yellen, Draghi upbeat despite tumult in Greece, China

1Thursday, July 16, 2015 | 6:00 p.m.
2The Chinese stock market is experiencing a terrifying freefall, and Greece is teetering on the brink of financial collapse.
3But the top central bankers in the United States and Europe see no reason for panic.
4European Central Bank chief Mario Draghi told reporters Thursday that the European economy is showing modest signs of improvement.
5And in congressional testimony this week, Federal Reserve Chair Janet Yellen said that the U.S. economy has proven resilient so far in the face tumultuous world events.
6The Fed, she said, is still likely to raise short-term interest rates later this year.
7Yellen said low oil prices and an improving job market are likely to boost the U.S. economy and easy money policies by the world's central banks will help the global economy.
8In a report Thursday, chief economist Nariman Behravesh and senior research director Sara Johnson of IHS predicted the world economy will grow 2.6 percent this year and 3.3 percent in 2016.
9"The recent political turmoil in Europe and financial convulsions in China will not do serious damage to global growth_unless they get a lot worse," they wrote.
10After peaking June 12, Chinese stocks have plunged around 30 percent, raising more doubts about China's decelerating economy.
11And Greece is locked in contentious negotiations with its creditors that have put its future in the eurozone in jeopardy.
12Yellen said this week that the Fed might reconsider a 2015 rate hike if the tumult in China or Greece threatened to spread.
13But for now, the Fed is on track this year to raise the short-term rate it controls from near zero, where it's been since December 2008.
14The American economy has been improving since getting off to a terrible start this year.
15After shrinking at an annual rate of 0.2 percent from January through April - a dip blamed largely on bad winter weather and other temporary factors - the U.S. economy is picking up momentum.
16It is expected to register a modest 2 percent to 2.5 percent growth for the year.
17In the labor market, employers have added more than 2.9 million jobs the past year.
18Unemployment tumbled last month to a seven-year low 5.3 percent.
19Still, some are cautious about the U.S. economic outlook.
20International Monetary Fund chief Christine Lagarde has urged the Fed to delay a rate increase until 2016, arguing that the risk that a premature hike will damage growth outweighs the risk that a tardy hike will unleash inflation or swell a dangerous bubble in the price of stocks, real estate and other assets.
21Yellen herself remains worried that American wage growth is subpar.
22Europe continues an agonizing economic recovery.
23The collective economy of the 19 countries that use the euro currency grew modestly from January through March.
24Prices across the eurozone rose in June for the third straight month, easing fears that the region was at risk of sinking into a destructive deflationary spiral.
25Draghi took part of the credit, noting that the ECB's 60 billion euros in monthly bond purchases have supported eurozone growth.
26Draghi repeated his pledge to do everything within his power to prevent fallout from the Greek crisis from damaging the broader European economy.
27And he did his part for Greece, too: The ECB on Thursday agreed to increase financial support for Greece's banks, which have been closed since June 29 and were in danger of collapsing entirely.
