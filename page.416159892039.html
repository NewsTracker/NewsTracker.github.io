9CHICAGO (AP) - Hillary Rodham Clinton's presidential campaign will center on boosting economic security for the middle class and expanding opportunities for working families, while casting the former senator and secretary of state as a "tenacious fighter" able to get results, two senior advisers said Saturday.
10They provided the first preview of the message that Clinton planned to convey when she launches her long-anticipated campaign on Sunday with an online video.
11Until now, the former first lady has offered only hints of what would drive her if she were to run a second time for the White House.
12The strategy described by Clinton's advisers has echoes of President Barack Obama's 2012 re-election campaign.
13He framed the choice for voters as between Democrats focused on the middle class and Republicans wanting to protect the wealthy and return to policies that led to the 2008 economic collapse.
14The advisers said Clinton will argue that voters have a similar choice in 2016.
15Clinton also intends to sell herself as being able to work with Congress, businesses and world leaders.
16That approach could be perceived as a critique of Obama, Clinton's rival for the nomination in 2008.
17He has largely been unable to fulfill his pledge to end Washington's intense partisanship and found much of his presidency stymied by gridlock with Congress.
18The Clinton advisers spoke on condition of anonymity in order to discuss her plans ahead of Sunday's announcement.
19People familiar with the plans say Clinton will travel to Iowa and other early-voting states to hold small events with residents in the days after the video's release.
20In New York on Saturday, at the final event put on by "Ready for Hillary," a group not connected with her campaign that's worked for the past few years to stoke excitement for it, enthusiastic supporters joined elected officials and local party leaders to celebrate the launch to come.
21"After she left the State Department she could have slipped into grandmother-hood, but people want to call her back into public service," said Jarret Berg, 29, a Democratic staffer in the New York legislature.
22"It's time for her."
23Clinton is not expected to roll out detailed policy positions in the first weeks of her campaign.
24Advisers said she planned to talk about ways families can increase take-home pay, the importance of expanding early childhood education and making higher education more affordable.
25It's not yet clear whether that will include a noticeable break with Obama on economic policy.
26The GOP has hammered Obama's approach as anti-business and insufficient in the wake of the recession.
27The White House says the economy has improved significantly in recent years.
28Clinton will enter the race as the overwhelming favorite for her party's nomination.
29Still, her team has said her early strategy is designed to avoid appearing to take that nomination for granted.
30The early events will include discussions at colleges, day care centers and private homes, and stops at coffee shops and diners.
31After about a month of such events, Clinton will give a broader speech outlining more specifics about her rationale for running.
32Clinton's husband, Bill, and daughter, Chelsea, are unlikely to appear at her early events.
33Bill Clinton, the former two-term president, said recently that he wanted to play a role as a "backstage adviser" in his wife's campaign.
34Advisers said Bill Clinton has been engaged with his wife in some of the policy discussions leading up to this weekend's rollout.
35To prepare for the campaign, Clinton has spent months meeting with economic policy experts, including Heather Boushey, whose research focuses on inequality, and Teresa Ghilarducci, a labor economist and retirement policy expert.
36The policy development process has been overseen by aides Jake Sullivan and Dan Schwerin.
37In the days before her announcement, Clinton has been holding lengthy meetings with her staff, sometimes joining them at her crowded personal offices in midtown Manhattan and other times participating by phone.
38She is expected to reach out to donors in the coming weeks, but does not plan to headline many fundraising events over the next month.
39Clinton's growing team of staffers began working Friday out of a new campaign headquarters in Brooklyn.
40They gathered Saturday to hear from campaign manager-in-waiting Robby Mook, who told them the campaign would value teamwork, diversity, discipline and humility.
41Mook distributed a memo titled "We are Hillary for America" that included several guiding principles, including a vow to work together and respect each other.
42The campaign, the memo said, "is not about Hillary Clinton and not about us - it's about the everyday Americans who are trying to build a better life for themselves and their families."
43The meeting was described by a Democratic official in attendance who spoke on condition of anonymity to describe a private strategy session.
44The memo was first reported by Politico.
45Associated Press writers Jonathan Lemire in New York and Ken Thomas in Washington contributed to this report.
