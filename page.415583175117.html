Rushing to a train? This app will tell you if you're going to make it

Yahoo shutting down China office in latest cost-cutting move

1A look at Yo!Train, the winner of a $10,000 prize in a contest to build NYC transit apps.
2Rushing to the Bergen Street subway station during a snowstorm a few months back, Brooklyn tech developer Duong Nguyen almost fell into the street as he checked his phone for train times and frantically considered a surface alternative.
3He muttered the common refrain: "There has to be a better way."
4Nguyen channeled that frustration into an invention, a mobile app called YoTrain!, which will alert users within about 200 feet of a subway station when the next train is coming - by voice and text alert, eliminating the need to navigate the phone.
5Today, Nguyn's invention won a $10,000 grand prize in the MTA and AT&T's App Quest Challenge, an annual contest to build new commuter tools using city data.
6Nguyen won in a category designated for apps that mean to help transit users with disabilities, but thinks there's a broad market for his tool.
7"Having it all hands free and have it spoken to you makes everyone's lives a little bit better," he said.
8Now in its third year, the MTA and AT&T app challenge is meant to find creative ways to use the transit system's publicly available system data to make lives easier.
9The new apps, mostly still in beta testing and not yet available for public download, come just days after we were reminded of our especially painful commutes.
10Nguyen encountered difficulties in testing their app - highly ironic challenges that underscore the evolving, patchwork nature of Internet connectivity in the subway system.
11Some stations and lines enjoy excellent WiFi and cellular signals and real-time train arrival data.
12Others are still digital black holes, like Nguyen's home station, Bergen Street.
13YoTrain!'s full features don't work without real-time arrival data the MTA has on the numbered lines and the L line.
