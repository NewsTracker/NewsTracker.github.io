'Red Army' is a tribute to the Soviet soul on ice

Ukraine, pro-Russian rebels agree to cease-fire

1If you want to understand ice hockey, the USSR, the power of Western cash, Russians, the dictatorial nature of the KGB, the authoritarian NHL and puck shooting as Marxist propaganda - while being entertained every step of the way with terrific humor - check out "Red Army."
2Gabe Polsky's from-Russia-with-love documentary is an utter delight, the rise-and-fall-and-rise saga of five star athletes of Soviet hockey from the 1970s to the present.
3The "Ice Brotherhood" quintet played as a remarkably creative unit.
4They won universal admiration for their graceful skating and prolific scoring, but scant understanding.
5From the end of World War II until the collapse of the Soviet Union, Russians were viewed in the West not only as villains but as alien life-forms.
6When the U.S. hockey team upset the Soviets in the 1980 Olympics "Miracle on Ice," it was cause for national celebration.
7As youthful Ronald Reagan says in a black-and-white Cold War introduction at the beginning of the film, "How we meet the Communist challenge depends on you."
8"Red Army" fills those gaps, telling a side of the story few hockey fans have encountered, balancing scenes of the team's breathtaking athletics with their touching, sometimes absurdly funny lives.
9This is the sort of lighthearted film that presents a Soviet anthem with an animated image of a bouncing hammer and sickle.
10Polsky, who played hockey while growing up in Chicago and as an undergraduate at Yale, created the film with extensive filming in Russia.
11Telling the story in authentic human rather than strictly political terms, he assembles a fine cast of sports stars and Cold War warhorses, all presented as memorable individuals.
12The central character is team captain Viacheslav (Slava) Fetisov.
13A legendary defenseman, he's also a great (though sometimes grumpy) interview subject.
14If Polsky's inquiries go on too long, Fetisov, now 56, raises his middle finger during the interview.
15When he opens up, however, he's dazzling.
16He recalls his devotion to the sport and the Soviet national team since childhood, and the path that made him the unit's youngest captain.
17Through his memories and remarkable archival footage we meet the squad's fatherly mentor, Anatoly Tarasov, coaching his proteges through an inspired combination of chess and Bolshoi-quality ballet.
18He taught hockey as a sophisticated passing game, leading the team to years of East-West success, outperforming squads of American and Canadian all-stars.
19A lovable, burly old bear of a man, he helped his team work through training camps worthy of a "Rocky" film, sweating right alongside them.
20Then their aging tutor was replaced by a KGB-chosen slave driver.
21Viktor Tikhonov was a martinet whose browbeating control made the team despise him so much that Fetisov considered throwing enough games that their coach would be dismissed.
22If they needed a heart transplant, the team said, they'd want Tikhonov's since he'd never used it.
23Ostracized by Soviet hockey's chain of command in the late 1980s, Fetisov chose to move West and play pro.
24He entered the NHL in 1989, playing 10 seasons with the New Jersey Devils and Detroit Red Wings.
25He helped recruit other Russians to the Wings, creating thrilling teams that earned back-to-back Stanley Cups in 1997 and 1998, but his American career became as troubled as his first.
26His wife and daughter never felt accepted abroad and yearned to go home.
27Ultimately he returned to Russia.
28Having won every medal there is in hockey, he began an equally successful political chapter.
29His surprising climax resembles a fairy-tale happy ending, except that it was designed by Vladimir Putin.
30From its zippy opening to the hilariously unexpected finale, "Red Army" never misses a beat.
31It's a miracle on ice all its own.
