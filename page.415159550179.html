Tamir Rice case: Cleveland mayor apologises after city claims 12-year-old to blame for his own police shooting death

1The video, though silent and impersonal, remains chilling.
2On a cold autumn morning in Cleveland a boy ambles around a bandstand in a park playing with a pellet gun.
3Suddenly a police car appears, and without even leaving the driver's seat an officer shoots the boy, 12-year-old Tamir Rice in the stomach and torso.
4Tamir Rice was shot and killed after a caller reported he was pointing a gun at people.
5Other details of this killing, ones not clear on the security camera footage, are even more horrific.
6As Tamir lay dying his 14-year-old sister who had been playing nearby, and who ran to his aid, was thrown to the ground police, cuffed, then locked in the police car.
7Then she watched as her brother kicked and died in the snow.
8The officers rendered no aid for four minutes.
9It somehow compounds the tragedy to know that the police had been called to the scene by a man who is also visible on the footage - sitting in the bandstand - who must have known that the boy posed no threat, yet who nonetheless called and reported male with a pistol that was "probably fake".
10Twice he was asked if the subject of his report was black or white.
11And to know that the officer who pulled the trigger, Timothy Loehmann, had been forced out of a nearby local police department for being unable to follow "basic functions as instructed" and for his "dismal failure" in weapons handling.
12Killed: 12-year-old Tamir Rice.
13Last November's shooting shattered the Rice family and added to the intensity of nation-wide protests over winter against police killings of unarmed African-Americans.
14Now the city of Cleveland's response to a federal lawsuit bought against it by the Rice family has reopened the unhealed wounds.
15According to the city's lawyers, the shooting was "directly and proximately caused by [Tamir and his family's] own acts. . .," and added that Tamir caused his own death "by the failure. . . to exercise due care to avoid injury."
16The Rice family is appalled.
17"What they said is incredulous [sic] at best. It's unbelievable," said Walter Madison, one of the Rice family's attorneys, during an interview with The Washington Post on Monday in which he said the city's response was further evidence of police arrogance.
18"There are a number of things that we in society don't allow 12-year-olds to do. We don't allow them to vote, we don't allow them to drink. In court we don't try them as adults. They don't have the capacity to understand the consequences of their actions."
19Later on Monday Cleveland's mayor Frank Jackson called press conference to apologise to the Rice family and to Cleveland's residents, "for our poor use of words and our insensitivity" in the filing.
20"In an attempt to protect all of our defences, we used words and we phrased things in such a way that was very insensitive," he said.
21The document was "very insensitive to tragedy in general, the family and the victim in particular," and would be amended.
22The response comes as the federal Justice Department nears completion of an investigation into allegedly discriminatory behaviour by the Ferguson Police Department, which has been the subject of protests since the shooting of Michael Brown.
23Officials who have been briefed on the report have told the New York Times that it will criticise the department for disproportionately ticketing and arresting African-Americans and using the proceeds of the fines to balance the city's budget.
24The practice created years of racial animosity leading up to the shooting and the subsequent protests and riots.
25The report charts the frustration of the African-American community that the police department remains overwhelmingly white in a predominantly African-American community.
