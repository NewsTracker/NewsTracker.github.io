Jeb Bush and Hillary Clinton support bankruptcy rights for Puerto Rico

1Both 2016 presidential candidates support giving Puerto Rico the same legal bankruptcy rights that states have.
2Puerto Rico's governor has been lobbying hard for that right.
3There's a bill in Congress to give Puerto Rico what it wants, but it has no co-sponsors.
4That's why Bush and Clinton's backing could be key.
5"I think that Puerto Rico ought to be treated as other states are treated as it relates to restructuring," Bush said during a visit to the island in late April.
6Other Republican candidates have largely stayed silent on the issue thus far.
7On Tuesday, Clinton jumped on board as well.
8"Congress should provide Puerto Rico the same authority that states already have to enable severely distressed government entities, including municipalities and public corporations, to restructure their debts," she said in a statement on her campaign website.
9Former Maryland governor Martin O'Malley has been a vocal supporter as well.
10What it means: To be clear, no state can file for bankruptcy.
11But the 50 states do have the right to allow municipalities -- cities, towns and other local government entities -- within their states to file for bankruptcy.
12Puerto Rican municipalities want to be able to file for what is known as Chapter 9 bankruptcy like Detroit and Vallejo, California did.
13The benefit of bankruptcy is that is allows a broke government to shed some of its debt -- if a judge approve a municipality's game plan.
14Will Congress act?
15The bill in Congress would give Puerto Rico the same rights that states have under Chapter 9.
16So far, the bill's only supporter in Congress is Puerto Rico's representative, who get to vote.
17It might seem like a small issue in Washington, but it's a big issue for financial markets.
18Puerto Rico has a tiny population -- about the size of Connecticut -- but it has a debt the size of a big state like New York.
19Over 20% of U.S. bond funds hold Puerto Rican debt.
20The island doesn't have enough money to pay back its creditors.
21Some have even taken to calling it "America's Greece."
22Governor Alejandro Garcia Padilla says Puerto Rico is in a "death spiral."
23A lot of the trouble stems from Puerto Rico's power company, known as PREPA.
24It alone has $9 billion.
25It would be a game changer if PREPA could declare bankruptcy.
26Related: Who owns Puerto Rico's debt?
27Why bankruptcy?
28"Chapter 9 is really the only way to impose some sort of restructuring on all the bondholders," says David Tawil, a lawyer and president of Maglan Capital.
29Bond holders don't want Puerto Rico to get that because it opens the possibility that they would be forced to take a loss on their bond investment if a bankruptcy judge sides with the island.
30But the proposal has little currency in Washington.
31"The bill that is in Congress has no co-sponsors and no support in Congress, although it does have a fair amount of support among bankruptcy experts," says Juliet Moringiello, a professor at Widener University School of Law and a former scholar of the American Bankruptcy Institute.
32Moringiello notes that Chapter 9 bankruptcy is rare, although the majority of filings have been public hospitals and utilities like PREPA, not cities.
33Where the candidates stand: Jeb Bush has been a vocal supporter of statehood for Puerto Rico -- if the island's citizens want that.
34"I think statehood is the best path, personally," Bush said in April.
35Since then, he has clarified that he thinks the island needs to make a number of changes to deal with its debt beyond just declaring bankruptcy, including reducing some benefits for workers.
36On the Democratic side, O'Malley was an early supporter of Puerto Rico getting Chapter 9 bankruptcy rights.
37Senator Bernie Sanders has voiced his support for Greece, but has yet to say much on Puerto Rico.
