Social Q?s: Advice on Being Left Out of Family Photos

Boko Haram attacks Nigeria town of Gamboru, kills 11: witnesses

1Eighteen years later, we are still together, but have decided not to marry.
2His aunt apologized to me ages ago.
3But my boyfriend's brother still insists that I not be included in family photos.
4My boyfriend stood up to him, and this brother is no longer welcome in our home.
5But when we attend family functions, he acts friendly to me.
6How should I handle this?
7Here's a theory: This may have nothing to do with you.
8You didn't mention any spat or ill will with the brother.
9Perhaps he acts all friendly toward you because he likes you.
10And this Kodak drama is really just one brother tweaking another.
11Trust me: I have two.
12And none of our partners have ever signified as strongly as the ancient rivalries of 30-year-old footraces or urinating-the-farthest contests.
13(No offense, in-laws.)
14Encourage the brothers to spend time together, explicitly not trying to resolve this petty feud.
15Perhaps a woodsy hike or movie date would restore some fraternal feeling.
16As for your behavior, you can never go wrong taking the high road, even though this guy has hurt your feelings.
17Acting politely would only heighten his jerk-itude.
18Though in (partial) defense of your aunt's rule, the best family portrait I have includes a guy I dated for six minutes in 1992.
19Airbrush, anyone?
20In the past year, I attended the funerals of two friends' parents and made generous contributions to suggested charities in their honor.
21Now, my beloved father has died.
22(We provided envelopes for my father's charity at his wake.)
23The first friend attended the wake and funeral and sent a lovely card.
24The second sent a nice note, period.
25I would like to salvage these friendships, but I believe that they are dissing my dad by not making a contribution.
26Should I discuss this with them?
27I am sorry for your loss.
28Please do nothing, for now.
29I am pretty sure that when the dust settles, and you begin feeling a little better, you will see that these friends did no damage that requires salvaging.
30They both sent their condolences.
31You may have some free-floating anger now (mixed in with your grief), and this "suggested donation" business may be convenient flypaper.
32Wait a few months.
33If you are still upset, ask yourself: Is playing tit-for-tat over charitable contributions the best way to honor my father?
34I bet he would want you to let this go.
35And gifts are always optional.
36Our son is getting married.
37We invited his fiancee's parents (who were visiting from out of town) to our home for dinner.
38We had a good time, and they reciprocated by inviting us out to eat.
39At the table, the fiancee's father announced that they like to share meals at restaurants, and without asking what we would like, he ordered the entire meal.
40(If it matters, we didn't care for anything he ordered. Still, we went along with him.)
41My wife got over this quickly, but I find it offensive.
42How can we politely put our foot down next time?
43Question for you: Did you ask your in-laws-to-be whether they liked coq au vin before you served them dinner at your place?
44(I know, who doesn't? But still.)
45And I assume they picked up the tab at the reciprocation meal, which gives them a wee bit more leeway as hosts.
46They probably got carried away, ordering family style to symbolize the union of two great clans.
47(Or they may be a gigantic control freaks.)
48Next time, simply ask: "What are you thinking of ordering? I'd love to try the cassoulet."
49Hard to imagine anyone taking offense at that.
50And if sharing food is not your thing (some people hate it), just say, "How about communal starters, then ordering our own main courses?"
51Finding a compromise here seems the least you can do for you son - particularly since he may never order food for himself again.
