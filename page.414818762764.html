Kosovans risk perils of roads, forests and criminals in chase for better life in EU

Ukraine truce fades as sides refuse to withdraw big guns

1The name of this restaurant and hotel complex in Palic, northern Serbia, resembles "Freedom Villa" in Albanian - a nod to its clientele's hopes for a better future outside of their impoverished and isolated home country.
2Until last week, when Serbian authorities finally shut it down, Vila Lira had connected migrants and people smugglers who promised to get them across the nearby border to Hungary illegally.
3For 200 ( 148) apiece, they bought their entry into the EU's Schengen area.
4On one of the days in the week before it closed, 70 Kosovan men, women and children were gathered outside.
5A thirtysomething man with blond hair emerged from inside with a beer in hand.
6"Didn't I tell you not to stand out there on the roadside? I don't want you to be seen outside. Get inside now," he snapped at the migrants in colloquial Kosovan Albanian.
7"We know people who have been tricked by the owner of Vila Lira," said a Kosovan-Albanian man in his 20s called Fitim, from Broja in central Kosovo, standing with a friend on the side of the road in the muddy outskirts of Palic.
8"After taking everyone's money he shops some to the Serbian police, so they have to pay them too. We are going to walk to Hungary on our own at dusk - we are not paying any trafficker."
9As Kosovo marks the seventh anniversary since it declared independence from Serbia on Tuesday, a sudden exodus is indicative of a country that has endured a difficult birth.
10Kosovan police say about 25,000 people have crossed illegally from Kosovo into Hungary since September to seek a new life in the EU.
11According to the Kosovo Intelligence Agency, that number is 50,000.
12Other estimates run as high as 100,000.
13In one of Europe's poorest regions, it is clear - from the nightly crowds at Pristina's bus station, to the well-worn paths through the forests of Vojvodina and the packed trains in Hungary - that the number is substantial.
14The migrants usually cross into northern Serbia and then pay people smugglers to take them illegally across.
15By December, Kosovan citizens accounted for the largest share of people - 40% - detected illegally entering the EU's external borders, according to Frontex, which patrols the bloc's borders.
16Hungary received 13,000 asylum requests in January, mostly from Kosovans.
17But for the majority, their final objective is to reach Germany.
18For these migrants, leaving is a big gamble.
19Kosovans have sold off land, homes and cars, and given up steady jobs in some cases, just as many others like them have done during the past 60 years.
20The journey is dangerous, and they stand a good chance of being caught by authorities.
21Most of those who are caught try to seek asylum, which only serves to buy time, in some cases in detention centres.
22Yet it seems for many, any shot at escaping is worth taking.
23Earlier this month in Lluge, a village in Podujeve, Zahide Konushevci was putting ski gloves on her three children, Nita, Rinesa and Gentrit, preparing them for the long crossing across the cold forest on the Serbia-Hungary border.
24"It will be tough on kids to cross that border, walk on foot in this winter weather, but it is better to suffer hardship for a few days rather than suffer hardship for the rest of your life," their father, Isa Konushevci, said.
25About 200 people have left this village of 2,000 people in the past four months.
26Isa, along with 100 others, used to work at a factory that produced sunflower oil.
27When business slowed, the company cut their staff back to 30 people.
28Last week, the Konushevci family was one of those struggling through the crowds at the bus station in Pristina.
29In recent weeks, the station has been packed with would-be migrants and their families, wrapped in goodbyes.
30Additional bus routes, have been added to meet demand.
31When the Konushevcis eventually found their bus, they had to stand because it was so crowded.
32"I don't know exactly where I am heading but I am dreaming of a place where my children will have proper education and where they won't need connections to get a job once they graduate and where neither of us need to bribe the doctor if we need health services," Isa said on board the bus.
33"The government did not prove they can provide us with any of this, and I never thought I would be here 15 years after the war."
34The family's journey would not be an easy one; Serbian police arrested them a few days later in Subotica, near the Hungarian border.
35They were released after paying a fine, according to Isa's brother, Gazmend.
36"They are now in Budapest and they told me they suffered tremendously on the road," he said.
37It is not known what exactly has caused the dramatic increase in migration from Kosovo in recent months, though many point to the country's economy.
38Unemployment has stayed between 40% - 50% for a decade, despite average annual economic growth of 4%.
39"People do not die of hunger in Kosovo and they are not leaving because of lack of food on the table," says Armend Malazogu, who runs Frutomania, a company that employs 70 people all year round and 300 seasonal workers making fruit juices.
40Malazogu said he knew of two of employees who had left for the EU.
41"They are leaving because the authorities have not been able to provide good education and good health for them - so all in all, they leave because they want their kids to achieve more than their parents in terms of education and because they want to be served by a health system that does not let them down."
42The surge in migration has not failed to attract attention in Kosovo and the EU.
43This week, Germany said it would send 20 police officers to the border between Hungary and Serbia to help control the flood.
44Frontex forces are also expected to be strengthened there.
45Kosovo's president Atifete Jahjaga, a former policewoman, has pleaded with citizens to stay put.
46"We know that Kosovo is not in the condition which we wanted it to be today but emigration is not the solution," she said in a town hall meeting in Ferizaj earlier this month.
47Agron Demi of the GAP Institute, a Pristina-based economic thinktank, disagrees, saying history has told many Kosovans that migration can bring significant economic benefits.
48The diaspora numbers in the hundreds of thousands and grows each year, with the largest number in Germany and Switzerland.
49They typically send home more than a 500m each year - an important driver of the home economy.
50"Considering that a large part of Kosovo's population currently lives on remittances - money sent back by family members living and working abroad in places like Germany - Kosovans who are sick of living like that just want to try making money for themselves," he said.
51The odds are stacked against them, however.
52In Germany, about 99% of asylum applications from Kosovans were rejected last year and in January the approval rate was reported to have dropped even lower to 0.3%.
53"The hope of a better life in Germany is not a justified one," Manfred Schmidt, president of Germany's Federal Office for Migration and Refugees told Deutsche Welle.
54"We've always been told that the vast majority of the illegal migrants have no status rights and will be sent back," Kovoso's interior minister, Skender Hyseni, said.
55"I hope that happens quickly."
1Fighting has eased in many areas since a ceasefire came into force on Sunday, but the rebels have refused to halt attacks on a town where Ukrainian forces are encircled and Kiev says it will not pull back its big guns until the truce holds.
2Monitors from the OSCE security group were expected to try to reach the besieged town, Debaltseve, after Germany said it had agreed steps with the leaders of Russia and Ukraine to ensure the observers had "free access" in the east.
3But a call by Berlin for the withdrawal of heavy weaponry to start seemed to have fallen on deaf ears, though officials from Russia, Ukraine, the OSCE and the rebels were expected to discuss implementation of the peace deal by phone.
4"We do not have the right (to stop fighting for Debaltseve). It's even a moral thing. It's internal territory," Denis Pushilin, a senior separatist representative, told Reuters in the rebel stronghold of Donetsk.
5"We have to respond to fire, to work on destroying the enemy's fighting positions."
6Asked about plans to carry out the agreement to withdraw big guns, he said: "We are ready at any time, we have everything ready for a mutual withdrawal. We will not do anything unilaterally. That would make our soldiers targets."
7Ukraine's military reiterated that its forces could also not start withdrawing big weapons such as heavy artillery, as set out in the deal reached at marathon talks brokered by France and Germany in the Belarussian capital Minsk last Thursday.
8"The pull-back can happen only on fulfilment of the first point of the Minsk agreements -- the ceasefire. In the last 24 hours there has been firing so there is no ceasefire and so there is no precondition for a pull-back of heavy weapons either," military spokesman Andriy Lysenko said.
9Asked by Reuters whether the pull-back could start without a ceasefire at Debaltseve, a strategic railway junction, he said: "The Minsk agreements talk about a general ceasefire. So my answer is 'No, not possible'."
10Hopes that Thursday's deal will end a conflict that has killed more than 5,000 people have been dampened by the collapse of an earlier truce last month.
11The European Union kept pressure on Russia and the rebels by announcing a new list of separatists and Russians targeted with sanctions on Monday.
12Moscow promised an "adequate" response.
13The United States said it was "gravely concerned" by the fighting in and around Debaltseve and that it was closely monitoring reports of a new column of Russian military equipment moving toward the region.
14"These aggressive actions and statements by the Russia-backed separatists threaten the most recent ceasefire," U.S. State Department spokeswoman Jen Psaki said in a statement.
15"We call on Russia and the separatists it backs to halt all attacks immediately."
16Russia denies sending arms or troops to back the rebels in mainly Russian-speaking areas of east Ukraine despite what Kiev and the West say is incontrovertible proof.
17The separatists said soon after the ceasefire came into effect they had no intention of observing it at Debaltseve, where they have been advancing since January and now have a Ukrainian unit all but encircled.
18The OSCE said on Sunday the rebels had refused to allow its monitors into Debaltseve after the truce took effect.
19Fighting began in east Ukraine after the overthrow of a Moscow-backed president in Kiev a year ago and Russia's annexation of the Crimea peninsula a month later.
20The West fears Putin, who has called parts of Ukraine "New Russia," wants the conflict to fester for years so that Kiev cannot control east Ukraine and is prevented from joining NATO, while Russia can retain influence there.
21Moscow accuses the West of waging a proxy war in Ukraine to seek "regime change" in Moscow and "contain" Russia.
22Western countries say they reserve the option of expanding economic sanctions on Moscow over the crisis, hoping a growing financial crisis in Russia will persuade Putin to use his influence with the rebels to stop the fighting.
23Some analysts say Putin is banking on Ukraine's economy collapsing much faster than Russia's and on Russians remaining united behind him over the seizure of Crimea, which has boosted his popularity ratings at home.
