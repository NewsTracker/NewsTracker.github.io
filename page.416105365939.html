Facebook seeks to halt privacy case in Austria

1The first day of hearings began with a four-hour session in which Facebook's lawyers tried to convince the judge not to admit the suit brought by law student Max Schrems.
2The 27-year-old is claiming 500 in damages for each user.
3The suit is the latest of several legal challenges in Europe and the United States to Facebook for the way it shares users' personal data with businesses or governments.
4Mr Schrems has said this may become a test case for European data protection laws.
5"The lawsuit is inadmissible on the procedural level - the court is not responsible," Facebook's lawyer Nikolaus Pitkowitz told the judge.
6Mr Schrems accused Facebook of engaging in delaying tactics.
7The judge said a written decision on whether the court can handle the suit will come before the summer.
8In the first hearing, attorneys for Mr Schrems and Facebook battled on technical grounds about whether the student has the status of a private Facebook consumer and if the 25,000 plaintiffs are legally allowed to confer their rights on him.
9Mr Schrems is claiming damages for alleged data violations by Facebook, including by aiding the US National Security Agency in running its PRISM programme, which mined the personal data of Facebook users.
10"I think we can heighten data protection with this lawsuit," Mr Schrems' lawyer Wolfram Proksch told reporters after the session.
11Facebook's lawyers did not address the details of the privacy concerns mentioned in his suit and declined to comment further outside of the court room.
12A specialist financier will bear the legal costs if Mr Schrems loses the case and will take 20% of the damages if he wins, meaning users can join the case at no financial risk.
