When heavyweights like Apple, Microsoft, and Exxon move, the market reacts

1NEW YORK - At $654 billion, Apple is the stock market's heavyweight.
2Like a handful of other "big cap" companies such as Microsoft and Exxon Mobil, the iPhone maker's market value means that when its shares move, so can the market.
3And these are the companies that influence your stock portfolio.
4"The combination of Apple's size and price moves, results in an enormous impact on indices," says Howard Silverblatt, a senior index analyst for S&P Dow Jones Indices.
5Lately, Apple's influence has been a drag.
6Since closing at a record $133 on February 23, Apple has slumped to $115.40, wiping $115 billion off the value of its stock.
7Between then and Wednesday's close, the Standard & Poor's 500, the most widely-tracked stock index, was down 0.5 percent.
8Without Apple, the decline would have been a gain of 0.1 percent, according to S&P Dow Jones Indices, the company behind the S&P 500 index and the Dow Jones industrial average.
9Of course the opposite is also true, when Apple's shares rise, the market can get a lift.
10Either way, for every $1 change in Apple stock, the S&P 500 rises or falls 0.6526 points, says Silverblatt.
11The impact of the technology giant is so big that S&P Dow Jones Indices has created a technology index that excludes Apple.
12That allows investors to separate what's going on with tech stocks from what's going on with Apple because, while they're similar, the two might not be the same.
13For example, the S&P 500 information technology sector is down 1.3 percent since Apple's record close.
14Take out Apple and the group has actually gained 1.8 percent.
15History suggests that companies as dominant as Apple don't tend to stay top dog for long.
16Competitors emerge, new products roll out, and growth can slow.
17Exxon Mobil, which Apple surpassed to become the biggest publicly-traded company in August 2011, has slumped over the last year along with the price of oil.
18To be sure, Exxon Mobil is still big, with a market value, or capitalization, of $322 billion.
19But it has slipped to third in the ranking of S&P heavyweights, behind Microsoft.
20In the early 1980s, computer company IBM was the stock market's big gun.
21Personal computers were still a new concept and IBM was one of the industry's dominant players.
22At the end of 1985, the company's stock made up 6.4 percent of the S&P 500's total value, much bigger than Apple's current 3.6 percent.
23Since then, the company has had to grapple with fierce competition from more nimble rivals.
24Like Exxon, Big Blue is still powerful, but it now ranks as the 28th largest in the S&P 500, making up 0.8 percent of the index.
