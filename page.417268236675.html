Greece says wants to make debt payments but needs aid urgently

1ATHENS (Reuters) - Greece intends to make good on its debt obligations but needs aid urgently to be able to do so, the government said on Monday, after several senior officials insisted Athens had no money to pay a loan installment falling due next week.
2Shut out of bond markets and with bailout aid locked, Greece is running out of cash to pay its bills.
3It must repay four loans totaling 1.6 billion euros ($1.76 billion) to the International Monetary Fund next month, starting with a 300 million euro payment on June 5 that is seen as the next crunch point for state coffers.
4Athens has the money to make monthly wage and pension payments this week, government spokesman Gabriel Sakellaridis told a news conference.
5But he was less direct when asked about the June 5 payment, reiterating the government's official stance that it has the responsibility to pay all its obligations.
6"Based on the liquidity problems that we have, there is an imperative need for us and the euro zone to have a deal as soon as possible," Sakellaridis said.
7"To the degree to which we are able to pay our obligations, we will pay our obligations. It's the government's responsibility to be in a position to pay all of these obligations."
8A growing list of senior members of the government -- the interior minister among them -- have openly said Athens does not have the means to pay the IMF, and would prioritize paying civil servants and pensioners instead.
9"We haven't got the money. We won't pay. It's that simple," Deputy Foreign Minister Nikos Chountis, who holds the European affairs portfolio, told Greek TV on Monday.
10Greek officials have frequently threatened to default in recent weeks, arguing the country does not have cash, which euro zone officials have dismissed as a negotiating tactic to raise pressure on creditors to disburse aid.
11Adding pressure on the government, prominent opposition lawmaker Dora Bakoyianni said the country risked facing capital controls to stem deposit outflows if it did not reach a deal for aid with the government this week.
12Sakellaridis dismissed such "doomsday scenarios", saying there was "just no chance" of imposing capital controls.
13He also dismissed reports that the government would try to pay all its June obligations in one lump sum to the IMF.
14He voiced optimism about a deal by early June but acknowledged that agreement on value-added tax hikes, pension and labor reform and lower primary surplus targets had yet to be found in negotiations with EU and IMF lenders.
15Canadian financier and Fairfax Financial Holdings Chief Executive Prem Watsa met Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras on Monday and expressed confidence in the country's recovery and on a deal with lenders, even "at the last minute".
16Fairfax owns a 13.6 percent stake in the Greek lender Eurobank.
17"A deal is possible because it is the only logical solution and both sides need it," Watsa told Eurobank and other officials, according to a statement from the bank.
18Not accepting cuts to wages and pensions has been one of the government's so-called red lines in over four months of negotiations since taking power in January.
19Such cuts are a sensitive issue in a country where unemployment has soared and incomes have fallen during the six-year economic crisis.
20Sakellaridis denied reports that the government was considering an extension to its bailout program that expires in June or that the idea had been proposed to the government.
21Ahead of talks between technical teams from Greece and its creditors in the "Brussels Group" which resume on Tuesday, the country's negotiating team convened to discuss the progress of the negotiations with Tsipras late on Monday.
22VAT rates, pension reform as well fiscal targets remain open issues, a Greek government official said, and the two sides disagree over the size of the fiscal gap for this year - the lenders put it at 2 billion euros while Athens puts it at under 1 billion.
23On Sunday, the ruling Syriza party's central committee voted in favor of reaching a mutually beneficial solution with lenders.
24A proposal by the party's hard leftists stating that paying wages and pensions should be a priority versus external debt obligations if the liquidity crunch continues was rejected by 95 members, as many as 75 members voted in favor.
