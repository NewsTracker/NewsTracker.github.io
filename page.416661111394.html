Nepal quake could have been much worse: Here's why

1KATHMANDU, Nepal (AP) " The structural engineer strides through Kathmandu's old city, past buildings reduced to rubble, buildings whose facades are cracked in dozens of places, like the fractured shell of a hardboiled egg. But it's the many buildings that made it unscathed through the earthquake that amaze Kit Miyamoto.
2"It could have been so much worse," said Miyamoto, head of a global earthquake and structural engineering firm, who flew to Nepal soon after he heard about last weekend's 7.8- magnitude quake.
3He shakes his head, topped by a white hardhat.
4Before landing, he'd envisioned a flattened moonscape of dust and debris.
5He thought as many as 40,000 people could be dead.
6The danger, however, may not be over.
7Dozens of mostly small aftershocks have hit Nepal since the quake.
8A more powerful aftershock a bit closer to the capital could cause immense damage.
9"If a magnitude 6 or 6.5 quake happens within 20 kilometers of Kathmandu, it's going to be a nightmare," said Sandeep Donald Shah, a structural engineer with Miyamoto International, during the walk through Kathmandu.
10"The probability is pretty high that this may happen because we just had a (huge) earthquake, and the fault line has been activated."
11The general state of Kathmandu's buildings " with their ancient soot-and-exhaust-stained concrete, their uneven bricks, their drooping facades and crooked balconies " raises questions about how so many still stand after such a big quake.
12Remaining upright depended on a combination of factors, including age, size, building material and strength, location and the underlying soil.
13But the simplest explanation is that Kathmandu largely sits outside the danger zone of last week's quake.
14Because the epicenter was about 80 kilometers (50 miles) from the capital, the quake's power had partially dissipated by the time it got to Kathmandu, said Miyamoto, who is also a seismic safety commissioner for the state of California.
15Even so, some of Kathmandu's remaining buildings look "very bad, seismically speaking," with weak foundations and structures, Miyamoto said.
16They've also been "softened up" by the quake, making them more likely to collapse or be seriously damaged if another, closer quake hits.
17The region will likely see aftershocks for another year, including some big ones, Miyamoto said, but it's impossible to predict where or when they will occur.
18The two biggest aftershocks so far have been more than 60 kilometers (38 miles) from Kathmandu.
19A direct or even a near hit on Kathmandu by the April 25 quake would have meant a massive death toll.
20The Nepal quake released 16 times the energy of the 2010 Haiti earthquake, where death estimates ranged from 100,000 to 300,000, yet the death toll in Nepal now stands at more than 6,000.
21This is a huge loss of life, but far less than recent estimates that 100,000 people might die in Nepal's next major earthquake.
22Driving through the city, the juxtaposition of what crumbled and what survived is striking.
23At some big hotel compounds it's almost as if the quake never happened: honking geese wander over manicured lawns and foreign guests start their days with hot showers before lining up at brimming breakfast buffets, eyes locked on phones connected to Wi-Fi.
24Outside the gates, even many of those whose homes weren't ruined slept out in the open for days after the quake because of fears of aftershocks.
25In some closely packed quarters, there is spectacular damage, with tall buildings leaning against their neighbors like tipped dominoes.
26Many villages in the countryside, outside the capital, have been virtually flattened.
27Generally, the older and bigger the building, the worse off it fared.
28So the so-called old city, home to many of Kathmandu's precious world heritage buildings, is obliterated in places.
29All over the city, destroyed brick walls spill into the streets like ocean waves breaking on beaches.
30In much of Kathmandu, however, roads are choked with traffic, and businesses have begun to reopen.
31You can go blocks sometimes without seeing any obvious earthquake damage.
32"It's getting back to normal, but ... it still doesn't feel safe," said Prabhu Dutta, a 27-year-old banker in Kathmandu.
33He has started sleeping again inside his home, which has some cracks in the walls but is still standing.
34However, the dozens of aftershocks he has felt since the quake make him uneasy.
35Many people in Kathmandu have been leaving for the countryside because of fears of a big aftershock.
36In the final equation, buildings collapse, or stand, because of the power and length of a quake's shaking.
37The strength of the shaking depends on the magnitude of the earthquake, the distance from the epicenter, the depth of the earthquake " shallower quakes do more damage than deeper ones " and the type of soil, according to Susan Cutter, director of the Hazards and Vulnerability Research Institute at the University of South Carolina.
38While both the Haiti and Nepal quakes were shallow " 10 kilometers (6 miles) deep for Nepal, 13 kilometers (8 miles) for Haiti " the soil in Haiti made the shaking more severe and longer, Cutter said.
39Port-au-Prince was also much closer to the epicenter than Kathmandu " 25 kilometers (15 miles) rather than 80 kilometers (50 miles).
40Old or unreinforced masonry normally fares poorly in an earthquake, though much depends on the quality of the materials and the building methods, as well as on building codes and their enforcement.
41If Nepal is far below most Western nations in terms of construction quality and code enforcement, most experts also believe it is better than Haiti.
42Miyamoto, the structural engineer, called the damage in Nepal's capital, and the possibility that aftershocks could cause much more, a wakeup call.
43The government and outside nations, he said, should begin work to strengthen existing buildings and construct stronger new ones.
44But that may prove difficult for Nepal's leaders.
45After the quake, as he stood outside a multi-story home where emergency teams were pulling out the body of a 12-year-old girl, Transport Minister Tek Bahadur Garung said that while Nepal does issue building regulations and licenses, there's no monitoring or enforcement.
