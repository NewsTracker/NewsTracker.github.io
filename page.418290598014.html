4LONDON (AP) - The Greek people are voting on a future in which they face two painful prospects: the slow grind of years more of austerity cuts or the country's potentially catastrophic exit from the 19-nation eurozone.
5The question is whether their vote on Sunday can help them escape either.
6"Yes" to more budget cuts demanded by creditors in exchange for a financial aid package for the country?
7Or reject it in the hope it will not lead the country out of the euro?
8For the radical left-led Greek government, the proposals are unacceptable.
9It's urging a "no" vote and says that will have no impact on Greece's euro status.
10Proponents of a "yes" vote, including a parade of former prime ministers and the main opposition party, say backing the government will jeopardize Greece's place in the euro.
11Instead, they argue that by voting "yes" Greece would get a new deal quickly to shore up the economy.
12In fact, what might happen in each case is unclear.
13A "yes" vote backing the reform proposals creditors had made would likely see Greece turn immediately to talks on a new rescue package.
14Whether that leads to a swift deal that might allow Greece to reopen its banks and restore a semblance of normality is another question.
15The government has said it will respect the verdict.
16Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis has said he'll resign in the event of a "yes" vote and Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras has hinted as much.
17If the government does not collapse, it could try to build a new coalition with other parties, Varoufakis hinted.
18It's not clear, however, if that would involve new elections.
19That would take time and without financial assistance, Greece would surely go bankrupt.
20Greece is no longer in a bailout program since its previous package expired.
21So it would have to negotiate a new one with its creditors that involves more money for the government and the banks and new economic austerity measures.
22That is unlikely to be agreed on overnight, meaning the banking restrictions on money withdrawals and transfers may remain in place for longer than anticipated.
23Varoufakis says banks will reopen Tuesday whatever the referendum's outcome.
24That's unlikely to happen unless the European Central Bank agrees to increase the credit to Greek banks.
25An additional difficulty is that the International Monetary Fund has said it will not get involved in a third bailout unless it includes substantive debt relief for Greece.
26The Europeans, on the other hand, have ruled out debt relief until Greece makes its reforms.
27Despite the Greek government's assertion that a "no" vote will not lead to a euro exit, most people agree it would open up more uncertain outcomes, especially if the ECB halts its life-support measures to Greece's banks.
28A number of European politicians, including Jeroen Dijsselbloem, the top eurozone official, have said a "no" vote would jeopardize Greece's place in the euro.
29Others, such as the leaders of France and Italy, appear to be holding the door ajar for further talks.
30But investors are likely to believe that a "no" vote increases the chance of a Greek exit from the euro, or "Grexit."
31The word 'Grexit' has dominated the past months of negotiations on Greece.
32But the country will not return to the drachma - Greece's former currency - immediately.
33Rather, the risk increases the longer there is a deadlock in talks.
34Without a deal and without money, Greece will default on more of its debt repayments and will not be able to pay salaries or pensions.
35The banks will run dry, even with the cash withdrawal limits.
36In such a case, printing a new currency may be the only option available, which almost everyone thinks will be a short-term disaster for the Greek economy.
