MH17 anniversary: Vladimir Putin calls tribunal into the disaster 'counterproductive'

1Vladimir Putin has rejected proposals for an international tribunal into the MH17 air disaster, in the clearest sign yet that Russia may veto a resolution on the plans at the UN Security Council next week.
2In a telephone conversation with Mark Rutte, the Dutch prime minister, Mr Putin said that establishing an international tribunal to prosecute those behind the downing of Flight MH17 over Ukraine would be “counterproductive” and “premature”.
3"Vladimir Putin in detail explained the Russian position regarding the premature and counterproductive nature of an initiative by a number of countries, including the Netherlands, to establish an international tribunal to criminally prosecute individuals responsible for the destruction of the Malaysian airliner," the Kremlin said following the call.
4Malaysian Airlines Flight 17 was destroyed over eastern Ukraine on July 17 last year, killing all 298 people on board, including 10 Britons.
5The Netherlands, which lost 193 citizens in the disaster, is leading two separate international enquiries into the crash.
6The first, led by the Dutch Safety Board, is expected to report in October.
7The second, a criminal investigation by Australian, Belgian, Dutch, Malaysian, and Ukrainian detectives, is expected to run on at least to the end of the year and will produce a dossier outlining charges of murder and possibly war crimes.
8A Malaysian proposal for a tribunal, backed by all the countries involved in the joint criminal investigation, is expected to be debated at the United Nations Security council on Tuesday.
9But Russian diplomats have hinted they will veto the proposals, arguing that the question of a tribunal should be postponed until after the Dutch-led investigations have reported.
10The contents of the Safety Board report has not been made public, but CNN reported on Wednesday that a draft version blames separatists firing a surface-to-air missile, citing sources who had seen the document.
11An independent investigation by the Telegraph published on Thursday suggests separatists fired a Buk SA-11 missile from a position south of the rebel-held town of Snizhne, after mistaking MH17 for a Ukrainian jet.
12On Wednesday, the families of 18 victims filed a $900 million lawsuit in a Chicago court against Igor Girkin, the former Russian intelligence officer who was in command of separatist forces at the time, who they say orchestrated the attack.
13A spokesman for Mr Girkin, who also goes by the nom-de-guerre Igor Strelkov, declined to comment on the allegations.
14Officials from the self-proclaimed Donetsk People’s Republic (DNR) deny possessing technology capable of destroying the airliner, and blame Ukraine for the shooting down of the plane.
15Meanwhile, Russia’s civilian aviation agency said it would prepare its own investigation, the conclusions of which would be released following the official Dutch-led reports.
16“Russia has never pushed its own version of events, but has simply called for all possibilities to be considered,” Oleg Storchevoy, the deputy head of Rosaviatsia, said at a press conference in Moscow.
17He said the agency had submitted information to the official enquiries and also offered to carry out analysis of sub-munitions and shrapnel fragments found amongst the wreckage of the airliner to determine the “exact type of missile involved".
18“We are ready to carry out all the necessary studies in the presence of experts from all countries.
19We have nothing to hide,” he said.
20The call to investigate “all possible scenarios” came a day after Russia’s chief criminal investigator appeared to resurrect the theory – widely rejected by Western experts – that MH17 was downed by a Ukrainian fighter jet.
21“We have information, including based on expert analysis, that the aircraft was shot down by an air-to-air rocket.
22Furthermore, experts have established the kind of rocket and it is not of Russian make,” Vladimir Markin, the head of the investigative committee, told the Interfax news agency on Wednesday.
23Mr Markin said the finding confirmed testimony from a former Ukrainian air force mechanic, Yevgeny Agapov, who the investigative committee has previously cited to support that claim.
24The theory contradicts a report released last month by Almaz-Antey, the Russian company that builds Buk missiles, which said MH17 was shot down by a specific model of the Buk M1 anti-aircraft missile known to be used by the Ukrainian armed forces.
25Almaz-Antey said the missile model involved, the 9M38M1, went out of production in 1999 and that the Russian armed forces had since sold off their inventory.
26It also said the missile was fired from an area that the DNR claims was under Ukrainian control at the time.
27Independent experts have broadly agreed with Almaz-Anetey’s identification of the missile, but have questioned both the claim that Russia no longer posses that model and the methodology leading to their suspected launch site.
