Greece signals summer concessions in bailout talks

5Greece's finance minister on Thursday indicated his country could make concessions on key demands by bailout lenders - but only in the summer, after an initial deal to unlock delayed rescue funds is reached.
6Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis made the comments as his reshuffled negotiating team prepared to meet bailout inspectors in Brussels.
7Greece's creditors argue the country has fallen behind on long-terms reforms that would allow it to get back on its feet: lowering state funding for pensions, increasing turnover of staff in state jobs, further dismantling job protection rules, and simplifying sales-tax rates.
8But Greece's left-wing government argues the reforms cannot continue with the same speed and severity as in previous years, after the country suffered a drastic drop in output and increase in unemployment.
9"After June, we are willing to look at many issues," Varoufakis told Sto Kokkino radio.
10He signaled, for example, he'd be willing to raise some sales tax rates.
11Varoufakis said he would "not move an inch" on such issues until an initial agreement is reached to unlock bailout money that Greece needs to avoid defaulting on debt obligations in coming weeks.
12Despite the tough stance, he said it would be possible to reach such a deal in the "next few days."
13The confrontation with creditors has taken its toll on the fragile Greek economy.
14Late Wednesday, Moody's ratings agency further downgraded the country's sovereign rating to Caa2 from Caa1, two notches above default.
15The main reason, it said, for the action was "the high uncertainty over whether Greece's government will reach an agreement with official creditors in time to meet upcoming repayments on marketable debt."
16Greece faces a tough debt redemption schedule in 2015, and it remains unclear whether it can cover a repayment to the International Monetary Fund on May 12 worth some 750 million euros ($840 million).
