Greek leader defends bailout plan in talks with creditors

1Greece's prime minister entered a showdown with creditors overnight in Brussels, where each side would present proposals in the hope of reaching a deal to unlock bailout loans and save the country from financial disaster.
2Some officials dampened expectations of a breakthrough on Wednesday, even though Greece is running out of cash and faces debt repayments as soon as Friday.
3But French President Francois Hollande said the talks were at least heading in the right direction: "We are some days, not to say some hours away from a possible agreement."
4Greece has been negotiating for four months with its creditors over what budget reforms it should make to get the 7.2 billion euros (NZ$11.3 billion) in loans that are left over in its bailout fund.
5Wednesday's meetings are part of a string of high-level diplomatic efforts to bring the negotiations to a successful end.
6Ahead of his meeting with Commission head Jean-Claude Juncker, Greek leader Alexis Tsipras stressed the need for compromise.
7"Today more than ever it is necessary for the (creditor) institutions and mainly for the political leadership of Europe to sign up to realism," Tsipras said.
8Tsipras spoke by teleconference with Hollande and German Chancellor Angela Merkel ahead of his meeting with Juncker, a Greek government official said.
9The three agreed on the need for Greece to have lower primary surpluses " the budget balance without taking into account debt servicing " "and for a solution to be found quickly," the official said, on condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to speak on the record.
10Lower primary surpluses than those initially demanded under Greece's five-year bailout have been one of Athens' main requests, although it appears to have been the easiest to overcome in the negotiations.
11Sticking points appear to have been labor and pension reforms, as well as some changes to consumer tax.
12The eurozone's top financial official, Jeroen Dijsselbloem, was also in Brussels and met with Juncker before Tsipras's arrival.
13He was less upbeat than others, saying: "We still have a lot of work to do."
14That didn't prevent Greek markets from rising on hopes of a deal, with the main stock index closing up 4.1 percent.
15Time is pressing.
16Greece must repay 1.6 billion euros to the IMF this month alone.
17The first installment of just over 300 million euros is due Friday, with other installments on June 12, 16 and 19.
18Although Athens insists it intends to repay its debts, it is unclear how much longer it will be able to do so without outside help.
19One option Greece could choose is to bundle this month's IMF payments into one on June 30, giving more time for negotiations.
20While allowed under IMF regulations, the option is rarely used.
21Without bailout funds, Greece could eventually default on its debts and crash out of the euro, possibly pushing Europe and potentially the global economy into turmoil.
22"We are working with high pressure" to find a solution, Merkel said in Berlin.
23Tsipras says he submitted his proposal to creditors on Monday night but gave no details of what fiscal reforms Greece was suggesting in return for its final bailout funds.
24Greece needs the loans because it is locked out of the international bond markets as wary investors demand prohibitively high interest rates to lend it money.
25The country has not received bailout funds since August.
26So far it has managed to scrape together enough to repay its debts by seizing the reserves of state enterprises, including municipalities, schools, embassies and hospitals.
27But those funds will not be enough to sustain the country through the summer.
28Elected in January on promises to repeal the deeply resented austerity measures Greece had to impose in return for its five-year bailout, Tsipras said his government had made concessions during the negotiations.
29The 40-year-old premier faces dissent from hardliners from within his own radical left Syriza party, some of whom openly say they prefer a rupture in negotiations and even a euro exit, to capitulation on pre-election promises.
30One of the most vocal critics of compromise has been Energy Minister Panagiotis Lafazanis.
31"The logic of a deal at any cost ... is a logic of the country's total surrender to the worst neo-colonial regime," Lafazanis was quoted as telling the news website Real.gr Wednesday.
32"A deal will either be compatible with Syriza's progressive program, or it will not exist."
