Google prepares to launch U.S. wireless service as Europe circles the wagons

1Pedestrians walk past Google Inc. signage displayed in front of the company's headquarters in Mountain View.
2Google is preparing to roll out a new wireless service in partnership with established carriers.
3Hate paying for unused data on your wireless plan?
4It won't be long before you can switch to Google.
5Google will unveil its new U.S. wireless service as early as Wednesday, allowing customers to pay only for the amount of data they use each month, according to the Wall Street Journal.
6The planned service will be the start of Google as Mobile Virtual Network Operator (MVNO).
7It's a strategy that will intrigue consumers looking for a deal on wireless service, and it will give Google an immediate competitive edge in an already crowded marketplace.
8While the service only works with the Nexus 6 phones (for now), it could quickly affect current wireless service plans of the major carriers.
9So how could Google redefine wireless?
10Most wireless plans require subscribers to pay for data they never use.
11The average smartphone user overpays for $28 each month on unused data, according to the Journal, citing data from a 2013 study by wireless research firm Validas.
12As competition increases, some services are offering alternative data plans.
13Small companies like Republic Wireless offer users the ability to pay for data as they go.
14T-Mobile and AT&T allow customers to roll over unused data to the next month, which doesn't make much of a difference to customers who aren't using their full data plan anyway.
15Google might appeal to the same users who have already cut the cord on cable, customers who simply don't want to pay for what they don't use.
16This will force the larger companies to look at how their data plan is structured and change it, accordingly.
17Google's new service will run on Sprint and T-Mobile networks, switching between the two depending on which one has the strongest signal.
18The service could also reduce users' bills by routing calls and data through Wi-Fi networks.
19If consumers see savings by routing calls through available Wi-Fi, expect to see the big carriers adopting the practice soon.
