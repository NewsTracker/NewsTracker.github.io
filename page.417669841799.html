Prosecutor: Oklahoma man detailed assaults on Kenya children

1FILE - In this Monday, Aug. 4, 2014 file photo, Stephen Jones, attorney for Matthew Lane Durham, talks with the media outside the Federal Courthouse in Oklahoma City, following a detention hearing for Durham.
2A Jury of eight men and four women, plus three alternate jurors, were seated after U.S. District Judge David Russell, prosecutors and defense attorneys asked about 70 prospective jury members questions, including whether they have been victims of sexual abuse.
3Matthew Lane Durham, 20, of Edmond, was a volunteer at the Upendo Children's Home in Nairobi, Kenya, and faces 17 counts of sexual misconduct including aggravated sexual abuse and engaging in illicit sexual conduct in foreign places.
4OKLAHOMA CITY (AP) - An Oklahoma man accused of sexually abusing several children at a Kenyan orphanage where he volunteered confessed to his misdeeds in writing, a federal prosecutor told jurors Wednesday at the outset of the trial.
5Assistant U.S. Attorney Don Gifford spent part of his opening arguments reading excerpts of 20-year-old Matthew Lane Durham's confession, which Durham's lawyer says isn't true and was coerced.
6"I would take her to the bathroom at night and hold her down and rape her," Gifford said, reading a section pertaining to the alleged assault of a 12-year-old girl.
7Quoting another that pertained to a boy at the Upendo Children's Home in the Kenyan capital of Nairobi, Gifford read: "At night I took him to the bathroom and had him perform oral sex on me."
8Durham, of Edmond, has pleaded not guilty to the 17 charges he faces, including aggravated sexual abuse and engaging in illicit sexual conduct in foreign places.
9If convicted on all charges, he faces up to life in prison.
10Six of the children, who speak Swahili, were scheduled to testify through an interpreter.
11The first began to testify after opening statements concluded, but not before the judge cleared the gallery and closed the courtroom to the public and media.
12During his opening statement, defense lawyer Stephen Jones said Durham is innocent and that he only confessed to the crimes because he was under duress.
13He said officials at the orphanage had detained and isolated him, and had taken away his passport.
14"He's in fear for his life," Jones said.
15He described Durham as "an emotionally vulnerable teenager" who was struggling with "sexual identity and development" while also being a devout Christian.
16Jones said the orphanage, on hearing the allegations, initially didn't notify police, medical officials or the U.S. Embassy, and that others who lived in the Upendo home didn't witness any wrongdoing alleged to have occurred.
17"They never saw anything in the close confines," Jones said.
18U.S. District Judge David Russell said the trial could last up to two weeks.
19In addition to the six children, orphanage officials and 10 missionaries are among the 39 witnesses prosecutors intend to call to the stand.
20Jones has said Durham will testify.
21The judge agreed last week to close the courtroom during the children's testimony after prosecutors complained Durham and his family had discussed a plan to "pack the courtroom" with supporters.
22The government feared that testifying in open court could pose "substantial psychological harm" to the children.
23Durham had volunteered at the orphanage since 2012.
24Prosecutors allege he assaulted children last year.
