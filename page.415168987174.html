37Don't call it a comeback: Blocks have been here for more than 100 years.
38In 1913, progressive educator Caroline Pratt designed "unit blocks" as a central part of the curriculum at the school she founded in New York City, which still uses blocks.
39Pratt wrote of blocks: "I wanted something so adaptable children could use it without guidance or control, I wanted to see them build a world ... to re-create on their own level the life about them..."
40Pratt's vision and ideas greatly influenced the founders of Bing, which is a laboratory school.
41That means Stanford's psychology department conducts research on child development there, and students assist the professional staff of preschool teachers.
42"Pratt observed that the real learning was occurring when they (kids) had unscheduled, free play time," says Winters.
43"Children still need those hands-on, tactile materials to make sense of the world."
44Several early childhood studies have shown that children who play with blocks have better language and cognition skills than control groups.
45Others have looked at the power of blocks to help teach math, as well as the relationship between unstructured play materials and learning.
46Research has shown that math skills are the biggest predictor of later academic success.
47"What's special about the blocks is that they provide an excellent venue, an excellent platform for parents and children to engage with one another. Which is really critical, not just to children's language development, but to their cognitive development generally and their social development as well," says Dr. Dimitri Christakis, a pediatrician, researcher and professor.
48But they're also just a lot of fun.
49At Bing there is no prescribed "block period."
50The kids are simply given time for open-ended exploration.
51Tightly scheduled events aren't part of this school.
52"It's a little bit of sucking the joy out of it: 'Now you have to do this,' as opposed to wanting to do that," Winters says.
53The school's emphasis is on free-range play.
54Here blocks - along with clay, paint, sand and water - are the basic materials preschoolers use every day.
55Wander in, wander off.
56Play hard, live free.
57"When children are allowed time to think, to observe, to interact with their environment, their minds are bringing in all this information," says Todd Erickson, one of Bing's head teachers.
58"With that gift of time, it really unlocks a lot of creativity, a lot deeper thinking because they can mull and let their unconscious work on it."
59And blocks, lots of them, aren't just used inside.
60In the school's spacious, grass-covered yard, 4-year-old Yuri works alone turning large, hollow outdoor building blocks into her imaginative play area.
61A stage is born.
62She looks at the spaces between two sides and starts to grapple with different sized pieces to bridge the gap.
63See, in a sense, she and other kids working with blocks are really starting algebra, Erickson says.
64"Essentially they're solving for X," he says.
65"They've got one piece on one side and one piece on the other and a distance to fill. So what is that amount going to be, what does the length of that block have to be to bridge, to sit at both edges of the block. It's the beginning of mathematics, really."
66The blocks are all multiples and fractions of a basic unit.
67Director Winters says it is really a way to start on math.
68"They're not just stacking up and making a wall," she says.
69"Children can do complex math projects with blocks even into elementary years, and we forget that."
70Ten focused minutes later, Yuri loses interest.
71A swing beckons.
72But two other girls wander over and turn Yuri's half-built stage into a makeshift obstacle course.
73Dramatic, imaginative play ensues with blocks as the props.
74Erickson says he's regularly in awe of the continuing pedagogic and play power of these basic pieces of sanded maple.
75"I think they are so simple that's it's very easy to look down on them, almost, to disqualify them in terms of their complexity," he says.
76"There's work with balance. Science. Math. Social emotional language. All these things interweave so beautifully in such a sophisticated way for such a basic material. It's really amazing."
77Today, blocks are still in heavy use in pre-K and kindergarten classes across America.
78But block use overall is in decline.
79No, there are no hard numbers on this.
80But anecdotally, teachers say free play time is slowly getting chipped away to make time for more formal academic exercises and proscribed projects.
