1Mark Latimer coos this to his newborn daughter, Lizzie, soon after birth.
2It's said with shaky determination and deep regret, and amid the Latimer's grief and marital struggles, a "right" childhood seems impossible.
3But they and everyone else in the town of Broadchurch soldier on, each on a mission for redemption - the driving force of Season 2.
4What's been remarkable, then, is how conflicting and destructive each of these redemptive missions is.
5And no one has brought this idea to life more brilliantly than Marianne Jean-Baptiste, playing defense attorney Sharon Bishop, helping the wrong man for the right reasons.
6Despite sharing scenes with some of the greatest British actors alive and stepping into a well-established universe, the conflicted, raging Baptiste is emerging as the season's MVP.
7What do we know about Baptiste's Bishop?
8She's an outsider in many ways: as a Londoner, as an acidic skeptic, as one of the few black characters to arrive in this very pasty town.
9(The other black actor to get significant screen time, Chloe's boyfriend, Dean, slunk around the edges as a cocaine connection/secret paintballer and has not returned this season.)
10She's trained her junior partner to be sharp, charming and ruthless, but still keeps her distance: When Abby Thompson gets a head start on a part of the case, Sharon declines to use her work; she'd rather do it herself.
11She relishes power, being in the spotlight, and antagonizing people, and she's extremely good at all three.
12In this episode, we finally discover the reason for the massive chip on her shoulder.
13On the morning of a trial date, Sharon rises heavily at 3:30 in the morning and makes the long trip to visit her incarcerated son, Jonah.
14When Abby had previously alluded to this looming plot point in Sharon's office, she avoided Sharon's steely gaze and then changed the subject.
15But the fearsome lawyer is a completely different animal when sitting across the table from Jonah, who has six more years to go and stares at his mother with a weary detachment.
16"You've got to stay positive about this," she pleads, but it's not clear that she believes it herself, and she moves on to a bland question about the jail food.
17It's far from an emotional breakdown, but Baptiste reveals Sharon's underbelly with brilliant subtlety.
18From these few moments we immediately see why she took on Joe Miller's case: she's trying to right the wrongs of a jumpy and prejudiced court system.
19When Sharon returns to the courtroom, she's as cunning and harsh as ever.
20She skillfully navigates herself out of a tight spot regarding Joe and Danny's email correspondence, sending Alec Hardy from the room in a fury.
21And when she gets Ellie Miller on the stand, she eagerly circles her prey, picking apart every detail from her sex life to her sleeping pills.
22As she drops the sledgehammer by insinuating an affair between Ellie and Hardy, you see these two formidable women transforming in different directions: Sharon sharpening all her penned up fury into a pointed stick, and Ellie slowly realizing she's being backed into a corner, lips quivering and composure slipping from frame to frame.
23"Together, you've put an innocent man in the dock," Sharon rails against the detective pair, and in this twisted moment, she's the tortured mother demanding justice for her beloved child, by shielding a very guilty child killer.
24While Sharon is becoming more and more of a conflicted hero (or antihero), a clearer villain emerges.
25Claire Ashworth inches from victim to suspect with every additional moment that her ex-husband Lee is back in her life.
26She angrily turns on Hardy, who has been protecting her for months, goads Ellie into a terrible double one-night stand, and gives a couple different vague accounts of what happened the night of the Sandbrook murder.
27She has a real femme fatale vibe.
