Inside Hungary's 10 billion euro nuclear deal with Russia

1BUDAPEST, March 30 (Reuters) - Its currency is wounded and its economy besieged by sanctions, yet Russia still has money to spare for potential allies overseas.
2Even as it scrabbles for foreign funds, Moscow is poised to make a 10 billion euro loan to Hungary, one of the European Union members most sympathetic to it.
3Budapest plans to draw on the first tranche of the loan this year, a Hungarian government commissioner told Reuters.
4Officially the loan is to finance the expansion of the Paks nuclear power plant, Hungary's only atomic power station, which supplies about 40 percent of the country's electricity.
5But critics say there is another motive as well: Russia buying favour with a European Union (EU) government.
6"This Paks deal is camouflage," said Zoltan Illes, a former lawmaker in the ruling Fidesz party who was a state secretary for the environment until 2014.
7"This is a financial transaction, and for the Russians this is buying influence."
8Illes, who opposes the use of nuclear energy, believes the deal is more about pumping money into the economy of Hungary, where Prime Minister Viktor Orban faces re-election in 2018, than providing electricity.
9For years, Moscow has used commercial relationships - in particular gas sales - to exert influence across Europe.
10Now those methods are coming under closer scrutiny after the United States and EU imposed tough economic sanctions on Russia for annexing Crimea and supporting separatist fighters in the east of Ukraine.
11In return, Russia is striving to retain ties, commercially and diplomatically, from the Baltic states to Europe's southern rim.
12The loan to Hungary, agreed last year, is seen by some as part of that undeclared struggle for influence.
13Government spokesman Zoltan Kovacs rejected such claims.
14"The rationale of the Paks investment is not about election campaigns and chances. It serves the country's long-term energy security," he said.
15He added that Russia was helping to build reactors in other countries and that Russia had less economic influence in Hungary than in other Western European states.
16Officials in Moscow and Budapest say the nuclear deal was concluded purely on commercial and energy grounds and was good for both countries.
17Hungarian Foreign Minister Peter Szijjarto told Reuters the deal was "the business (transaction) of the century."
18Rosatom, the Russian state nuclear firm, and the Russian finance ministry responsible for the loan to Hungary did not respond to requests for comment.
19Hungary had initially planned to put the contract to expand Paks out to tender, and some Western firms showed interest, along with Rosatom.
20But Reuters found that Hungary abruptly dropped the idea of a tender.
21Specialists in the Development Ministry who had worked on plans to expand the Paks plant were sidelined, said two people familiar with Hungary's energy sector.
22Instead, a small group close to Prime Minister Orban chose to award the contract to Rosatom.
23Russia offered a loan as part of the deal.
24Kovacs, the government spokesman, said: "The whole project is being carried out with very serious professional preparations. Decisions of a political nature are naturally made by politicians."
25Since the agreement was struck, Orban has appeared much more friendly towards the Kremlin than his EU peers have done.
26He has said Europe was shooting itself in the foot by imposing sanctions on Russia, though he did not go so far as blocking sanctions.
27Orban is also leading a push for a new pipeline to take Russian gas to southeast Europe, bypassing Ukraine.
28Last month, Orban hosted Putin in Budapest.
29He is the only EU leader to invite the Russian president on an official bilateral visit since Malaysian airliner MH17 was shot down over Ukraine in July 2014.
30Western officials say the plane was most likely brought down by a Russian missile; Russia denies any responsibility.
31Standing alongside Putin in the Hungarian parliament, Orban adopted a conciliatory approach to Moscow.
32He said EU governments were "chasing ghosts" if they believed they could get by without cooperating with Russia.
33Orban regularly flouts EU rules with policies that critics label populist.
34Since he was elected with a two-thirds parliamentary majority in 2010, Orban has imposed windfall taxes on banks, telecoms companies and retail firms to keep the budget deficit in check.
35He's clashed with Brussels over curbs on the media.
36And he has consolidated his power with measures that critics say weakened democratic checks and balances - an allegation the government denies.
37At the same time, he is not a natural Kremlin ally.
38As a young student in 1989, he burst onto the political scene with an impassioned speech demanding the withdrawal of Soviet forces from Hungary.
39He and Putin appear to have little personal affinity; at their Feb. 17 meeting in Budapest, their body language was stiff.
40However, people who know Orban say he is a pragmatist.
41"I think power is incredibly important to him per se," said John Alderdice, who was a leading member along with Orban of an organisation called Liberal International, a global network promoting liberalism.
42In November 2010, soon after he was elected, Orban met Putin in Moscow for talks on economic issues, including further cooperation at the Paks plant.
43The plant is a huge concrete structure built in the 1970s by Soviet technicians on a floodplain next to the Danube River.
44Orban was looking to spur growth in Hungary's economy, and Russia could help him achieve that.
45The two men talked for hours, including over lunch, said a source familiar with the discussions.
46But no decision was taken on the Paks project.
47Instead, a team of energy specialists at the Development Ministry in Budapest prepared for an open tender for a contract to expand the plant, according to a former energy official.
48In addition to Rosatom, French company Areva expressed interest in bidding, as did U.S. firm Westinghouse, according to three people with knowledge of the preparations.
49In early 2013, the plans for a tender were still on track, according to comments by the chief executive of MVM, a Hungarian state-owned energy group, published in the journal of the Paks power station.
50Bidders were told then that a tender would go ahead, according to a diplomatic source in Hungary.
51Late that year the international context changed.
52In November 2013, then Ukrainian President Viktor Yanukovich rejected an association agreement with the EU and instead signed an aid deal with Moscow.
53Thousands of pro-Western protesters camped out in Kiev's central square, determined to make Yanukovich stick with the EU agreement or give up power.
54The stage was set for the biggest standoff between Russia and the West since the Cold War.
55In Budapest, too, there was a change of tack.
56On Dec. 17, the parliament's economy committee was convened at one day's notice.
57Antal Rogan, a lawmaker with the ruling Fidesz party and head of the committee, called the meeting.
58Orban's chief of staff, Janos Lazar, told the committee that the government was in advanced talks with Russia on extending the life of the Paks plant.
59"It was sudden," said Bernadett Szel, an opposition lawmaker.
60Pal Kovacs, who at the time was state secretary for energy and had a leading role in preparations for the Paks tender, had not been told the tender was being scrapped, according to a person with links to Hungary's state energy sector.
61The source said the deal with Russia was concluded by members of the prime minister's inner circle.
62Government spokesman Zoltan Kovacs said parliament's approval of the deal showed it had broad political support.
63Asked about the decision to scrap the tender and award the contract to Rosatom, Westinghouse said the decision was "abrupt."
64Areva declined to comment.
65Attila Aszodi, the state commissioner in charge of the Paks expansion, said the Rosatom deal stood out because the Russians had offered long-term financing for the entire construction project, something he said the other prospective bidders would not provide.
66He told Reuters in a December interview that a tender is "a good tool; however, it is not the silver bullet."
67The Hungarian government has also pointed out that the existing reactors at Paks were built with Soviet nuclear expertise.
68Critics say the deal's terms are generous.
69Hungary will begin repayments on the loan only once the new reactors are up and running in 2026 and will repay the loan over 21 years.
70Until 2026 the interest rate will be just under 4 percent, rising to 4.5 percent afterwards and 4.8 to 4.95 percent in the final 14 years.
71The terms compare well to market rates for financing, although conditions in every debt deal are different.
72The Russian loan finally agreed will cover 80 percent of the construction costs, and Hungary will put up the rest.
73Hungary plans to start drawing on the loan this year to finance planning work for the new reactors, Aszodi told Reuters.
74Moscow has voiced its happiness with Hungary's recent support for Russia.
75In November last year, Russian Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov said that Hungary - unlike other ex-Communist states in the EU - conducts itself "responsibly" and does not succumb to "Russophobic approaches."
76At a Kremlin ceremony, Putin called Hungary one of Russia's most important partners.
77Orban's invitation last month added to the mutual appreciation.
78During the visit, Putin and Orban agreed that Russia would give Hungary several years' grace to pay for gas that Budapest had committed to buy but never used.
79For Orban, though, the cost of staying close to Russia has gone up as the Ukraine crisis has deepened.
80Some EU governments are uncomfortable with what they see as a drift by Hungary into the Kremlin's orbit.
81The United States has also criticised some of Orban's policies towards Russia, and one U.S. diplomat said there had been a lack of transparency in granting the Paks contract.
82Illes, the former environment secretary, said the Paks deal was typical of Orban's pragmatic style of governing.
83In the short term he reaped domestic political benefits against opponents, and in the medium term the project will generate jobs.
84But for Orban, he said, "long-term considerations, they don't exist."
