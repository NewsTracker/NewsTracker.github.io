No. 2 leader of Islamic State killed in US-led airstrike: Iraq

1An airstrike by the US-led coalition on Islamic State group positions in Ramadi, Iraq last week.
2Baghdad: The Iraqi government has said that the second in command of the Islamic State had been killed in an airstrike carried out by the US-led coalition in northern Iraq, a claim that US officials said they could not confirm.
3Iraq's Ministry of Defence said on Wednesday that the militant leader, known as Abu Alaa al-Afari, was killed in a strike near the northern city Tal Afar.
4The US Central Command acknowledged that airstrikes were conducted against the Islamic State near Tal Afar on Tuesday and Wednesday but could not verify the government's claim.
5An Islamic State militant holding a pistol in an image from a propaganda video released in April.
6In a statement released on its website, the Defence Ministry said that, "based on accurate intelligence", a coalition airstrike had targeted al-Afari and several other leaders who had gathered in a mosque.
7Iraqi state television showed an aerial video of a building being blown up that it said was the strike in which al-Afari was killed.
8But it was unclear from the video whether the building was a mosque, and Central Command flatly denied that the airstrikes had been conducted against any mosque.
9The death of al-Afari, if true, would represent a serious blow to the leadership of the Islamic State.
10But Iraqi officials, over the years, have frequently made claims about the deaths of terrorist leaders that later turned out to be untrue.
11Islamic State militants captured by Shiite paramilitary forces in Tikrit in April.
12A previous leader of al-Qaeda in Iraq, the predecessor to the Islamic State, was claimed to have been killed several times, and his supposed death became a running joke in the Iraqi media.
13Another well-known Islamic State militant, an Iraqi named Shaker Waheeb, has on several occasions been reported dead by Iraqi officials, and each time he emerged alive.
14Al-Afari, who also goes by several other assumed names, was recently added to a US government list offering rewards for information leading to the death or capture of top Islamic State leaders.
15The list, which identified al-Afari as Abd al-Rahman Mustafa al-Qaduli and said he had been a top deputy to Abu Musab al-Zarqawi, the former leader of al-Qaeda in Iraq, offered a $US7 million ($8.6 million) bounty for al-Afari.
16Al-Afari is said to be the deputy to the Islamic State leader Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, who himself has been the subject of a swirl of rumours lately that he was injured in an airstrike in March.
17British newspapers reported that al-Baghdadi was incapacitated by an airstrike and had turned the day-to-day running of the terrorist organisation over to al-Afari.
18US officials have also said those claims could not be verified.
19On Wednesday, General Tahseen Ibrahim, the spokesman for Iraq's Ministry of Defence who announced the death of al-Afari, said that the best intelligence he had seen suggested that al-Baghdadi had, indeed, been injured, but was not incapacitated and was still in charge of the organisation.
