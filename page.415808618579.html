Nigerians turn out in millions to vote in close presidential election despite Boko Haram attacks

1Nearly 60 million people have cards to vote and determine the outcome of the first election in Nigeria's history where an opposition candidate has a realistic chance of defeating a sitting president.
2The vote takes place amid an Islamic insurgency in Nigeria's northeast in which thousands have been killed.
3Boko Haram extremists waving guns forced voters to abandon polling stations in three villages of northeastern Gombe state, witnesses said.
4The militants have vowed to disrupt elections, calling democracy a corrupt Western concept.
5Two car bombs exploded at two polling stations in south-central Enugu state but did not hurt voters, police said.
6Police detonated two other car bombs at a primary school in Enugu, said Enugu state police Commissioner Dan Bature.
7Boko Haram has been blamed for many car bombings but was not immediately suspected in the southeastern blasts far from its northeast stronghold.
8President Goodluck Jonathan denied the attacks, saying the governor of the state told him there were no blasts.
9The official website of the Independent National Electoral Commission was hacked but was quickly secured, said officials who said the site holds no sensitive material.
10Thousands of people forced from the homes by the Islamic uprising lined up to vote at a refugee camp in Yola, the northeast Adamawa state capital which is hosting as many refugees as its 300,000 residents.
11The oil-rich and heavily populated south is deeply contested and has become a political battleground since the main opposition parties united in a coalition two years ago, causing dozens of defections from Jonathan's party.
12Polling stations opened late in most places.
13Voters registration was scheduled to start at 8 a.m. (0700 GMT) and still was ongoing in the afternoon, when voting was supposed to start.
14Men and women formed separate lines at many polling stations.
15Earlier, officials rushed across the country delivering ballot materials by trucks, speedboats, motorcycles, mules and even camels, in the case of a northern mountaintop village, according to spokesman Kayode Idowu of the Independent National Electoral Commission.
16Good humour turned to anger and altercations as people waited hours to be registered to vote, only to find that machines were not reading new biometric voting cards.
17Even the president was affected.
18Three newly imported card readers failed to recognize the fingerprints of Jonathan and his wife.
19He returned two hours later and was accredited without the machine using visual identification.
20Biometric cards and readers are being used for the first time to discourage the kind of fraud that has marred previous votes.
21Afterward, Jonathan wiped sweat from his brow and urged people to be patient as he had, telling Channels TV: "I appeal to all Nigerians to be patient no matter the pains it takes as long as if, as a nation, we can conduct free and fair elections that the whole world will accept."
22Jonathan cast his ballot later in the day.
23Social media was abuzz with the problem.
24One tweeter said they solved their issue by having an official remove the protective plastic film from the screen supposed to read a fingerprint on the card reader.
25Trader Angela Okele expressed concern after getting accredited in Port Harcourt, Nigeria's southern oil capital.
26"The process is too slow, if it continues like this many people will not be able to cast their votes today," she said.
27Electoral officials stressed that once voting starts it will not end until the last person in line has voted, even if it takes all night.
28Voting began promptly in a Christian neighbourhood of northern Kaduna city though voters' privacy was not respected.
29An AP reporter watched as people milled around a booth where a voter is supposed to be alone.
30Then, voters handed their unsealed ballots to an official who put the papers into the ballot box.
31Voters are supposed to put their own ballots into the box.
32Jonathan and Buhari are front-runners among 14 candidates in the high-stakes contest to govern Africa's most populous nation and its richest.
33In addition to the Islamic uprising, Nigeria is beset by militants demanding a better share of oil revenues who attack petroleum installations in the south and deadly land disputes across the middle of the country between semi-nomadic Muslim cattle herders and mainly Christian farmers.
34This is only the eighth election since Nigeria's independence from Britain in 1960.
35In a country steeped in a history of military coups and bloodshed caused by politics, ethnicity, land disputes, oil theft and, lately, the Boko Haram Islamic uprising, the election is important as Africa's richest nation consolidates its democracy.
36There's a lot of international interest, especially among nervous foreign investors as Nigeria is Africa's largest destination for direct foreign investment.
37Its oil-dependent economy is hurting from slashed petroleum prices.
38Nigeria's military announced Friday it had destroyed the headquarters of Boko Haram's so-called Islamic caliphate and driven the insurgents from all major areas in northeast Nigeria.
39There was no way to verify the claim, which seems unlikely.
40Critics of Jonathan have said recent military victories after months of ceding territory to the Islamic extremists are a ploy to win votes - a charge the presidential campaign denies.
41The failure of Jonathan's administration to curb the insurgency, which killed about 10,000 people last year, has angered Nigerians in the north.
42International outrage has grown over another failure - the rescue of 219 schoolgirls kidnapped by Boko Haram nearly a year ago.
43The extremists have abducted hundreds more since then, using them as sex slaves and fighters.
44Jonathan and Buhari on Thursday signed a peace pledge and promised to accept the results of a free and fair election.
45But already dozens have been killed amid hate speech highlighting the religious, ethnic and geographic divisions among Nigerians.
46The Islamic uprising has exacerbated relations between Christians like Jonathan, who dominate the oil-rich south, and Muslims like Buhari who are the majority in the agricultural and cattle-herding lands of the north.
47The population of 170 million is almost evenly divided between Christians and Muslims.
48Some 1,000 people were killed in rioting after Buhari lost to Jonathan in the 2011 elections.
49Thousands of Nigerians and foreign workers have left the country amid fears of post-election violence.
50In 2011, there was no doubt that Jonathan had swept the polls by millions of votes.
51Now the race is much closer.
52The game-changer that transformed Nigeria's political landscape came two years ago when the main opposition parties formed a coalition and for the first time united behind one candidate, Buhari.
