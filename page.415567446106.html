1One year after Russian President Vladimir Putin sent troops to secure what Ukrainian and Western observers say was a sham referendum - which was followed by a March 18 decree formally absorbing the region - most locals apparently still approve (publicly, at least) of their new master.
2It helps, of course, that Russian state television, the main source of information for some 90 percent of the population, continues to herald the annexation as a heroic move that prevented alleged Ukrainian "fascists" from seizing power there and "oppressing" Russian-speakers.
3The day-to-day reality, though, is not so rosy: In Crimea, a sense of international isolation is deepening.
4The local economy is suffering.
5A political crackdown continues.
6To be fair, the state of affairs in mainland Ukraine is less than stellar.
7Ukrainian government forces are still battling Moscow-backed rebels, while officials are relying on Western creditors to bail out their ailing economy.
8Still, many critics say Crimea under Russian rule isn't exactly better off.
9Things get complicated when you're trying to operate your business in an internationally contested region.
10Only a handful of countries - among them Syria, North Korea and Afghanistan - believe Crimea belongs to Russia.
11The rest of the world recognizes the peninsula as a Ukrainian territory.

15That's on top of concerns over rising inflation - which in January was said to have represented the world's second-highest rate - for even the most basic goods, like food.
16It's difficult enough that Crimea shares only a narrow land border with Ukraine.
17The authorities in the Ukrainian capital of Kyiv have blocked train and bus service to the peninsula, which means air and ferry travel are the next best options for mass transit.
18(Cars are still allowed through.)
19Unfortunately, though, the ferry has limited capacity.
20That proved a major problem during last year's summer holiday season, Crimea's most economically important time of the year.
21A Russian low-cost airline briefly serviced the region, but it was forced to suspend flights last summer thanks to Western sanctions.
22(Flights on more expensive standard Russian airlines are still available.)
23Luckily, a close Putin confidante won a $3 billion contract earlier this year to build a major bridge over the Kerch Strait, which would connect Crimea to southern Russia.
24But that won't be built at least until 2018.
25The peninsula's remoteness also means it still depends on Ukraine for its water and electricity supplies.
26There's more bad news here, too: Kyiv has drastically cut supplies of both, resulting in sporadic blackouts and irrigation problems for local farmers.
27Russia's annexation of Crimea was accompanied by a state propaganda campaign that delivered one key message: The Ukrainian government is bad, and Russia is here to help.
28That message hasn't changed much since, and anyone who begs to differ appears to be unwelcome.
29Last week alone, the registered homes of two investigative journalists were raided by Russian security officials.
30One of the reporters was briefly detained by officers from Russia's Federal Security Service (FSB) - the successor to the KGB - without explanation, according to the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe.
31But that's just one small part of the crackdown.
32Tatar Muslims have been persecuted and forced to leave Crimea.
33Some members of the Crimean Tatar community, a Muslim minority group, have been forced out of the region by the local Moscow-backed authorities, persecuted or even mysteriously kidnapped, reportedly in retaliation for their pro-Ukraine stance.
34These days, even displaying the Ukrainian flag will earn you charges of "extremism," the Guardian newspaper recently reported.
35"The attitude of the de facto Crimean authorities, and their Russian masters, to their opponents is simple: leave or shut up," rights watchdog Amnesty International wrote in a report on rights abuses in Crimea, released Wednesday.
