Do TV Cooking Shows Make Us Fat?

Fears of food, water shortages persist as relief groups rush aid to Vanuatu

1If you've ever watched Giada de Laurentiis make gooey chocolate-hazelnut spread or a rich carbonara pasta dish, you may have wondered: How can she cook like this and maintain her slim figure?
2Well, sorry to say, but a new study published in the journal Appetite suggests that many of of us home cooks who watch cooking shows can't pull this off.
3"Our main finding is that it seems that if you watch food television and then actually cook the recipes that you see, you're at risk for having a higher BMI [body mass index]," says study author Lizzy Pope, who's a researcher in nutrition and food science at the University of Vermont.
4Pope, along with colleagues at Cornell University, surveyed about 500 women in their 20s and 30s.
5The researchers asked a range of questions about the women's cooking habits.
6And they documented the women's weight and height to calculate their BMIs.
7"In terms of weight, those who watched cooking shows and cooked frequently from scratch had a mean weight of 164 lbs," according to the paper.
8By comparison, women who watched the shows but didn't cook much from scratch weighed, on average, about 153 pounds.
9That's an 11-pound difference between the "doers" (the women watching and cooking) compared with the "viewers" (the women who watched but didn't cook).
10"Being a doer," Pope says, "may put you at risk for packing on extra pounds."
11This is not the first study to suggest that home cooking can potentially be bad for our waistlines - and perhaps our health.
12As we've reported, a study published in the journal Preventive Medicine found that the more time middle-aged women spent cooking at home, the more likely they were to develop symptoms of metabolic syndrome, which increases the risk of heart disease and diabetes.
13And these findings challenge the going belief - put forth by advocates such at Michael Pollan - that home cooking, compared to eating out or buying prepared foods, is best.
14But Pope says she doesn't want to turn people off to cooking: "As a dietitian, I want to encourage everyone to [cook at home] as often as possible."
15But she says you've got to be mindful of how and what you cook.
16And you may not find inspiration for healthy cooking by watching TV cooking shows.
17"If you're just watching Pioneer Woman, or Giada at Home, or Barefoot Contessa - which are great shows that I sometimes watch to relax - they're not necessarily portraying healthy recipes," Pope says.
18Take, for instance, de Laurentiis' chocolate hazelnut pie, which is loaded with butter.
19"Butter in your kitchen is still butter," Pope says.
20"And it has the same calories as if you ate it in a restaurant meal."
21And, please, we're not trying to pick on de Laurentiis.
22She's been asked about her rich recipes and her own eating habits.
23In one interview, she said it's the No.
241 question from fans of her show.
1Relief workers rushed to deliver desperately needed food and water Wednesday to survivors living on Vanuatu's outer islands after a monstrous cyclone wiped out entire villages and flattened vast swathes of the South Pacific nation's landscape.
2Planes carried food, water and medical supplies to hard-hit Tanna Island, where aerial assessments showed more than 80 per cent of homes or buildings had been partially or completely destroyed by Cyclone Pam.
3A boat stocked with canned goods, biscuits and water was expected to head to the island on Thursday.
4"There's a landscape of skeleton trees and patchworks of square outlines where houses used to be," said Angus Hohenboken from aid group Oxfam.
5"It's really quite a saddening sight."
6Lack of food was a growing worry for those who survived the storm, which packed winds of 270 km/h when it struck Saturday.
7"Everyone in Tanna and other islands in the south, they really live subsistence lives, so they grow what they need for a short period. ... And the reality is that much of that would have been washed away by this storm," said Tom Perry, spokesman for CARE Australia.
8"That's a grave concern because we desperately need to get food to people soon."
9The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs reported that 11 people were confirmed dead, including five on Tanna.
10Officials with Vanuatu's National Disaster Management Office said they had no accurate figures on how many were dead, and aid agencies reported varying numbers.
11Many people took shelter in larger buildings such as schools and churches, which likely spared their lives.
12Vanuatu has a population of 267,000 people, with about 47,000 living in the capital, Port Vila.
13The string of islands is frequently subjected to natural disasters such as earthquakes and storms.
14Because of that, relief groups have outreach programs that teach residents how to prepare for disasters, and most communities have local buildings designated as evacuation centres during storms, said Hanna Butler, an aid worker with the Red Cross in Vanuatu.
15Relief workers carrying medical and sanitation supplies, water, food and shelter equipment finally managed to reach Tanna and neighbouring Erromango Island after being stymied in their efforts for days by poor weather and a breakdown in communications.
16The two islands were directly in the path of the storm.
17An aerial assessment showed extensive damage on Erromango, with communities ranging from 70 per cent to 100 per cent destroyed on the archipelago's fourth-largest island.
18On other islands, plane crews saw people had made big, white "H" marks on the ground, and people on Tongoa island flashed mirrors to attract attention, said Colin Collett van Rooyen, Vanuatu director for Oxfam.
19On Tanna, the cyclone's fierce winds uprooted water tanks and blew them kilometres away, said Hohenboken from Oxfam, who travelled to the island.
20Crops were demolished and electricity was out, as the solar panels that power many homes were destroyed.
21The island's hospital was operating with a diesel generator, but there was only enough fuel to last for two weeks, and some of the hospital's water supply was unusable due to contamination, he said.
22On Wednesday, some residents were already beginning the process of rebuilding their houses and some had started re-planting crops, Hohenboken said.
23"I think Vanuatuans are very resilient and kind of motivated people, but it's been a shock to this community," he said.
24"It was a storm on a scale that people really had no frame of reference for."
25The storm, though massive, did not affect all islands equally.
26Butler, of the Red Cross, travelled to the southern islands of Futuna and Aneityum on Wednesday, and found both relatively unscathed.
27The village chief of Futuna told Butler that the only real damage was to the island's banana plantations and gardens; the sturdily-built houses weathered the storm just fine, and there were no deaths.
28"Fly over Tanna, there's just not a green leaf left on a tree, and not so far away, these two little islands have really survived and the storm has obviously bypassed them," she said.
29Military planes delivering aid have had difficulty navigating the small runways of Vanuatu's remote outer islands.
30Meanwhile, fears of a measles outbreak prompted aid workers to launch an emergency vaccination drive for children across Vanuatu, which has low rates of immunization and already suffered one outbreak of the disease earlier this month.
31Teams were travelling to evacuation centres and other storm-ravaged areas around Port Vila to vaccinate children, provide Vitamin A and hand out bed nets to help stave off mosquito-borne malaria, according to UNICEF.
32Baldwin Lonsdale, Vanuatu's president, returned to his country on Tuesday night from Japan, where had been attending a UN disaster conference when the cyclone struck.
33"I trust the people of Vanuatu. I trust my government. I trust the people that they will stand united together as a nation and to rebuild the nation," he said.
34Poor weather and communications issues have hampered relief workers' efforts to reach the outer islands for days.
35Most of the islands have no airports and those that do have only small landing strips that are tricky for large supply planes to navigate.
36On the main island of Efate, bridges were down outside Port Vila, impeding vehicle traffic.
37The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs said 3,300 people were sheltering in dozens of evacuation centres on Efate and in the provinces of Torba and Penama.
