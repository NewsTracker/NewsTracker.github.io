What Hillary Clinton must do to be the 'small business president'

1Small business is a big deal-not just for the economy, but for any politician hoping to get elected.
2So it's not surprising that Hillary Clinton, the Democratic frontrunner for the 2016 presidential election, has declared that she intends to be the "small business president."
3Expect the same, more or less, from every other candidate who makes the ballot.
4Clinton has identified a vital economic priority.
5Businesses with fewer than 500 workers account for half of all private-sector jobs and 64% of newly created jobs, yet Americans are losing their entrepreneurial spirit.
6The supposedly dynamic U.S. economy ranks 12th among developed nations in entrepreneurship, behind formerly communist Hungary, marginally socialist Sweden, and old-Europe Italy, according to Gallup.
7Clinton has a four-point blueprint for how to revitalize small business, which includes slashing regulations, expanding access to capital, cutting taxes on new businesses and improving their access to national and international markets.
8(And she posted it on LinkedIn, an entrepreneurial social media site popular among entrepreneurs.)
9But every politician says basically the same thing, and not much changes.
10Declare war on the federal bureaucracy.
11Virtually every president- including President Obama-promises to cut regulations on small business, a pledge business owners applaud.
12"Get out of the business's way," says Gunjan Doshi, CEO of InRhythm, a technology consulting firm in New York City.
13"Most small businesses want to focus on running the business. Regulations are a distraction."
14Here's the problem: There are dozens of federal agencies that regulate business and no single edict from the White House will ever change the nitpicky culture at all of them.
15What might is a concerted effort, with Congressional support, to consolidate agencies, shred millions of pages of outdated rules and streamline the government's vast regulatory apparatus, the way a private-sector goliath would reform and shrink itself in a crisis.
16Easy to say; in reality, unprecedented.
17Call out business-unfriendly states and cities.
18The real problem for business is often an overlapping thicket of rules at the federal, state and local level.
19"The cumulative weight of regulation is probably the most important impediment to starting a business," says economist Bob Litan of Brookings.
20"Large, mature firms have the resources to handle those. Startups might not even have a lawyer."
21Washington could create incentives by offering municipalities grants for boosting business creation, and finding other ways to identify overlapping rules that need the most attention.
22Stiffen consumer, worker and environmental protections.
23Liberals often oppose streamlining regulations because they suspect it's a sop to big business at the expense of consumers, employees and the environment.
24Yet it's possible to cut meaningless red tape while also clarifying the red lines that might trigger fines or prosecution, and even increasing the penalties for violators.
25Spurring business at any level requires this kind of bipartisan compromise.
26Let more immigrants in.
27Immigrants start businesses at a higher rate than native-born Americans, which is unambiguously good for the economy.
28The breakdown in Washington over immigration reform is largely due to the conflation of illegal immigrants - a genuine problem - with newcomers who arrive in America legally, contribute to the real (not underground) economy, and pay taxes.
29A new president willing to commit political capital and deal with the political heat might break this logjam.
30Smooth out tax rates for small businesses.
31This, again, is a federal-state problem that can't be completely solved by Washington, but some small businesses pay much higher tax rates than big corporations because they can't take advantage of complex international tax strategies the way multinationals can.
32"In California, where we are, our profits are taxed at the maximum personal rate of approximately 52%, state and federal combined," says Mike Patton, CEO of DSB+, a Bay Area floor-finishing firm.
33For the typical big firm, by contrast, the effective tax rate in all jurisdictions combined is less than 20%.
34That gap ought to be much smaller.
35Stop bashing big business.
36President Obama may have won populist points by trash-talking Wall Street CEOs, but big businesses may be less comfortable spending when they sense the government is out to get them.
37"Anti-business rhetoric causes Wall Street to hoard cash instead of investing in the Main Street economy," says Jim Blasingame, host of the radio show Small Business Advocate and author of The Age of the Customer.
38"Big businesses are our customers."
39In that regard, corporate tax reform aimed at big businesses would also help smaller firms, if it lowered tax rates, made the tax code more equitable and lured more foreign firms to the United States.
40Tackle the soaring federal debt.
41It will take years to revamp entitlements, fix the tax code and arrest the growth of the $18 trillion national debt.
42But meaningful progress would cheer small-business owners and give them a reason to invest more.
43"What creates uncertainty is the looming clouds of pensions, entitlements and health costs," says Gene Marks, president of the Marks Group, a 10-person consulting firm in Philadelphia.
44"They terrify most people I know who run companies and are afraid to invest long term for want of conserving cash."
45If Clinton can sow optimism among small-business owners-who are programmed to anticipate what might go wrong-the rest of America will likely follow.
