Few precedents seen as Germanwings investigation points to deliberate crash

1If Andreas Lubitz, Germanwings flight 9525's 27-year-old co-pilot, was determined to kill himself, he would not be the first to use an aeroplane as a tool for self-destruction or to kill others in the process.
2However, "aircraft-assisted pilot suicides," as the Federal Aviation Administration calls them, are rare.
3They include the November 2013 crash of a Mozambique Airlines plane bound for Luanda, Angola, that bears an eerie resemblance to the Germanwings plane's demise - when the flight's co-pilot left to use the toilet, the captain locked him out of the cockpit and manually steered the aircraft earthward.
4Officials said the co-pilot could be heard on the plane's black box recorder, knocking on the cockpit door.
5The crash of Egypt Airlines Flight 990 off Nantucket in 1999, which killed 203 passengers, was also deliberate, a National Transportation Safety Board investigation concluded.
6A relief pilot, Gameel al-Batouti, waited for the captain to leave the cockpit and then disengaged the autopilot.
7As the plane descended, he could be heard saying in Arabic, "I rely on God," over and over.
8Co-pilot Andreas Lubitz: police are investigating a personal-life crisis.
9A 2014 study conducted for the FAA showed that of 2,758 aviation accidents recorded by the agency from 2003 to 2012, only eight were determined to be suicides.
10The pilots of all the flights were male, the study found, and ranged in age from 21 to 68, although they tended toward middle age.
11Four had been drinking and two were taking antidepressant drugs at the time of the crash.
12Five had given hints of their intentions to others before their final flight.
13One of the cases involved a 45-year-old pilot who had a history of depression and at least one recent suicide attempt that he had not disclosed to the FAA.
14Witnesses observed the plane heading straight for the ground shortly after takeoff.
15In another case, a 47-year-old student pilot who was involved in a custody dispute took off in a Cessna 150 with a child as a passenger.
16After flying for an hour and half, the plane headed back for the airport but then took a steep dive, crashing into the pilot's ex-mother-in-law's house.The pilot and the child both died in the crash.
17Airline officials have said that Lubitz passed the medical and psychological tests required by the airline "with flying colours" but acknowledged an unexplained gap of a few months in his training.
18Experts on suicide say that the psychology of those who combine suicide with mass murder may differ significantly from those who limit themselves to taking their own lives.
19A pilot who crashes a plane carrying hundreds of passengers is probably more like the perpetrator of a mass shooting, said Michelle Cornette, executive director of the American Association of Suicidology.
20"This is not so different in some ways from someone who walks into a school and kills a bunch of people, and then kills themselves," she said.
21Cornette said that it was entirely possible that someone who was suicidal could pass psychological exams and receive a clean bill of health.
22"People know what's going to raise a red flag," she said.
