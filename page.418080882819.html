Greek prime minister puts bailout deal to referendum July 5

4The bitter standoff between Greece and its international creditors was extended into the weekend, just days before Athens has to meet a crucial debt deadline which could decide whether it goes bankrupt and gets kicked out of the euro currency club.
5In this image taken of State TV (ERT), Greece's Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras televised an address to the nation from his office in Athens, early Saturday, June 27, 2015.
6Greek prime minister sets referendum for July 5 on bailout deal with creditors.
7People stand in a queue to use ATMs of a bank in Athens, early Saturday, June 27, 2015.
8Greece's fraught bailout talks with its creditors took a dramatic turn early Saturday, with the radical left government announcing a referendum in just over a week on the latest proposed deal - and urging voters to reject it.
9Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras announced the July 5 referendum in a televised address to the nation, following an emergency meeting of his cabinet.
10"The Greek government has been asked to accept a proposal that places new unbearable burdens on the Greek people," Tsipras said.
11"Right now, we bear an historic responsibility concerning ... the future of our country. And this responsibility obliges us to answer (the bailout creditors') ultimatum based on the sovereign will of the Greek people."
12The move radically raises the stakes in Greece's confrontation with its increasingly irate creditors, whom Tsipras accused of seeking to "humiliate the country," demanding new pension cuts, sales tax hikes and labor market reforms.
13Worried Greeks have been pulling their money out of banks for months, and an estimated 4 billion euro left Greek banks last week.
14Queues were seen forming outside several Athens cash machines and fuel stations late Friday and early Saturday.
15Giorgos Pistevos, a retired bank employee, was in a queue withdrawing money at 3 am in the northern suburb Vrilissia, where many cash machines had 5 to 25 people waiting.
16"I withdrew the (daily) limit, 700 euros, and then I went for a second try, but it won't give me any more," he told The Associated Press.
17"I'll try again tomorrow."
18Pistevos said he went home after midnight and switched on the TV.
19"As soon as I saw (the news), I went straight to the bank because we don't know what will happen," he said.
20Tsipras said he would ask creditors Saturday for an extension "of a few days" to Greece's bailout program, which expires on Tuesday.
21In theory, without an extension, the country will lose access to any remaining bailout funds.
22The referendum announcement also raises severe questions over whether the debt-crippled country will be able to remain solvent and in the 19-state eurozone.
23Greece desperately needs a deal with its creditors.
24Without a 7.2 billion euro ($8.07 billion) bailout loan installment - which would only be available if there is a deal - the country will be unable to make a 1.55 billion euro payment to the International Monetary Fund on Tuesday, and even bigger payments later July.
25A Greek official close to the bailout negotiations said the country was unlikely to pay the IMF on Tuesday, adding that IMF rules allow a certain period during which a country is considered to be in arrears.
26By essentially defaulting on its debt mountain, Greece would likely see its banks collapse, as they depend on emergency European Central Bank funding.
27The government could soon run out of cash, face huge difficulties in paying pensions and civil servant salaries - and that could force it to leave the eurozone and adopt a weak national currency.
28But the country imports most key consumer goods, whose cost would rocket beyond most Greeks' reach under a new currency.
29Teneo Intelligence analyst Wolfgango Piccoli said Tsipras' move places Greece in "entirely uncharted waters."
30In a note, he said it also raises the risk of the country leaving the euro to "at least 50 percent," more than double the previous level.
31Greek opposition parties - except for the Nazi-inspired Golden Dawn - expressed horror at the referendum.
32Conservative main opposition leader Antonis Samaras accused Tsipras' radical left government of advocating an exit from the eurozone and the European Union.
33"Mr Tsipras has led the country to an absolute impasse," he said.
34"Between an unacceptable agreement and leaving Europe."
35But government officials insisted that the referendum would not be about currency change.
36"It's not a question of yes or no to the euro ... euro or drachma," Defense Minister Panos Kammenos told state ERT TV, referring to the old Greek currency.
37"There is no process for Greece to leave the euro," he added, referring to eurozone rules which contain no provision on a country being forced out of the currency club.
38An emergency session of Parliament will be called at noon Saturday to ratify the decision.
39The government has enough lawmakers to carry the vote.
40"The question will be acceptance or rejection of (the bailout creditors') proposal" for a new deal, Tsipras said.
41His government had already said it rejected the latest proposals from representatives of the European Commission, ECB and IMF.
42Later on Saturday, finance ministers from the 19-member eurozone were expected to meet in Brussels for what had been billed as a last attempt to reach a mutually agreed deal.
43Athens said its senior bailout negotiators will meet Saturday with ECB head Mario Draghi.
44Development Minister Panayiotis Lafazanis urged Greeks after the late-night cabinet meeting to vote against the creditors' proposal.
45"The answer of the Greek people will be a resounding no," he told reporters.
46"All Greeks will vote no."
47State Minister Nikos Pappas echoed the sentiment.
48"Our people will vote no, you will see," he said.
49"This is a very good night ... the Greek people will soon be able to decide" for themselves.
50The surprise announcement follows days of frantic negotiations with the EC, ECB and IMF in Brussels.
51On Friday, officials in Brussels said Athens had agreed to key reforms that are close to what creditors have demanded.
52The creditors in return offered Greece a five-month extension to its bailout program.
53According to the text of the creditors' proposals seen by The Associated Press, Athens was offered an extension to its bailout program through November, with loans worth 15.5 billion euros.
54That includes the 7.2 billion euros from the existing rescue program and money left over from a bank rescue fund.
55The European Central Bank has been supporting the Greek banks - and frequently increased that aid this week as they struggled to cope with the drain of deposits.
56But it would be under pressure to pull the plug on the banks if Greece's bailout program expired and there was no prospect of a financial rescue for the country.
57Government officials early Saturday dismissed fears of a bank meltdown or imposition of capital controls.
58It remains unclear whether the referendum would be legitimate, as Greece's constitution bans such votes on fiscal matters, and how the government would handle the result.
59"It is at this point difficult to see how this situation could end well for Athens," Piccoli said.
60"Even if a majority were to back the proposal, the question is whether a government now openly opposed to the agreement would be able and willing to implement the attached reform conditions. Equally, if the "No" camp wins, it is entirely unclear at this point what the government's plan B would look like."
61Greece has survived on bailouts, conditional on deep reforms and spending cuts, since it lost the confidence of money markets in 2010.
62But the austerity measures worsened a punishing recession, pushed unemployment well over 25 percent and cost the average Greek at least a third of their income.
63Elected in January, Tsipras' government has long been adamant it would not impose any new austerity measures, after cuts made in previous years put the country through years of recession and caused mass unemployment and poverty.
64His concessions to creditors have mainly consisted of tax increases, mainly on businesses.
65Creditors worried that, while such taxes might spare the poor in the short-term, it would hurt the overall economy too much.
66Pistevos, at the northern Athens bank queue, said he expected the referendum to be "very divisive."
67"I think the results will be unpredictable and in the end, the referendum will end up being a yes or no to Europe," he said.
68"The question isn't whether we stay in the euro. We're already out of that. We could end up out of Europe."
