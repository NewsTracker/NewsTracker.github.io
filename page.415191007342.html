US general: Iran role in Tikrit assault 'may be positive'

AP Analysis: Speech fallout could determine Netanyahu's fate

1Tens of thousands of Iraqi troops and Shiite militiamen have moved in around the city.
2Dempsey said on Tuesday that the involvement of Iranian-backed Shiites in pushing "Islamic State" (IS) out of Sunni areas of northern Iraq could be "a positive thing" - provided it did not exacerbate sectarian tension.
3The general, who is chairman of the US Joint Chiefs of Staff, told US senators that Iran's military assistance for Shiite militia was nothing new, having been a reality since 2004.
4However, he said, it was now being carried out in a more open manner, with Iraqi forces pushing to retake the city of Tikrit from IS.
5"This is the most overt conduct of Iranian support, in the form of artillery and other things," Dempsey told members of the Senate Armed Services Committee.
6"Frankly, it will only be a problem if it results in sectarianism."
7Baghdad officials claim the force sent to win back the city totals some 30,000 troops.
8Dempsey said about two-thirds of the force was Iranian-based and armed Shiite militia, with Iraqi government forces making up the remainder.
9The Senate committee also heard from US Defense Secretary Ash Carter, who claimed a US briefing in February - which said the Iraqis might launch an attack on Mosul in April or May - had been a mistake.
10Carter said the information was inaccurate and that, in any case, it should not have been "blurted out" to reporters.
11As Iraqi forces closed in on Tikrit on Tuesday, they found their progress hampered by militant sniper positions and booby traps.
12"They are using urban warfare and snipers, so we are advancing in a cautious and delicate way, and we need more time," a lieutenant general on the ground told the AFP news agency.
13Forces are reported to be moving in on Tikrit - 160 kilometers (100 miles) north of Baghdad - from several directions, with the plan being to encircle the militants.
14Commanders say the operation in Tikrit is a stepping stone for the mission to free Mosul, a major hub for IS and the largest town within its self-declared caliphate.
1Which way it goes for Netanyahu may hinge on whether Israel is in some perceptible way punished by the White House for its leader's extraordinary offensive against a U.S. president.
2If he is seen as having bravely spoken truth to power and escaped consequences, the episode will likely help him at the polls.
3That could deeply affect the Middle East for years to come on issues far beyond Iran, most notably the conflict with the Palestinians, which some consider even more important than the nature of any future deal on Iran's nuclear program.
4At home and abroad, Netanyahu is seen by many as a leading obstacle to peace with the Palestinians and perhaps the Arab world.
5And his insistence on continuing Jewish settlement of the occupied West Bank has many fearing Israel will never be able to extricate itself from the territory and its millions of Palestinians - with or without peace - destroying its character as a Jewish state.
6For this and other reasons, Netanyahu has found himself at increasingly toxic loggerheads with the country's elites - from the security establishment to academics, journalists, cultural figures and much of the business world.
7This has been recently compounded by scandals involving his expenses, and rage from middle-class Israelis who struggle to make ends meet and can no longer afford to buy homes.
8Polls show Netanyahu's Likud party slightly trailing the Zionist Union, which is the main grouping of the center-left opposition.
9In the highly fractured political environment, it seems plausible that the union's head, Isaac Herzog, may be given a first chance to form a majority coalition in the 120-seat Knesset after the March 17 vote.
10Under these circumstances, while Netanyahu's speech was delivered to the U.S. Congress, his primary audience may well have been Israel's voters half a world away.
11In a comedy show that aired just before the speech, a mock Netanyahu was portrayed filling out U.S. immigration forms on the plane.
12Under "reason for visit" he declared: "one or two seats" in parliament.
13The images of Netanyahu mingling with congressional leaders and receiving standing ovations may achieve just that, or even better.
14Netanyahu made no mention of the election - but he deftly touched on Israeli fears and emotions with a litany of talking points that form the core of his political playbook.
15He condemned Iran as dangerously hostile, with tentacles stretching across the Middle East - and managed to connect it to this week's Jewish holiday of Purim, in which ancient Jews defeated a Persian enemy.
16He presented Israel as peace-loving and progressive and stressed its deep bonds to the U.S. He even produced Nobel laureate and Holocaust survivor Elie Wiesel, who waved as Netanyahu intoned "Never Again."
17Even opponents acknowledged the masterful political theater.
18"There is no doubt that Prime Minister Netanyahu knows how to speak," said Herzog.
19But it was risky: Netanyahu's references to an emerging "bad deal" undermined a centerpiece of the U.S. administration's foreign policy and challenged its insistence that no deal has yet been struck.
20The event was engineered by the Republican House speaker, John Boehner, and Netanyahu's ambassador to Washington, U.S.-born Ron Dermer, who once worked as a Republican operative.
21The White House made no secret about its unhappiness, making sure Secretary of State John Kerry and Vice President Joe Biden were not present and refusing to meet the Israeli leader while he was in town.
22Dozens of congressional Democrats, caught between support for Israel and loyalty to the president, skipped the address.
23Some who attended gave it a cool response.
24Nancy Pelosi, the top Democrat in the House, said she was "near tears" and "saddened by the insult to the intelligence of the United States."
25But the mood in Netanyahu's entourage was upbeat afterward.
26In a statement released after he landed back in Israel on Wednesday, Netanyahu said his speech to Congress offered a "practical alternative" to a possible Iranian nuclear deal and that U.S. lawmakers now "understand better" why the deal is bad.
27Avraham Diskin, a political scientist at Hebrew University, said that to an Israeli audience Netanyahu's speech was "so exciting, so powerful" that "it will definitely help him in the campaign."
28Diskin noted, though, that the remaining two weeks are a long time in Israeli politics.
29Israel's media on Wednesday reflected that.
30Many praised the form, such as Meir Uziel, who gushed in the Maariv daily that "the speech was artistry."
31But there was also a strong sense that Netanyahu had upset Obama for little practical gain on Iran, using the august foreign setting for an electoral maneuver that Sima Kadmon, writing in Yediot Ahronot, called "the most ornate (political) survival speech ever."
32Much now depends on the White House response.
33Although Obama is somewhat unpopular in Israel, if Netanyahu is perceived as having truly unsettled relations with the U.S., it could boomerang.
34On Tuesday Obama dismissed the speech as nothing new and noted that it is the president who runs foreign policy.
35But Israelis fear much worse.
36The White House has already stopped briefing the Israelis on some sensitive details from the ongoing nuclear talks.
37And some in Israel fret that if Netanyahu is re-elected, relations with the White House will be poisonous for the remaining two years of Obama's term.
38That could undermine everything from weapons sales to critical and nearly automatic U.S. support at the United Nations and other international organizations where Israel finds itself increasingly isolated and under assault by the Palestinians.
39"Netanyahu remains alone and Israel remains isolated," Herzog said.
40"The speech therefore caused damage of the utmost severity in relations with the United States ... and will only widen the rift with our great friend and our only strategic ally."
41The Palestinians are also unhappy with events.
42Senior Palestinian official Nabil Shaath said the images from a supportive Congress cast doubts on America's impartiality in peace talks.
43"I think honest brokers don't do what the congressmen did with Mr. Netanyahu - the man who leads the occupation, the aggression against the people of Palestine," Shaath said.
44Israel's opposition will try to turn attention back toward domestic issues, where Netanyahu is more vulnerable, and away from both Iran and the Palestinian issue.
45Herzog favors a far more conciliatory position, genuine negotiations and limits on settlements.
46But decades of inconclusive talks and intermittent violence have made Israelis tired of the Palestinian issue and inclined to look away - until it suddenly seizes center stage, as it so regularly does.
