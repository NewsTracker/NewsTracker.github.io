EU parliament urges Blatter to quit FIFA immediately

1Photo: FIFA communications director Walter De Gregorio has resigned.
2FIFA's director of communications Walter de Gregorio has resigned after making a joke on television about the scandal-hit soccer body, while pressure mounts for his boss Sepp Blatter to step aside immediately.
3The European Parliament said Blatter's failure to depart immediately would block efforts to rid FIFA of rampant corruption.
4The FIFA president announced his resignation last week as US and Swiss authorities widened their investigations into bribery and corruption at the sport's global governing body, but he is still at his desk.
5EU politicians heaped pressure on Blatter, 79, voting in favour of an eight-page resolution with a series of demands to clean up football.
6The resolution - non-binding but still indicative of public opinion in Europe - welcomed Blatter's decision to resign, but expressed "serious concern" that he might stay on for a further nine months before a new leader is appointed.
7But Blatter ruled out the demand.
8"FIFA is perplexed by the European Parliament's resolution," a FIFA spokesman said.

10Who's driving.
11Answer? The police.
12"As is well known, following his re-election, the FIFA president already decided, owing to the special circumstances in which FIFA finds itself, to lay down his mandate at an extraordinary elective Congress."
13The election to find a replacement for Blatter will take place in Zurich between December and February, FIFA announced, with the exact date decided at an executive committee meeting on July 20.
14The soap opera at FIFA took another turn when it announced that De Gregorio was leaving two days after making a joke at FIFA's expense on a Swiss chat show.
15"The FIFA president, secretary-general and media director are sitting in a car. Who's driving. Answer? The police," De Gregorio said.
16A FIFA statement gave no reason for De Gregorio leaving what had become an increasingly thankless job of defending FIFA during the scandal.
17"Walter has worked incredibly hard for the past four years and we are immensely grateful for all he has done," FIFA secretary-general Jerome Valcke said.
18Swiss-Italian De Gregorio, who had taken up his role September 2011, had become a prominent figure within FIFA as a mouthpiece for the organisation, appearing in public debates and on television.
19He had fronted up to the media storm two weeks ago in the immediate aftermath of a dawn raid on the luxury hotel when seven soccer officials, wanted by United States prosecutors on corruption charges, were arrested by Swiss police.
20He also introduced the news conference where Blatter announced his resignation and appeared close to tears as the departing president announced his exit.
21The race to succeed Blatter gathered a new challenger on Wednesday when football great Zico formally declared his candidacy for the FIFA presidency, but the Brazilian admitted his chances of success were limited.
22Photo: Brazilian great Zico has declared his candidacy for the FIFA presidency.
23Under FIFA rules he needs the backing of five countries affiliated to world soccer's governing body, which he does not yet have, while he said UEFA boss Michel Platini had advised him to run for head of the Brazilian Football Confederation instead.
24The rules governing how the FIFA president is elected have come under the spotlight since the corruption scandal erupted, but any plans to alter the voting system are likely to meet stiff opposition.
25Soccer chiefs in Asia, Africa, Latin America and the Caribbean voiced concerns on Thursday at German proposals to change the one-member-one-vote system and hand a greater influence to more established soccer powerhouses.
26The current system has been widely credited with providing Blatter with his power base as he ruled FIFA for 17 years, aided by votes from less prominent soccer nations that received development funds aimed at promoting the sport.
27"If tomorrow, one member association is going to be more equal than the other, then of course that is going to raise serious issues, and that would not be good for world football and that would not be good for FIFA," Pakistani football chief Faisal Saleh Hayat said.
28Zimbabwe Football Association spokesman Xolisani Gwesela said it would be unfair for countries in Africa and the developing world not to have the same vote as Germany or England.
29Raymond Tim Kee, head of the Trinidad and Tobago Soccer Federation, also opposed the German plan, saying: "Big nations will trample on small countries like us in the Caribbean."
30Jacinto Reyes, president of the Nicaragua Football Federation, added: "I am totally against this attitude because we have always spoken about racial discrimination and this idea is also discrimination."
31As the corruption scandal widened, Paraguay's congress approved a measure withdrawing immunity from the headquarters of South America's CONMEBOL football confederation.
