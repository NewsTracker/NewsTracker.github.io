Dominating TV, Donald Trump a ratings draw

1Republican presidential candidate Donald Trump waves to supporters during a campaign rally in Mobile, Ala., on Friday, Aug. 21, 2015.
2FILE - In this Aug. 21, 2015 file photo, Republican presidential candidate Donald Trump speaks during a campaign pep rally in Mobile, Ala.
3NBC's "Meet the Press" had its biggest audience in more than a year for its Trump interview on Aug. 16, leading that show's biggest competitors, ABC's "This Week" and CBS' "Face the Nation," to feature phone interviews with the New York businessman this past Sunday.
4Opinion polls are one thing, but Nielsen numbers speak more loudly to television executives: Republican presidential frontrunner Donald Trump's ability to pull in viewers makes him catnip for news programs and wins a level of coverage that feeds on itself.
5After CNN turned Jake Tapper's interview with Trump into a prime-time special last week and earned its best ratings at that hour in a month, the network repeated it two nights later.
6Two Trump interviews on Sean Hannity's Fox News Channel show this month both brought in around 2.2 million viewers, well above his typical audience.
7Trump is generally considered the biggest reason why Fox reached a startling 24 million people for the first GOP presidential debate earlier this month - the most watched program in Fox News history.
8That instantly made him a big "get" for TV producers, and the media savvy ex-reality show host has eagerly played along.
9Keenly aware of his drawing power, Trump suggested in a Time magazine interview that he could ask CNN to pay $10 million to charity for his participation in the next GOP debate.
10"He's getting a lot of attention that he should get because he's doing so well in the polls and he's getting a lot of attention because he's Donald Trump, and you never know what he's going to say," said David Bohrman, a television consultant and former CNN Washington bureau chief.
11"It's not negligence to cover him," he said.
12Aware of that drawing power, cable news outlets cover Trump events with an intensity the other 16 Republican candidates can only envy.
13A town hall meeting in New Hampshire last week drew live coverage.
14CNN and Fox News both cast aside regular programming Friday to pick up Trump speaking at a rally in Alabama.
15Trump's unpredictability is a bonus.
16His critical comments about Fox's Megyn Kelly for her debate questions, made during an interview with CNN's Don Lemon on what would normally be a sleepy August Friday night, put that show in headlines for the entire weekend.
17Viewership during the seven call-in interviews that Trump has given to MSNBC's "Morning Joe" since June 18 rose 22 percent from what the talk show averaged 10 minutes prior to his call, the Nielsen company said.
18On July 24, the size of the audience jumped 47 percent in only a few minutes when Trump spoke.
19Danny Shea, editorial director of The Huffington Post, compared the attention news networks are giving to Trump to CNN's non-stop coverage of the missing Malaysian airliner last year.
20He was on "Morning Joe" last week to defend HuffPo's declaration that it would only cover Trump's candidacy in its entertainment section, a decision that grows harder to defend with each new poll.
21"There's an open secret that (Trump's campaign) is a joke and a spectacle," Shea said, "and by going wall-to-wall on it you're just legitimizing it."
22Morning Joe co-host, Joe Scarborough, rejected the argument that Trump had more ratings than news value.
23During off-the-air meetings, "nobody ever says, 'OK, Donald Trump is great for ratings.' What we say is, 'what the hell is going on? Can you believe this? What is happening?' He is a very real story, and the longer he stays in front, the more of a story he's going to be."
24In past campaigns, the media's "invisible primary" gave bursts of early attention to candidates before voters settled things, to which Gary Hart, Howard Dean, John McCain, Jimmy Carter can attest.
25None matched Trump for attention, said Thomas Patterson, acting director of the Joan Shorenstein Center on the Press, Politics and Public Policy at Harvard University's Kennedy School of Government.
26Only two months ago the GOP had a shapeless field of candidates, and now the non-Trumps are so starved for attention that some will struggle to survive until voting begins.
27Bohrman said Trump is getting a level of attention he normally doesn't see until spring of an election year, when nominations are all but decided.
28"It's kind of a 'no time for losers' policy in the newsroom," Patterson said.
29Trump frequently mixes it up with the media, often through Twitter.
30Sometimes he's playful, like suggesting that "Morning Joe" co-host Mika Brzezinski be paid more than Scarborough.
31Often he's quite serious, and his reaction to Fox's Kelly struck some who heard it as offensive.
32At the Alabama rally, Trump asked the audience what they thought of cable networks.
33Fox got cheers and MSNBC boos, making the latter network's decision not to televise the speech live fortunate.
34At this point, it's hard to tell how much attention Trump is receiving because he's a frontrunner, and how much is because producers know he'll provide a reliable ratings bump.
35"At the end, does it really matter if both are valid reasons for covering him?"
36Bohrman said.
37"There's not a lot of other compelling stuff on television now. It's the reality show of the season."
