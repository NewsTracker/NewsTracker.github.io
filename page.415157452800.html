Ukraine leaders seeks peacekeeping mission for eastern regions

Police come under questioning in case of New York boy missing since 1979

1KIEV, Ukraine - Ukraine's president signed a decree Monday opening the way to a formal request for international peacekeepers to be stationed in eastern regions where government forces are battling Russia-backed separatists.
2President Petro Poroshenko's office said the appeal for a contingent of peacekeepers will be addressed to the United Nations and the European Union.
3His office gave no specific details on the mission's composition or any timetable for it but Russia is strongly against the idea.
4Fighting has waned substantially in eastern Ukraine in recent days as a cease-fire deal forged last month increasingly takes effect, but both sides have complained of sporadic violations.
5Military spokesman Col.
6Andriy Lysenko said Monday that one serviceman was killed and another four were wounded over the previous day.
7He did not specify the circumstances of the casualties.
8The U.N. human rights office has raised its toll for the fighting, saying more than 6,000 people have died since the conflict began in April.
9Under the terms of the cease-fire accord, the warring sides must pull back their heavy weaponry from the front line.
10That drawback started last week, although progress has been uncertain.
11Separatist military spokesman Eduard Basurin said Sunday that rebels have pulled back all their weaponry as stipulated in the peace agreement.
12That claim has yet to be confirmed by the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe, which is overseeing the withdrawal process.
13Lysenko accused separatists of making a show of drawing back weapons only to return them to their original front line positions at night.
14He said Ukrainian troops would continue their own withdrawals only if the situation did not worsen.
1NEW YORK (Reuters) - Attorneys for a former grocery store worker accused of the 1979 killing of a New York City boy pressed a police detective on Monday about his interrogation techniques, as they seek to prove the accused man's confession was coerced.
2Pedro Hernandez, on trial for the kidnapping and murder of 6-year-old Etan Patz, confessed in 2012 to police that he choked the boy, stuffed him in a box and left him in a New York alley.
3Patz vanished as he walked alone for the first time to a school bus stop in his Manhattan neighborhood on May 25, 1979.
4His disappearance sparked a national movement to find missing children, with his picture one of the first to appear on milk cartons.
5Hernandez's attorneys say his confession was coerced and that he is mentally ill and suffers hallucinations.
6Testifying in state Supreme Court in Manhattan, New York Police Detective James Lamendola said he repeatedly told Hernandez: "The lies must stop" and "we need the truth" in the hour they were alone together in a room before Hernandez confessed.
7Hernandez made his videotaped confession after about six hours of questioning.
8He was questioned and arrested in 2012 after police got a tip that he had confessed to the crime to a church prayer group in New Jersey.
9Defense attorney Harvey Fishbein questioned the detective about his interview style, suggesting he used psychological pressure on Hernandez.
10"You don't get suspects to tell you the truth by yelling at them, screaming at them," the detective testified.
11"You get suspects to tell you the truth by being nice to them, compassionate to them, keeping them calm."
12Lamendola testified earlier that he talked to Hernandez about a "cycle of abuse," in which victims of abuse commit abuse themselves.
13Hernandez had told police he was abused by his father.
14Patz has never been found.
15He was declared dead in 2001.
16Hernandez, 54, told police he lured the boy into the Manhattan grocery store where he worked with the offer of a soda.
17His attorneys blame another man, Jose Antonio Ramos, who for many years was a prime suspect in the case.
18Ramos is a convicted child molester in prison in Pennsylvania.
19They say Ramos' neighbors identified Patz as a boy seen in a Manhattan apartment building where Ramos lived at the time of the boy's disappearance.
