As Ukraine erects defensive fence on Russian border, critics fear expensive failure

1HOPTIVKA, Ukraine - The flimsy, razor wire-topped fences popping up along bare expanses of Ukraine's eastern frontier are the first line of defense against a much-feared Russian invasion.
2Trenches fortified by timber have been hollowed out for soldiers to take up positions.
3And bulky, metallic obstacles looped together with more barbed wire are laid across the fields to halt advancing tanks and infantry.
4A little more than a year and-a-half ago, the very idea of creating such fortifications - reminiscent of World War I trench warfare - would have struck many as perverse.
5But the project was announced with fanfare in summer as fighting against Moscow-backed separatists reached peak intensity.
6In December, Prime Minister Arseniy Yatsenyuk said that the program, dubbed "Project Wall," would cost almost $520 million and take four years to complete.
7With much of eastern Ukraine in the hands of Russian-backed separatists, large swaths of the border remain impossible to secure - meaning enemy troops can just sneak in through areas under rebel control.
8And cash-strapped authorities have already had to revise budgets downward, so there's little money for building defenses.
9The project is for now centered on the Kharkiv region, which lies north of the conflict zone and shares a 315-kilometer border with Russia.
10To insulate that region from any separatist offensive, another layer of protection is being built on the frontier with areas under rebel control.
11Views are mixed among residents in the city of Kharkiv, the regional capital, about the wisdom of building defenses.
12Some in the economic powerhouse of 1.4 million people embrace the idea, and look with distress to the fate that befell the neighboring, mainly Russian-speaking Donetsk and Luhansk regions.
13More than 6,000 people have died to date as a result of fighting between government and rebel forces.
14"We need to protect ourselves somehow," said Sergei Kotlyar, 46.
15"But, of course, this won't give us 100 percent guarantees, even if it holds back the enemy for a little time."
16Others believe investing in fences and trenches is a waste of money, noting that anti-tank defenses will be of limited use against the rocket launchers widely deployed over the course of the war.
17"Who is it going to stop?" said 22-year old Kharkiv resident Anatasia Duda.
18"A country like Russia definitely has the means to deal with slabs of metal. And what's the use of that wall when the border with Donetsk isn't even under control?"
19In earlier times, lines separating the republics of the Soviet Union were viewed as little more than a formality, so families and communities straddled inconspicuously across borders.
20On paper, that changed with the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991.
21But scant security along the 2,300-kilometer (1,400-mile) demarcation between Russia and Ukraine has for two decades ensured a smuggler's dream.
22Farmers and their livestock blithely crisscrossed in areas where it has never quite been entirely clear which country one was in.
23Separatist fighters in the Donetsk and Luhansk regions have exploited the porous borders, moving about with ease.
24Ukraine accuses Russia of pouring its own army's equipment and manpower across the border to assist rebel offensives.
25Moscow dismisses all satellite imagery and anecdotal evidence underpinning those claims.
26Ukrainian border guards fought bitterly to keep the frontier sealed against overwhelming odds.
27"We know from the experience of fighting in the Donetsk and Luhansk regions that fire comes from the neighboring state (of Russia) without them having to cross the border," said Oleksandr Kruk, head of the Kharkiv regional division of the border service.
28On top of creating physical barriers, Ukraine is also fortifying obstacles of red tape to deter the numerous Russian citizens known to have voluntarily joined forces with local separatists.
29Rules for crossing official frontier points have been stiffened.
30In Hoptivka, people stand impatiently in crowded and slow-moving lines to get into Russia for work or to visit relatives.
31Russian citizens can only enter Ukraine on their international travel passports, whereas they could formerly sail through with their national ID cards.
32Closer checks by border guards have also slowed things down.
