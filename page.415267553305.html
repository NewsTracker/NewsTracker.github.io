Islamic State will be forced out of Tikrit, says top US general

1General Martin Dempsey, chairman of the joint chiefs of staff, told reporters on an aircraft on his way to Iraq that he believes the Islamist militants will be pushed out of Tikrit.
2"The numbers are overwhelming," he said, as the fighting in Iraq gathered pace.
3Dempsey said about 23,000 Iranian-based Shiite militiamen and Iraqi soldiers are involved in the offensive, compared to only "hundreds" of IS fighters.
4The offensive is not what the Americans would consider textbook military tactics, he said, describing a hodge-podge of Iraqi Humvees, trucks and other vehicles surging toward Tikrit like "rush hour on the Washington Beltway".
5"I wouldn't describe it as a sophisticated military manoeuvre," he said.
6Dempsey was flying to Iraq to meet US commanders and Iraqi government leaders on Saturday.
7Isis forces surprised the US by taking control of much of northern Iraq in August last year, including Tikrit, which is the birthplace of former Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein.
8But the US and its allies have since launched hundreds of airstrikes at Isis targets and credits its attacks with halting the group's territorial advances.
9In the Tikrit offensive, which began Monday, the US is on the sidelines.
10It is watching as Iran asserts influence by providing training, weapons and leadership for Iraqi Shiite militias who are leading the charge on the city.
11Dempsey said he saw no evidence that the Iranian military is actually doing any of the fighting.
12They have improved the Iraqi militias' fighting capabilities, but their role also has raised worries among America's coalition allies, who include Gulf Arab nations who despise Iran.
13Dempsey plans to visit one of those Gulf allies, Bahrain, during his trip.
14The general said that while Iran is getting credit for enabling the Tikrit offensive, the full story of how it was made possible has not been told.
15"If it weren't for the [US-led coalition] air campaign over time depleting the [Isis] forces in Beyji ... then the current campaign would not be militarily feasible," he said.
16Isis forces had surged into Beyji, which lies just north of Tikrit, in hopes of controlling a key oil refinery there.
17But they have been halted and tied down by a series of airstrikes, Dempsey said.
18That little-noted setback has divided and weakened its forces, he added.
19"The important thing about this operation in Tikrit is less about how the military aspect of it goes and more about what follows," Dempsey said.
20The mostly Sunni population of Tikrit must be allow to returned to their homes, and the Shiite-dominated government in Baghdad must step in with reconstruction and humanitarian aid, he said.
21The key task for Iraq's leaders, Dempsey said, is to balance the Iranian role in empowering Shiite militias with Iraq's partnership with the U.S. and other coalition members.
22"The only one that can balance that is the prime minister of Iraq," Dempsey said.
23"So I want to get his views on how he is seeking to balance that concern."
