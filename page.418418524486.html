Tunisia says it's doing all it can to protect foreign tourists

1TUNIS, Tunisia -- Tunisia's prime minister insisted Friday that his country has done "everything in our power" to protect foreign tourists, as the British government urged travellers to flee because of the threat of more attacks.
2Britain on Thursday called for all U.K. tourists to leave Tunisia because a terrorist attack is "highly likely," saying the North African country hasn't done enough to enhance security.
3There were 30 British tourists among the 38 victims killed by an Islamic extremist at a Tunisian beach resort last month.
4The decision is a new wound for Tunisia's struggling tourism industry and for Tunisia's reputation as it tries to solidify its new democracy in a volatile region.
5Another attack, on the National Bardo Museum in Tunis in March, left 22 dead, mostly foreign tourists.
6Tunisian Prime Minister Habib Essid said early Friday that his government would help evacuate British citizens and that he would speak to British counterpart David Cameron later in the day about repercussions of the decision.
7"We did everything in our power to protect (British) citizens and their interests, as well as those of all other countries," Essid told lawmakers during a debate on security.
8Hundreds of thousands of Britons visit Tunisia each year.
9Many left after the Sousse attack, but some 3,000 are still in Tunisia.
10Travel company Thomas Cook, which has about 2,000 British customers in Tunisia, said it would add an extra flight Friday to bring Britons home, and will be bringing customers back on its 10 scheduled flights this weekend and use other airlines if needed.
11The head of the Islamist party Ennahda's group in parliament, Noureddine Bhiri, called the British decision "manifestly damaging to Tunisia and its democratic process."
12The Tunisian Parliament is debating counter-terrorism legislation that rights groups say would threaten hard-won freedoms.
13The government says its compatriots must allow limits on some freedoms to ensure their security amid increasing threats from the Islamic State group and extremists in neighbouring Libya.
14The prime minister said his government has carried out 7,000 security operations since the museum killing, arresting 1,000 people, preventing attacks and stopping 15,000 young people from travelling to fight jihad abroad.
15Essid admitted "shortcomings" in the government's security efforts but says authorities are working to remedy them.
16"The security situation remains fragile, and terrorist threats still exist," he said.
17"Our country is going through a delicate situation, and is in danger."
