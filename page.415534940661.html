Altercation, death threats at Indian school before nun was raped

The latest way to pay someone from your phone: Facebook

1For 19 years, the Catholic school in the town of Ranaghat in West Bengal state had been teaching children of workers at a nearby jute factory without incident.
2But three weeks ago, the school received death threats and demands for money in anonymous phone calls just days after the nuns had an altercation with the father of a boy who was expelled.
3The attack also comes amid a trend of worsening security for India's Christian minority, less than one year since the government of Hindu nationalist Prime Minister Narendra Modi came to power.
4Police are investigating if there was a link between the expulsion of the boy, the threats and Friday night's attack, when a group of ten men broke into the school offices before heading to the nuns' quarters.
5"This is a well-planned attack and conspiracy cannot be ruled out," said a police officer in Ranaghat contacted by telephone, who asked not to be named because of the sensitivity of the case.
6He said it was not yet clear if the three incidents were linked and that police were also investigating the religious angle.
7While the rape and robbery did not have clear religious overtones, it took place against the backdrop of Hindu nationalistic rhetoric from groups allied with the government that critics believe provides a sense of impunity for attacks against minorities.
8The assault also marks a disturbing convergence of two social currents in contemporary Indian, violence against women and attacks against minority religions.
9A rape is reported on average every 21 minutes in India, and acid attacks, domestic violence and molestation against women are common.
10About a fifth of India's 1.27 billion people identify themselves as belonging to faiths other than Hinduism.
11Muslims make up the largest minority, about 14 percent of the population, while Christians comprise about 2.5 percent.
12Since December, half a dozen churches have been vandalised, at the same time as conservative groups have campaigned to convert members of "foreign religions" such as Islam and Christianity to Hinduism.
13In the Ranaghat attack, the men roamed around the school and damaged a bust of Jesus Christ in a chapel before breaking into the nuns' quarters.
14Two attackers grabbed the 75-year-old nun who was in charge of the school's finances and demanded the keys to a cupboard, the officer said, citing a report given by another nun who was an eyewitness.
15When she refused, she was raped by one man, the officer said.
16Other nuns were threatened with similar consequences unless they handed over gold chains and cash.
17In total the men made off with more than 1 million rupees ($15,950).
18Police have detained nine men, but no arrests have been made.
19"We have never had to face a painful situation like this in India," said Sister Janet, who supervises a network of 40 Convent of Jesus and Mary schools in India.
20She said nuns in the school told police about the telephone threats in late February.
21They came days after the nuns argued with the father of a boy who was expelled for posting photos of a girl student online and making lewd remarks on her Facebook page.
22Rather than opening an investigation into the threats, the police registered their concerns in a book reserved for minor complaints, Sister Janet said.
23"Police should have provided protection to the nuns but they did not take the complaint seriously," she said.
24Modi said on Tuesday he was deeply concerned about the rape.
25Hardline Hindu nationalist groups that support him also condemned the rape, but said it would not affect their mission.
26"We speak the truth and mobilise support to fight for the well-being of a Hindu society," said Surendra Jain, spokesman for the radical Vishwa Hindu Parishad or World Hindu Council.
27"Why should we stop from promoting what we believe is the absolute truth? This is a Hindu nation and Christians and Muslims should acknowledge this fact."
28The leader of the Hindu nationalist Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS), a group that is the ideological parent of Modi's ruling party, said in February the charitable work of Mother Teresa was aimed at conversion.
29While there is no suggestion that the school attack was carried out because of this, some commentators have noted that Mother Teresa, a Nobel peace laureate who has been beatified, also was a Catholic nun doing charitable work in West Bengal.
30Despite a commitment to the preservation of the Catholic faith, and religious classes for Catholic children, many of the Ranaghat school's 800 pupils are Hindus.
31"If they would be converting our children then none of the Hindus would have ever admitted their children to this school," said Abiram Gupte, whose three sons study there.
32"We cannot blame anyone," said Sister Janet.
33"But the statements by leaders of hardline Hindu organisations do make an impact on the society, they want people to start viewing our charitable work as a strategy to expand Christianity."

2Now Facebook has joined the fray, adding payments to its Messenger service.
3Facebook's "like" symbol at the entrance to the company's campus in Menlo Park, Calif.
4'Today we're adding a new feature in Messenger that gives people a more convenient and secure way to send or receive money between friends,' the firm said in a blog post.
5Facebook's entry, announced Tuesday, is free, works with debit cards and is available on Apple and Android mobile devices and on desktop computers.
6The world's largest social network will process the payments and emphasized the service's security features.
7Facebook will likely expand internationally after the service becomes available to U.S. users over the coming months.
8To send money, Messenger users can tap a new "$'' icon that's next to the buttons that let you send photos, stickers or a thumbs up sign.
9Then, enter the amount you want to send, tap "pay" on the top right corner and enter your debit card number.
10To receive money for the first time, enter the card number.
11Once you add your card number, you can create a pin code that you will need to enter the next time you want to send money.
12On Apple devices that have Touch ID, you can enable this instead and authorize transactions using your fingerprint.
13To help allay concerns about security, Facebook noted that it handles more than a million payments transactions on its site every day for its advertisers and game players.
14Its payments systems are stored separately from other parts of the Facebook network and receive additional monitoring and control, the company said in a blog post Tuesday.
15'A dependable and trusted payments processor for game players and advertisers since 2007, Facebook processes more than one million transactions daily on the site and also handles all the payments processed on Messenger.
