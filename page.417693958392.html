Virginia 17-year-old pleads guilty to helping Islamic State via Twitter

1U.S. Homeland Security Secretary Jeh Johnson, seen here on June 9, 2015, was meeting with a Muslim group on Thursday in Sterling, Virginia as part of the department's outreach to Islamic communities around the country.
2WASHINGTON - A Virginia 17-year-old pleaded guilty in U.S. federal court to charges of conspiring to help Islamic State militants, the Justice Department said on Thursday.
3Ali Amin, of Manassas, Virginia, used social media to provide instructions on how to use the virtual currency Bitcoin to send funds to militants, and he helped another Virginia resident travel to Syria to join the group, the department said.
4Amin faces up to 15 years in prison, the department said.
5"Around the nation, we are seeing ISIL use social media to reach out from the other side of the world," Assistant Attorney General John Carlin said in a statement, using an acronym for Islamic State.
6"This case serves as a wake-up call that ISIL's propaganda and recruitment materials are in your communities and being viewed by your youth," he said.
7As part of the plea agreement, Amin admitted to using Twitter, with the handle @Amreekiwitness, to provide advice, encouragement and resources to Islamic State (also known as ISIS or ISIL) and its supporters.
8He also admitted he helped Reza Niknejad, an 18-year-old Prince William County resident, travel to Syria to join Islamic State in January.
9Niknejad was charged on Thursday on terrorism charges involving the militant group.
10Northern Virginia has a large Muslim community.
