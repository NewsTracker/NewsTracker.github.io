Theater shooting prosecutors build case that Holmes was sane

1This screenshot made from a pdf released by the Colorado Judicial Department on Wed., May 27, 2015, shows a portion of Aurora shooter James Holmes' notebook, after it was presented as evidence in the Holmes murder trial on Tues., May 26, 2015, in Centennial, Colo.
2The notebook was released into public domain as per court procedure.
3Prosecutors are methodically building a case that James Holmes knew right from wrong when he planned and carried out the deadly Colorado theater shooting, hoping to convince jurors that he should be convicted and executed and not sent to a mental hospital.
4After a month of testimony from victims and investigators, prosecutors this week shifted to Holmes' mental state.
5They showed jurors Holmes' notes on how long it would take police to respond to an attack on the suburban Denver movie theater, as well as his dating website profile that asked, "Will you visit me in prison?"
6A state-appointed psychiatrist who examined Holmes, William Reid, is expected to testify Thursday.
7District Attorney George Brauchler has already told jurors that the doctor concluded Holmes was legally sane.
8In the coming days, prosecutors also plan to show 22 hours of videotaped interviews that Reid conducted with Holmes.
9They will periodically stop the video to question Reid about the conversations.
10Holmes pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity in the July 20, 2012, attack that killed 12 people and injured 70 others.
11Under Colorado law, the burden of proof is on Brauchler's team to convince the jury that Holmes was sane.
12Prosecutors on Tuesday showed jurors a notebook Holmes kept, containing scribbled maps and cramped handwriting that sketched out a chilling list of choices: mass murder or serial murder; attack a theater or an airport; use guns, bombs or biological warfare.
13It details which auditoriums in the theater complex had the fewest exits and offered the least chance he would be detected.
14One map shows the theater complex and a nearby police station and National Guard building.
15"ETA response (is about) 3 mins.," Holmes wrote.
16Coupled with other prosecution evidence about Holmes' behavior, the notebook is a serious blow to the defense, said Steven Pitt, a forensic psychiatrist who has worked on sanity cases but isn't involved in the Holmes trial.
17The defense has said Holmes suffers from schizophrenia and the disease had so distorted his mind that he did not know right from wrong - Colorado's standard for an insanity verdict.
18But the notebook "speaks to his appreciation of wrongfulness," Pitt said.
19Mental illness alone is not enough to satisfy an insanity verdict in Colorado, he said.
20Pitt said the notebook provides strong evidence that Holmes was mentally ill, which the defense could use to argue against the death penalty if he is convicted.
21"This is a guy who is really struggling and is clearly mentally ill," Pitt said.
22Defense attorney Daniel King told jurors that much of the notebook consists of Holmes' confused musings about his life.
23King cited Holmes' ramblings on the meaning of life and death, as well as the question, "Why?" repeated over eight pages.
24In about three weeks, defense lawyers will start calling witnesses to buttress their argument that Holmes was insane.
25In the notebook, Holmes tries to document his self-diagnosed mental illness, listing 13 ailments including schizophrenia and "borderline, narcissistic, anxious, avoidant and obsessive compulsive personality disorder."
26He took five pages to list mental illness symptoms.
27"So, anyways, that's my mind," he wrote.
28"It's broken. I tried to fix it."
29It wasn't clear when Holmes wrote the notebook, and no expert witnesses have confirmed Holmes' self-described conditions.
30The notebook entries on these two pages discuss the pros and cons of various methods and venues for committing mass murder and, at right, is a diagram of the theater in Aurora where Holmes carried out the killings.
