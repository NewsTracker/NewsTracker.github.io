Brazil's Attorney General Announces Anti-Corruption Measures

Rand Paul urges Clinton to return foreign government money

1Rodrigo Janot made the proposal just two days after President Dilma Rousseff announced similar measures in the wake of last weekend's massive rallies calling for her impeachment and protesting rampant corruption in Brazil.
2Both Janot and Rousseff call for the criminalization of slush funds used to finance election campaigns, the seizure of assets of people found guilty of corruption, and the requirement that government officials have no record of crimes.
3The attorney general's proposals also would classify corruption as a heinous crime punishable by up to 15 years in prison.
4They would make illicit enrichment a crime, with penalties determined by the amount of money involved.
5"Corruption steals food, medicine and the education of Brazilians. He who steals millions, kills millions," federal prosecutor Delton Dallagnol said at the event where the proposals were announced.
6"Like homicide, corruption kills, and leaving Brazil without health or education is a heinous crime," said Dallagnol who is coordinating the investigation into the corruption-kickback scandal engulfing Brazil's state-owned oil company, Petrobras.
7Federal prosecutors say at least $800 million in bribes were paid by construction and engineering firms to politically appointed former executives at the oil company in exchange for winning inflated contracts.
8Investigators say some of the money was funneled back to the campaign coffers of the Workers' Party and its allies.
9Dozens of lawmakers and some former executive branch officials, including two former chiefs of staff to Rousseff, are under investigation.
10The president, who served as chairwoman of Petrobras' board during several years when the graft occurred, is not implicated.
1MANCHESTER, N.H. (AP) - Sen. Rand Paul on Friday urged Hillary Rodham Clinton to return donations several foreign governments made to her family's philanthropic foundation.
2Campaigning in New Hampshire, the Kentucky Republican and likely 2016 presidential candidate said Clinton should give back money from Saudi Arabia, Brunei and the United Arab Emirates because of their "horrific" rejection of women's rights.
3He listed several examples, such as women who were punished for reporting rapes or who aren't allowed to vote or sit on juries.
4"All of these countries appear to have a war on women, and I think it's unconscionable," he said.
5"I don't know how Hillary Clinton can condone that or accept that money. I think she should immediately send the money back."
6Nick Merrill, spokesman for the former secretary of state and probable Democratic presidential candidate, declined to comment.
7Hillary Clinton, who is building a campaign team, remains the leading Democratic presidential contender if she enters the 2016 campaign.
8But as she prepares for a likely launch, she's been dogged by questions over her use of a private email account and server during her tenure as secretary of state as well as scrutiny of foreign donations supporting the foundation.
9The Clinton foundation, launched more than a decade ago by former President Bill Clinton, has faced criticism for accepting money from foreign governments while his wife was secretary of state and after she left that job in early 2013.
10Hillary Clinton has said she is proud of the foundation's global work and that she has been clear about standing up for the rights of women.
11"I think that people who want to support the foundation know full well what it is we stand for and what we're working on," she said recently.
12Paul is expected to announce his presidential campaign in his home state April 7, followed by trips to the early nominating states of Iowa, New Hampshire, South Carolina and Nevada.
13Visiting New Hampshire on Friday, he declined to discuss those plans except to say: "We're very close to making a decision and should have a decision by then."
14Paul spoke at Dyn Inc., an Internet performance company, where he told about two dozen workers that he prefers how those in the technology sector, unlike government, focus on solutions.
15"They're not waiting for government to fix problems," he said.
16One employee questioned Paul's call to eliminate the federal Education Department, asking how much his taxes would go up to replace whatever support the federal agency provides.
17"I don't think that much," Paul said.
18"I'm not positive where the money from the Department of Education is going, or whether you'd miss it," he said.
