9A week after a massive earthquake shook Nepal, angry villagers in some parts of central Sindhupalchok district say they're still waiting for aid to reach them.
10In Pauwathok, three trucks sped by without stopping Saturday.
11One of the trucks had police with automatic weapons, escorting the two others, which were covered by tarps.
12"What about us?" screamed the villagers.
13But the trucks sped off without halting.
14Of the 85 homes in Pauwathok, all but a handful were destroyed in the magnitude-78 quake that struck April 25.
15"Nobody has come here to help us. No government, no police, no aid," said 71-year-old Badri Giri.
16The villagers said they were barely eating once a day, surviving with whatever they could forage from the debris of their collapsed homes.
17"We have no homes, no place to live anymore. We are sleeping outside," Giri said.
18A week after the devastating earthquake, life is limping further back to normal in Nepal, with street cleaners back at work and vegetable vendors offering their wares in the capital.
19"Most of us are out in the street cleaning up. Only a handful of workers had reported for work in the past few days," Hari Nepali said Saturday as he swept the street in Kathmandu's upscale Baluwatar neighborhood.
20"But today, we have a big force."
21Nepalese authorities have temporarily banned the slaughter of animals and sale of meat and fish in several parts of Kathmandu.
22The city's chief administrator, Ek Narayan Aryal, said the measures were imposed over a fear that contaminated meat would increase the chances of disease breaking out.
23Vegetable vendors were back Saturday, setting up their stalls in Kathmandu markets.
24Rama Maharjan, who runs a stall at the main Ason market in the heart of the capital, spread out cucumbers, green beans, radishes and okra, and waited for buyers.
25"Business is good and I am trying not to make any profit here. Only give people fresh vegetables," Maharjan said.
26"We get accused of overcharging and taking advantage of the situation. But how can we be so cruel? If I could, I would hand them out for free."
27A week after a massive earthquake hit Nepal, police say the death toll from the disaster has climbed past 6,600.
28The deputy inspector general of Kathmandu police, Kamal Singhbam, said Saturday that 6,624 people died in the April 25 earthquake.
29He said 14,025 people were injured in the magnitude-7.8 quake, the worst to hit Nepal in 80 years.
30Efforts are ongoing to retrieve bodies from the rubble, though hopes have faded for finding anyone still alive.
31Nepal is renewing its appeal to international donors to send tents and tarpaulins for temporary shelter along with grain, salt and sugar.
32The government also asked donors to send money to help with relief efforts if they cannot send things that are immediately necessary after last weekend's devastating earthquake.
33"We have received things like tuna fish and mayonnaise. What good are those things for us? We need grains, salt and sugar," Finance Minister Ram Sharan Mahat told reporters Friday.
34Mahat said the government has decided to exempt taxes on tents and tarpaulins.
35Information Minister Minendra Rijal said Nepal would immediately need 400,000 tents and so far has only been able to provide 29,000 to the people who need them.
36A European Union official says some 1,000 Europeans in Nepal had not reached out to their embassies since the powerful earthquake there over the weekend.
37The EU's Ambassador to Nepal Rensje Teerink told reporters Friday that "of course doesn't mean they are dead. It just means they haven't reported back."
38Most of the people not accounted for were tourists and trekkers and it's near impossible to keep track because most do not register with their embassies.
39The U.N. humanitarian chief, Valerie Amos told reporters at the U.N. compound in Nepal's capital that aid workers face "immense logistical challenges" trying to get aid to mountainside villages where helicopters couldn't land and where some roads were destroyed.
40Amos saw some of the isolated Himalayan villages by air Friday during her visit to the country hit by an earthquake April 25 that caused more than 6,200 deaths.
41"Of course we are worried that it is taking so long to get to people who desperately need aid. Some of those villages are virtually flattened. But it's very, very hard to see how we're going to get to them," she said.
42While the helicopters could deliver aid to places within walking distances that would do little to help the injured who need to get to hospitals.
43Nepalese leaders and outsiders were working to get aid to those in need.
44But the scale of the devastation, she said, would challenge any government.
45The U.N. humanitarian office says more than 130,000 houses were reported destroyed.
46The World Health Organization says a quick assessment of Nepal's worst-hit districts has found some hospitals damaged or destroyed but most are coping well with no extra staff or beds required.
47However, they are in need of essential medicines, equipment and materials.
48The WHO says the Nepalese health system took measures to prepare for such emergencies.
49The agency says it is focused on preventing the possible spread of diarrhoeal diseases among at least 2.8 million displaced people, especially those living in 16 makeshift camps in Kathmandu.
50So far, the number of cases has not exceeded expectations and no camps have reported an increase in disease or any outbreaks.
51A day after two people were pulled alive from underneath the earthquake rubble, Nepali rescuers in orange jumpsuits and wearing masks and hardhats are working amid piles of debris - bricks, broken slabs of concrete, wooden floorboards, a mangled ceiling fan.
52A generator runs noisily with cords snaking into the narrow passageways where others labor with tools and other machinery to search for any additional survivors.
53At a nearby command center, exhausted soldiers and rescuers sit in the shade, some sleeping, near tools and other equipment laid out in neat rows with a sign that reads "logistics."
54Members of the British army Gurkha engineers are back in their native Nepal to help restore water supply in the capital.
55One of them, the 34-year-old Cpl.
56Besh Gurung, says he has been away from Nepal for 13 years and now is glad "I could serve my countrymen when they really needed something so necessary like clean drinking water."
57Nearly 2,000 people in tented camps and the Nepalese soldiers stationed in the former royal palace area are the immediate beneficiaries.
