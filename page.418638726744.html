Shoppers return to Kenya's Westgate mall nearly two years after terrorist attack

1Customers enter the reopened Westgate shopping mall on Saturday.
2Nairobi: It was the site of a mass killing and a national embarrassment.
3But when Westgate mall reopened on Saturday morning, shoppers and politicians poured through the security scanner into the glittering, light-filled interior in a rare moment of hope and closure.
4Ben Mulwa, 34, a survivor of the September 2013 terrorist attack in the Kenyan capital that left at least 67 people dead, was among the first five to escape after hiding in a flower bed as four terrorists walked by him.
5Their calm, expressionless faces haunt him to this day.
6He was one of the first to return Saturday, coming, he said, "to finish that business that brought me to Westgate mall", a lunch meeting with a friend.
7September 2013: policemen take cover near the main entrance of the Westgate shopping mall during the attack that killed at least 67 people.
8"Today is an inspirational moment for us," Mulwa said.
9"Many people didn't make it, as we did. Today, we are excited because we are back on our feet, and we can convince the world that terrorism is not bringing us down any time soon."
10The assault was carried out by the four gunmen who passed Mulwa by.
11The militants from the Somali group al-Shabab launched a siege that dragged on for days, with initial fears that their numbers might be greater and dozens of people might be held hostage.
12The gunmen freed those who could recite the Islamic profession of faith and shot down others, including children.
13The attack underscored how terrorists could inflict a devastating toll on a soft civilian target, without the need for suicide vests, explosives or bombs.
14A young Kenyan girl and her brother play on the floor while other shoppers walk around the reopened Westgate Shopping Mall.
15Al-Shabab's attack on a university in the northern Kenya town of Garissa in April used the same template, with a small group of gunmen storming student residences, shooting Christians and even taunting parents and loved ones on their victims' mobile phones.
16At least 148 died in the assault, which came after authorities failed to heed protests about poor security on the campus.
17Westgate mall, once Nairobi's most elite shopping spot, was closed after the 2013 attack.
18Its hulking empty frame conveyed not only the country's security failures in its battle against al-Shabab but was a grim reminder of other unpleasant moments: police officers looting stores left empty after the gun battles; authorities offering misleading statements - some called them downright lies - during the crisis; the squabbling between arms of the security forces that stymied the response.
19Mulwa, a communications consultant, had been driving into the mall's rooftop parking garage when the first shots came.
20He abandoned his car, blocked from behind, its motor still running.
21September 2013: an armed policeman searches for gunmen during the attack at the Westgate shopping mall.
22"The shots went on so intensely that we had to get out of our cars," he said.
23"I went to hide in a flower bed on the way to the rooftop. While I was hiding, I saw the terrorists walk through the entrance. They shot at me. I remember the particular shot that grazed my head. The bullet ricocheted off the wall and hit my leg.
24"They shot the security guard who was right in front of me. He died in front of me because he was shot in the head."
25The faces of the killers are still locked in Mulwa's mind.
26Shoppers visit the reopened Westgate mall on Saturday.
27Josephine Kinotei, 34, was working at the Healthy U food outlet in the Nakumatt supermarket and was trapped for hours in a washroom before Kenyan forces rescued her and others.
28"We were terrified," she said.
29"We couldn't leave because people were still shooting. We started praying and praying. We prayed a lot."
30A security guard walks past a wall mural depicting US President Barack Obama in Nairobi.
31Kinotei lost several close friends in the attack, and for months was troubled with images of the dead that haunted her dreams.
32Yet she did not hesitate to return to work Saturday: "This is my first time to come back here. All we are praying for is peace: peace in this mall and peace in our country."
33Security was heavy at the reopening, with army and police vehicles on site and guards patrolling with hand-held radios.
34Inside, staff at a health-food shop held candles.
35The event came less than a week before US President Barack Obama's planned visit to Kenya, when he will underscore American support for the war against al-Shabab.
36US drone strikes have killed a series of top Shabab commanders in Somalia in recent years.
37The reopening of the Westgate comes ahead of an official visit by US President Barack Obama, whose father was Kenyan.
38But al-Shabab appears to have gained strength within Kenya in the last year, mounting a series of deadly attacks, mainly on the coast and in the north-east.
39And despite the heavy protection offered at Westgate on Saturday, security at many malls and other soft civilian targets remains lax.
40Early this month, 14 people were slain in the northern town of Mandera.
41Security analyst Mwenda Mbijiwe said unless security was improved at the Israeli-owned mall, "there will be another attack here. Terrorists are not stupid. They will come to check it out. They will want to put a nail in the coffin. This is an Israeli facility. They will want to shame them."
42Security is high in Kenya for Barack Obama's long-awaited visit.
