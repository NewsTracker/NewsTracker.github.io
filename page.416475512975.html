Fiancee of Ebola victim hopes memoir closes chapter

1In this photo made Tuesday, April 7, 2015, Louise Troh is handed her two-week-old grandniece Tyra Glekiah from her niece Angnes Glekiah at Troh's home in Dallas.
2The fiancee of the first Ebola victim in the U.S. hopes her memoir provides some resolution to a story that spanned two decades, from Africa to a Dallas hospital.
3Troh wrote "My Spirit Took You In" with a former journalist.
4It will be released next week.
5DALLAS (AP) - The dark days of quarantine are over, yet Louise Troh remains captive to the disease that killed the man she loved.
6"War took the life I made in Liberia. Disease took the life I made in America," she writes in a memoir being released next week.
7The fiancee of the first Ebola victim in the U.S., Thomas Eric Duncan, hopes "My Spirit Took You In" provides some resolution to a story that spanned two decades, from a border town in Ivory Coast to a Dallas hospital.
8The book, written with former journalist Christine Wicker, traces Troh's life as a refugee from Liberia's civil war to an exile in America, through the lens of that love.
9With Duncan gone, and the frenzy to contain the disease quieted, Troh finds some comfort in the idea that his illness raised awareness in the United States about Ebola, which was ravaging West Africa at the time and killed more than 10,000 people in the latest outbreak.
10"Suppose Eric had not come here to become the face of Ebola, then the whole of Liberia would be in darkness," she told The Associated Press in her first interview since his death.
11Duncan came to visit Troh in Dallas last September and the two planned to marry, cementing the relationship that started nearly two decades earlier.
12They had a son, Karsiah, who's now 19.
13But while Troh was able to emigrate to Boston in 1998, Duncan couldn't get a visa.
14After years of trying, he finally obtained one.
15Troh borrowed $2,500 from a Liberian acquaintance and bought his ticket.
16But four days after arriving, Duncan was in the emergency room at Texas Health Presbyterian Dallas.
17Troh writes that she told two nurses that Duncan had come from Liberia, though the hospital says it did not learn then of Duncan's origins.
18Troh wonders whether greater emphasis could have helped.
19"I wish I said to everyone, the nurses, the doctor, 'Liberia. Liberia. Liberia. He is only six days here from Liberia,'" she writes.
20Duncan was diagnosed with a sinus infection and sent home with a prescription for antibiotics.
21As his condition deteriorated, Troh, a nurse's aide, wrote that she suspected his diarrhea was caused by the antibiotics, and the fever by malaria or typhoid.
22She tells AP she never contemplated Ebola, since Duncan had been screened at the Liberian airport.
23"If he was sick on the plane, don't you think he would have been set aside? No, he became sick here. He did not mean to bring Ebola to the U.S.," she said.
24Then, while Troh was at work, her daughter, Youngor Jallah, found Duncan shivering and fully dressed in bed.
25Jallah, also a nurse's aide, called an ambulance, warning the crew that Duncan had just come from a "viral country."
26They returned wearing face masks and rubber gloves.
27"I was never thinking that he had Ebola. When he got sick, I thought, let me call the ambulance and just for their protection, tell them that he was from a country with this disease. That's all. I'm not a doctor," Jallah says.
28When Duncan tested positive for Ebola, Dallas County officials immediately confined Troh, her son, Duncan's nephew and a family friend to the two-bedroom apartment where he had become sick.
29Without air conditioning and with curtains drawn, the apartment was hot and dark.
30In the book, Troh recalls spraying bleach on surfaces and bagging used linens, shaken with fear that she would contract Ebola.
31Her dread grew when a crew in hazmat suits sealed off the apartment with plastic.
32"It's like I wasn't on Earth any more. I was expecting death at any time," she told AP.
33Outside, officials scrambled to contain the disease and deal with public concern.
34After two of Duncan's nurses contracted Ebola, the number of people in quarantine being monitored by Dallas County rose to 179.
35Some schools were closed.
36Sen. Ted Cruz pushed for a ban on flights from the hardest-hit countries in Africa.
