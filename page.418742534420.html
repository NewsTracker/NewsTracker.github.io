Apple Music streaming service: Clunky, cool, catchy

1The playlists are the thing.
2Since music became digital, devalued, and shareable - a transformation expertly detailed in Stephen Witt's new book, "How Music Got Free" - there's no shortage of the stuff.
3It used to take a lifetime to build a personalized music collection.
4Now, you turn on your phone, and whichever streaming service you're using - whether it be Spotify or Tidal or the ballyhooed new Apple Music, which launched June 30 - you've got 30 million or so songs at your thumbtips.
5The question is: What to play?
6That's the entertainment anxiety of the streaming age.
7With so much to choose from, where do I start?
8Innumerable options await anyone willing to cough up a $10-per-month fee, or listen at no monetary cost while enduring occasional ads, the choice made by more than two-thirds of Spotify's 75 million active users worldwide.
9Apple comes late to the streaming party - but it has the significant advantage of being the world's richest company, possessing the credit-card information of more than 800 million iTunes users.
10The service is in some surprising ways poorly designed, overbusy and clunky.
11The elegant simplicity of Steve Jobs-era Apple design seems to have gone missing with Tim Cook in charge (see also: the Apple Watch).
12But Apple Music is also loaded with smartly compiled, frequently refreshed and imaginatively curated programming that's poised to outflank its competitors.
13There are a lot of buttons at the bottom of your Apple Music screen, and after you leave behind something good, it can be hard to keep track of where you left it.
14There's a customized "For You" feature, and a "New" page, which contains plenty of music that's not new.
15There's a "Radio" tab with Beats 1, the hyped 24/7 "global radio station," plus lots of Pandora-style algorithm-driven genre channels.
16And "Connect," the not-so-interesting social element.
17In addition, there are "Playlist" and "My Music" tabs to integrate your own iTunes into the holistic streaming experience.
18I'll get deeper into logistics.
19But first, some things I've heard on Apple Music that make me think it's not going to be so easy to let go of it at the end of that three-month free trial.
20(During that time, Apple, lest you think it is a company made of enlightened altruists, intended heinously not to pay royalties to artists . . . that is, until superwoman Taylor Swift stepped in to stop the madness.)
21The "global radio station" aspect of Beats 1, headed up by former BBC DJ Zane Lowe, is overblown.
22Isn't everything on the Internet "global"?
23But while it's essentially a millennial-targeted pop station, the programming is refreshingly free of the intense formatting that makes most commercial American radio such a drag.
24For sure, you'll hear Swift and Drake, but you also might hear Fela Kuti, the Alabama Shakes, or British electronic synthesist George Fitzgerald.
25It's by no means dumbed down.
26And then there are the celebrity deejays.
27Elton John's weekly Rocket Hour featured Bob Dylan's "Thunder on the Mountain" and Sly & the Family Stone's "Family Affair," as well as Miguel and Wale covering "Bennie and the Jets."
28I could have done without the Ed Sheeran, but that was a small price to pay.
29All the Beats 1 celeb shows are archived, which is how I dug into St. Vincent's Mixtape Delivery Service.
30It made me love the Texas guitarist even more.
31It's a show in which Annie Clark, who is St. Vincent, fields listener requests for mixtapes, and hand-selects tunes to fit the bill.
32The edition I listened to mixed Welsh songwriter Cate Le Bon, Gil Scott-Heron, Metallica, and a heavy dose of country, including Loretta Lynn and George Strait.
33Playlists programmed by Dr. Dre, Ezra Koenig of Vampire Weekend, Josh Homme of Queens of the Stone Age, and Pharrell are also archived.
34The "New" section is also loaded with worthwhile stuff.
35I tend to use the streaming services in a true on-demand way.
36I've got a good idea what I want to hear, and I'm happy to find it there.
37(And unhappy when I don't: For instance, despite the Beatles' deal with Apple to sell their music on iTunes, there is no streaming Fab Four on Apple Music.)
38So I don't bother with the mood-enhancing playlists (Chilling Out, BBQing) meant to function as background music.
39But the Curator Playlists section is compelling.
40Pitchfork has worthies such as "Forgotten '90s Grrrl Gems" and "Starter: Todd Rundgren"; Mojo proffers "Nat King Cole: Jazz Genius" and the punky "Made in Britain: Sound of a New England, 1977-1983."
41My curatorial fave, though, is the Fader, who does a great job with "New Songs To Make You Like Country Music" (with Sturgill Simpson and Kelsey Waldon) and the producer-focused "Beat Construction."
42I liked the "For You" section even better.
43With its genre and artist bubbles that you click on to explain your tastes, it seems hokey.
44But the selections are intriguing.
45You get me, Apple!
46I've enjoyed computer-generated suggestions such as "Nicki Minaj: Straight Spittin'," "Elvis Presley: The '70s Singles," and "Sampled: Curtis Mayfield."
47One way Jay Z's Tidal service (which appears likely to be the one wiped out by the Apple Music tsunami) has sought to distinguish itself from Spotify is by re-creating the look of a record store.
48Apple does a better job than either of them.
49Apple Music is far from perfect, though.
50From what I've tried so far, it seems far easier to build personalized playlists and embed them on Spotify.
51I'm a convert to Sonos, the wireless home-stereo system, but since there's no Sonos deal with Apple yet, I've mainly been listening on earbuds or tinny speakers.
52And how easy is it to use Apple Music?
53Simple, in theory.
54Not an app, per se, on Apple devices, it just appears on the bottom of the screen when you upgrade the latest operating system.
55That sounds easy, but in my case, it wasn't.
56Trying to upgrade my phone while connected to my laptop proved to be a harrowing experience that required an hour on the landline with Apple support.
57Streaming's chief selling point is convenience.
58It's all there for you, whenever you want it.
59But while WiFi is supposed to be everywhere these days, is it really?
60It's not hard to max out your data plan in a hurry if you're firing up these streaming services while away from your router, whether it's on the bus, beach or bike.
61That would be particularly easy with Apple Music because there's so much cool stuff to check out.
62As a music-discovery service, despite its flaws, it already tops Spotify, which, in the spirit of competition, is running its own three-months-for-99-cents special.
63It's a dilemma.
64Eventually, music consumers are going to have choose, unless they're willing to shell out for two different services that essentially have the same 30 million songs, albeit organized in different ways.
65And while I'm in no hurry to get rid of Spotify and all the self-made playlists I've compiled there over the years, I'm having so much fun playing around with Apple Music so far that I can't see myself giving that up, either.
