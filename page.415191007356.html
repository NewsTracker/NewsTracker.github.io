Bali nine pair Chan and Myuran the 'nice criminals' facing the firing squad

1In this 2013 interview, he explains how he pursued redemption through art.
2A few weeks before the devastating news that his clemency bid had been rejected by the Indonesian president, Myuran Sukumaran made contact with this reporter through back channels with a request: could Fairfax Media please stop using a particular photograph of him and fellow death row convict Andrew Chan.
3The photograph of the pair, shaved heads, arms crossed, Chan in sunglasses and bristling with testosterone, is one of the few in recent times that depicts them together, so it has become a constant feature.
4But, he said, it upset his mum.
5Andrew Chan arrives at Cilacap airport.
6"She said that the pic makes us look like mean criminals, when actually we're very nice criminals!"
7Sukumaran's message said.
8Nice criminals is how both young men turned out after almost a decade living in Kerobokan prison.
9Their rehabilitation - as genuine as it is remarkable - is the defining feature of their time on death row.
10But mean criminals is how they started out, and what they were sentenced for in 2006 - Chan as the "Godfather" of the Bali nine syndicate, and Sukumaran as the "Enforcer".
11Executions delayed: Myuran Sukumaran arrives at Cilacap airport en route to Nusakambangan.
12The shadow cast by those descriptions is likely in the next few days to claim their lives.
13Sukumaran was born in London in 1981 to Sri Lankan parents, and Chan in Sydney in January 1984 to Cantonese-speaking Chinese parents, but both grew up in the melting pot of western Sydney and were students at Homebush Boy's High School.
14Chan was always described by schoolmates, and by his little "gang" of intensely loyal friends on the streets of western Sydney, as a trickster, a joker, always ready with a quip.
15Others described him as a bully.
16He described himself as the "black sheep" of his family.
17After leaving school, he worked as a casual food runner for Eurest, the caterer at the Sydney Cricket Ground, where he met a number of other of the Bali nine runners.
18He was just 21 when he was apprehended in April 2005, but had already made two smuggling trips to Bali.
19Even so, he still lived at home - something he laughed about later when described as the "godfather".
20Sukumaran was the eldest of three, and after leaving school, worked as a mail-room clerk at a finance company.
21While Chan was quick-witted and brazen, Sukumaran was quiet and reserved.
22A school reference, written by his principal a few years before described the boy known even then as Myu, as "a charming young man, always polite and courteous and takes great pride in his appearance".
23Police surveillance in Bali showed that, between them, Chan and Sukumaran were the key figures on the ground in Bali during the operation to intercept the 8.3 kilograms of drugs from the Golden Triangle.
24Chan met and collected the drugs from Cherry Likit Bannakorn, the never-apprehended courier who brought the drugs from Thailand to Denpasar.
25Chan was the one who had organised the flights, accommodation and mobile phones of the other members of the nine, and he was the one who bought the baggy floral shirts the couriers were dressed in to hide their drugs.
26At pretty much every step of the way, Sukumaran was with him.
27He was the only one of the nine whose names were not known to the Australian Federal Police when they tipped off the Bali police, and in Bali, they referred to him as "the black one," or "the negro".
28They believed he was Chan's bodyguard.
29On April 17, 2005, Chan was caught already on a flight to Sydney, and Sukumaran was caught with a number of others in a hotel room and a suitcase full of drug paraphernalia.
30But more than these facts, what really sealed their fates - apart from the alleged unwillingness to bribe the prosecutor and judges - was the testimony of the mules that they were the bosses.
31Chan was particularly pinpointed as having allegedly threatened to kill their families.
32Making it worse was that neither Chan nor Sukumaran would admit to anything or express remorse.
33Chan's evidence was internally contradictory and laughable; Sukumaran simply gave none.
34At one point he claimed to have suffered amnesia.
35In a legal system where expressions of remorse and guilt are taken very seriously, both men flouted the convention.
36Of the nine, six received life sentences, Renae Lawrence received 20 years, and they were given death.
37The sentence survived every appeal.
38Still neither man has identified who were the real big fish in their syndicate.
39Sukumaran said this was to safeguard his family.
40His request about our "mean criminals" photograph suggests that protecting his mum still important to him.
41Their rehabilitation, though - their transformation into "nice criminals" - is genuine.
42Chan's religious conversion, counselling and activity in the church is well known inside prison.
43In the shadow of his own death, he was ordained as a pastor and many young Indonesian prisoners testify that he has saved their lives.
44He remains a joker.
45Sukumaran not only learnt art, but set up a number of workshops and educational rooms, forging a creative hub in the middle of a tough, drug and gang-riddled prison.
46In a February, 2013 interview, Sukumaran told Fairfax Media that art was something to focus on, a way to work through his problems, but more importantly: "To find out who I am, where I fit in the world, how I can contribute to the world. Not just through my painting, but [through] building workshops. That's also sort of like art, yeah? That's what I think."
47Towards the end, as the death sentence began to weigh heavier, according to Melbourne artist Matthew Sleeth, who visited him regularly, it had become something else, too: "A tool to stay sane during what can only be described as torture".
