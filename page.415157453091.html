20WASHINGTON (AP) - Given anywhere else, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's speech Tuesday wouldn't cause such a fuss.
21"It establishes there is a strong bond between the two nations," said Kathleen Hall Jamieson, director of the University of Pennsylvania's Annenberg Public Policy Center.
22"In that context, a speech that provokes controversy at home or abroad is problematic."
23So leaders speaking against the backdrop of the chamber's 8-foot-tall American flag tend to gloss over national differences and steer clear of internal politics.
24Some of these speeches are powerful - think Winston Churchill rallying Americans for a long, hard war or Nelson Mandela calling them to lead all humanity toward democracy and peace.
25Others, frankly, are so bland they're dull.
26It's not unusual for staffers and student pages to be sent in to fill the seats of lawmakers who didn't bother showing up.
27Netanyahu's appearance, in contrast, is so contentious that several Democrats are skipping it in protest.
28Demand for seats in the visitors' gallery is high.
29In a preview of his speech to Congress, Netanyahu told a pro-Israel conference in Washington Monday that he has a moral duty to warn that President Barack Obama's nuclear negotiations with Iran may imperil Israel.
30Republican House Speaker John Boehner, who broke protocol by inviting Netanyahu without consulting the Democratic White House, says Americans need to hear their trusted ally's fears.
31The Obama administration says the invitation has injected destructive partisanship into U.S.-Israel relations.
32History offers scant precedent for Netanyahu's speech.
33The tradition of inviting foreign dignitaries dates to the Marquis de Lafayette, the French hero of the American Revolution, who gave separate speeches to the House and Senate in 1824.
34It took another 50 years for the two bodies to come together to hear a foreigner - King Kalakaua, of what was then the Kingdom of Hawaii.
35Since World War II, these joint meetings - similar to the "joint sessions" that hear U.S. presidents - have become the favored venue for foreign leaders and dignitaries.
36Usually their appearances get more notice back home than among Americans, an element that makes Netanyahu's address just two weeks before he's up for re-election in Israel even more controversial.
37Israel is one of a few allies that dominate the rostrum.
38With Netanyahu's return, it will tie Britain and France at eight speeches apiece.
39In all, 110 foreigners and a handful of Americans - mostly early-Space Age astronauts - have addressed joint meetings.
40As Australian prime minister in 2011, Julia Gillard nailed the mutual-admiration genre.
41Her voice breaking, Gillard recalled being a small girl amazed that Americans had landed on the moon.
42"On that great day I believed Americans could do anything," she said.
43"I believe that still."
44The closest comparison to Netanyahu's latest speech to Congress may be Netanyahu's last speech to Congress.
45The Israeli prime minister's 2011 visit went through diplomatic channels and included a stop at the White House.
46But Netanyahu's appearance with Obama turned frosty.
47He sternly rejected Obama's suggestion that peace negotiations with the Palestinians should start from Israel's boundaries before it seized territory in 1967.
48In his speech to Congress, Netanyahu repeated his rejection of "indefensible boundaries," prompting scathing reactions from Palestinian leaders.
49Inside the House chamber, however, Netanyahu was embraced enthusiastically by Republicans and Democrats alike.
50The speaker who famously denounced a president's war policy was an American.
51In 1951, President Harry S. Truman stunned the world by firing Gen. Douglas MacArthur as commander of U.S.-led forces in the Korean War.
52MacArthur had publicly challenged Truman's decision to limit the conflict and avoid all-out war with bordering China.
53Acclaimed for his World War II exploits in the Pacific, MacArthur was welcomed home as a hero.
54Congressional Republicans invited him to speak and the Democrats controlling Congress agreed.
55Truman didn't object, calling it a fitting honor.
56MacArthur was unbowed.
57"In war there can be no substitute for victory," he declared, accusing Truman of faltering against the communist Chinese.
58The public soon rejected MacArthur's ideas.
59"Old soldiers never die," MacArthur said.
60"They just fade away."
61A foreigner invited to brace up the president still can stir controversy in Washington and at home.
62British Prime Minister Tony Blair spoke in defense of the Iraq War in July 2003, months after the invasion, when the failure to find weapons of mass destruction and the realization that fighting would not end quickly was souring public opinion in the U.S. and further inflaming war opposition in Britain.
63Congressional Democrats were starting to question President George W. Bush's rationale for going to war.
64Still, members from both parties cheered Blair, who assured them, "We will be with you in this fight for liberty."
65Nearly two decades earlier, another British leader, Margaret Thatcher, gave a rousing endorsement of President Ronald Reagan's Cold War strategy.
66She called her speech to Congress "one of the most moving occasions of my life."
67Netanyahu will become only the second leader to address a joint meeting three times.
68The other - British Prime Minister Churchill - gave one of the Capitol's most dramatic speeches in 1941.
69Churchill rushed across the Atlantic to make war plans with President Franklin Roosevelt when the United States entered World War II.
70Speaking under the newsreel cameras' bright lights, his voice carried live to anxious Americans by radio, Churchill voiced outrage at Japan's sneak attack on Pearl Harbor but openly rejoiced that the United States "has drawn the sword of freedom," bringing hope to its battered European allies.
71A few speakers bring Congress a transcendent vision.
72Mandela did it twice.
73In 1990, newly freed from 27 years in prison, the anti-apartheid leader rallied Americans to help black South Africans end segregation and achieve democracy.
74In 1994, Mandela returned as South Africa's first black president.
75He urged Americans to look beyond their national borders and take the lead in creating a world of democracy, peace and prosperity.
76"Once you set out on this road," Mandela said, "no one will need to be encouraged to follow."
77Boehner invited him to become the first leader of the world's Roman Catholics to address Congress, on Sept.
78They may not agree with all the pope has to say, but Obama and Republican and Democratic lawmakers are heralding it as a landmark occasion.
