Google founder defends accident records of self-driving cars

1SAN FRANCISCO (AP) - Google co-founder Sergey Brin sees little reason to release the accident reports involving the Internet company's self-driving cars because he believes there's nothing new in documents that have been withheld so far to protect the privacy of other motorists.
2Brin, who oversees Google's fleet of self-driving cars, outlined his rationale Wednesday during a sometimes-testy exchange with a long-time critic at the company's annual shareholders meeting in Mountain View, California.
3As part of his effort to show Google has nothing to hide, Brin disclosed that one of the company's self-driving cars had been rear-ended at a traffic signal during the past week.
4With that collision, Google's self-driving cars have now been involved in 12 accidents while covering more than 1.7 million miles during the past six years, according to the company.
5The self-driving cars have never been at fault, according to Google, though a company employee was in control at the time of one crash.
6"We don't claim that the cars are going to be perfect," Brin said.
7"Our goal is to beat human drivers."
8Google is planning to begin testing the latest version of its self-driving car around the streets of Mountain View and other nearby public roads this summer.
9About 25 of the pod-like, two-seat vehicles initially will be cruising around at speeds topping out at 25 miles per hour.
10Consumer Watchdog, a group that has been lambasting Google for years, is demanding that the company release the self-driving cars' accident reports filed with the California Department of Motor Vehicles to give the public a better understanding of the risks posed by the vehicles.
11The Associated Press also has requested the records from Google and the DMV.
12Both Google and the DMV have cited privacy concerns for keeping the reports under wraps.
13Even if the accident records were released with the names of the drivers censored, Brin said the documents wouldn't disclose anything different from a summary that the company posted online three weeks ago.
14Including the most recent accident, Google says its self-driving cars have been rear-ended eight times, side-swiped twice and hit by another vehicle rolling through a stop sign.
15"I suppose we could give more detail and we are open to that, but you are not going to learn anymore," Brin told John Simpson, Consumer Watchdog's privacy project director.
16The driverless cars are among several ambitious projects that Google has been funding outside its primary business of Internet search and advertising.
17The list of far-flung initiatives include a contact lens that monitors glucose levels in diabetics and Internet-beaming balloons hovering over remote areas of the world with little or no online access.
18Google CEO Larry Page, the company's other co-founder, describes the projects as "moonshots" that he hopes will make the world a better place.
19He assured Google shareholders Wednesday that "everything is getting better, in general, by quite a bit," thanks to technology.
20"I think we should be optimists and be excited by all the things we are building and contributing to the world," Page said.
21This story has been corrected to clarify that Google says a company employee was in control of a self-driving car in the only accident caused by one of the vehicles.
