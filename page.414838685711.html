Report: Islamic State Militants Burn 45 People To Death In Iraq

Myanmar puts army in charge in troubled Kokang region

1The motive was unknown but Colonel Qasim al-Obeidi said on February 17 he believed some of the victims were members of the security forces.
2He said a compound that houses the families of security personnel and local officials was now under attack, and pleaded for help from the government and international community.
3The report could not be immediately confirmed due to the fighting and the poor communications in the area.
4IS fighters captured much of the town of Al-Baghdadi, near Ain al-Asad air base, last week.
5Militants subsequently attacked the base on February 13, but the attack was repelled.
6U.S. and Iraqi officials said eight militants were killed in that attack.
7Some 300 U.S. military personnel have been training Iraqi troops at the base.
8Earlier this month, IS posted a video showing militants burning alive a Jordanian air force pilot, whose plane crashed in Syria in December.
1Two announcements, signed by Myanmar President Thein Sein, were read out on national television late Tuesday declaring a 90-day state of emergency and that a military administration had been imposed in the northern special region.
2A state-run newspaper, the Myanmar Ahlin daily, reported an attack on a Myanmar Red Cross convoy.
3It says the seven-car convoy carrying 100 civilians was under a Red Cross flag when it was attacked about 50 km south of the regional capital of Laukkai.
4A Red Cross worker and a truck driver were hurt.
5The Ministry of Information blamed the attack on Kokang rebels.
6Myanmar, a predominantly Buddhist nation of 50 million people, only recently emerged from a half-century of military rule.
7Thein Sein's elected government, which came to power in 2011, has previously declared states of emergency because of sectarian conflict in the western state of Rakhine and for localized instances of fighting between Buddhists and Muslims.
8But it was the first time his government has imposed a military administration, which gives the army executive and judicial powers in the designated region.
9The clause permitting this is in the 2008 constitution, which was drawn up under a military government, and is one of several that enables the military to continue to dominate the government.
10Independent political analyst Yan Myo Thein said the assumption of such powers by the military is likely to draw scrutiny from other ethnic groups which, like the Kokang, are seeking greater autonomy in their areas.
11And it comes as the government tries to sign a nationwide peace accord with those groups and ahead of the elections that are supposed to be held later this year.
12According to the government, as of Monday, more than 50 troops had been killed and 73 wounded since last week, while 26 were killed on the Kokang side.
13State-run media said Tuesday that during the visit to a hospital treating wounded soldiers, Thein Sein vowed that his government "will not lose an inch of Myanmar territory."
14The fighting has forced people to flee over the border.
15Citing government officials, the Chinese state-owned news site Yunnan.cn said Monday that there had been 30,000 border crossings by Myanmar nationals over the past week into Yunnan province.
16Lin Sen, a volunteer helping refugees in Yunnan, said by phone Tuesday that as many as 50,000 refugees may have escaped Kokang.
17Kokang is dominated by ethnic Han Chinese, and the region has been embroiled in fighting since conflicts between the Kokang rebels and government troops intensified on Feb. 9.
18"The announcement of military administration has no impact on us. This will only make the lives of Kokang people living in Laukkai difficult," Htun Myat Lin, general secretary of the Kokang rebel group, said by phone Tuesday.
19He said government troops had occupied Laukkai.
20He says his group is seeking autonomy for Kokang and its ethnic Han Chinese people.
21"We want to have a high degree of autonomy where we can manage our own affairs, but we do not wish to split the country of Myanmar," Htun said, adding that ethnic Han Chinese are treated as second-class citizens in Myanmar.
22Though preliminary peace pacts have been reached with most of the ethnic groups, clashes occasionally occur with Kachin, Shan and others.
23In addition to control over jade, timber and other natural resources in areas under their control, they want assurances that they will have some say over future troop movements.
