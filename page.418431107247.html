Review: Microsoft Office navigates a world of multiple devices

1NEW YORK -- Microsoft's new Office apps do a good job of helping us navigate a world in which we frequently switch from one device to another -- from a Mac to a Windows PC, with a smartphone or tablet along the way.
2The company released Microsoft Office 2016 for Mac on Thursday and new apps for Android phones two weeks ago.
3New ones for Windows phones and tablets are coming this summer and for Windows PCs this fall.
4They join apps for iPads, iPhones and Android tablets.
5An Office 365 subscription gets you all this -- $70 for a single Mac or Windows PC at a time, or $100 for five.
6Mobile apps are free, though a subscription unlocks advance features that most people won't need.
7Mac and Windows versions are sold the traditional way, too, with a one-time payment, though you can't use Office on another PC without buying it again.
8This product image provided by Microsoft shows Microsoft Word for Mac, part of the new Microsoft Office 2016 Mac suite.
9It took five years, but the Mac version of Office no longer feels second-class.
10The new Office 2016 for Mac is very similar to the existing Windows version, which will get its own Office 2016 update this fall.
11Tabs such as "Home," "Insert" and "Design" are in the same order in both versions, unlike Office 2011 for Mac.
12The Mac gets various keyboard shortcuts I'm used to on Windows.
13No longer do I have to navigate menus to get to things I use often.
14New to Office 2016 is a quick-access sharing button to collaborate more easily.
15If you keep files on Microsoft's OneDrive storage service, changes made by others will sync with your copy.
16No more looking for the latest version of a file in email.
17Again reflecting the multi-device nature of our lives, the "Recent" button will get you the files you recently opened, regardless of the device (at least if you use OneDrive).
18And with one click in Word, you can jump to where you left off on the previous device.
19For spreadsheets, the new version brings a "Recommended Charts" feature from Windows.
20You're shown previews of charts that make the most sense for a given data set.
21For presentations, the Mac gets advanced transitions and other animations previously restricted to Windows.
22Those with an Office 365 subscription can get the Mac Office starting Thursday at http://office.microsoft.com.
23Microsoft will sell Office for a one-time payment starting in September.
24Prices haven't been announced; previous versions started at $140 for a base package that doesn't include Outlook.
25Word for writing, Excel for spreadsheets, PowerPoint for presentations, Outlook for email and OneNote for organization are available for the Mac and all other devices, while less-used apps such as Publisher and Access are for Windows desktops and laptops only.
26This product image provided by Microsoft shows Microsoft Excel for Mac, part of the new Microsoft Office 2016 Mac suite.
27There will be two versions of Office for Windows -- PC and mobile.
28You can run both at once, but I don't recommend it.
29Sometimes a document will open in one version when I really want the other.
30Choose one and stick with it.
31-- Office 2013, to be replaced by Office 2016 this fall, is designed for desktops and laptops.
32While it can also run on tablets, it is better for devices with keyboards and mouse controls.
33-- Mobile versions are designed for tablets and optimized for touch screens.
34But they can also run on desktops and laptops -- at least those that get Windows 10 when the free update comes out July 29.
35(Windows 10 phones will also get new mobile apps.)
36Mobile apps are complementary rather than substitutes for the PC versions.
37They have limits, regardless of whether you use Windows, Android or Apple's iOS.
38For instance, you can open only one document at a time, making cutting and pasting between documents cumbersome.
39They aren't meant for writing novels, at least without a physical keyboard accessory.
40Phones have even fewer features than tablets.
41But mobile is great for proofreading and editing existing documents.
42You can add comments and sync changes across devices through OneDrive.
43I've turned to the tablet on trains and planes, even when a laptop is in my backpack.
44This product image provided by Microsoft shows the new PowerPoint app for Android devices.
45Android versions of Word, Excel and PowerPoint are now available in the Google store.
46Documents preserve fonts, formatting and page breaks from device to device.
47Text is tiny when fitting a full page on a phone's screen, but there's a "Reflow" button to temporarily reformat text for the smaller display.
48Some devices require manual saving, and locations change for tasks such as search and Excel text wrap.
49The iPad app is the most complete, though the upcoming one for Windows tablets comes close.
