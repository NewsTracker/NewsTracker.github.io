'Time running out' for Greece deal

1The eurozone's top official has said "time is running out" for Greece to reach a deal with bailout creditors.
2Eurogroup President Jeroen Dijsselbloem said there is a "great sense of urgency" from all sides to get a deal done.
3But as he went into discussions with his colleagues in the 19-country eurozone he dashed any expectations that one would be secured at the meeting in the Latvian capital of Riga.
4He said it is particularly important for Athens that a deal is secured soon so the country can get the financial support it needs "to make sure there's enough money available to keep the government running".
5Greece had a target of the end of April to agree to more reforms in exchange for rescue money its creditors had set aside.
6Without the money, Greece faces potential bankruptcy.
7Mr Dijsselbloem noted that "April isn't over yet" but said the deadline was far more important for Athens than the Eurogroup.
8For weeks, the meeting in Riga was expected to be the one where Greece's immediate financial future would be sorted out.
9Its left-wing government, elected in January on a mandate to bring crippling austerity to an end, was expected to present its reform plans to its creditors in the eurozone.
10If all worked out as planned, Greece would be handed the remaining money available in its bailout programme - 7.2 billion euro ( 5.2 billion) - to pay off upcoming debts to its creditors in the eurozone and the International Monetary Fund.
11But after weeks of tortuous discussions that have yielded little besides distrust, Greek finance minister Yanis Varoufakis is not expected to present any reform plans at the meeting.
12Creditors have demanded reforms that include sweeping changes to pensions and labour rules.
13But the Greek government has ruled out many key demands, arguing it was elected to end the kind of stifling budget austerity that contributed to a 25% contraction in the economy.
14Its focus is geared far more on fighting corruption and increasing its tax take than more cuts.
15Though fears of a potential Greek debt default and a subsequent exit from the euro have ratcheted up in recent weeks, the prevailing view in markets is a deal will be reached after much soul-searching and bickering - as in the past.
16Many in the markets think Greece has until May 11, when eurozone finance ministers are scheduled to meet next, to secure a deal.
17"While the prospects of possible Greek default haven't receded, it would appear that the timing has been delayed once again as both sides try and come to a deal that they can somehow sell as a solution that will satisfy everybody," said Michael Hewson, chief market analyst at CMC Markets.
18Mr Varoufakis did not speak to reporters as he entered the meeting but insisted in a blog post published today that the "current disagreements are not unbridgeable".
19He said there already is common ground, particularly on revamping the tax system and making pensions fit for purpose by, for example, limiting early retirement.
20"The Greek government wants a fiscal-consolidation path that makes sense, and we want reforms that all sides believe are important," he said.
21Slovakia's finance minister, Peter Kazimir, showed little optimism that a deal would be agreed in the coming days and even ventured that Athens has until the end of June.
22"The end of June is the most important," he said.
23He commented on the lack of progress, saying he was "a little bit tired" by the failure of the Greek authorities to present a properly costed set of proposals that would help break the logjam.
