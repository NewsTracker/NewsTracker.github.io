German tourists hand mayor $1200 as their share of WWII debt to Greece

1Reminder of Nazi occupation in World War II adds tension to talks on contemporary Greek debt.
2Berlin: Last week in the Greek village of Nafpolio, Germans Ludwig Zaccaro and Nina Lange handed 875, about $1200, to the mayor.
3The money, which the mayor said would be given to a local charity, was what the couple figured was their share of Germany's World War II debt to Greece.
4They'd always loved Greece, they said in an interview shown on Greek television, and felt bad about their country's role in the current economic difficulties.
5"Our politicians pretend the Greeks owe debt to Germany, but the reality is that it is the other way around," Ms Lange said.
6Their point of view differs widely from the general German attitude about Greece - 80 per cent, polls show, don't want Germany to give any more aid to Greece and 50 per cent want Greece gone from the eurozone.
7But it strikes at an argument that the new Greek government is pressing: Germany owes Greece money, not the other way around.
8Germany has never repaid money that Germany forced Greece to lend it during World War II, the Greek government says.
9Now the Greeks would like it back, to help repay the $US330 billion ($430 billion) the country owes - $67 billion to Germany.
10The German government of Chancellor Angela Merkel bristles at the suggestion.
11It insists that any German debt from World War II was eliminated with the so-called Two-plus-Four Treaty that made possible the reunification of Germany in 1990.
12"Greece will not be able to cover their debts by constructing German responsibilities dating back to World War II", German Finance Minister Wolfgang Schaeuble said recently.
13"Greece suffers not because of Berlin, or Brussels, but because its own elites have failed for decades."
14"This has all been settled; there can be no more claims," said Volker Kauder, a member of the German parliament.
15But the view is not unanimous.
16Norman Paech, a retired law professor at Hamburg University and one of Germany's leading experts on war reparations, has been making a case for more than a decade that the Greeks have a case.
17"The Greek claims with regard to the loan and German war crimes are legitimate from a political, legal and even more so from a moral point of view," he said.
18Dr Paech argues that German officials are fighting against this obligation for a simple reason: there are other claims.
19He said the legal problem is that the 1953 London Treaty officially put all claims against Germany on hold until a lasting peace treaty could be reached.
20The 1990 treaty that unified Germany is that document, but it was signed only by the two Germanys and the United States, Great Britain, France and Russia.
21That means claims from any other countries are now active - for example, from Greece.
22Gaps are appearing among Germany politicians, too.
23Can Germans really claim anything from a nation they occupied and looted only 70 years ago?
24Not just the fringe parties are raising the question.
25Some members of the Social Democratic Party, partners in government with Ms Merkel's Christian Democrats, are leaning toward the position taken by Mr Zaccaro and Ms Lange in Nafpolio: Something is owed.
