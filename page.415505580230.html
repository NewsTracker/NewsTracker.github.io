2 French police officers on trial over electrocution of 2 youths

Facebook clarifies guidelines on acceptable posts

1Officers Sebastien Gaillemin and Stephanie Klein will face a court in Rennes on charges they failed to prevent the electrocution deaths of Bouna Traore, 15, and Zyed Benna, 17, after the pair hid from police in a high-voltage electricity transformer near their Clichy-sous-Bois housing project northeast of Paris.
2Benna and Traore died in the fenced-off power facility on the afternoon of October 27, 2005 as police milled nearby.
3Their friend Muhittin Altun survived with severe burn injuries.
4If found guilty of failing to help two boys, the officers could face a fine and up to five years in prison.
1Facebook on Monday updated its "community standards" guidelines, giving users more clarity on acceptable posts relating to nudity, violence, hate speech and other contentious topics.
2The world's biggest social network said it does not allow a presence from groups advocating "terrorist activity, organized criminal activity or promoting hate."
3The new guidelines say Facebook will take down "graphic images when they are shared for sadistic pleasure or to celebrate or glorify violence."
4Nudity is also removed in many cases but allowed for images of breastfeeding, art or medical conditions.
5"These standards are designed to create an environment where people feel motivated and empowered to treat each other with empathy and respect," said a blog post from Facebook global policy chief Monika Bickert and deputy general counsel Chris Sonderby.
6"While our policies and standards themselves are not changing, we have heard from people that it would be helpful to provide more clarity and examples, so we are doing so with today's update."
7The new guidelines say Facebook members should use their "authentic name," a move that appears to head off criticism from people who used stage or performance names instead of their legal name.
8In October Facebook said it would ease its "real names" policy that prompted drag queen performers to quit the social network and sparked wider protests in the gay community and beyond.
9Facebook's new guidelines said it would remove content, disable accounts and work with law enforcement "when we believe that there is a genuine risk of physical harm or direct threats to public safety."
10But it also pointed out "that something that may be disagreeable or disturbing to you may not violate our community standards."
11The move comes with Facebook and other social media struggling with defining acceptable content and freedom of expression.
12"It's a challenge to maintain one set of standards that meets the needs of a diverse global community," the blog post said.
13"This is particularly challenging for issues such as hate speech. Hate speech has always been banned on Facebook, and in our new community standards, we explain our efforts to keep our community free from this kind of abusive language."
14Facebook said earlier this year it was putting warnings on "graphic content," which would also be banned for users under 18.
15In 2013, Facebook ended up banning a beheading video after outrage followed a lifting of the ban.
16Twitter meanwhile has become the latest online platform to ban "revenge porn," or the posting of sexually explicit images of a person without consent.
17Twitter faced threats after blocking accounts linked to supporters of the Islamic State, but one study showed at least 46,000 Twitter accounts have been linked to the group.
18Facebook at the same time released its report on government requests for user data in the second half of 2014, showing a modest uptick to 35,051 from 34,946 in the prior period.
19"There was an increase in data requests from certain governments such as India, and decline in requests from countries such as the United States and Germany," the blog post said.
20The amount of content restricted for violating local law increased by 11% 9,707 cases from 8,774.
21"We saw a rise in content restriction requests from countries like Turkey and Russia, and declines in places like Pakistan," Facebook said.
