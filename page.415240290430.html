1It's unclear how many Iranian troops are on the ground for the campaign to wrest control of Salaheddin province, and the strategic city of Tikrit, back from IS, but some are.
2More importantly, their leader is the head of Iran's paramilitary Quds Force, Major-General Qasim Suleimani, and Iranian-trained, Iraqi Shiite militias are boasting that they make up more than two-thirds of a 30,000-strong force marshalled by Baghdad.
3In an eerie echo of the burning of Kuwaiti oil fields during the Gulf War, IS militants set fire to oil wells north-east o Tikrit to obstruct an assault by Shiite militia and Iraqi soldiers trying to drive them from the area.
4A witness and a military source said IS fighters ignited the Ajil oil field to shield themselves from attack by Iraqi military helicopters.
5US officials deny any coordination with the Iranians.
6But, according to The New York Times, US war planners monitor the Iranian war effort by various means - including the use of radio channels that each side knows are used by the other.
7And Iraqi command centres also act as intermediaries, to ensure that there is no conflict between the forces of Washington and Tehran.
8A volunteer Shiite fighter prepares to join the battle against IS for control of the Iraqi city of Tikrit.
9US and Australian military trainers are still working to retrain the Iraqi Army, which collapsed in the face of IS attacks last year, which means that the ground campaign that is such an essential adjunct to the US-led air campaign against IS is effectively being run from the Iranian capital.
10Former Obama special adviser Vali Nasr, now dean of the School of Advanced International Studies at Johns Hopkins University, told the Times: "The only way the Obama administration can credibly stick with its strategy is by implicitly assuming that the Iranians will carry most of the weight and win the battles on the ground.
11"You can't have your cake and eat it too - the US strategy in Iraq has been successful so far largely because of Iran."
12The battle for Salaheddin is not the first such joint effort - General Suleimani led an Iranian contingent on the ground while the US bombed from the air to help win the Shiite town of Amerli back from IS control.
13And when it seemed that IS was within striking distance of the northern Kurdish stronghold of Erbil, it was the Quds Force leader who arrived with two aircraft laden with military supplies.
14Appearing before a congressional committee this week, the chairman of the US Joint Chiefs of Staff, General Martin Dempsey, made a discreet nod to the Iranian contribution when he said that the involvement of the Iranian-trained militias, all drawn from Iraq's Shiite community, could be "a positive thing".
15He went on: "This is the most overt conduct of Iranian support, in the form of artillery and other things - frankly, it'll only be a problem if it results in sectarianism."
16Others in the region are not so sure.
