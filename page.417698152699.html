Greece crisis: IMF dramatically walks out of creditor negotiations

1The International Monetary Fund dramatically walked out of the Greek creditor negotiations and the president of the European Council has issued a stark warning to Athens that "there is no more time for gambling".
2Greece needs its EU and IMF creditors to release bailout funds in order to repay 1.6bn in liabilities due by the end of the month.
3But both sides have still to reach agreement in a cash-for-reforms deal, pushing the country closer to a potentially disastrous technical default.
4"There are major differences between us in most key areas," said the IMF's spokesman, Gerry Rice, as the fund's lead negotiators returned from Brussels to Washington.
5"There has been no progress in narrowing these differences recently and thus we are well away from an agreement."
6Donald Tusk the European Council president, who had a private meeting with the Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras on 11 June, also issued what amounted to an ultimatum.
7"We need decisions, not negotiations, now," he said.
8"It's my opinion that the Greek Government has to be, I think, a little more realistic. There's no more space for gambling, there's no more time for gambling. The day is coming, I'm afraid, where someone says the game is over".
9Jens Weidmann, the president of Germany's Bundesbank, added to the pressure, saying that Greece's "time was running out" to secure an agreement and that "the risk of insolvency is increasing by the day".
10If Greece fails to make its 1.6bn payment to the IMF by the end of the month, it will be in technical default - which could mean the European Central Bank will no longer accept the country's bonds as security for emergency liquidity lending.
11That could prompt the collapse of the Greek banking system.
12Mr Tsipras has been in meetings with the head of the European Commission, Jean-Claude Juncker, who has been leading attempts to broker a deal between Athens and its creditors.
13Despite the departure of the IMF team from Brussels, Mr Rice said "the IMF never leaves the table. We remain engaged - but the ball very much is in Greece's court right now."

15Gabriel Sakellaridis, a spokesman for the Greek Government, suggested that a deal was coming: "The Greek delegation, as agreed, is ready to intensify deliberations in order to conclude a deal soon, even in the coming days."
16The points of contention between Greece and the creditors are understood to be pensions, taxes and budget surpluses.
17In a sign of the domestic opposition that Mr Tsipras is likely to face if he does a deal, hundreds of supporters of the communist-affiliated anti-bailout trade union PAME demonstrated in Athens.
18They draped a banner across five storeys of the finance ministry showing two previous prime ministers who negotiated with creditors.
19Next to them was a portrait of Mr Tsipras above the slogan "Bailout Number Three".
