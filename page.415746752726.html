Germanwings crash: pilot locked out of plane's cockpit, say reports

1Evidence from a cockpit voice recorder recovered from the Germanwings flight 4U9525 that crashed on Tuesday suggests that one pilot left the cockpit before the plane's descent and was unable to get back in, according to two separate reports.
2French air accident investigators say they have extracted a recording that contains "voices and sounds" from the cockpit of the plane before it crashed in the Alps, killing all 150 people on board.
3A senior military official involved in the investigation told the New York Times that there was a "very smooth, very cool" conversation between the two pilots during the early part of the flight from Barcelona to Dusseldorf.
4Later in the recording, the audio suggests that one of the pilots left the cockpit and could not re-enter.
5The audio gives no indication of the condition of the pilot who remained in the cockpit.
6"The guy outside is knocking lightly on the door and there is no answer," the investigator, who asked to remain anonymous, told the New York Times.
7"And then he hits the door stronger and no answer. There is never an answer.
8"You can hear he is trying to smash the door down."
9"We don't know yet the reason why one of the guys went out," said the official, who requested anonymity because the investigation is continuing.
10"But what is sure is that at the very end of the flight, the other pilot is alone and does not open the door."
11The French news agency AFP reported that it had been told by a source close to the investigation that one of the pilots had been "locked out" the cockpit.
12The agency said the cockpit recording indicated that one of the pilot's seats was pushed back and the door opened and closed.
13Knocking is then heard, said the source, adding "there was no more conversation from that point until the crash".
14The source said an alarm indicating the proximity to the ground could be heard before the moment of impact.
15French air accident investigators confirmed on Wednesday that they have extracted a recording that contains "voices and sounds" from the cockpit of the plane.
16The French Bureau d'Enquetes et d'Analyses (BEA) said it was "optimistic" that the second black box, the flight data recorder, would be found and the mystery of the crash solved.
17"We have an audio file we can use," said Remi Jouty, the director of the BEA, at a press conference in Paris.
18The task of the investigators in the first instance is "a job of understanding the sounds, the alarms, the voices, to attribute the voices to different people," he added.
19"The crash zone is two hectares, which is big but not massive. We are combing the site and we will find the flight data recorder, which is built to resist a severe crash. I am confident we will find out what happened."
20The Airbus A320 that ploughed into an Alpine mountain flew "right to the end", the investigators said, and did not explode mid-flight.
21It also appeared not to have suffered a sudden drop in pressure.
22The BEA, said that "usable" material had been extracted from the black box cockpit voice recorder found at the crash site, in the French Alps.
23Jouty said it covered the entire flight but would not say what conversations, if any, between the pilots had been captured on the recording, nor what language they had been conducted in.
24Neither of the plane's two pilots have been named, but the captain had more than 10 years of experience and had clocked up more than 6,000 flight hours on the Airbus model.
25The co-pilot joined Greenwings in September 2013, directly after training, and had flown 630 hours, Lufthansa, the parent airline of Germanwings, said.
26The A320 was on its second flight of the day, having earlier left Dusseldorf for Barcelona, landing at 8.57am, before its second takeoff for the 90-minute flight.
27On Wednesday Lufthansa CEO Carsten Spohr, himself a pilot, said he found the crash of a plane flown by two experienced captains while at cruising altitude "inexplicable."
28Cruise is considered the safest part of a flight, with only about 10% of crashes occurring at that altitude.
29"We still cannot understand what happened there yesterday," he said.
30"Lufthansa has never in its history lost an aircraft in cruise flight and we cannot understand how an airplane that was in perfect technical condition, with two experienced and trained Lufthansa pilots, was involved in such a terrible accident."
31In Seyne-les-Alpes, the picturesque alpine mountain village of 1,500 people which has been transformed into a makeshift centre of operations, support staff received the first relatives of the victims.
32Many families had chosen to fly to Marseille overnight and be taken by bus to the Alps to get as near as possible to the crash site and to begin grieving as they waited for relatives' remains.
33The French president, Francois Hollande, visited the area on Wednesday afternoon with the German chancellor, Angela Merkel, and Spanish prime minister, Mariano Rajoy.
34At a joint press conference they pledged to do everything they could to find the cause of the crash.
35In the village, there was a mood of grief and sadness.
36Looking up at the mountain beyond which the plane had crashed, Maurice Borel, a retired volunteer firefighter, said: "We're in shock, nothing of this scale has ever happened here before."
37The Seyne-les-Alpes youth centre, which would normally be packed with riotous children's sports clubs on a Wednesday afternoon, had been made into a silent makeshift chapel and remembrance centre for the victims' relatives.
38"We just feel so powerless," said Rene Dufour, a retired textile worker, as he looked out towards the snowy peaks beyond which the plane crashed.
39"All we can do is send all our thoughts to the families."
