Justice Dept. opens Baltimore police probe; 1K protest

1The Justice Department said Tuesday it has opened a civil rights investigation into the death of Freddie Gray, a black man who suffered a fatal spinal-cord injury under mysterious circumstances after he was handcuffed and put in the back of a police van.
2After the probe was announced, at least 1,000 people gathered at a previously planned rally at the site of Gray's arrest.
3Protesters marched to a police station a couple of blocks away, chanting and holding signs that read "Black Lives Matter" and "No Justice, No Peace" - slogans that have come to embody what demonstrators believe is widespread mistreatment of blacks by police.
4Pricilla Jackson carried a sign reading, "Convict Freddie's killers," that listed the names of the six officers suspended with pay while local authorities and the U.S. Justice Department investigate the death.
5Jackson, who is black, said she wants Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake to know that she and others have been brutalized by police.
6At least one activist was detained when he jumped past police barriers.
7Gray's mother, Gloria Darden, was overcome with grief and carried away at one point by several men, writhing and sobbing uncontrollably, her face obscured by a hood and dark glasses.
8The crowd parted to let her through.
9Another female family member collapsed in tears and was also helped away.
10As the sun fell, some protesters lit candles and the demonstration peacefully dispersed.
11More protests were planned for later in the week.
12Gray, 25, was taken into custody April 12 after police "made eye contact" with him and another man in an area known for drug activity, police said.
13Gray was handcuffed and put in a transport van.
14At some point during his roughly 30-minute ride, the van was stopped and Gray's legs were shackled when an officer felt he was becoming "irate," police said.
15Police Commissioner Anthony Batts said that Gray asked for an inhaler, and then several times asked for medical care.
16He was eventually rushed to a hospital.
17Gray died Sunday - a week after his arrest - of what police described as "a significant spinal injury."
18Exactly how he was injured and what happened in the van is not known.
19Justice Department spokeswoman Dena Iverson said investigators are "gathering information to determine whether any prosecutable civil rights violation occurred."
20It's not uncommon for federal investigators to look into allegations of excessive police force.
21Justice Department investigations in the last year include probes into the fatal shooting of an unarmed black 18-year-old in Ferguson, Missouri - a case that resulted in no charges against the officer - and an ongoing review of a police chokehold death of a New York City man.
22There's a high threshold for bringing federal civil rights charges against police officers in such cases.
23Federal investigators must show an officer willfully deprived a person of his or her civil rights by using more force than the law allows, a standard that's challenging in rapidly unfolding confrontations in which snap judgments are made.
24The mayor said she welcomed the Justice Department probe.
25"Whenever a police force conducts an internal investigation, there are always appropriate questions of transparency and impartiality," she said.
26"My goal has always been to get answers to the questions so many of us are still asking with regards to Mr. Gray's death."
27In the Baltimore case, the six suspended officers have been on the force anywhere from three years to 18 years.
28According to court documents, Officer Garrett Miller accused Gray of carrying a switchblade, which was discovered in Gray's pocket after he was stopped.
29- Lt. Brian Rice, 41, with the department since 1997.
30- Sgt. Alicia White, 30, with the department since 2010.
31- Officer Caesar Goodson, 45, who has been there since 1999.
32- Officers William Porter and Edward Nero, who along with Miller, all joined in 2012.
33Kim Deachilla, a spokeswoman for the Baltimore Fraternal Order of Police Lodge 3, said a law firm that contracts with the union is representing them.
34The officers' specific roles in the arrest were not released by city officials.
35Bystander video of the arrest shows officers on bicycles, in patrol cars and outside the transport van.
36The lawyer for Gray's family said he believes the police had no reason to stop him.
37"Running while black is not probable cause. Felony running doesn't exist, and you can't arrest someone for looking you in the eye," attorney Billy Murphy said.
38Batts said the reason for Gray's stop is "a question we have to dig into."
39Harold Perry, 73, a retired small businessman who is nearly blind, said he heard the arrest through his bedroom window.
40A young man was screaming "You're hurting me! Get your knee off my back," Perry said.
41In the bystander video, Gray is screaming, but it's not clear what he is saying.
42At a news conference Monday, officials vowed transparency and pledged to hold those found responsible accountable.
43Batts said the investigation will be completed by May 1 and the results will be sent to the state attorney's office to determine whether criminal charges will be filed.
44Batts also said he is ordering that police review and rewrite "effective immediately" its policies on moving prisoners and providing them with medical attention.
