Ukraine arrests two Emergency Services Ministry officials on live TV, sacks oligarch governor

1Moscow: In a carefully orchestrated spectacle calculated to dramatise a newly aggressive anti-corruption campaign, Ukrainian authorities arrested two senior government officials during a televised cabinet meeting.
2Prime Minister Arseniy Yatsenyuk, who was in the room, declared it a warning to public officials about abusing their offices or stealing from taxpayers.
3With the cameras rolling on Wednesday and top government officials sitting around a huge wooden conference table, police officers in thick bulletproof vests and knit caps handcuffed the director of Ukraine's Emergency Services Ministry, Sergiy Bochkovsky, and his deputy, Vasyl Stoyetsky, and led them from the room.
4Ukrainian President Petro Poroshenko has fired the billionaire governor of the eastern region of Dneprotrovsk.
5Both Mr Bochkovsky and Mr Stoyetsky were wearing dark uniforms covered in medals.
6"This will happen to everyone who breaks the law and sneers at the Ukrainian state," Mr Yatsenyuk told journalists who watched the arrests.
7The Interior Ministry later said that Mr Bochkovsky and Mr Stoyetsky would be charged with crimes including embezzlement and abuse of power.
8Emergency Services Ministry deputy Vasyl Stoyetsky is arrested during a Cabinet meeting broadcast live on TV.
9The Ukrainian government has long been hobbled by deep-rooted corruption and mismanagement, a source of angry, public frustration that was a factor in the street protests in Kiev last year that ousted president Viktor Yanukovych.
10However, the efforts to fight corruption and hold former public officials accountable have proved to be frustratingly slow and largely ineffective, as the new government has been forced to deal first with Russia's invasion and annexation of Crimea, and then with a war waged by Russian-backed separatists in the eastern regions of Donetsk and Luhansk.
11With Ukraine's economy collapsing, officials sought international assistance.
12Western allies of the Kiev government have been eager to help but have stepped up pressure for a more aggressive approach on corruption, making it a condition for assistance.
13Even so, a new anti-corruption bureau, formed partly at the behest of the International Monetary Fund, still does not have a director.
14However, in recent weeks, several steps have been taken in the government and in Parliament, including the dismissal of the country's top prosecutor.
15Last month, the authorities arrested a former leader of Mr Yanukovych's Party of Regions, Oleksandr Yefremov, on charges of abuse of power in connection with another embezzlement scheme; this one involving overpayment by the government for coal.
16Mr Yefremov was released on bail.
17And this month, the authorities in Spain arrested a former Ukrainian finance minister, Yuri Kolobov, who is accused - along with other senior officials in the Yanukovych government - of misappropriating millions of dollars.
18So far, he is the only former minister to be detained, though others are being sought, including several who have fled to Russia.
19The arrests of the two emergency services officials came hours after President Petro Poroshenko dismissed the billionaire governor of Dneprotrovsk, Igor Kolomoisky, in a dispute that critics said reflected other murky dealings between the government and the country's richest businessmen.
20Mr Kolomoisky was angry over legislation that curtailed his power over two state-controlled energy companies in which he owned a minority stake.
21The battle with Mr Kolomoisky, who had been one of the government's staunchest allies, raised questions about other disputes that may unfold between the country's oligarchs.
22The oligarchs have long found ways to benefit from the mismanagement and malfeasance in the Ukrainian government.
23Private militias financed by Mr Kolomoisky had helped prevent Russian-backed separatists from advancing from Donetsk and Luhansk into the heart of Ukraine.
24Some prominent members of Parliament had called for Mr Kolomoisky's removal and on Wednesday said it should be just the start of a campaign to reduce the influence of the oligarchs.
25In the meantime, the government seemed intent on making an example of the emergency services officials, who, they said, had overseen a wide-scale corruption scheme in which fuel for government vehicles was purchased at inflated prices.
26Anton Gerashchenko, a member of Parliament who serves on a board overseeing the Interior Ministry, said that up to 20 per cent of the amount spent on fuel was diverted to bank accounts of Mr Bochkovsky and Mr Stoyetsky in Cyprus and in Jersey.
27"Search operations conducted by the Ministry of Internal Affairs over the last six months helped detect and document in detail the whole vertical of corruption led by the top leadership [of the Emergency Services Ministry], Mr Gerashchenko said.
