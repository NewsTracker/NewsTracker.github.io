Apple Music Review: Messy Yet Magical

1Apple's new on-demand streaming music service has a fantastic collection of playlists, but for $10 a month, it's still far from perfect.
2WSJ's Joanna Stern reviews Apple Music in song.
3Photo/video: Drew Evans/The Wall Street Journal.
4Let me get this out of the way: I don't love Apple Music.
5But I didn't love the original iPod, either.
6I didn't consider buying Steve Jobs' little white phenomenon when it was first released.
7No need, I already had a perfectly functional Rio MP3 player.
8Of course, we all know how that song ended.
9Rio didn't live to see 'N Sync break up, while the iPod upended the music industry.
10Apple's brand new streaming music service is a lot like the first iPod.
11If you've already used Spotify, Rdio or the handful of other streaming music services, there's nothing remarkably new or different about Apple's.
12In some respects, it isn't as good as them.
13But it still stands to change how the world listens to music.
14Apple Music is jam packed with music recommendations.
15The perfect mixtape for Joanna Stern's experience getting to know Apple Music.
16See, with a small software update, Apple is putting on-demand streaming of 30 million songs on most iPhones, iPads, Macs and iTunes-equipped Windows PCs-completely free for three months.
17After the trial period, it costs $10 a month, $15 for up to six people in your family.
18All you need is an Internet connection and, well, to decide if streaming is worth it for you.
19So, if you can try it free, why do you need to read this review?
20Because, having used all of the leading competitors, I can say that while Apple Music may be taking the secret of on-demand streaming to the masses, the app itself isn't ready for them.
21Here's how Apple Music isn't at all like that first iPod: It is so jam packed with features, it lacks polish and simplicity.
22But its ability to know what I want to listen to next is so darn good, we may all be willing to live with the shortcomings.
23Think of Apple Music's interface as a game of Memory.
24The iPhone and iPad app, organized into five tabs, is so crammed with items, lists and menus that it's hard to find things initially, harder still to remember where they are later.
25Seriously, it's like Russian nesting dolls: menus within menus within menus!
26Worse, things you'd assume would be simple aren't.
27It's what I call the "Am I an idiot?" interface problem.
28For instance, I couldn't figure out how to jump to Elton John's artist page when "Your Song" popped up in a great suggested playlist.
29Apple Music is divided into five different sections.
30Turns out I wasn't the idiot.
31Instead of just tapping the artist's name (which brings up a star rating option), you have to tap the three-dot menu button (as opposed to the three-line menu button) and then the song name (but not "Show in iTunes," which takes you to a completely different app!)
32Even when I had mastered that, I often landed on a broken "Unknown Artist" screen.
33That's why they call it the blues, indeed.
34Siri to the rescue!
35At times the best workaround for interface confusion was beckoning Siri with commands like "Search for Elton John in Apple Music."
36Not only can she search for specific songs or artists, she knows the top charts better than Casey Kasem ever did.
37"Play the best songs of 1979," and next thing you know you're dancing to "My Sharona."
38(But beware: As with HomeKit, Siri must be commanded with specific strings of words.)
39You begin by telling the app what sort of music you like.
40Too bad Siri can't help you navigate iTunes on a Mac or PC.
41Apple's bloated 14-year old media manager needed more features like CVS needs another toothpaste shelf.
42The already sluggish and hard-to-navigate software is made worse by the addition of Apple Music.
43I got used to the interface in a couple of days but, like on iOS, I encountered bugs in both the Mac and Windows versions.
44All your iTunes-purchased tracks and any locally stored music are found under the My Music tab.
45(You can also sync all your old songs, either with the $25-per-year iTunes Match, or Apple's new iCloud Music Library, part of Apple Music which is required for other features.)
46You can add anything you hear to My Music, even playlists, and download selections for offline listening.
47As long as you keep paying for Apple Music, you'll "have" those new tracks.
48Spotify and Rdio's more mature Web and mobile apps are easier to use for the time being, but oddly it is Apple Music's clunky iTunes integration that makes it so good at guessing what you want to hear.
49It takes your years of iTunes listening and analyzes your tastes, then goes further, asking you about your preferences.
50When you first use it, you see a screen of floating magenta circles (a survey tool Apple got when it bought Beats Music).
51A tap on the Pop circle told Apple I like the hits, double taps on Classic Rock and Indie expressed my love for the good stuff.
52And a press-and-hold on Metal said I hate the sounds of Megadeth and Metallica.
53You can tell Apple what you don't want to hear, too.
54Immediately, the For You tab populated with fantastically curated and cleverly named pop and classic-rock playlists.
55"Lying in a Hammock" captured my love for Cat Stevens and Bon Iver alike, while "Love Is Just a Lie" reminded me that it had been far too long since I'd listened to Dire Straits and Joni Mitchell.
56(Apple: Even though I'm a big fan of your breakup mixes, I promise you I am happily married.)
57The page also recommended whole albums by Eric Clapton, Paul Simon, Taylor Swift, Sara Bareilles and Third Eye Blind, a near-perfect snapshot of my wide range of tastes.
58Those same albums are available on Spotify and Rdio, but they've never been recommended to me.
59And with so many millions of songs available, I never went looking for them.
60(Most of these services have virtually the same libraries, including almost every big name artist you're looking for, with the exception of The Beatles and Prince. Taylor Swift is only available in her entirety on Apple Music.)
61In the week since Apple Music launched, I periodically missed Spotify's selection of acoustic playlists, but in the long term, what I may miss most are Spotify's social tools.
62While you can create and share an Apple Music playlist on Twitter or Facebook, you can't follow friends or find fan-made playlists directly on the service.
63And Apple Music wasn't a perfect concierge, either.
64Despite me registering my dislike for country music, I continued to see playlists like "Redneck Women" and "Family-Friendly Country Songs" in my feed.
65A week later, I'm still periodically offered some Carrie Underwood or Kenny Chesney.
66Apple says the more feedback I give the service, the better it will get.
67Even if Apple doesn't nail your tastes, that's just one slice of the pie.
68There's the New tab, which is teeming with even more human-made playlists, top chart lists and music videos.
69I spent a full hour this weekend getting happily lost in there.
70The Radio tab lets you tune into Beats 1, the service's 24/7 global radio station hosted by some of the world's most famous DJs.
71When I tuned in, I felt like I was at a club with a bunch of people I wasn't cool enough to hang with.
72Not to worry, there are over 50 other human-powered radio stations and a Pandora-like option for automated streams based off stuff you like.
73As it stands right now, it's hard to give Apple Music my $10 a month.
74I love how well it integrates with my own personal music collection, and recommends great music throughout the day, but there are too many bugs and frustrations to battle.
75And while it will work on my home Sonos speaker system, it doesn't yet.
76For now, my $10 feels better spent with Spotify.
77I suspect this is really why Apple has given us all three months of free service.
78Not only does it let us see if streaming is even right for us, but it gives the company time to fix some issues before paying users get angry.
79The real decision doesn't come now, but in three months.
80And that may be all the time Apple needs.
81After all, when the second-gen iPod came out, I replaced my Rio with one.
82I ended up loving it.
