Netflix and Net Neutrality: Whose Briar Patch Is This Anyway?

Obama: Racial bias within Ferguson police not isolated

1The Federal Communications Commission hasn't yet officially released its order containing the new net neutrality rules, but there are already a couple of fascinating developments that may give new meaning to the law of unintended consequences.
2Netflix Netflix, which is not only the reigning king of over-the-top video but the most dominant source of Internet video consumption, is a particularly fascinating case study.
3Netflix bestrides the online video world like a colossus, with its streaming usage increasing 350% in the last 10 quarters, and expectations of over 100 million global subscribers by 2020.
4But on net neutrality policy and practice, I can't decide whether they look more like John Kerry in the 2004 U.S. Presidential campaign or old Brer Rabbit if he had forgotten which briar patch he wanted to be thrown into.
5As you may recall, then-U.S. Senator (now Secretary of State) John Kerry, in the 2004 campaign, while explaining his position on funding for the Iraq War, famously stated that "I actually did vote for [it] before I voted against it."
6Netflix has gone a silly little step further - it seems to be promoting positions both for and against net neutrality at precisely the same time!
7Shall I explain?
8In late winter 2014, Netflix famously signed direct, paid "pairing agreements" with both Comcast Comcast and Verizon intended to "improve streaming quality for [Netflix] viewers."
9And this Netflix approach to working directly with Internet Service Providers didn't end last year.
10Just this past week, it was reported that Netflix has entered into an arrangement with Australia ISP iiiNet to exempt Netflix's video traffic (and its users) from broadband caps imposed by the ISP.
11None of this activity is particularly shocking for a company like Netflix given how critical the quality of the video performance is for its customers (who wants to keep watching Internet video if you're fighting buffering the whole time?)
12But it should hardly warm the heart of pro-net neutrality activists.
13In the last week, Netflix seems to be pushing its 2004 Kerry impersonation to the limits.
14A year ago, very shortly after it negotiated its Comcast and Verizon deals, Netflix CEO Reed Hastings unleashed an oft-cited blog post entitled "Internet Tolls And The Case For Strong Net Neutrality."
15Netflix has been aggressive in its support for a tough net neutrality approach for a year, rallying supporters online, and declaring at the end of FCC FCC debate that "The FCC settled it: consumers win."
16Yet two days ago, Netflix's CFO publicly stated: ""Were we pleased it pushed to Title II?
17Probably .[W]e were hoping there might be a non-regulated solution." Huh? My kingdom for a consistent message!
18Now here's where I'm seeing a little twist on Brer Rabbit from Netflix.
19Remember how Brer Rabbit begged Brer Fox Fox not to be thrown into the briar patch, a ruse because the rabbit "grew up in the briar patch?"
20Well, Netflix certainly has been clamoring to be thrown into the Title II common carrier regulation briar patch, but unlike Brer Rabbit, why did it get so uncomfortable so quickly?
21Does Netflix perhaps see a more favorable set of private agreements it can do with ISPs here in the fashion it just negotiated in Australia?
22Nothing in the FCC's statements to date indicates that ISPs can't impose broadband caps charge heavy Internet users more (usage based-pricing).
23As I noted last week in Forbes, I expect that the new Title II regime will encourage a far more forceful move by ISPs to implement broadband caps and usage-based pricing for consumers.
24This is not exactly a new idea - former FCC Chairman Julius Genachowski effectively endorsed such an approach three years ago, and leading media analyst Craig Moffett has been writing for some time now that ISPs ought to be charging for "transport" across all of their service platforms (including multichannel video).
25This is a rational next step for ISPs given ever-increasing programming costs (and squeezed margins) in the video business, and the new FCC authority to limit wholesale agreements on the broadband side (which might mitigate ISP costs).
26But I don't see this as too appealing to the heaviest retail users or a boost to the overall growth of broadband usage (after all, online usage really took off when AOL went in the opposite direction - to all you can eat pricing).
27Netflix has shown in Australia how it might seek to preserve its position even under such an approach.
28Is Netflix now concerned that this type of arrangement will come under attack here?
29There are many miles to go before any of us sleep on the net neutrality issue, and I've got far more questions than answers about what comes next.
30I do expect many surprising twists and turns for and by ISPs, application providers, technology vendors, content creators, brands and many others who never before knew they needed to pay such close attention to what happens in Washington, DC on the 8th floor at the Portals (otherwise known as the FCC floor where the Chairman and commissioners work) and in courts that will review everything that takes place there.
31And probably a whole lot of confusing briar patches along the way.
