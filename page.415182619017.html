Kenya torches 15 tonnes of illegal ivory in anti-poaching fight

1Kenya has burnt 15 tonnes of ivory and promised to destroy all of its stockpile this year in the latest step in its campaign against elephant poaching.
2Poaching has surged in the past few years across sub-Saharan Africa, where gangs kill elephants and rhinos to feed Asian demand for ivory and horns for use in folk medicines.
3A 2014 United Nations and Interpol report estimated that about 20,000 to 25,000 elephants were killed in Africa every year, out of a total population of as many as 650,000.
4"As part of Kenya's continued policy to put ivory beyond economic use ... I will today burn 15 tonnes of ivory at this historic site in Nairobi National Park," president Uhuru Kenyatta said during a ceremony at the park.
5"In order to underline our determination to eradicate poaching, my government shall burn the rest of the stockpile within this year."
6The ceremony was to mark the African Union-designated Wangari Maathai Day, in honour of the late environmentalist and Nobel Peace Prize laureate, and the World Wildlife Day.
7Kenya has imposed longer jail terms and bigger fines for wildlife poaching and trafficking, saying poaching is harming tourism, a major earner of foreign exchange.
8In January 2014, a Kenyan court convicted a Chinese man of smuggling ivory and ordered him to pay a fine of 20 million shillings ($233,000) or serve seven years in jail, the first sentencing since Kenya introduced the new anti-poaching law.
9A Kenyan man has also been taken into police custody accused of being behind an international poaching syndicate involving elephant tusks and has been linked to a three-tonne haul of ivory seized in Mombasa last June.
10"Illegal trade in wildlife has become a sophisticated transnational form of crime, comparable to other pernicious examples, such as trafficking of drugs, humans, counterfeit items and oil," UN secretary general Ban Ki-Moon said in a statement.
11"It is driven by rising demand and is often facilitated by corruption and weak governance. There is strong evidence of the increased involvement of organised crime networks and non-state armed groups."
12In 2011, then-president Mwai Kibaki set fire to five tonnes of contraband ivory.
13His predecessor, Daniel Toroitich arap Moi, did the same in 1989.
