Islamic State battle to dominate US-Iraq White House talks

1WASHINGTON: Iraq's prime minister will plead for more US military support in the war against insurgent Islamic State jihadists during a first White House meeting with President Barack Obama on Tuesday.
2Embarking on his inaugural prime ministerial trip to Washington, Haider al-Abadi said his top priority would be to secure a "marked increase" in the US-led air campaign and in the "delivery of arms."
3Abadi arrives in the United States with swathes of his country -- including the second city, Mosul -- still under occupation by militants bent on establishing an Islamic caliphate.
4His armed forces, ravaged by years of war, desertion and underfunding, have parried the Islamic State's advance on Baghdad, but struggled to get on the front foot.
5The IS group has lost control of 25 to 30 percent of the territory it holds in the country following the Iraqi offensive and nearly 1,900 coalition air strikes, according to the Pentagon.
6Pentagon spokesman Colonel Steve Warren said the lost territory amounts to some 13,000 to 17,000 square kilometers (5,000 to 6,600 square miles).
7Critics say Obama's drawdown of US troops in Iraq was a strategic blunder that allowed the Islamic State to flourish.
8A recent Iraqi effort to wrest control of Tikrit stalled until the US military pounded IS positions with air strikes.
9There has already been an increase in US support, "but we want to see more," said Abadi before boarding his plane bound for the American capital.
10Obama has made degrading and destroying the Islamic State a priority, fearing the organization's foothold in Iraq and Syria provides a base for terror operations, while destabilizing those countries and the region.
11The United States is leading an international coalition waging a campaign of air strikes against Islamic State and also providing arms and training for Iraqi security forces.
12Tuesday's talks at the White House are expected to focus on defining the next steps in that fight.
13Tikrit had initially been seen as a dress rehearsal for Abadi's much-touted "Spring offensive" to dislodge insurgent fighters from the vital oil hub of Mosul.
14But amid skepticism that Iraqi forces are up to the task, Abadi last week announced the next steps may come in Anbar province.
15Washington now says the attack on Mosul "needs to happen when it's ready to happen" and "shouldn't be on a fixed timetable but rather when all the ducks are in a row."
16"It's going to take a lot of capacity, which is going to take time to build," said one official.
17There is concern in Washington that Shiite militias fighting the IS group are controlled by the Iranian government rather than Abadi's.
18Whatever the strategy, victory and stability are unlikely to come quickly.
19"This is a long-term campaign," said one US official.
20"It is going to be a long, long, long haul, I cannot overemphasize that."
