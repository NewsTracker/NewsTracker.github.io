Germanwings crash: Lubitz told Lufthansa of depression

1Lufthansa's chief executive had previously said the company was not aware of anything that could have driven the co-pilot to crash the Airbus A320 into the French Alps and that he was 100 per cent fit to fly.
2Lufthansa said Andreas Lubitz broke off his pilot training for a period of several months but then passed medical checks confirming his fitness to fly.
3When he resumed training in 2009, he provided the flight school with medical documents showing that he had gone through a "previous episode of severe depression," Lufthansa said, citing emailed correspondence between Lubitz and the flight school.
4Dusseldorf state prosecutors said on Monday that Lubitz had been treated for suicidal tendencies before getting his pilot's licence.
5They last week found torn-up sick notes showing that Lubitz was suffering from an illness that should have grounded him.
6Germanwings said it had not received a sick note from Lubitz for the day of the crash.
7Lufthansa said it had passed the email correspondence and additional documents to the Duesseldorf prosecutors after internal investigations.
8Lufthansa was already facing unlimited liability for damages in the crash, lawyers have said, and has told its insurers to set aside $300 million to deal with claims, recovery costs and the loss of the aircraft.
9Tuesday's statement is likely to raise further questions over pilot screening processes.
10Lawyers representing some of the families of victims of the Germanwings crash called on Tuesday for more psychiatric testing of pilots.
