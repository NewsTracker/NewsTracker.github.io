More than 3,200 confirmed dead in Nepal earthquake

1The official death toll in Nepal following a 7.9 magnitude earthquake at the weekend has risen to 3,218.
2The toll for Nepal had previously stood at 2,430.
3Around 90 people are also known to have died in neighbouring countries, including India and China.
4Hundreds of Nepalis were fleeing the capital Kathmandu for the plains after two days of powerful aftershocks and faced with shortages of food and water.
5Roads leading out of the mountain valley city of one million were jammed with people, many with babies in their arms, trying to climb onto buses or hitch a ride aboard cars and trucks.
6Many said they had slept in the open since Saturday's quake, either because their homes were flattened or they were terrified that the aftershocks would bring them crashing down.
7Overwhelmed authorities were trying to cope with a shortage of drinking water and food, as well as the threat of disease.
8The sick and wounded were lying out in the open in Kathmandu, unable to find beds in the devastated city's hospitals.
9Surgeons set up an operating theatre inside a tent in the grounds of Kathmandu Medical College.
10In the Himalayas, hundreds of foreign and Nepali climbers were trapped after a huge avalanche ripped through the Mount Everest base camp, killing 17 people in the single worst disaster to hit the world's highest mountain.
11Across Kathmandu and beyond, exhausted families laid mattresses out on streets and erected tents to shelter from rain.
12People queued for water dispensed from the back of trucks, while the few shops still open had next to nothing on their shelves.
13More than 6,500 were injured in Saturday's earthquake.
14Another 66 were killed across the border in India and at least another 20 in Tibet, China's state news agency said.
15Nearly a million children have been "severely affected" by the earthquake, a spokesman for the United Nations Children's Fund said, as rescue and aid workers struggle to cope.
16UNICEF said its relief workers were watching for waterborne and infectious diseases.
17"What we know that at this point is there are nearly a million children who are severely affected. Our biggest concern for them right now is going to be access to clean water and sanitation, we know that water and food is running out," UNICEF's Christopher Tidey said.
18The toll is likely to rise as rescuers struggle to reach remote regions in the impoverished, mountainous country of 28 million people and as bodies buried under rubble are recovered.
19With so many people sleeping in the open with no power or water and downpours forecast, fears mounted of major food and water shortages.
20Across Nepal, hundreds of villages have been left to fend for themselves.
21Several countries rushed to send aid and personnel.
22India flew in medical supplies and members of its National Disaster Response Force.
23China sent a 60-strong emergency team.
24Pakistan's army said it was sending four C-130 aircraft with a 30-bed hospital, search and rescue teams and relief supplies.
25A Pentagon spokesman said a US military aircraft with 70 personnel is due in Kathmandu today.
26Australia, Britain and New Zealand said they were sending specialist urban search-and-rescue teams to Kathmandu at Nepal's request.
27Britain, which believes several hundred of its nationals are in Nepal, was also delivering supplies and medics.
28However, there has been little sign of international assistance on the ground so far, with some aid flights prevented from landing by aftershocks that closed Kathmandu's main airport several times yesterday.
29In the Himalayas, hundreds of climbers felt tremors yesterday powerful enough to send snow and boulders cascading towards them.
30Another was felt early this morning.
31A Co Antrim man in Nepal has described the situation in Kathmandu as "ugly" and "very tense".
32Barry Torrens from Portrush, who has had been trekking in Nepal, said there is no electricity anywhere in Kathmandu except a couple of the larger embassies, which have generators.
33He said the loss of power and communications is one thing, but "it's a very different matter running out of water".
34Speaking on RTE's Morning Ireland, he said there are a "tremendous amount of gangs of people, locals roaming the city looking for water."
35He said they were able to buy some water first thing yesterday morning, but all the shops had run out by noon local time.
36Mr Torrens said he along with 200 other people are sitting in the garden of the British Embassy where they had sought shelter.
37He said they slept outside their accommodation on Saturday night and there were aftershocks every two to three hours.
38Mountain rescue teams, helped by clear weather, used helicopters to airlift climbers stranded for two days at high altitude on Mount Everest after the earthquake.
39Romanian climber Alex Gavan said on Twitter that three helicopters had reached camps one and two, which are at altitudes of more than 6,000 metres.
40Each helicopter is only capable of carrying two climbers due to the thin air, Mr Gavan said from base camp.
41Carsten Lillelund Pedersen, a climber from Denmark, posted a video on Facebook showing a helicopter landing to evacuate two climbers and then taking off again 30 seconds later.
42Survivors on Everest described a cloud of rock and ice that smashed into base camp on Saturday.
43More than 60 people were injured, leading climbers to send frantic messages calling for helicopter assistance to evacuate the wounded.
44An estimated 100 climbers and guides were safe but trapped at both camps which rendered the treacherous Khumbu icefalls leading up to them from base camp impassable.
45Japan's foreign ministry on Monday said a Japanese man was among those killed on the Everest, the fourth foreign national claimed by the avalanche.
46Three Americans were killed, the US State Department said yesterday, including a Google engineer, a medic and a filmmaker.
