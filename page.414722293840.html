Girl who escaped Boko Haram reveals Chibok kidnapping inspired them

1When Islamic extremists snatched more than 270 girls from the Chibok boarding school in Nigeria in the dead of night, protests broke out worldwide.
2The U.S. pledged to help find them, and the #BringBackOurGirls hashtag was born.
3Some 10 months later, most are still missing.
4The Boko Haram extremist group sees the mass kidnapping as a shining symbol of success, and has abducted hundreds of other girls, boys and women.
5The militants brag to their new captives about the surrender of the Chibok girls, their conversion to Islam and their marriage to fighters.
6'They told me the Chibok girls have a new life where they learn to fight,' says Abigail John, 15, who was held by Boko Haram for more than four weeks before escaping.
7Dorcas Aiden, 20, was living at home when the war came to her village.
8The kidnappings reflect the growing ambition and brazenness of Boko Haram, which seeks to impose an Islamic state across Nigeria, Africa's most populous country.
9Some 10,000 people have died in the Islamic uprising over the past year, compared to 2,000 in the previous four years, according to the U.S. Council on Foreign Relations.
10'It's devastating,' said Bukky Shonibare, an activist in Abuja, of the kidnappings.
11John was among three girls interviewed by The Associated Press who recently escaped from Boko Haram.
12While their stories could not be independently verified, they were strikingly similar, and all spoke of their captors' obsession with the Chibok girls.
13The girls had no idea whether the militants were telling the truth or making up stories to taunt their victims.
14John says the fighters enjoyed relating how they had whipped and slapped the Chibok girls until they submitted.
15When the Nigerian air force dropped a bomb on the house where John was confined, she tried to escape, she says.
16She wrestled with the fighters, but they broke her am and hauled her off to another house.
17At the end of last year, the Nigerian army liberated the town where she was held.
18She is now in Yola with her father, sister and six brothers, in a house overcrowded with refugees.
19She finally was able to get medical attention for her fractured right arm, which remains in a cast.
20John, 15, was held by Boko Haram for more than four weeks before escaping.
21The kidnappings of the Chibok girls in April brought Boko Haram to the world's attention in a way the group could not have imagined.
22The hashtag #BringBackOurGirls was tweeted more than 480,000 times globally in early May, and U.S. first lady Michelle Obama held it up in a sign to television cameras.
23On Wednesday, Nigerian President Goodluck Jonathan again promised the girls will be brought home alive, saying he is 'more hopeful' about their fate now that a multinational force is being formed to fight Boko Haram.
24'Give us some time over the Chibok girls.
25The story will be better in a few weeks,' Jonathan promised, as he has many times in the past, on a nationally televised program.
26In the 10 months since the mass kidnapping, Boko Haram has increased the tempo and ferocity of its insurgency.
27In August, it began seizing and holding towns, and - copying the Islamic State group - declared it would recreate an ancient Islamic caliphate in the region.
28The fighting has since spilled across Nigeria's borders, and the African Union this month authorized a multinational force of 8,750 troops to try to stamp it out.
29Dorcas Aiden, 20, was another of those caught in Boko Haram's siege.
30She had finished high school and was living at home when the war came to her village.
31Fighters took her to a house in the town of Gulak and held her captive for two weeks last September.
32The more than 50 teenage girls crammed into the house were beaten if they refused to study Quranic verses or conduct daily Muslim prayers, she says.
33When the fighters got angry, they shot their guns in the air.
34Aiden finally gave in and denied her Christian faith to become Muslim, at least in name, she says.
35One day, the fighters stormed into the room where she was kept locked up with a dozen other girls.
36They showed a video of the Chibok girls, dressed in hijabs, with only their faces visible through their veils.
37Aiden says she was so overwhelmed that she cried.
38The fighters said the Chibok girls were all Muslims now, and some were training as fighters to fight women, which Boko Haram men are not supposed to do.
39Aiden's captors boasted about how they had married off the Chibok girls, she says.
40One fighter said he would marry her.
41She balked.
42'I said, 'No, I will not marry you,'' Aiden recounts.
43Aiden says the insurgents threatened to break the legs of any girl who tried to escape, but she and six others ran anyway.
44As she made her way through abandoned farm fields, she noticed that Boko Haram had filled about 10 other houses with kidnapped girls and women.
45Aiden, who is now in Yola with tens of thousands of other refugees, dreams of going to university, in defiance of the extremists' insistence that girls should be married, not educated.
46Another escapee, a shy 16-year-old captured in September, begs that her name not be published because she escaped only a few weeks ago and believes the fighters are actively searching for her.
47After the girl's village was attacked four times, she fled to a great-aunt.
48Then that village also was targeted, she says.
49The fighters held her for four months.
50When she escaped, she walked through the bush and across the border into Cameroon to avoid areas under Boko Haram's control.
51She is now taking refuge in a Catholic church in Yola.
52All the girls say they were not raped, despite the fears of some villagers.
53Instead, the fighters said they wanted the girls to remain virgins until they were married off.
54'They said they are doing the work of God, so they will not touch us,' the 16-year-old recounts.
55As she tells her story, she fidgets and looks down at her hands, clasped in her lap.
56She recounts how one fighter, nicknamed 'Tall Arab,' was set on marrying her.
57The man told her the Chibok girls were 'enjoying their matrimonial homes,' she remembers.
58He also said the Chibok girls had turned against their parents, and were 'ready to slit their parents' throats' if they ever saw them again.
59Some never will.
60Even if the girls are released, people in Chibok say at least 13 of their parents have died since they were seized, in Boko Haram violence or possibly stress-related illness.
61While dozens of Chibok girls escaped on their own after their kidnapping, 219 are still missing.
62Nigeria's military initially feared any action could lead to the girls being killed.
63But villagers reported last week that air force jets have begun bombing the Sambisa Forest - the area where fighters told Aiden some girls still are held captive.
64Meanwhile, suspected Boko Haram militants attacked a village on the shore of Lake Chad early Friday, marking the first such violence against the neighbor contributing the most military might to the regional fight against the Nigeria-based terror group.
65The assault took place in the village of Ngouboua, according to army Col.
66Azem Bermandoa.
67It was not immediately known how many civilians were killed during the attack, he said.
68'The assailants have scattered and the army is now pursuing them,' Bermandoa told The Associated Press by telephone.
69Boko Haram has expanded its attacks against neighboring countries over the past week following threats to punish any nations contributing to the fight against the terror group.
70Chad, Niger, Cameroon and Benin all have pledged to send military support though Chadian soldiers are already fighting Boko Haram militants inside Cameroon and Nigeria.
71The multinational force to fight Boko Haram is expected to be formally launched in the coming weeks.
72Ngouboua is already home to nearly 3,300 refugees who had fled the Boko Haram-related violence back home in Nigeria, according to the United Nations.
