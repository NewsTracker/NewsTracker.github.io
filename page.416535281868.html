Nepal earthquake: We warned you, say experts

1Rescue dogs head to the mountains and aid groups work furiously to reach survivors after an earthquake of historic proportions hits Nepal.
2"A massive block of Earth's crust, roughly 75 miles long and 37 miles wide, lurched 10 feet to the south Saturday over the course of 30 seconds. Riding atop this block of the planet was the capital of Nepal - Kathmandu - and millions of Nepalese."
3That's the description of Saturday's earthquake from University of Colorado geologist Roger Bilham, a world-renowned expert on Himalayan earthquakes.
4The 7.8-magnitude earthquake that flattened historic buildings in Kathmandu and has taken more than a thousand lives is the latest release of built-up strain from the collision of two of Earth's tectonic plates.
5A collapsed building in Kathmandu following the earthquake.
6The Indian plate is inexorably sliding, in a halting, ground-shaking fashion, northward, beneath the much larger Eurasian plate.
7The process has created the lofty Tibetan plateau and pushed up mountains that reach nearly 30,000 feet above sea level.
8The Himalaya front can produce earthquakes that are much more powerful than the one on Saturday - such as the 8.2-magnitude earthquake that hit Nepal in 1934.
9But this one was relatively shallow, which intensifies the surface shaking, and its epicentre was closer to Kathmandu than the 1934 temblor.
10"The earthquake ruptured under the city, very close to the city, so this is as bad as our worst-case scenario, probably," Bilham said.
11As news reports filtered in, experts predicted the death toll would mount steadily.
12"I expect that there's devastation scattered all around Nepal that we're not even glimpsing at this point," said Susan Hough, a geologist with the US Geological Survey who has made multiple trips to Nepal.
13The news bulletin of the massive quake hit Hough and colleagues hard.
14Theirs can be a frustrating profession, because they know there are natural disasters and humanitarian crises about to happen somewhere - but they can't predict precisely where and when.
15This one, however, had been long anticipated.
16For years now, experts on seismic hazard have kept a list of cities most vulnerable to a catastrophic earthquake.
17Kathmandu has always been high on that list.
18Geology, urbanisation, architecture and building codes have increased the vulnerability of the Nepalese, experts say, and the only major unknown has been the timing of the disaster.
19"We knew it was going to happen. We saw it in '34," Hough said.
20"The earthquakes we expect to happen do happen."
21Scientists, engineers and government officials have worked in recent years on retrofitting schools and hospitals to make them sturdier in a temblor.
22But at the same time, civil unrest has pushed more people into urban areas, where they inhabit newly constructed, unreinforced-masonry buildings that in many cases are not designed to withstand the strong motion of a quake.
23Another problem: buildings often have what engineers call a "soft first storey", because merchants want open spaces to sell their wares and there are fewer sturdy walls to limit the shaking in an earthquake.
24"It was clearly a disaster in the making that was getting worse faster than anyone was able to make it better," Hough said.
25"You're up against a Himalayan-scale problem with Third World resources."
26Bilham agreed: "The message has not been ignored, it's just that the scope of the reconstruction required to strengthen all the buildings in Kathmandu is so enormous."
27The orthodoxy among seismologists is that earthquakes don't kill people; buildings kill people.
28The challenge of improving building codes has become all the more urgent in an era when urbanisation is surging in many parts of the world, including in the Kathmandu Valley.
29"It seems that the rural-to-urban migration of people has resulted in really rapid construction of housing which, as far as I can see from my visits, has been unregulated and is just very, very vulnerable," said Brian Tucker, founder and president of GeoHazards International, a non-profit devoted to reducing casualties from natural disasters.
30On Saturday, he recalled a conversation in the late 1990s with a Nepalese government minister who told him, "We don't have to worry about earthquakes anymore, because we already had an earthquake."
31That was a reference to the 1934 quake.
32"I took him to the window and had him look out and said, 'As long as you see those Himalaya Mountains there, you will know that you will continue to have earthquakes,'" Tucker said.
