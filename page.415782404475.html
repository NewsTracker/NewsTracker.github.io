Germanwings flight 4U9525 crash: Investigators find torn up sick notes in Andreas Lubitz's home

1Dusseldorf: Documents show that Andreas Lubitz, the co-pilot who is believed to have deliberately crashed a Germanwings jet into the French Alps on Tuesday, had a medical condition that he hid from his employer, German prosecutors in Dusseldorf have said.
2The documents, which were found in his home, included torn-up and current sick leave notices from a doctor, including one covering the day of the crash declaring him "unfit to work".
3Hid his illness from his employers: Germanwings co-pilot Andreas Lubitz.
4"We have found a letter that indicated that he was declared by a medical doctor 'unfit to work'," said Dusseldorf Prosecutor Christoph Kumpa.
5"Documents with medical contents were confiscated that point towards an existing illness and corresponding treatment by doctors," said the prosecutors' office in Duesseldorf, where the co-pilot lived and where the doomed flight from Barcelona was heading.
6"The fact there are sick notes saying he was unable to work, among other things, that were found torn up, which were recent and even from the day of the crime, support the assumption based on the preliminary examination that the deceased hid his illness from his employer and his professional colleagues," the German prosecutors said.
7Police carry computer, a box and bags out of the residence of the parents of Andreas Lubitz, co-pilot on Germanwings flight 4U9525 in Montabaur, Germany.
8They found no suicide note or confession, "nor was there any evidence of a political or religious background to what happened", they added.
9The statement came after German newspaper Bild reported Lubitz had been treated for at least one "serious depressive episode" in the past and may have tried to hide psychological troubles.
10The account also cited security sources saying Lubitz has been in a "life crisis" that included troubles with his girlfriend.
11French investigators work through the debris of the crash in the French Alps.
12It said Lubitz had slowed his flight training because of treatment for unspecified psychological issues, and was temporarily deemed "unfit to fly" during instructions at Lufthansa's aviation school outside Phoenix.
13Lubitz's personnel file contained a special code saying he needed to have "special regular medical examinations," Bild added.
14The report drew no direct connection between Lubitz's present psychological state and the crash.
15Police carry a computer out of Andreas Lubitz's house.
16Investigators have been combing through his background, including whether he might have had a history of depression or psychological problems.
17They are also looking into his family background, and whether he had financial troubles or difficulties in his personal relationships.
18Lubitz's mental health - and what Germanwings and parent company Lufthansa knew about it - could become central questions in any future legal case over the crash.
19Under German law, employees are required to inform their employers immediately if they are unable to work.
20Lubitz, 27, is believed to be responsible for slamming Germanwings Flight 4U9525 into a mountainside in the French Alps on purpose, killing all 150 on board, while en route from Barcelona, Spain, to Dusseldorf.
21Prosecutors are examining several theories, including that the crash was a suicide or a mass murder.
22On Thursday, the French prosecutor leading the investigation said the evidence from the cockpit voice recorder suggested that Lubitz, a former flight attendant with a passion for flying, had out of the cockpit and deliberately set the plane on its lethal descent.
23The crash claimed from more than a dozen countries, including Germany, Spain and the United States.
24Carsten Spohr, the chief executive of Lufthansa, the parent company of Germanwings, said Lubitz had passed the company's health checks with "flying colours".
25"He was 100 per cent flightworthy without any limitations," Mr Spohr said.
26But he said there had been an instance six years ago when Lubitz took a break from his training for several months.
27He said that if the reason was medical, German rules on privacy prevented the sharing of such information.
28Spohr said the revelation of Lubitz's actions had left him stunned.
29Some international airlines responded to the crash by introducing new rules requiring that two crew members always be present in the cockpit, after the French prosecutor revealed that Lubitz had locked the plane's pilot out of the cockpit before starting the deadly descent.
30The airlines that said they were instituting a two-person rule in the cockpit included Air Canada, easyJet and Norwegian Air Shuttle.
31All German airlines will introduce that requirement, the German aviation association said on Friday.
32Thomas Winkelmann, the head of Germanwings, however, expressed doubt that such a rule would have prevented Tuesday's crash.
33"I ask myself, when a person is so bent on committing a criminal act, whether that is preventable if for example a stewardess or steward is in the cockpit," Mr Winkelmann told the German public broadcaster ZDF.
34"The suffering and pain this catastrophe has caused is immeasurable," he was quoted as saying in a message posted on Twitter by Germanwings on Friday.
35"No words can express it and no amount of consolation is sufficient."
36Investigators are still trying to understand why the pilot left the cockpit, although most airlines allow it during noncritical phases of flight.
37There are no regulations requiring that a second crew member be present in the cockpit when one pilot leaves, usually for physiological reasons.
38The French prosecutor, Brice Robin, said it was reasonable to assume the pilot left the cockpit to use the toilet.
39Members of a flight crew would typically use a numeric code to open the door if someone in the cockpit could not or would not let them in.
40The pilot would have known the code, Spohr said.
41However, the co-pilot could have activated a switch that prevents the door from opening for five minutes, or he could have found some other way to block the door, Spohr said.
42Robin said that the Germanwings flight had begun prosaically, with polite exchanges between the two pilots as the flight began its course to Dusseldorf.
43However, about a half-hour into the flight, he said that Lubitz appeared to have locked out the pilot of the plane and did not let him back in, prompting the pilot to demand access.
44Investigators, citing the plane's voice recorder, said they could hear the sound of someone trying to break down the door.
45Robin said the plane's voice recorder showed that Lubitz was breathing normally in the moments leading up to the crash, indicating that he had deliberately crashed the plane.
