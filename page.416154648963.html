Review: Many choices, indecision with Apple Watch

1Both sessions at the Apple store began with indecision: What size do I want?
2Which band do I prefer?
3I didn't have good answers as my Apple Watch try-on visits started - at first modestly, with models that cost about the same as an iPhone.
4The try-on session typically begins with making an appointment online.
5If you're looking for a luxury "Edition" made of 18-karat gold alloy, you specify that.
6Only some stores offer those, including the one I visited on New York's Upper West Side.
7I bypassed the appointments because Apple's media reps in Cupertino, California, had arranged my sessions.
8Stores will try to accommodate walk-ins, but appointments are encouraged.
9As someone who can't decide what to eat for lunch, I expected to be told which watch I wanted.
10Sensing my indecision, the employees in both cases picked one just to get started.
11I began with a smaller version of the stainless steel case, with a brown leather band called Modern Buckle ($749).
12The band looks like a leather strap you buckle, but has magnetic clasps.
13It felt loose, even at the tightest setting.
14I was told not every band is going to fit every wrist.
15I then tried a large stainless steel case with a black leather loop ($699) - also clasped magnetically, but without the holes you normally see with a buckle.
16The band comes in two sizes, but only the large one was available for trying.
17It was too long.
18It turns out stores don't have all 54 configurations available for trying out.
19Most stations have 18 watches to choose from, locked in a drawer that requires a tap from the employee's security device.
20You get a feel, but not necessarily in your color or length.
21I tried the Modern Buckle again, this time in black.
22Still loose.
23That was followed by large and small cases with a classic buckle.
24The fit was much better.
25I also tried models with a synthetic-rubber sports band and a stainless steel link bracelet.
26Bands are easily interchangeable by pressing a release button, and some are sold separately.
27I can use the sports band for running and a classic buckle for regular wear.
28Likewise, individual links on the bracelet can be removed without special tools.
29But the try-on visits aren't set up for that.
30Nor are they set up for lefties; everything will appear upside down in the watches' demonstration mode.
31You can change that - once you buy it.
32I returned several hours later to meet with an Edition specialist.
33I wasn't feted with chocolate and champagne, but I did get a more personalized experience.
34You're taken to a private room in some stores, but mine didn't have one, so luxury appointments are done on the main floor, to the side.
35As the specialist assisted me, another employee went into a backroom to bring out specific models on request - in nice boxes that give the watch a luxury feel, while doubling as a charger (there's a charging port in the back).
36The process wasn't as quick as I expected, but what's the rush when you're ready to spend $10,000 to $17,000?
37I tried one with a blue classic leather buckle ($15,000) and another with a white sports band ($12,000).
38The synthetic-rubber sports band looked out of place on a case made of gold alloy, but the gold clasp matches.
39Likewise, the metal on the buckle straps are also gold to match.
40I tried four others from non-luxury lines.
41One also had a classic buckle to get a feel for the difference in weight.
42At 69 grams, or 2.4 ounces, the gold model I tried is 38 percent heavier than stainless steel.
43I didn't notice it, though, until someone pointed it out.
44Apple does offer six configurations for $17,000, but my specialist says they are designed for women.
45The $15,000 blue buckle I tried was one of the two priciest for men.
46It fits nicely on my wrist and looks sharp - possibly because it just feels rich.
47I'm sure the other ones will work just fine, including the cheapest ones at $349 for the small case and $399 for large.
