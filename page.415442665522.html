Cyclone Pam leaves trail of destruction in Pacific's Vanuatu

1In this photo provided by non-governmental organization 350.org, debris is scattered over a building in Port Vila, Vanuatu, Saturday, March 14, 2015, in the aftermath of Cyclone Pam.
2Winds from the extremely powerful cyclone that blew through the Pacific's Vanuatu archipelago are beginning to subside, revealing widespread destruction.
3WELLNGTON, New Zealand (AP) - Winds from an extremely powerful cyclone that blew through the Pacific's Vanuatu archipelago were beginning to subside Saturday, revealing widespread destruction and unconfirmed reports of dozens of deaths.
4Communication systems in many of the hard-hit outer islands remained down, meaning it could take some time before the full extent of the damage caused by Cyclone Pam is known.
5Chloe Morrison, a World Vision emergency communications officer who is in Port Vila, said the capital's streets were littered with roofs blown from homes, uprooted trees and downed power lines.
6She said she's hearing reports of entire villages being destroyed in more remote areas.
7She said there is no power or running water in the capital and that communication remains unreliable.
8"It's still really quite dangerous outside. Most people are still hunkering down," she said.
9"The damage is quite extensive in Port Vila but there are so many more vulnerable islands. I can't even imagine what it's like in those vulnerable communities."
10U.N. Secretary General Ban Ki-moon said the impact and scope of the disaster wasn't yet clear, but he feared the damage and destruction could be widespread.
11"We hope the loss of life will be minimal," he said during a World Conference on Disaster Risk and Reduction in Japan.
12The U.N. said it was preparing to deploy emergency rapid response units.
13The president of Vanuatu, Baldwin Lonsdale, who was attending the conference, told participants: "I do not really know what impact the cyclone has had on Vanuatu."
14"I am speaking to you today with a heart that is so heavy," he said.
15"I stand to appeal on behalf of the government and the people to give a helping hand in this disaster."
16Morrison said the winds seemed to peak between midnight and 1 a.m. Saturday.
17She said she was in a fully boarded-up cyclone-proof house but still spent a frightening night as a tree and a tin roof from a nearby home hit her house.
18A westward change of course put populated areas directly in the path of Cyclone Pam's 270 kilometer (168 mile) -per-hour winds.
19The U.N. Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs said there were unconfirmed reports of deaths in Vanuatu's northeastern islands after Cyclone Pam moved off its expected track.
20Australia was preparing to send a crisis response team to Vanuatu if needed, Australian Foreign Minister Julie Bishop said.
21"There are destructive winds, rain, flooding, landslides, sea surges and very rough seas and the storm is exceedingly destructive there," she said.
22"We are still assessing the situation, but we stand ready to assist."
23Located about a quarter of the way from Australia to Hawaii, Vanuatu has a population of 267,000 spread over 65 islands.
24About 47,000 people live in the capital.
25The tiny Pacific island nation has repeatedly warned it is already suffering devastating effects from climate change with the island's coastal areas being washed away, forcing resettlement to higher ground and smaller yields on traditional crops.
26The cyclone has already destroyed some homes and caused damage to other Pacific islands including Kiribati and the Solomon Islands.
27David Gibson, acting director of the Vanuatu meteorology and geo-hazards department, said the winds could cause severe damage to the nation's buildings.
28Alice Clements, a spokeswoman for relief agency UNICEF who is in Port Vila, said Friday the capital was like a ghost town as people took shelter.
29She said the pelting rain was blown horizontally by the wind.
