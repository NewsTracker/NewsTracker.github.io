IOC to order tests for viruses at Rio's Olympic water venues (Yahoo Sports)

1RIO DE JANEIRO (AP) -- The International Olympic Committee said Sunday it will order testing for disease-causing viruses in the sewage-polluted waters where athletes will compete in next year's Rio de Janeiro Games.
2Before, the IOC and local Olympic organizers in Rio said they would only test for bacteria in the water, as Brazil and virtually all nations only mandate such testing to determine the safety of recreational waters.
3But after an Associated Press investigation published last week revealed high counts of viruses directly linked to human sewage in the Olympic waters, the IOC reversed course after being advised by the World Health Organization (WHO) that it should expand its testing.
4''The WHO is saying they are recommending viral testing,'' IOC medical director Dr. Richard Budgett told the AP.
5''We've always said we will follow the expert advice, so we will now be asking the appropriate authorities in Rio to follow the expert advice which is for viral testing.
6On Saturday, the International Sailing Federation became the first to break with the IOC's insistence on bacteria-only testing, saying it would do its own independent tests for viruses.
7''We're going to find someone who can do the testing for us that can safely cover what we need to know from a virus perspective as well as the bacteria perspective,'' said Peter Sowrey, chief executive of the ISAF.
8That came after the WHO told the AP on Saturday that it had advised the IOC to test for viruses.
9A five-month AP analysis of water at each of the venues where about 1,400 Olympic athletes will have contact with water showed dangerously high levels of viruses from sewage.
10The AP commissioned four rounds of testing in each of those three Olympic water venues, and also in the surf off Ipanema Beach, which is popular with tourists but where no events will be held.
11Thirty-seven samples were checked for three types of human adenovirus, as well as rotavirus, enterovirus and fecal coliforms.
12The AP viral testing, which will continue in the coming year, found not one water venue safe for swimming or boating, according to global water experts who analyzed the AP data.
13A risk assessment done based on the AP's study found that athletes who ingest just three teaspoons of water have a 99 percent chance of being infected by a virus - though that does not automatically mean they would fall ill.
14That depends upon a person's immune system and a number of other factors.
15The concentrations of the viruses in all AP samples were roughly equivalent to that seen in raw sewage - even at one of the least-polluted areas tested, Copacabana Beach, where marathon and triathlon swimming will take place and where many of the expected 350,000 foreign tourists may take a dip.
16In Rio, much of sewage goes untreated and runs down hillside ditches and streams into Olympic water venues that are littered with floating rubbish, household waste, and even dead animals.
17The pollution problem has been around for decades, and has sparked what top medical experts in Rio call an endemic public health crisis because of the dirty water in this otherwise stunningly gorgeous city circled by Atlantic rainforest and golden sand beaches.
18On Sunday, athletes competed under a blazing Rio sun in an Olympic test event in the triathlon.
19''It's been an interesting learning experience over the last few days.
20I think some athletes went back to Biology 101 to learn the difference between bacteria and viruses,'' said American triathlete Sarah True, who has qualified for next year's Olympics.
21True said she didn't think the Olympic venue could be moved from Copacabana even if the IOC's tests also find high viral counts.
22So far, neither the IOC nor the sailing federation has said who would do their testing.
23Virology experts in Brazil say there are only three or four labs with the molecular biology equipment and trained scientists who can carry out the testing for viruses in water.
24The AP's tests are being conducted by Fernando Spilki, a respected virologist who is a board member of the Brazilian Society for Virology and editor of its scientific journal.
25He is not being paid by the AP to conduct the testing, though the AP is purchasing the lab materials required to carry out the research.
26When Rio was awarded the games in 2009, it promised cleaning its waters would be an Olympic legacy.
