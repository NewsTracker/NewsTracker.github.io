Iran Nuclear Deal Concludes In Historic Announcement

1Iran reached a historic deal with six world powers on Tuesday that promises to curb Tehran's controversial nuclear program in exchange for economic sanctions relief.
2The accord was announced on Tuesday by Iranian Foreign Minister Mohammad Javad Zarif and the European Union's policy chief Federica Mogherini in a joint statement in the Austrian capital.
3"What we have in front of us today ... is the result of very hard work," Mogherini said.
4"It is a decision that can open the way to a new chapter in international relations," she continued, "I think this is a sign of hope for the entire world."
5Zarif acknowledged that the final agreement wasn't perfect, but described the announcement as a "historic moment."
6"Today could have been the end of hope," he said, "but now we are starting a new chapter of hope."
7The breakthrough comes after months of thorny negotiations between Iran and the so-called P5+1 group -- the five permanent members of the U.N. Security Council and Germany.
8Under the deal, Tehran's nuclear ability would be significantly limited for more than a decade, The New York Times reported.
9Tehran would also allow inspectors from the U.N's International Atomic Energy Agency to seek visits to Iranian military sites as part of their monitoring duties, a senior diplomat told The Associated Press.
10However, such visits could be denied or delayed by the Iranian government.
11In such cases, an arbitration board composed of Iran and the six world powers would have to be convened to determine the right of access.
12The head of the United Nations' nuclear watchdog announced early Tuesday that a "roadmap" had been signed between it and Iran.
13"This is a significant step forward toward clarifying outstanding issues regarding Iran's nuclear program," Yukiya Amano, the head of the IAEA, said.
14Just now, Iran and @iaeaorg agree to accelerate cooperation with aim to fully resolve all prior issues.
15In addition, Iran accepted a "snapback" plan that will restore sanctions in 65 days if it violates the accords, Reuters reported.
16"All the hard work has paid off and we sealed a deal. God bless our people," the diplomat told Reuters on condition of anonymity.
17The deal is the culmination of years of delicate negotiations between formerly bitter enemies U.S. and Iran, as well as negotiators from China, France, Russia, the United Kingdom and Germany.
18For more than a decade, international powers have raised concerns that Tehran is using its nuclear enrichment program to build a nuclear weapon.
19Iran says the program is solely for peaceful purposes such as energy and medicine.
20International sanctions imposed over the country's nuclear work have hampered Iran's economy and in 2013 Tehran agreed to freeze some nuclear activities in exchange for partial sanctions relief.
21The 2013 agreement, the first between Iran and the U.S. in more than three decades, paved the way for a framework agreement in April, and Tuesday's comprehensive nuclear accord.
22The deal still faces several hurdles.
23Opponents of the talks in the U.S. Congress have vowed to thwart any agreement that they deem has not gone far enough to prevent an Iranian nuclear bomb.
24Congress now has 60 days to review the deal before President Barack Obama can start removing congressional sanctions.
25Iran says it won't implement its end of the deal until sanctions are removed and supporters of the nuclear accord fear that prolonged delays could strengthen hardliners in Washington and Tehran.
26The deal also has its detractors all over Iran's neighborhood.
27The most outspoken of them is Israel, whose leaders have fought hard to obstruct a nuclear accord.
28They say the deal's lax restrictions will actually help Iran build a bomb, while sanctions relief will allow Iran to funnel more funds to terrorist groups in the region.
29On Tuesday, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu called the deal "a bad mistake of historic proportions."
30Miri Regev, Israel's culture and sports minister, said the nuclear deal gives the Islamic Republic a "license to kill" and was "bad for the free world (and) bad for humanity," AP reported.
31Gulf powers, including Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates, are also wary that their longtime rival will be strengthened by a rapprochement with the international community and have quietly voiced their concerns that a deal with Iran would tip the balance of power in the region.
