New study gives more evidence of Obamacare gains for millions

1People sign up for Obamacare at AltMed Health Services in Los Angeles earlier this year.
2As congressional Republicans move toward another vote on repealing the Affordable Care Act, new evidence was published Wednesday about the dramatic expansion of insurance coverage made possible by the law.
3Nearly 17 million more people in the U.S. have gained health insurance since the law's major coverage expansion began, according to a study from the Rand Corp., a Santa Monica nonprofit research firm.
4That tally takes into account 22.8 million newly insured people and 5.9 million who lost coverage in the last year and a half.
5Researchers found gains across all types of insurance, including employer-provided coverage, government Medicaid programs and policies offered through state insurance marketplaces created by the law.
6At the same time, the vast majority of Americans have seen no change in the source of their coverage, with 80% remaining in the same kind of insurance, researchers found.
7"The ACA has greatly expanded health insurance coverage in the United States with little change in the source of coverage for those who were insured before the major provisions of the law took effect," concluded the authors of the study, published online by the journal Health Affairs.
8The Rand study, based on surveys of 1,589 working-age adults, is not as large as other polls.
9But the findings are consistent with other national surveys as well as with data from the federal government, which have all shown a dramatic decrease in the nation's uninsured rate since the health law's coverage expansion began.
10A new Gallup poll released Wednesday also indicates that Americans are feeling substantially more satisfied with the "work the government is doing with healthcare," with 43% saying they are satisfied, up from 29% in 2013 and 24% in 2005.
11Rand's survey, which has tracked a panel of the same people since 2013, provides an important early snapshot of where coverage is expanding.
12The biggest growth has been in the marketplaces created by the law, which now have about 11 million adult customers, about a third or whom were uninsured in 2013, according to the study.
13Medicaid, another pillar of the health law, has seen enrollment grow by nearly 10 million adults since 2013.
14The marketplaces allow Americans who do not get health benefits through an employer to shop for health plans that must meet basic standards and cannot turn away consumers, even if they are sick.
15And more than half the states have expanded eligibility for their Medicaid programs through federal aid made available by the health law.
16Rand also found substantial growth in employer-provided coverage, with some 8 million more adults now in a health plan provided by an employer.
17In fact, employer coverage was the biggest source of new coverage for previously uninsured people, the study found.
18"The law has expanded coverage to more Americans using all parts of the health insurance system," said Rand economist Katherine Carman, the study's lead author.
19Nearly half of the newly insured people in an employer-provided health plan had access to such coverage in 2013 but elected not to sign up, Carman said.
20That suggests the new requirement in the law that Americans have insurance may be having an impact.
21The researchers did not find a large number of people who lost coverage when the health plans they had purchased on their own were canceled in 2013 or 2014, a phenomenon that generated fierce criticism of the health law at the time.
22"There were some doomsayers who were saying that this would have major impact," Carman said.
23"That wasn't the case."
24Whether the coverage gains can be sustained remains unclear.
25The Rand survey shows that increases in insurance slowed this year, compared with 2014.
26The insurance gains could also be reversed if congressional Republicans succeed in repealing the health law or if the Supreme Court this summer backs a lawsuit that argues that insurance subsidies provided through the law should not be available in more than 30 states that rely on the federal government to operate their insurance marketplaces.
