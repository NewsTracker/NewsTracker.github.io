Nepal earthquake: ADF to airlift survivors as international rescue effort intensifies

1A Nepalese police member tries to pull out one of the victims of the earthquake in Bhaktapur.
2Kathmandu: Australians stranded in Nepal following Saturday's devastating earthquake will be airlifted out by the Defence Force as aid agencies warn of deteriorating conditions in some affected parts of the country.
3The Foreign Minister, Julie Bishop said the Government plans to deploy ADF aircraft to assist Australians fly from the Nepal capital, Kathmandu, to Bangkok.
4British search and rescue workers load a truck in with specilaist equipment at Tribhuvan International Airport, Kathmandu.
5"Highly vulnerable Australians will be given priority," she said.
6The international airport in Kathmandu is open, although some scheduled flights have been affected and there are lengthy delays.
7Ms Bishop encouraged Australians to leave Nepal on a commercial flight if they could.
8The timing and frequency of the ADF flights will be subject to capacity constraints at Kathmandu airport and approval by the Nepalese Government.
9Chinese search and rescue team arrives at Tribhuvan International Airport, Kathmandu.
10"Once details of the military flights are known, DFAT will advise of the process for Australians to register their interest," Ms Bishop said.
11Australia will also send additional humanitarian experts to Kathmandu, including medical personnel to assist health coordination and needs assessments in the wake of the earthquake.
12A DFAT disaster expert will also join the United Nations Disaster Assessment and Coordination team.
13International search and rescue specialists arriving in Nepal are hopeful they can still find survivors buried beneath rubble.
14Volunteers and emergency workers search for bodies buried under the debris of one of the temples at Basantapur Durbar Square in Kathmandu, Nepal.
15The emergency response is gearing up with expert teams from Britain, Germany and China arriving in Kathmandu on Monday night to join other international humanitarian workers.
16Gary Francis, the team leader of a British crew from Search and Rescue Specialists in Disaster, said the response was "definitely still in the search and rescue phase.

19Hundreds of thousands of Nepalese spent a third night sleeping in the open on Monday night after the magnitude 7.9 earthquake that has been followed by nearly 60 aftershocks.
20Last night many people in the hard-hit capital huddled under blankets on median strips and footpaths without shelter.
21Some Kathmandu open spaces have been transformed into mini tent cities.

23"This is a really difficult time for us," he said.
24"We are living makeshift."
25The death toll from the quake in Nepal is now over 4000 and about 7500 people are reported to be injured.
26Unicef says nearly 1 million children in earthquake affected areas are in urgent need of humanitarian assistance.
27Aid workers report dwindling supplies of water and food, power outages, and downed mobile networks.
28Limited access to clean water and hygiene has increased the risk of diseases spreading.
29Mr Francis' team, which has 14 fully-trained rescue technicians and 1.5 tonnes of specialist support equipment, will work with a team of sniffer dogs from Germany.
