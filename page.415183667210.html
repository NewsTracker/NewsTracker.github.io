Keurig coffee pods creator John Sylvan regrets ever creating them

The Bali Nine: where are they now?

1In an interview with The Atlantic, John Sylvan, who came up with the idea for the coffee pods and the machine in the early 1990s, revealed that he 'feels bad' about the negative impact that the pods are having on the environment.
2Sylvan, who no longer uses the plastic pods himself, also confessed he wishes he had never created them in the first place.
3'I feel bad sometimes that I ever did it,' said Mr Sylvan, who sold his share of the company in 1997 for $50,000.
4Keurig is currently thought to be one of the most popular at-home brewing systems in America.
5Last year the company sold 9.8 billion Keurig-brewed portion packs and earned $4.7billion.
6The design is simple - a small plastic capsule containing ground beans that can be put into a machine to produce a cup of coffee at the touch of a button.
7However despite the multi-billion pound success of the company nothing has been done to try and make the packaging of the popular Keurig pods recyclable.
8And Mr Sylvan insists this will never happen.
9'No matter what they say about recycling, those things will never be recyclable,' he said.
10Mr Sylvan, who co-founded the company with Peter Dragone, added that the unique plastic used in the K-Cups - known as plastic #7 - is only recyclable in a small number of cities in Canada, and cannot as yet be processed yet in the United States.
11Unfortunately the plastic is also integral to the unique design of the pods.
12It ensures that the coffee inside stays completely protected from any outside elements and remains completely intact while the coffee is being brewed.
13The popularity of the simplistic coffee pods has also come across the shores to the UK.
14Adverts for K-Cup featuring the slogan 'Brew the Love', enticed American audiences.
15And in England thousands of customers have been lured into buying Nespresso machines following similar adverts featuring George Clooney.
16Nespresso is now the most prominent capsule company in the UK.
17The machines do not take the Keurig capsules but take similar plastic ones and Nespresso has established its own sustainability advisory board.
18Mr Sylvan also confessed that despite his earlier dedication to the brand, he no longer drinks coffee made using the capsules.
19In 1995 he had to go to hospital with heart palpitations after testing up to 40 cups of his coffee a day.
20He now no longer even owns a K-Cup machine and only drinks filter coffee.
21Keurig has come under fire on numerous occasions from environmentalists who claim that the number of K-Cups sold in 2014 alone, and subsequently put into landfills, would circle the earth a staggering 12 times if they were laid down end-to-end.
22They have started a Kill The K-Cup campaign, which features a YouTube video showing the world being destroyed by the plastic coffee capsules.
23But despite the continued criticism, Keurig Green Mountain, the brewing system's parent company, has yet to come up with a viable solution to the problem, despite insisting that they have been trying to find an environmentally-friendly alternative since 2006.
24'It's a warranted criticism,' Monique Oxender, chief sustainability officer for Keurig Green Mountain, told The Salt earlier this year.
25Not according to Mr Sylvan.
26The entrepreneur insists that he has come up with an alternative design to his original creation which would involve relying on the same kinds of materials used for foil ketchup packets.
27But while Mr Sylvan is convinced that he holds the answer to Keurig's ongoing environmental concerns, he added that the company have thus far refused to trial his new design.
28'I told them how to improve it, but they don't want to listen,' he added to The Atlantic.
29Ironically, Mr Sylvan's current work, running energy efficient solar energy company ZonBak, requires him to be much more mindful of the environment, which is perhaps why he is suffering from such feelings of guilt over his most infamous creation.
