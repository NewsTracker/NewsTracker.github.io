SXSW music notebook: Iggy's missed opportunity

For director, landing Pacino was something to sing about

1Whatever the amount, though, I'm pretty sure the Australian rapper didn't come to Austin just for the money.
2She was looking for a reset, too.
3The beginning of 2015 has been rough for Azalea, who dominated pop last year with three monster singles - "Fancy," "Problem" and "Black Widow" - that at one point were all in the top 10 of Billboard's Hot 100.
4So impressive was her ascent that when she was nominated for four Grammy Awards, many observers predicted (some worriedly) that she'd win them all.
5But then things started falling apart.
6Azalea was unexpectedly shut out at the Grammys.
7She quit social media in response to the "hatred and pettiness" she said she was subjected to online.
8And last week she postponed an upcoming arena tour, citing "production delays" widely assumed to be code for lack of interest.
9Even in the days before her performance here, the Internet pounced on a video showing Azalea rapping incoherently - a small crime for some but a damning one for a white MC many have criticized for appropriating a black style with little regard for its meaning.
10("The Vine that proves Iggy Azalea can't rap," the blog Vulture called the clip.)
11So with its high visibility and its audience happily susceptible to spin, SXSW offered Azalea a chance to reclaim her damaged narrative, to brush off the haters and show us why she's still worth paying attention to.
12Well, maybe next time.
13Sparsely attended by the sardine-can standards of many high-profile SXSW concerts of years past, Wednesday's gig felt like a missed opportunity.
14The problem wasn't her rapping, which was confident - and more or less comprehensible - in songs like "Beg for It" and "My World."
15Wearing a gold-and-black jacket over shorts and thigh-high boots, Azalea stomped across the stage like she belonged there - a victory of sorts, I suppose, given how often her very existence has been challenged.
16But she was taking no discernible pleasure in her exercise of power, even in "Trouble," which she introduced as "a feel-good song," and "Bounce," during which she instructed the crowd to do the festive dance known as the Nae Nae.
17(The 10 or 12 people not taking pictures with their phones complied.)
18And she embodied no palpable sense of struggle in moodier cuts like "Don't Need Y'all," about how fame feels "like a curse latched to my blessing."
19As she ran through the song with a blank look in her eyes, Azalea seemed drained by the task at hand - not just putting in her 45 minutes for a corporate-sponsored gig but figuring out the next step in her career.
20But then, right before the end of the show, she tore into "Iggy AZN," a delightfully bratty kiss-off, with the kind of intensity she'd seemed incapable of summoning until that point.
21Not every artist will shine in a setting like SXSW.
22It takes a certain mix of confidence and perseverance to survive this week, and with 20 minutes or less to set up a stage, any sense of perfectionism is better left at home.
23Tuesday night's highlight, Torres (real name: Mackenzie Scott), didn't waste any time getting on stage five minutes early and then packing high drama into taut, guitar-driven songs.
24Tired, stressed and "just 23," as she sang on "New Skin," Torres let her backing trio accentuate her emotional anxiety rather than go for broke.
25Rhythms went for a "Tell-Tale Heart"-esque pounding, keyboards lit a gasoline fuse that encircled the orchestrations and Torres' own guitar scraped along the concrete while her deep, forceful voice punched holes into the ground.
26There's a sense with Torres' songs that a whole life has been lived within them.
27"Sprinter" views the world as a predatory one, and "Strange Hellos," the lead single off her forthcoming sophomore album, simmers with tension-building guilt.
28Songs start out as ballads, and then become something louder, more brute, as Torres lets every chord change creak like a rusty door hinge.
29At a club next door, Natalie Prass took a more refined route, though the setting wasn't suited for it.
30This up-and-comer has already outgrown the tiny space she performed in, which meant getting a glimpse of the stage was mostly impossible.
31It wasn't until Prass introduced her band that anyone except those in the first row had any idea that there were four people on stage.
32Still, going by sound alone, Prass was in fine form.
33This is a compliment, but if Cinderella left the Disney films to start a band, it may sound something like Prass' mix of pop classicism and lush, yet restrained arrangements.
34Hints of jazz and varying shades of warmth appear throughout as Prass' tales of love gone wrong unfold, leaving ample space for an orchestra within them.
1(AP) - "Danny Collins" casts Al Pacino as a veteran pop star who still fills arenas, has a gaggle of granny-aged groupies and a signature song with a call-and-response element that smacks of "Sweet Caroline."
2But Danny Collins is not Neil Diamond.
3"I've got a kind of equal-dosage mix of Neil Diamond, Rod Stewart, a lot of Tom Jones and a little Barry Manilow - those seem to be the four that people ask about the most," said writer-director Dan Fogelman, whose big-screen dramedy opens this weekend.
4"Collins" actually was inspired by something that happened to another musician.
5Some 45 years ago, English folk singer-songwriter Steve Tilston did a magazine interview acknowledging a fear that wealth and fame may negatively affect his songwriting.
6That statement inspired John Lennon to dash off a letter to Tilston in 1971.
7"Being rich doesn't change your experience in the way you think," Lennon wrote.
8Tilston didn't even learn about the letter until 2005, nearly 25 years after Lennon's death, when a collector reached out to confirm authenticity.
9A 2010 story on Tilston caught Fogelman's attention, but, excepting the part about the Lennon letter - a key plot element of the film - "Danny Collins" isn't Tilston's story either.
10Tilston stayed true to his acoustic-folk roots and, as per Fogelman, has enjoyed happy and productive personal and professional lives.
11Collins sold his soul for stardom, and just may reclaim it in the most unexpected places: the embittered grown son he never knew (Bobby Cannavale) and a harried hotel manager (Annette Bening) who is unimpressed by Collins' star facade, but sees a promise for that man buried underneath it.
12Once the 39-year-old Fogelman (a co-writer on Pixar's "Cars" 'toons) got the rights to Tilston's story, he crafted the "Collins" script specifically for Pacino, whom the director idolized growing up in New Jersey.
13That was a big risk, given Fogelman had no relationship with the Oscar-winning actor.
14"When Al signed on to this movie, I was a 34-year-old guy who had never directed a movie before, who was best known for writing cartoon movies about talking cars," Fogelman said.
15"Honestly, him reading the script alone is a career highlight. And then him doing the movie is a career highlight. So, I'm kind of good."
