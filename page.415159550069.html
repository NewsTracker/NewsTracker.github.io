22Reuters White House Correspondent Jeff Mason interviewed President Barack Obama on Monday on topics including U.S.-Israel relations, Iran, China and Russia.
23REUTERS - Mr. President, thanks very much for joining us.
24OBAMA - Good to see you.
25REUTERS - Let's start right on Israel.
26Your administration has described Prime Minister Netanyahu's plans to address Congress tomorrow on Iran as destructive.
27What damage has really been done?
28OBAMA - Well, first of all, I think it's important to realize the depth of the U.S.-Israeli relationship.
29Under my administration, billions of dollars have gone to support Israel's security, including the Iron Dome program that has protected them from missiles fired along their borders.
30The military intelligence cooperation is unprecedented and that's not our estimation.
31That's the estimation of the Netanyahu government.
32And that bond is unbreakable.
33So we need to make clear from the outset how strong our alliance with Israel is.
34The second point is that we actually share a goal, which is making sure Iran does not have a nuclear weapon.
35That's something that I committed to when I was still a senator.
36It is a solemn pledge I made before I was elected president and everything that I've done over the course of the last several years in relation to Iran has been in pursuit of that policy.
37There is a substantial disagreement in terms of how to achieve that.
38And what it boils down to is what's the best way to ensure that Iran is not developing a nuclear weapon.
39Prime Minister Netanyahu thinks that the best way to do that is either through doubling down on more sanctions or through military action, ensuring that Iran has absolutely no enrichment capabilities whatsoever.
40And there's no expert on Iran or nuclear proliferation around the world that seriously thinks that Iran is going to respond to additional sanctions by eliminating its nuclear program.
41What we've said from the start is by organizing a strong sanctions regime, what we can do is bring Iran to the table.
42And the deal that we're trying to negotiate is to make sure that there's at least a year between us seeing them try to get a nuclear weapon and them actually being able to obtain one.
43And as long as we've got that one-year breakout capacity, that ensures us that we can take military action to stop them if they were stop it.
44Now, we're still in the midst of negotiations.
45What I've said consistently is, we should let these negotiations play out.
46REUTERS - Double digit years?
47OBAMA - Double digit years.
48If we've got that and we've got a way of verifying that, there's no other steps we can take that would give us such assurance that they don't have a nuclear weapon.
49Now, Iran may not agree to the rigorous inspection demands that we're insisting on.
50They may not agree to the low levels of enrichment capabilities they would have to maintain to ensure that their breakout is at least a year.
51But if they do agree to it, it would be far more effective in controlling their nuclear program than any military action we could take, any military action Israel could take and far more effective than sanctions will be.
52And we know that because during the period in which we applied sanctions for over a decade, Iran went from about 300 or a couple of hundred centrifuges to tens of thousands of centrifuges in response to sanctions.
53REUTERS - Let's talk a little bit specifically about the prime minister.
54Susan Rice said that what he has done by accepting the invitation to speak was destructive to the fabric of the relationship.
55Would you agree that it's destructive?
56And if so, will there be any consequences for him or for Israel?
57OBAMA - You know, I think that Prime Minister Netanyahu is sincere about his concerns with respect to Iran.
58And given Iran's record and given the extraordinarily disruptive and dangerous activities of this regime in the region, it's understandable why Israel is very concerned about Iran.
59We are too.
60But what we've consistently said is we have to stay focused on our ultimate goal, which is preventing Iran from having a nuclear weapon.
61Now, as a matter of policy, we think it's a mistake for the prime minister of any country to come to speak before Congress a few weeks before they are about to have an election.
62It makes it look like we are taking sides.
63REUTERS - But aside from that, what about that is destructive?
64OBAMA - I'm answering your question, Jeff.
65And the concern is, not only does it look like it politicizes the relationship but what's also a problem is when the topic of the prime minister's speech is an area where the executive branch - the U.S. president and his team - have a disagreement with the other side.
66I think those who offered the invitation and some of the commentators who have said this is the right thing to do, it's worth asking them whether, when George W. Bush had initiated the war in Iraq and Democrats were controlling Congress, if they had invited let's say the president of France to appear before Congress to criticize or to air those disagreements, I think most people would say, well, that wouldn't be the right thing to do.
67I guarantee you that some of the same commentators who are cheerleading now would have suggested that it was the wrong thing to do.
68I don't think it's permanently destructive.
69I think that it is a distraction from what should be our focus.
70And our focus should be,'How do we stop Iran from getting a nuclear weapon?' Now keep in mind the prime minister, when we signed up for this interim deal that would essentially freeze Iran's program, roll back its highly enriched uranium - its 20 percent highly enriched uranium - and so reduce the possibility that Iran might breakout while we were engaged in these negotiations, when we first announced this interim a deal, Prime Minister Netanyahu made all sorts of claims.
71This was going to be a terrible deal.
72This was going to result in Iran getting 50 billion dollars worth of relief.
73Iran would not abide by the agreement.
74None of that has come true.
75It has turned out that, in fact, during this period we've seen Iran not advance its program.
76In many ways, it's rolled back elements of its program.
77And we've got more insight into what they're doing with more vigorous inspections than even the supporters of an interim deal suggested.
78There's no good reason for us not to let the negotiations play themselves out.
79Then we'll show, here - here's the deal that's been negotiated, does it make sense?
80And I am confident that if, in fact, a deal is arrived at, then it's going to be a deal that is most likely to prevent Iran from getting a nuclear weapon.
81REUTERS - You obviously disagree about that.
82If the prime minister wins reelection, would you be able to work with him?
83OBAMA - Absolutely.
84We're working with him now on a whole range of issues.
85REUTERS - Would you meet with him?
86OBAMA - Of course.
87As I've said before, the only reason that we didn't meet with him this time is a general policy we don't meet with somebody two weeks before an election.
88I've met with Prime Minister Netanyahu more than any other world leader.
89And given the strong relationship between the United States and Israel, I would expect that to continue.
90REUTERS - Is it fair to say you're angry with him?
91OBAMA - This is not a personal issue.
92I think that it is important for every country in its relationship with the United States to recognize that the U.S. has a process of making policy.
93And although we have separation of powers, ultimately, the interaction with foreign governments runs through the executive branch.
94That's true whether it's a Democratic president or a Republican president.
95And that's true regardless of how close the ally is.
96REUTERS - Have Israel's actions been disruptive to the ability to get this deal?
97OBAMA - I think that it's been a distraction.
98I think that in the meantime negotiators are going full speed ahead.
99Ultimately, what's been remarkable is the international unity we've been able to maintain in saying to Iran, you have to show the world that you are not pursuing a nuclear weapon.
100You can have very modest enrichment capabilities for peaceful use, so long as there's a vigorous enough inspection process that we have assurances that you are not obtaining breakout capacity.
101And the biggest challenge right now to getting a deal is for Iran to recognize this is its path in order to ultimately re-enter into the community of nations.
102REUTERS - Have your communications with the Supreme Leader helped in this?
103OBAMA - You know, I would say that most of the work has been done directly between the negotiators and Secretary Kerry, Foreign Minister Zarif of Iran, the expert teams that have worked together along with our P5+1 partners.
104They've done the lion's share of the work.
105REUTERS - But has that been useful?
106OBAMA - I think it's been important for us to send a clear signal to all parties inside of Iran that we are not the aggressors here.
107We are looking to resolve this diplomatically if we can.
108But given the history of Iran engaging in covert programs, given the history of Iranian sponsorship of terrorism in the region and around the world, given the rhetoric that's come out from the Iranian regime including anti-Israel and anti-Semitic statements, it is important for them to understand that they have a high threshold that they have to meet in terms of proof and convincing the world that they're prepared to not pursue a nuclear program.
109If they do that, and we have ways of measuring that, very concrete ways, if they do that, that's the best path for us to take.
110What we should not do is to try to jettison the talks, undermine the talks.
111I'm less concerned, frankly, with Prime Minister Netanyahu's commentary than I am with Congress taking actions that might undermine the talks before they're complete.
112And what I've said to members of Congress, both Democrats and Republicans, is there will be plenty of time for us to reapply sanctions, strengthen sanctions, to take a whole range of other measures, if in fact we do not have a deal.
113But what we should not do is pre-judge the deal and initiate sanctions that might allow Iran to walk away and claim that the United States is the one that has eliminated the path to diplomacy.
114REUTERS - How would you judge, what's your assessment of the percentage likelihood now of this happening.
115OBAMA - The likelihood of?

118OBAMA - You know, I would say that it's probably still more likely than not that Iran doesn't get to yes.
119But I think in fairness to them, they have been serious negotiators.
120And they've got their own politics inside of Iran.
121It is more likely that we could get a deal now than perhaps three or five months ago.
122But there are still some big gaps that have to be filled.
123REUTERS - We're running short of time.

125A top opponent of President Putin was gunned down last week.
126What does this say about Vladimir Putin's Russia and do you believe that the Kremlin was not involved?
127OBAMA - What I've called for is a full investigation and, hopefully, an independent investigation of what happened.
128Whether that can occur inside today's Russia is not clear.
129The individual involved is somebody that I actually met with back in 2009.
130This is an indication of a climate at least inside of Russia in which civil society, independent journalists, people trying to communicate on the Internet, have felt increasingly threatened, constrained, and increasingly the only information that the Russian public is able to get is through state-controlled media outlets.
131That is a problem.
132It's part of what has allowed, I think, Russia to engage in the sort of aggression that it is has against Ukraine.
133REUTERS - You don't want to say whether or not the Kremlin was involved?
134OBAMA - I have no idea at this point exactly what happened.
135What I do know is more broadly the fact that free - freedom of the press, freedom of assembly, freedom of information, basic civil rights and civil liberties inside of Russia are in much worse shape now than they were four or five, ten years ago.
136REUTERS - Let me ask you about another area of the world, China.
137Are you concerned about how hard China is making it for U.S. tech companies to do business there?
138OBAMA - I am concerned.
139This is something that I've raised directly with President Xi, and my entire foreign policy team as well as people like Secretary of the Treasury Jack Lew and Secretary of Commerce Penny Pritzker have raised with them.
140They've got a couple of laws that are working their way through the system that would essentially force all foreign companies, including U.S. companies, to turn over to the Chinese government mechanisms where they could snoop and keep track of all the users of those services.
141And as you might imagine, tech companies are not going to be willing to do that.
142Those kinds of restrictive practices I think would, ironically, hurt the Chinese economy over the long term because I don't think there's any U.S. or European firm, any international firm, that could credibly get away with that wholesale turning over of data, personal data, over to a government.
143And so we've made very clear to them that this is something they're going to have to change if they expect to do business with the United States.

157That after 50 years of a policy that didn't work, we need to try something new that encourages and ultimately I think forces the Cuban government to engage in a modern economy.
