Erdogan, Turkish Cypriot leader urge Greek Cypriots to step up peace efforts

1ANKARA, May 6 (Reuters) - Turkish President Tayyip Erdogan and the newly elected north Cypriot leader said on Wednesday that renewed peace talks between Cyprus's estranged Turkish and Greek communities could lead to a solution this year if the Greek side shows more commitment.
2"We will make efforts to make 2015 year of the solution, but this doesn't depend just on us," Mustafa Akinci said after a meeting with Erdogan in Ankara.
3It was their first meeting since Akinci was elected on April 26, pledging fresh impetus in solving the conflict which has split the Mediterranean island for decades.
4Though welcoming Akinci's election, Greek Cypriots say any Cyprus settlement is contingent on Turkey, which bankrolls Turkish Cypriots and has some 30,000 troops in the heavily militarised breakaway state.
5At a joint news conference, Akinci and Erdogan said they aimed for a speedy resumption to stalled peace talks and called on Greek Cypriots to take steps toward peace.
6"It is time for the Greek Cypriots to show sincere efforts as well," Erdogan said.
7"If that happens, we believe it is possible for 2015 to be the year of solution".
8Akinci is due to meet Greek Cypriot leader Nicos Anastasiades to revive peace talks, which stalled last October, although an initial meeting was postponed.
9Erdogan showed no sign of animosity towards Akinci, days after criticising him for suggesting relations with Turkey should be on more equal footing.
10"Who's the one fighting for northern Cyprus in the international community? Does Mr. Akinci believe he can put up this fight on his own?"
11Erdogan had said at the time, adding that Turkey would continue to view northern Cyprus as a "mother looks at its child".
12Cyprus was split in a Turkish invasion in 1974 triggered by a brief Greek-inspired coup.
13The Greek Cypriot-controlled south represents the whole island in the European Union, although membership is in political limbo in the north, a breakaway Turkish Cypriot state.
14While communication between Greek and Turkish Cypriots was severed for years, Akinci was an exception.
15As mayor of the ethnically split Cypriot capital Nicosia in the 1980s, he and his Greek Cypriot counterpart worked on a plan for the city, with the hope it would be reunited.
