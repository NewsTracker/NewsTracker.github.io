Congo pushes back against UN peacekeeping force; cut planned

MH17: Fragment from crash site matches a Russian-made rocket, Dutch TV reports

1The top U.N. envoy to Congo, Martin Kobler, said the waivers "were given in a too generous way."
2He spoke to reporters shortly after Congo's foreign minister said the friction with the U.N.'s largest peacekeeping force over the two generals is not his country's fault.
3Foreign Minister Raymond Tshibanda told the U.N. Security Council that the time has come for Congo to "assume full responsibility" for its own security, indicating that his country wants the peacekeepers to leave.
4Tshibanda addressed the council after weeks of tension in which the U.N. mission backed out of a joint operation against a rebel force in the eastern part of the country.
5The U.N. says defeating the rebels is key to peace in the often troubled, mineral-rich region, but it objects to the two generals leading the operation.
6The Security Council is expected to vote on a resolution next Thursday to renew the mandate of the $1.3 billion peacekeeping force in Congo, which as of the end of February had just over 21,000 military and police personnel.
7Congo President Joseph Kabila wants the U.N. force cut by at least 7,000, and Tshibanda said negotiations start Monday on how to reduce the peacekeeping mission.
8Diplomats say a more modest cut of 2,000 is expected.
9Kobler warned that making the peacekeeping mission leave Congo too early would be "disastrous."
10The current tensions started with the dispute over the two generals.
11The U.N. has said Gen. Bruno Mundevu and Gen. Fall Sikabwe have been heavily involved in "massive human rights violations" that it hasn't publicly detailed.
12U.N. policy requires that any support to outside security forces will not contribute to serious violations.
13But Kobler told reporters that the mission worked with one of the generals in an operation last year and with the other in a 2010 operation.
14He said the peacekeeping mission can issue waivers for officers case by case with the expectation that Congo's government will meanwhile take punitive action against them, but that hasn't happened in several cases.
15"The waivers, they are an exception, they are not the rule," he said.
16He said 118 Congolese officers have been listed by the U.N. as "red" for human rights violations since 2010, while 2,085 officers have been cleared.
17He said the peacekeeping mission tells the Congo government about "red" officers whenever the affected officers ask for support from the mission.
18The mission recently rejected requests for support from seven Congolese officers over human rights concerns, a U.N. report released this month said.
19Amid the argument over the two generals, Congo's army on its own in late February launched an offensive against the FDLR rebel group, which includes Hutus who committed the 1994 Rwanda massacres.
20Tshibanda said the offensive has had significant progress, with more than 200 rebels arrested, including 91 child soldiers who will be handed over to the U.N. mission.
21Kobler told the council in closed-door consultations that other joint projects between Congo and MONUSCO are being affected by the friction.
22He told diplomats that operations against another rebel group, the ADF, are increasingly being led by Congo's military without U.N. support.
23And a force intervention brigade, which has a mandate to take offensive military action against rebel groups, is effectively paralyzed.
24In his public remarks to the council, Kobler appealed to Congo to "reset" their relationship with the U.N. mission on the basis of mutual trust.
25"We do not want to choose between fighting the FDLR or upholding human rights," Kobler said.
1Amsterdam: A metal fragment from the crash site of Malaysia Airlines flight MH17 matches a surface-to-air BUK rocket, a Dutch broadcaster has said, supporting a theory that the plane was downed by pro-Russian separatists in eastern Ukraine.
2The fragment was recovered by a Dutch journalist from the village of Hrabove several months ago near where the plane was brought down last July, killing all 298 passengers and crew.
3Dutch broadcaster RTL said it had had the shrapnel tested by international forensic experts, including defence analysts IHS Jane's in London, who said it matched the explosive charge of a BUK, a Russian-made anti-aircraft missile system.
4The downing of the plane was a turning point in the conflict in Ukraine, which pits the separatists against Kiev's forces.
5Kiev and its Western supporters blamed the rebels for the incident and it stiffened the resolve of Western governments to impose sanctions against both leading separatists and Moscow.
6The Dutch Safety Board, which is investigating the cause of the crash, said in a reaction that its investigation was in "full progress and focuses on many more sources than only the shrapnel".
7In preliminary conclusions published last year, the board said the plane had been hit by high velocity projectiles but did not specify the source.
