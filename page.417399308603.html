Blatter Says FIFA Corruption Probe Won't Lead to Him

1Having come through another FIFA election largely unscathed, Sepp Blatter was in a typically defiant mood Saturday when addressing the challenges that still lie ahead.
2The 79-year-old FIFA president dismissed suggestions that a United States government investigation of corruption in football could lead to his door.
3Several senior FIFA officials have been arrested already, but Blatter shrugged off the notion that he could be next.
4"Arrested for what? Next question," Blatter said curtly when meeting international media for the first time since American and Swiss federal cases rocked FIFA's home city on Wednesday.
5"I forgive but I don't forget," Blatter also said at FIFA headquarters, referring to a European-led attempt to oust him after 17 years in office.
6A busy first day of his new four-year presidential term saw the leader of the world's most popular sport scold critics and take acclaim from allies.
7Blatter criticized U.S. Attorney General Loretta Lynch and received a congratulatory telegram from Vladimir Putin, president of 2018 World Cup host Russia.
8Gone was the tension which put a tremor in his voice after Swiss police raided FIFA's favorite luxury hotel in Zurich early Wednesday.
9Blatter insisted he had nothing to fear from the U.S. federal case which alleged a $150 million bribe scheme linked to broadcast rights for tournaments in North and South America.
10Two FIFA vice presidents were among seven men arrested.
11"I do not see how FIFA could be directly affected by this," Blatter said.
12He was equally adamant when responding to questions about whether the probe can't still directly affect him.
13Was he the "high-ranking FIFA official" mentioned in the Department of Justice indictment who wired $10 million to corrupt North American officials?
14The apparent bribes were paid from a FIFA account in exchange for voting for South Africa as the 2010 World Cup host.
15"Definitely that is not me," Blatter said.
16"I have no $10 million."
17It was clear that, after winning a closer vote than he would have liked Friday, Blatter wanted to come out fighting - first criticizing Lynch in an interview with his local Swiss broadcaster.
18Lynch said Wednesday that FIFA and marketing officials - 14 indicted and four who made guilty pleas - had "corrupted the business of worldwide soccer to serve their interests and to enrich themselves."
19"I was shocked by what she said," Blatter told French-language broadcaster RTS.
20"As a president I would never make a statement about another organization without knowing."
21Blatter suggested the U.S. Department of Justice went too far on FIFA's turf.
22"Listen, with all the respect to the judicial system of the U.S. with a new minister of justice," Blatter said, "the Americans, if they have a financial crime that regards American citizens then they must arrest these people there and not in Zurich when we have a congress."
23"I have especially no concerns about my person," he said about the investigation, which U.S. federal agencies claim is just starting.
24The seven football officials detained are resisting extradition and face 20 years in prison.
25Any questioning and plea bargaining could take American authorities deeper into the heart of FIFA.
