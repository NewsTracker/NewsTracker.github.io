Fugitive ex-NSA contractor Edward Snowden in talks on returning home: lawyer

Scathing report finds patterns of racial bias in Ferguson

1Moscow: A Russian lawyer for Edward Snowden said on Tuesday the fugitive former US spy agency contractor who leaked details of the government's mass surveillance programmes was working with American and German lawyers to return home.
2Anatoly Kucherena, who has links to the Kremlin, was speaking at a news conference to present a book he has written about his client.
3Moscow granted Mr Snowden asylum in 2013, straining already tense ties with Washington.
4"I won't keep it secret that he... wants to return back home. And we are doing everything possible now to solve this issue. There is a group of US lawyers, there is also a group of German lawyers and I'm dealing with it on the Russian side."
5The United States wants Mr Snowden to stand trial for leaking extensive secrets of electronic surveillance programmes by the National Security Agency (NSA).
6Russia has repeatedly refused to extradite him.
7Mr Snowden has said in the past he would like to return home if he was assured he would be given a fair trial.
8A deeply divisive figure, Mr Snowden is praised by some as a civil rights campaigner and whistleblower and condemned by others as a traitor who had compromised US security.
9Mr Kucherena said last August Mr Snowden had been granted a three-year residence permit in Russia.
1The dual reports marked the culmination of months-long federal investigations into a shooting that sparked protests and a national dialogue on race and law enforcement as the tenure of Attorney General Eric Holder, the first black person to hold that office, draws to a close.
2In pairing the announcements, the Obama administration sought to offset community disappointment over the conclusion that the shooting of Michael Brown was legally justified with a message of hope for Ferguson's majority-black citizens.
3Officials announced 26 recommendations, including training officers in how to de-escalate confrontations and banning the use of ticketing and arrest quotas, for the police force and municipal court.
4Holder called the federal report a "searing" portrait of a police department that he said functions as a collection agency for the city, with officers prioritizing revenue from fines over public safety and trouncing the constitutional rights of minorities.
5"It is not difficult to imagine how a single tragic incident set off the city of Ferguson like a powder keg," Holder said.
6Ferguson Mayor James Knowles III said the city had co-operated with the Justice Department and has made some changes, including a diversity training program for city employees.
7The decision not to prosecute Darren Wilson, the white officer who was cleared in November by a state grand jury and has since resigned, had been expected.
8To win a federal civil rights case, officials would have needed to prove that Wilson wilfully deprived Brown of his rights by using unreasonable force.
9Instead, the report found no evidence to disprove Wilson's testimony that he feared for his safety during the Aug. 9 confrontation.
10Nor were there reliable witness accounts to establish that Brown had his hands up in surrender when he was shot, Justice Department lawyers said.
11One of Wilson's attorneys, Neil Bruntrager, said his client was satisfied with the outcome.
12Brown family lawyer Benjamin Crump said the family was not surprised but very disappointed, and one of Brown's uncles, Charles Ewing, said he believed Wilson was "getting away with it."
13"I really was hoping they would have come up with better findings because this whole thing just does not add up," Ewing said.
14"Everything just doesn't make sense."
15While the federal government declined to prosecute Wilson, it found the shooting occurred in an environment of systematic mistreatment of blacks, in which officials swapped racist emails and jokes without punishment and black residents were disproportionately stopped and searched without good reason, fined for petty offences and subjected to excessive police force.
16The report on the department found its lack of racial diversity -- only four of 54 commissioned officers are black -- undermined community trust.
17It also said the city relied heavily on fines for raise revenue and issued arrest warrants for minor infractions including jaywalking and late fees.
18The confrontation that led to Brown's death began when Wilson directed him and a friend to move from the street to the sidewalk.
19The document was filled with examples of what it called a discriminatory criminal justice system.
20Among them: One black woman spent six days in jail because of a parking violation.
21A lawful protest was broken up with a police warning of "everybody here's going to jail."
22And a black man sitting in a car with tinted windows was accused without cause of being a pedophile by an officer who pointed a gun at his head.
23Between 2012 and 2014, black drivers were more than twice as likely as others to be searched during routine traffic stops, but 26 per cent less likely to be carrying contraband.
24The report also included seven racially tinged emails, including some from city officials who remain employed, that did not result in punishment.
25The writer of one 2008 email stated that President Barack Obama wouldn't be in office for long because "what black man holds a steady job for four years."
26Knowles, the mayor, said one employee was fired and two others are on leave over the emails.
27The report's recommendations, if accepted by city officials, could lead to an overhaul of basic police and court practices.
28Those include improving officer supervision, better recruiting, hiring and promotion, new mechanisms for responding to misconduct complaints and a new system to reduce fine amounts.
29Federal officials on Wednesday described Ferguson city leaders as co-operative and seemingly open to change, saying there were already some signs of improvement.
30The city, for example, has eliminated bond requirements for many municipal offences and has extended a program that allows individuals to have warrants recalled and assigned a new court date.
31In the last five years, the Justice Department has investigated roughly 20 police departments over alleged civil rights violations.
32Some have led to the appointment of independent monitors and have been resolved with negotiated agreements in which the police department commits to major changes in its practices.
33Federal officials say they hope to avoid a protracted court fight with Ferguson to force change.
34"It is time for Ferguson's leaders to take immediate, wholesale and structural corrective action," Holder said.
35Associated Press writers Jim Suhr and Jim Salter in St. Louis and Alan Scher Zagier in Ferguson contributed to this report.
