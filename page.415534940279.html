1On Tuesday, March 17, 2015, health officials said four more medical aid workers have been flown from West Africa to the United States, for monitoring for Ebola.
2The latest arrivals bring to 16 the number of aid workers evacuated in the last week from Sierra Leone.
3NEW YORK (AP) - Four more American aid workers arrived back in the United States on Tuesday from West Africa to be monitored for Ebola, health officials said.
4The latest arrivals bring to 15 the number of aid workers who have returned from Sierra Leone since Friday.
5None of them have been diagnosed with Ebola, but they will be isolated and monitored during the next three weeks for signs of the disease.
6Officials have released few details, citing patient privacy.
7But all are connected to - or had direct physical contact with - another American who came down with Ebola last week in Africa.
8The unidentified man is in critical condition at a National Institutes of Health hospital in Bethesda, Maryland.
9The other aid workers are staying near the Maryland hospital or hospitals in Atlanta and Omaha with special isolation units - in case they become sick, according to officials with the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.
10The sick American works with Partners in Health, a Boston-based international aid organization that has been treating patients in Liberia and Sierra Leone since November.
11Organization officials did not respond to calls and emails from The Associated Press on Monday or Tuesday.
12In a statement Tuesday, co-founder Dr. Paul Farmer said the group is working with the CDC and others to investigate how the man was infected.
13The Ebola virus is spread through contact with a sick patient's blood or bodily fluids.
14The World Health Organization estimates the virus has killed more than 10,000 people, mostly in Guinea, Liberia and Sierra Leone.
15While deaths have slowed dramatically in recent months, the virus appears stubbornly entrenched in parts of Guinea and Sierra Leone.
16Since last summer, several U.S. aid workers have gotten Ebola and have been flown back to the U.S. for care.
17A few other individuals have returned for monitoring; none got sick.
18The aid workers who have returned since Friday are by far the largest group.
19And more may be on their way later this week, CDC spokeswoman Kathy Harben said Tuesday.
20The sick American worker was working at a government hospital in Port Loko in western Sierra Leone.
21But medical superintendent Dr. Peter George said the hospital hasn't had any Ebola cases for quite some time.
22He said it's likely the man also worked at a nearby Ebola treatment center, which George also manages.
23George said a Partners in Health colleague told him the American passed out while at the hospital.
24"At that time, nobody knew it was Ebola, so they assisted him," he said.
25Everyone who was working on the ward with the man is now being monitored, George said.
26He said that many of the foreigners working in Port Loko, including some of the Americans, live at the same tent camp.
27He did not know if the sick health worker was living there.

9Kohli is described as the successor to Sachin Tendulkar, which applies in the sense that Kohli has become India’s No 4 batsman in Test cricket and reels off centuries.
10But Kohli exudes passion, acts brashly, fields dynamically, and enjoys not only captaincy but all the baubles of international stardom – his girlfriend is the current queen of Bollywood – as Tendulkar never did.
11It must be so much easier to win the World Cup – not that any Englishman would know – if you have done something similar in your youth.
12In 2008 Kohli captained India to the Under-19 World Cup.
13They faced some tough opposition: New Zealand had Kane Williamson, Tim Southee, Corey Anderson and Trent Boult, the pillars of their successful World Cup run this time under Brendon McCullum.
14Only once have England won this Under-19 World Cup, even though they have often been the only professional team in the competition, while other countries have sent amateur schoolboys.
15It was not such a huge step for Kohli, then, when he won the senior World Cup in 2011 as the most junior member of India’s triumphant team.
16Suresh Raina and Ravi Ashwin, who are also due to play in their quarter-final against Bangladesh in Melbourne tomorrow, were members of India’s cup-winning squad; but only Kohli and the captain MS Dhoni survive from the side who won the final on that heady night in Mumbai.
17Kohli grew up in Delhi and, like a number of middle-class Indian boys that must be running into millions, was sent to learn cricket at a private academy.
18What made him stand out was his toughness – the toughness that had him scoring four centuries in the four Tests in Australia this winter and blowing kisses (not bowing his knee) to Mitchell Johnson, as well taking over the Test captaincy when Dhoni retired from the format.
19The caveat is that Australian pitches in the series against India, including Adelaide where Kohli scored two hundreds in one epic Test, were as flat as any in living memory.
20His technical idiosyncrasy was not exposed – that of not bringing his bat down straight but in something approaching a semi-circle, which James Anderson exploited in England last summer, limiting Kohli to 134 runs in his 10 Test innings.

22The same day his father died.

24He reached 90 before being given out caught behind, erroneously it is said, and thus established himself.

33Kohli, still only 26, has scored 22 one-day centuries – way more than the whole England team combined – at an average of 52 and a strike-rate of 89.
34Sure, he has scored most of those hundreds in Asia, on pitches manufactured for batting and with less than vast boundaries, but he still deserves to be ranked sixth in the highest all-time ODI averages.
