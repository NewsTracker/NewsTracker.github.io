Documents show bitter CIA dispute over pre-9/11 performance

1The lobby of the CIA Headquarters Building in McLean, Virginia, August 14, 2008.
2WASHINGTON Top CIA officials fought bitterly in the years after the Sept.
311, 2001 attacks over whether U.S. intelligence agencies could have done more to stop the deadliest terrorist strikes in American history, documents released on Friday show.
4The once-secret documents include a more complete version of a 2005 report by the spy agency's inspector general, which found that the CIA did not have a comprehensive strategy or marshal adequate resources to combat al Qaeda before hijacked planes hit New York's World Trade Center and the Pentagon on 9/11.
5A redacted summary of that report was first made public in 2007.
6But the documents released by the CIA on Friday also reflect the arguments of former CIA Director George Tenet and his lieutenants that U.S. intelligence was intently focused on al Qaeda and leader Osama bin Laden.
7At stake in the years-long dispute are the legacies of former top CIA officials and the agency itself.
8None of the documents focus directly on how President George W. Bush and his White House dealt with the al Qaeda threat after taking office in January 2001.
9Some former officials, including Bush counter-terrorism czar Richard Clarke, have said Bush did not initially make al Qaeda a priority.
10In a heated June 2005 letter to then CIA Inspector General John Helgerson, Tenet rejected Helgerson's critical draft report.
11"Your report challenges my professionalism, diligence and skill in leading the men and women of U.S. intelligence in countering terrorism," Tenet told Helgerson.
12"I did everything I could to inform, warn and motivate action to prevent harm," he wrote.
13"Your report does not fairly or accurately portray my actions, or the heroic work of the men and women of the Intelligence Community."
14After the bombings of U.S. Embassies in East Africa in 1998 and the USS Cole in October 2000, Tenet said, he warned President Bill Clinton "to expect from five to fifteen attacks against United States' interests."
15Tenet has previously said that he developed a plan to go after al Qaeda in 1999, and worked to increase U.S. intelligence funding, slashed during the 1990s.
16"I said when the executive summary was made public eight years ago that the IG's report was flat wrong," Tenet said on Friday.
17"Nothing in the additional material just released changes that judgment in the slightest."
18The documents, which former CIA officials pressed the agency to release, include a July 2005 memo from 17 top officers of the CIA's Counterterrorism Center disputing Helgerson's report.
19Helgerson was not immediately available for comment.
