Picking strawberries in Mexico for U.S. tables leaves workers asking for more

1SAN QUINTIN, Mexico, May 15 (Reuters) - Huddled around a single flickering candle in a tiny wood and cardboard shack on scrubland in Mexico's northwest, laborer Genaro Perfecto and his family prepared to bed down for the night on a bare earth floor.
2His 3-year-old daughter asked for an extra blanket to ward off the cold, but they had run out - a measure of their hard-scrabble life spent harvesting fruit bound for U.S. dining tables.
3Since March, thousands of day laborers have blocked roads, staged marches and held meetings with lawmakers to protest the grind of picking strawberries, raspberries and blackberries in the Baja California peninsula for what they say is as little as $1 an hour.
4Perfecto is part of a growing underclass whose frustration over pay and conditions is pressuring companies that supply U.S. markets to make improvements.
5At least one company told Reuters it would reexamine its suppliers' treatment of workers.
6Kevin Murphy, CEO of U.S. fruit company Driscoll's, said his company was reevaluating standards in the wake of the fruit picker protests, and was going to audit living conditions.
7"We're going to go back and look at them again and reevaluate them and put in some improvements," Murphy said.
8Companies operating in the area say they pay workers fair wages and provide them with adequate healthcare coverage.
9Local government officials, meanwhile, say recent protests over wages by fruit pickers were politically motivated.
10Having moved north to escape poverty in southern Mexico 15 years ago, Perfecto, a father of five, said he is too poor to simply move away from this dusty stretch of industrial farmland known as San Quintin.
11"If you're ill, or cut yourself in the fields, they don't pay the day (if you are out for treatment)," he said, flanked by plastic bags dangling from the low roof that serve as storage for their belongings, a few threadbare clothes and blankets.
12"You keep quiet, and keep working covered in blood," added Perfecto, a 38-year-old whose main diet consists of refried beans or flour tortillas sprinkled with salt.
13In the last few months, laborers have expressed increasing anger over conditions that even some conservative Mexican media have characterized as "near slavery".
14On March 18, more than 200 protesting workers on the peninsula were arrested in a clash with local authorities.
15Several protestors were injured on Saturday in fresh disturbances.
16The boom in sales, meanwhile, has enabled fruit companies to pay above the minimum wage, which in Mexico is 70.1 pesos ($4.57) a day.
17On average, Perfecto picks around 110 kg (243 lbs) of strawberries a day, and up to 200 kg (440 lbs) in high season, he said.
18Across the border in the United States, a kilo of strawberries fetched $5.19 on average in 2013, according to U.S. government data.
19But Perfecto said he earns between 850 and 1,200 pesos ($56-$79) in a week that regularly exceeds 50 hours, roughly between $1 and $2 an hour.
20Five of some three dozen workers interviewed by Reuters showed payslips reflecting earnings of between 782 pesos ($51.10) and 1,210 pesos ($78.80) per week.
21The slips did not provide a clear breakdown of the hourly compensation.
22When asked how much it paid per kilo, a representative for BerryMex, one of Driscoll's suppliers, stated only that workers had an "average earning opportunity" of $5 to $9 an hour with top workers making up to $10 per hour.
23This, BerryMex added, resulted in average weekly earnings of 3,600-7,200 pesos ($238-$476) in a 48-hour week.
24Among the laborers hauling heavy crates packed with strawberries was Carmen Reyes, 34, who is seven months pregnant.
25Reyes says she will keep working as long as possible before giving birth in order to keep making money, as she has done during her previous nine pregnancies.
26One of the children died at 2 months.
27Like Perfecto, she lives in a makeshift shelter made from cardboard and plastic sheeting, and complains of rashes and skin discoloration from her work in the fields.
28"When we're nearby cutting fruit, they don't care, they continue to fumigate" with pesticides, she said, gesturing to a white patch of skin on her forehead.
29Behind Reyes sat one of her daughters, aged 15 and caked in dirt.
30Outside, her husband, gaunt and dusty, worked on a rudimentary extension to their shack.
