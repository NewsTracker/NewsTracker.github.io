Each death in Baltimore makes mistrust harder to overcome

1Baltimore's mayor and police commissioner came in making big promises to the inner-city residents and police who spent decades staring each other down in neighborhoods ravaged by crack and heroin.
2But with each death of a black man in custody, their efforts to overcome mistrust have hit hard walls of skepticism and outrage.
3Two and a half years into his job leading the city's police department, Commissioner Anthony Batts is frustrated that the people he was appointed to serve have lost their faith in justice.
4"They don't believe what I say," Batts said in an interview Thursday with The Associated Press.
5Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake hired Batts in September 2012 after a previous round of police scandals involving excessive use of force, and she was still supporting her commissioner ahead of more protests Saturday.
6Batts also rejected demands by several African-American ministers that he resign.
7"That's not going to happen," he announced Friday.
8He promised to be as transparent as possible without compromising an investigation that could lead to criminal charges.
9Six officers have been suspended with pay since Freddie Gray died of a spinal injury he suffered during an arrest Batts characterized as questionable.
10Both Batts and the mayor, who took office in 2010, are African-American and no strangers to communities like Sandtown, a set of public housing projects not far from Baltimore's Inner Harbor and the Orioles' baseball stadium where Gray tried to outrun three bicycle cops who spotted him on a street corner on April 12.
11Gray, 25, was pinned to the ground and already injured when he was handcuffed and hoisted into a van, police acknowledged Friday.
12He repeatedly begged for medical help, but officers instead added leg irons and locked him inside again.
13Gray was eventually hospitalized in critical condition, and died a week later.
14Batts, who spent decades policing in Long Beach and Oakland, California, before taking this job, insists he is part of the solution in Baltimore.
15He said he's fired 50 police employees and reduced officer-involved shootings, and the use-of-force reports police must file dropped from 598 in 2012 to 435 in 2014.
16"I have been a reform commissioner. I have taken an organization that has many challenges and faced them head-on," he insisted, even as he acknowledged that some cases have "tarnished this badge and the reputation of the department."
17With each death in custody and each police brutality settlement, the trust erodes even more.
18A Baltimore Sun investigation revealed last year that the city has paid roughly $5.7 million in brutality settlements since 2011, involving 102 instances of excessive force.
19And taxpayers keep paying: Just days after Gray's death, the city's Board of Estimates approved $80,000 for 37-year-old Timothy Ashe, whose teeth were punched out by police officer after parking illegally.
20A week earlier, $175,000 was awarded to the family of Michael Wudtee, who was fatally shot by an officer at a gas station in July 2012.
21- Tyrone West, 44, died in July 2013; he stopped breathing after he was tackled and handcuffed by officers during a traffic stop.
22The autopsy blamed a heart condition aggravated by the struggle and hot temperatures.
23An internal review board said the officers were justified, but said officers "made several tactical errors."
24West's family is suing.
25- Anthony Anderson, 46, died in September 2012 after police said they broke up his drug deal.
26Officers said he fought with them and was slammed to the ground.
27His death, of internal bleeding from blunt force injuries, was ruled a homicide, but an internal review board ruled officers were justified, and prosecutors declined to charge them.
28- George King, 19, died in a hospital in May 2014 after officers shocked him six times with a stun gun.
29The state's attorney's office filed no charges against police.
30King's autopsy showed that King died of "acute epidural abscess and meningitis with complications."
31- Trayvon Scott, 30, arrested on a charge of attempted murder, died in custody in February 2015 after showing signs of distress in a holding cell.
32Police called paramedics who took Scott to the hospital, where he died.
33After the newspaper's expose last year, Batts asked the U.S. Justice Department to review the department's policies and procedures.
34Now the Justice Department has opened a second probe, by its Civil Rights division, examining Gray's death.
35Batts told the AP that distrust is so pervasive that his best efforts are falling short.
36"I bring independent review boards in, and they don't believe what they're saying either. Last week the community told the DOJ folks that they don't believe in that process either. How do you build trust?"
37Baltimore had one of the nation's busiest markets for heroin and crack cocaine when Martin O'Malley ran for mayor in 1999.
38The future Maryland governor and Democratic presidential candidate imposed a "zero tolerance" policy that did reduce crime, but also resulted in thousands of arrests without cause.
39In 2010, the ACLU and NAACP reached an $870,000 settlement with the city that required police to track their arrests.
40But by 2012, an independent auditor found Baltimore officers still couldn't justify 35 percent of their arrests.
41"When we adopted zero tolerance policing we were embedding in the police culture this mindset of being at war with the citizenry," said Sonia Kumar, an attorney at the ACLU.
42"At no point since then has that mindset been disrupted or challenged in any meaningful way."
43Batts and the mayor said they're still battling the legacy of this zero tolerance policy: "Although crime decreased, the high number of arrests for minor offenses ignited a rift between the citizens and the police, which still exists today," they wrote in a report last year.
44Residents of Sandtown also say nothing's changed.
45"The police will chase you, throw you on the ground," said Davon Crawford, 28.
46"They're supposed to protect and serve. Who are they protecting?"

48Gray died from spinal injuries about a week after he was arrested and transported in a police van.
