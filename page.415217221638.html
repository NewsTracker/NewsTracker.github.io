North Korea opens marathon to foreigners after Ebola ban lifted

Timeline: The Selma-to-Montgomery marches

1TOKYO - After lifting travel restrictions it imposed because of concerns over the Ebola virus, North Korea says foreigners can now take part in one of its most popular tourist events - the annual Pyongyang marathon, a travel agency said Thursday.
2Even though no cases of Ebola have been reported anywhere near North Korea, the country shut out foreign tourists in October with some of the strictest Ebola regulations in the world, including saying that only local runners would be allowed into the marathon in April.
3But Uri Tours, one tour company that takes tourists into North Korea, said on its website Thursday that it had been informed that North Korean authorities had "decided to re-open the doors to foreign amateur runners for the 2015 Pyongyang Marathon."

6The 50th anniversary of the Selma-to-Montgomery march celebrates a series of peaceful protests carried out against often extreme violence that resulted in one of the most momentous pieces of civil rights legislation in U.S. history - the passage of the 1965 Voting Rights Act.
71962-1963 - Representatives of the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee come to Selma and begin staging protests.
8Oct. 7, 1963 - In what would be known as "Freedom Day," about 350 blacks line up to register to vote at the Dallas County Courthouse.
9Registrars go as slowly as possible and take a two-hour lunch break.
10Few manage to register, most of those are denied, but the protest is considered a huge victory by civil rights advocates.
11July 9, 1964 - Dallas County Circuit Court Judge James Hare issues an injunction effectively forbidding gatherings of three or more people to discuss civil rights or voter registration in Selma.
12Dec. 28, 1964 - Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. presents the SCLC plan, the "Project for an Alabama Political Freedom Movement," a plan conceived by James Bevel that calls for mass action and voter registration attempts in Selma and Dallas County.
13Jan. 2, 1965 - King begins his Selma campaign when about 700 African Americans show up for a meeting at Brown Chapel in defiance of the injunction.
14Jan. 18, 1965 - Black civil rights advocates meet at Brown Chapel.
15Following speeches and prayers, King and John Lewis lead 300 marchers out of the church.
16Selma Police Chief Wilson Baker allows them to march in small groups to the courthouse to register despite Hare's injunction, but Sheriff Jim Clark has them line up in an alley beside the courthouse, where they are out of sight, and leaves them there.
17None is registered.
18Jan. 19, 1965 - Protesters return to the courthouse to register and demand to remain at the front of the building.
19Clark arrests them, including Hosea Williams of the SCLC, Lewis of the SNCC and Amelia Boynton.
20State Troopers advance on marchers, from left, Hosea Williams, John Lewis, Albert turner Sr. and Bob Mantz at the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma on Bloody Sunday, March 7, 1965.
21Jan. 22, 1965 - Since local teachers can be fired, few have taken overt roles in the civil rights movement, but Margaret Moore and the Rev.
22F.D. Reese, who is also a teacher at Hudson High, organize the unprecedented teachers' march.
23Almost every black teacher in Selma - 110 of them - marches to register to vote.
24Clark and his deputies push them down the courthouse stairs three times, but they are not arrested.
25Jan. 25, 1965 - King leads another march of about 250 people to the courthouse.
26When Clark painfully twists the arm of Annie Lee Cooper, 54, and shoves her, she slugs him - twice.
27Feb. 1, 1965 - King and Ralph Abernathy lead a protest and refuse to break into smaller groups.
28Both are arrested and placed in the Selma jail, and refuse to be bonded out.
29Feb. 4, 1965 - One day after addressing students at Tuskegee Institute, Malcolm X speaks to a crowd at Brown Chapel, carefully avoiding speaking about his previous differences with King concerning non-violence.
30Feb. 4, 1965 - President Lyndon Johnson makes his first public statement supporting the Selma campaign.
31Feb. 6, 1965 - President Johnson says he will urge Congress to enact a voting rights bill during the session.
32February 1965 - Gov. George C. Wallace bans nighttime demonstrations in Selma and Marion, and assigns 75 troopers to enforce it.
33Feb. 18, 1965 - State troopers attack marchers during a protest in Marion.
34State trooper James Bonard Fowler shoots and kills Jimmie Lee Jackson, a 26-year-old deacon of the St. James Baptist Church.
35Fowler was charged with murder in 2007.
36He pleaded guilty to second-degree manslaughter in 2010, when he was 67, saying he thought Jackson had been reaching for a weapon.
37He was sentenced to six months, but was released after five because of failing health.
38March 5, 1965 - King flies to Washington to speak with President Johnson about the Voting Rights Bill.
39Then announces the plan for a massive march from Selma to Montgomery.
40March 6, 1965 - Alabama whites, calling themselves the Concerned White Citizens of Alabama, come to Selma to march in support of black rights.
41Klan members have followed them into town to protest their march, and the demonstration breaks up as it is clear violence is about to break out.
42With state troopers barring their way on the steps of the Alabama State capitol at Montgomery on March 25, 1965, Civil rights marchers swarm into the plaza in front of capitol to end five day march from Selma, Ala.
43March 7, 1965 - In what would become known as "Bloody Sunday," John Lewis and Hosea Williams lead about 600 people on what is intended to be a march from Selma to Montgomery.
44But Alabama state troopers, some on horseback, and Clark and his deputies meet the marchers at the Edmund Pettus Bridge.
45When the marchers refuse to disperse, they are driven back with billy clubs and tear gas, with 16 being hospitalized and at least 50 others injured.
46The national coverage of the event galvanizes the country, and King calls for volunteers from throughout the nation to come to Selma for another march on March 9.
47March 8, 1965 - Fred Gray and the SCLC file Hosea Williams v George Wallace before U.S. District Judge Frank M. Johnson Jr. in Montgomery, asking the court to prevent state troopers from blocking the march.
48Wallace representatives argue that the march should be blocked because it would block roadways, interfering with state commerce and transportation and be a threat to public safety.
49Johnson, concerned about the safety of the marchers, says the march should be put off until the court can hold a formal hearing and make a decision.
50March 9, 1965 - Martin Luther King Jr. leads another march to the Edmund Pettus Bridge.
51About 2,000 people, more than half of them white and about a third members of the clergy, participate in the second march.
52King leads the march to the bridge, then tells the protesters to disperse.
53The march becomes known as Turnaround Tuesday.
54March 9, 1965 - James Reeb, a Unitarian Universalist minister who had come from Boston and marched in the protest earlier in the day, is beaten severely by KKK members.
55He dies of head injuries two days later at the age of 38.
56March 11, 1965 - Upset with the way the SCLC is handling things in Selma, James Forman and much of the SNCC staff move to Montgomery and begin a series of demonstrations.
57The group also asks for students from across the country to join them.
58Tuskegee Institute students come to Montgomery in an attempt to deliver a petition to Wallace.
59March 13, 1965 - President Johnson meets with Wallace to decry the brutality surrounding the protests and asks him to mobilize the Alabama National Guard to protect demonstrators.
60March 14, 1965 - SNCC staff members lead 400 Alabama State University students, joined by a group of white students from across the country, on a march from the ASU campus to the Capitol.
61Although Montgomery police react peacefully to the march, as the students approach the Capitol, state troopers, the sheriff's office and a posse it has deputized attack the marchers.
62Photos of the violence make national headlines.
63March 15, 1965 - President Johnson addresses Congress in support of a Voting Rights Bill, quoting the famous civil rights cry "We shall overcome."
64March 17, 1965 - Federal District Court Judge Frank M. Johnson Jr. rules in favor of the marchers after receiving a Justice Department plan outlining their protection during the march.
65March 17, 1965 - Despite the arguments between the SCLC and the SNCC, King joins Forman in leading a march of 2000 people in Montgomery to the Montgomery County courthouse.
66After the march, King announces the third Selma-to-Montgomery march.
67City of Montgomery officials apologize for the assault on SNCC protesters by county and state law enforcement and ask King and Forman to work with them on how best to deal with future protests in the city; student leaders promise they will seek permits for future protest marches.
68But Wallace continues to arrest protestors who venture on to state-controlled property.
69March 18, 1965 - Wallace blasts Judge Johnson's ruling, saying the state cannot afford to provide the security the marchers need and that he will ask the federal government for help.
70March 19, 1965 - Wallace sends a telegram to President Johnson asking for help in providing security for the march.
71March 20, 1965 - President Johnson issues an executive order authorizing the federal use of the Alabama National Guard to supply protection.
72He also sends 1,000 military policemen and 2,000 Army troops to escort the march from Selma.
73March 21, 1965 - About 8,000 people assemble at Brown Chapel before starting the five-day march to Montgomery's Capitol.
74March 24, 1965 - Marchers rest at the City of St. Jude, a Catholic church and school complex on the outskirts of Montgomery, where Harry Belafonte, Tony Bennett, Joan Baez, Sammy Davis Jr., Nina Simone, Frankie Laine and Peter, Paul and Mary perform at a "Stars for Freedom" rally.
75March 25, 1965 - During the Selma-to-Montgomery march, about 25,000 demonstrators join the marchers when they reach Montgomery for a final rally at the state Capitol.
76King delivers his famous "How Long, Not Long" speech.
77March 25, 1965 - That night, Viola Liuzzo, a white mother of five who had driven from Detroit to help protest for black civil rights, is shot and killed by Ku Klux Klansmen as she drives toward Montgomery to pick up a carload of marchers.
78She was 39.
79August 6, 1965 - President Johnson signs the Voting Rights Act into law.
