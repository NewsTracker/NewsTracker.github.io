Apple conspired to fix e-book prices: U.S. appeals court

1Apple conspired to fix e-book prices: U.S. appeals court Add to...
2A divided federal appeals court on Tuesday said Apple Inc. orchestrated a conspiracy with five publishers to increase e-book prices, in a victory for the U.S. Justice Department.
3By a 2-1 vote, the 2nd U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals agreed with a lower court judge that the conspiracy violated federal antitrust law, and that the judge acted properly in imposing an injunction to prevent a recurrence.
4Writing for the majority, Circuit Judge Debra Ann Livingston said that by organizing the conspiracy, "Apple found an easy path to opening its iBookstore," while ensuring that marketwide prices rose to a level that Apple and the publishers wanted.
5The ruling will uphold not just Apple's civil liability but also the terms of an injunction that limited its agreements with publishers.
6The decision also means Apple will be required to pay $450-million (U.S.) as part of a related settlement with 33 attorneys general and lawyers for a class of consumers.
7The accord had been contingent on Apple's liability being upheld.
8Neither Apple nor the Justice Department, which had pursued the civil lawsuit to trial, responded immediately to requests for comment.
9The appeal followed a 2013 decision by U.S. District Judge Denise Cote in Manhattan finding that Apple played a "central role" in a conspiracy with publishers to eliminate retail price competition and raise e-book prices.
10The publishers that the Justice Department said conspired with Apple include Lagardere SCA's Hachette Book Group Inc., News Corp.'s HarperCollins Publishers LLC, Penguin Group Inc, CBS Corp.'s Simon & Schuster Inc. and Verlagsgruppe Georg von Holtzbrinck GmbH's Macmillan.
11In a dissenting opinion, U.S. Circuit Judge Dennis Jacobs said he would have reversed Cote's 2013 ruling, finding that Apple's behaviour was pro-competitive in taking on a "monopolist," Amazon, which controlled 90 per cent of the market.
12"Apple took steps to compete with a monopolist and open the market to more entrants, generating only minor competitive restraints in the process," Jacobs wrote.
