Death toll reaches 11 in Vanuatu, where Cyclone Pam damage limits rescuer access

1We bring you the morning's biggest stories so you can get across the news quickly.
1The United Nations revised the death toll caused by Cyclone Pam, which tore through the South Pacific island nation of Vanuatu, downward from 24 to 11 Tuesday, as relief workers struggled to reach the country's remote outer islands.
2The storm has caused "severe and widespread damage," the agency said in the latest data released Monday, displacing 3,300 people who have sought shelter in 37 evacuation centers.
3Residents of cyclone-ravaged Vanuatu hunkered in emergency shelters for a second straight night Saturday after venturing out to find their homes damaged or blown away by the powerful storm, aid workers said.
4Running water and electricity were slowly being restored in the capital of Port Vila, three days after what the country's president called a "monster storm."
5The cyclone knocked out or damaged an estimated 80 percent of power lines there, the United Nations said, and is believed to have destroyed or damaged 90 percent of buildings in Port Avila alone, President Baldwin Lonsdale told the Associated Press.
6A 7 p.m. curfew has been put in place there.
7"This is a very devastating cyclone in Vanuatu," Baldwin told the AP.
8"I term it as a monster&After all the development that has taken place, all this development has been wiped out."
9Radio and telephone communications have still not been restored on some of the island's outer islands, the UN said.
10For days, relief workers had battled poor weather and communications problems, which stalled their efforts to reach the islands.
11As the weather cleared Monday, officials carried out aerial assessments with military aircraft from New Caledonia, Australia and New Zealand.
12Commercial flights to Port Vila, the capital and largest city, resumed.
13But most of the islands have no airports, and those that do have only small landing strips that can be tricky for large supply planes to navigate.
14Aid groups are providing emergency food rations to evacuees, the UN said, but infrastructure damage from the storm is hampering rescuers' efforts to reach hard-hit areas.
15Crops throughout the country have largely been destroyed, and the UN estimates that about 19,000 households will need food aid based on the cyclone's path, it said.
16Further supplies are expected to be deployed soon via aircraft from France, Australia, and New Zealand.
17The New Zealand Red Cross has sent more than 1,000 tarps, as well as water containers and first aid kits, but shelter and non-food supplies are "critically low," the UN said.
