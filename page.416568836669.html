Maryland governor declares state of emergency in Baltimore over rioting

7Baltimore braced for a night of violence and police clashes after looting, fires and rock-throwing erupted Monday hours after the funeral for Freddie Gray, whose spine was severed in police custody.
8Maryland Gov. Larry Hogan declared a state of emergency as night fell and activated the National Guard to address the violence and unrest in the city that has been racked by increasingly tense protests since the weekend.
9The baseball game between the Baltimore Orioles and the Chicago White Sox was postponed as a precaution.
10At least seven officers were seriously injured Monday with broken bones, and one was described as "unresponsive," officials said.
11The violence began in the early afternoon and built throughout the day.
12Television images showed angry, rock-throwing people confronting police around the area of the Mondawmin Mall.
13Hours after the funeral of Freddie Gray - a man who died after being taken into police custody - Baltimore police and protesters face off.
14Rioters in the streets of Baltimore are looting stores.
15Police fired tear gas and used pepper spray to hold back crowds.
16Several police vehicles were disabled and one was seen burning as people looted a drugstore, which was later set on fire.
17"This is a group of lawless individuals with no regard for people," said Baltimore Police Capt.
18Eric Kowalczyk.
19"We don't know who is out there. We do know they are criminals and have attacked officers without provocation."
20By early evening, throngs of people were still on the move at the shopping center and toward downtown.
21People with clubs were breaking into stores, including private loan outlets.
22Hogan issued a statement saying he had dispatched the Maryland State Police.
23"Today's looting and acts of violence in Baltimore will not be tolerated," Hogan said.
24"I strongly condemn the actions of the offenders who are engaged in direct attacks against innocent civilians, businesses and law enforcement officers. There is a significant difference between protesting and violence, and those committing these acts will be prosecuted under the fullest extent of the law."
25The city has been on edge since Gray, a black man, died April 19, a week after he was arrested by Baltimore police.
26Last week's protests began peacefully, but dozens were arrested over the weekend when six officers were injured despite repeated calls from community leaders and city officials for calm and restraint.
27In a footnote to the growing unease, Baltimore police announced Monday that they had received a "credible threat" that three violent gangs, the Black Guerrilla Family, the Bloods and the Crips, were working together to "take out" law enforcement officers.
28It was unknown whether the threat was connected to Gray's death or to the rioting, police said.
29The latest violence seemed to stem from a flier that circulated widely among city school students via social media about a "purge" to take place at 3 p.m., starting at Mondawmin Mall and ending downtown.
30Such calls have circulated before and are based on the film "The Purge," which posits a dystopia where all laws are suspended for 12 hours.
31George Roy, 49, a surgical technician, lives about six blocks from where Gray was arrested - and about a block from a burning CVS pharmacy downtown.
32"They destroyed the city and they didn't wait for the investigation to occur," he said, referring to the rioters.
33As he stood on metal steps by his apartment and smoked a cigarette, he pointed to the streets up and down his block, where many of the businesses were boarded up.
34All the businesses have been looted, he said, calling it "outright crazy."
35"Now we don't have stores to go to. We don't have fast food we can eat at because they've destroyed it all."
36Helicopters hovered overhead as crowds of people milled around.
37A firefighter who stood by a hydrant said protesters had tried to cut the fire hoses.
38"We're in a code red crisis," the Rev.
39Jamal H. Bryant, who hours earlier delivered Gray's eulogy, said on CNN.
40"It's disappointing."
41Hours earlier, officials from city government to the White House attended the funeral for Gray, who died of a severed spine a week after he was arrested by police.
42Gray, hands cuffed behind his back and restrained by leg irons, was apparently injured during transport, slipped into a coma and died.
43Mourners gathered for the televised funeral, which lasted almost two hours.
44It drew such dignitaries as Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake and Rep.
45Elijah E. Cummings (D-Md.)
46, as well as a host of civil rights leaders including the Rev.
47Jesse Jackson, Dick Gregory, Maryland Rep.
48John Sarbanes and former Maryland Rep.
49and NAACP leader Kweisi Mfume.
50Gray's family sat in a front pew of the sanctuary that holds more than 2,000 people.
51Eight floral arrangements surrounded Gray's white coffin in front of the pulpit.
52Screens on the walls showed the words: "Black Lives Matter & All Lives Matter," which have become slogans at demonstrations around the country in the last year since a white police officer shot an unarmed black man in Ferguson, Mo.
53That case was followed by demonstrations over the deaths of African Americans in Staten Island, N.Y.; Cleveland; Tulsa, Okla.; and South Carolina.
54"The eyes of this country are all on us because they want to see whether we have the stuff to make this right," William Murphy Jr., a lawyer who is representing the Gray family, told the mourners.
55"We need justice not just for Freddie Gray, for the Freddie Grays to come," he said.
56"We will not rest until we address this and see that justice is done," Cummings said of the Gray case.
57"And so, this is our watch. We will not fail you."
58The investigation is continuing into the incident that began April 12 when Gray was walking with a friend and made eye contact with police.
59Both fled and police gave chase, catching Gray.
60Video of the arrest shows Gray with his hands cuffed behind him being put into the police van.
61The wagon stopped at least twice.
62At one stop, Gray was taken out by police, placed on the ground and his legs put in irons.
63He was returned to the van.
64At the second stop, another prisoner is put in the van, separated by a metal barrier.
65Throughout, Gray said he needed medical attention and at one point asked for an inhaler, police said.
66On Friday, Baltimore Police Commissioner Anthony Batts said he was appalled that Gray did not receive proper medical care immediately.
67He also said officers should have given Gray timely medical care during the transport to the police station.
68Batts also said there are no excuses for Gray not being buckled in a seat belt while in the van.
69Exactly what happened to Gray remains a mystery that will be answered when the full autopsy is released.
70Officials have said he died of a severed spine, confirming the family's original claim.
71The family also has said Gray's voice box was crushed and his neck snapped before he slipped into a coma and died a week after his arrest.
72Baltimore officials are scheduled to submit their findings into the death by Friday.
73Five of the six officers have been interviewed by police as have several witnesses, including some who shot video of at least one of the stops made by the van.
74Erica Garner, 24, the daughter of Eric Garner, who died in New York police custody, attended Gray's funeral.
75She said she came after seeing the video of Gray's arrest.
76"It's like there is no accountability, no justice," she said.
77"It's like we're back in the '50s, back in the Martin Luther King days. When is our day to be free going to come?"
78Civil rights leader the Rev.
79Al Sharpton said Monday he plans to visit Baltimore this week to discuss Gray's death.
80In a statement, Sharpton said he also wants to plan a two-day march in May from Baltimore to Washington.
