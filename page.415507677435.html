Netanyahu rules out establishment of Palestinian state in heated election vow

1JERUSALEM - Israel's prime minister, Benjamin Netanyahu, on Monday ruled out the establishment of a Palestinian state and vowed to strengthen construction in east Jerusalem settlements as he appealed to hard-line voters in a frenzied, final day of campaigning on the eve of national elections.
2While Netanyahu's comments were election rhetoric, they nonetheless put him further at odds with the international community, boding poorly for already strained relations with the U.S. and other key allies if he wins a third consecutive term.
3Netanyahu, who has governed for the past six years and has long been the most dominant personality in Israeli politics, has watched his standing plummet in recent weeks.
4Recent opinion polls show his Likud Party lagging behind Isaac Herzog's Zionist Union.
5Herzog, a centrist who has vowed to revive peace efforts with the Palestinians, repair ties with the U.S. and reduce the growing gaps between rich and poor, confidently predicted an "upheaval" was imminent.
6Tuesday's election caps an acrimonious three-month campaign that is widely seen as a referendum on Netanyahu.
7The hard-line leader has portrayed himself as the only politician capable of confronting Israel's numerous security challenges, while his opponents have focused on the country's high cost of living and presented Netanyahu as imperious and out of touch with the common man.
8As Netanyahu's poll numbers have dropped in recent days, he has appeared increasingly desperate, stepping up his nationalistic rhetoric in a series of interviews to local media to appeal to his core base.
9Netanyahu has also complained of an international conspiracy to oust him, funded by wealthy foreigners who dislike him, and on Sunday night, he addressed an outdoor rally before tens of thousands of hard-line supporters in Tel Aviv.
10The strategy is aimed at siphoning off voters from nationalistic rivals, but risks alienating centrist voters who are expected to determine the outcome of the race.
11Speaking to the nrg news website, Netanyahu said that turning over captured territory to the Palestinians would clear the way for Islamic extremists to take control and attack Israel.
12"Whoever ignores that is burying his head in the sand. The left is doing that, burying its head in the sand time after time," he said in the video interview.
13When asked if that means a Palestinian state will not be established if he is elected, Netanyahu replied, "Indeed."
14It was the latest - and clearest - attempt by Netanyahu to disavow his earlier support for Palestinian independence, which he first laid out in a landmark 2009 speech.
15"If we get this guarantee for demilitarization and necessary security arrangements for Israel, and if the Palestinians recognize Israel as the state of the Jewish people, we will be willing in a real peace agreement to reach a solution of a demilitarized Palestinian state alongside the Jewish state," he said at the time.
16Despite that pledge, two rounds of peace talks have failed and Netanyahu has continued to expand Jewish settlements.
17The international community overwhelmingly supports the establishment of a Palestinian state in the West Bank, east Jerusalem and Gaza Strip, areas captured by Israel in 1967, and opposes settlement construction.
18Netanyahu's tough new position is likely to worsen his already strained ties with his western allies if he is re-elected.
19It also raises questions about what kind of vision he has for solving the conflict with the Palestinians.
20Most demographers agree that if Israel continues to control millions of Palestinians, the country will not be able to remain both Jewish and democratic.
21Palestinian official Hanan Ashrawi said Netanyahu's comments were "dangerous" and could plunge the region into violence.
22"This is the real Netanyahu," she said.
23"From the beginning, he was attempting to carry out a grand deception by pretending to be in favour of the two-state solution. But what he was actually doing on the ground is destroying the chances of peace."
24Earlier, Netanyahu paid a last-minute visit to Har Homa, a Jewish development in east Jerusalem that Netanyahu helped build during his first term as prime minister in 1997.
25The sprawling district now houses more than 20,000 residents.
26Netanyahu told a hastily convened news conference that the neighbourhood it prevents "territorial contiguity" for a Palestinian state.
27The corner apartment was draped with banners saying "Only Netanyahu Can" and "It's Either Them or Us," two of the Likud's latest slogans.
28"There was a Palestinian attempt to connect Bethlehem and to burst into the city from the south," Netanyahu wearing a dark suit and a blue tie, and squinting into the sun, told a group of reporters, along with a few neighborhood residents.
29"The pressure on me not to build here was intense, but I insisted and it was worth it. Today there are tens of thousands of residents here. I and my friends in Likud will preserve Jerusalem and continue to build it."
30While Israel considers the area a part of its capital, the international community considers it an illegal settlement on occupied land.
31The Palestinians seek east Jerusalem as their capital.
32"We will preserve Jerusalem's unity in all its parts. We will continue to build and fortify Jerusalem so that its division won't be possible and it will stay united forever," Netanyahu said, explaining that Har Homa was built to contain Palestinian development in the nearby West Bank town of Bethlehem.
33Netanyahu dissolved his government in December and ordered the new election, two years ahead of schedule, in the belief that he would cruise to a new term.
34On Monday, it was Herzog, Netanyahu's chief rival, who appeared confident and upbeat.
35Visiting his party headquarters, Herzog, a trained lawyer and scion of a prominent political family, talked about a "crucial" vote for the country and warned against splitting the anti-Netanyahu vote among the various centrist parties.
36"Whoever wants an upheaval has to vote for us," Herzog said.
37Yair Lapid, leader of the centrist Yesh Atid party, which has also focused on the plight of Israel's middle class, received a warm welcome at a campaign stop in the coastal city of Netanya.
38Supporters warmly embraced him and stopped him to take selfies.
39He accused both Netanyahu and Herzog of working outside deals with special interest groups and said that only he was tackling the real issues facing the Israeli middle class.
40Lapid has so far refused to commit to either Herzog or Netanyahu, though he is widely seen as a natural ally of Herzog's in a future coalition.
41Exit polls are expected immediately after voting stops at 10 p.m. Tuesday night.
42But the true victor may not be known for several weeks.
43Under Israel's electoral system, no party has ever won an outright majority in the 120-member parliament.
44Instead, the party with the best chance of forming a coalition - usually the largest party - is given the chance to form a coalition.
45That decision is taken by the country's president, Reuven Rivlin.
46Since neither Likud nor the Zionist Union is expected to earn more than a quarter of the votes, Rivlin will meet with party leaders to determine who should be prime minister, followed by a lengthy period of negotiations to assemble a coalition.
47A potential kingmaker could be found in the new centrist party of Moshe Kahlon, who is running on an economic platform that deals almost exclusively with bread-and-butter issues while putting Israel's diplomatic challenges on the back burner.
48Kahlon, who broke off from Netanyahu's Likud, is demanding to become finance minister in the next government and has given no indication as to whom he would prefer as prime minister.
