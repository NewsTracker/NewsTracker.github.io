Putin says Nemtsov assassination a 'disgrace'

10 reasons to visit Slovenia in 2015

1Russian president Vladimir Putin has described the killing of opposition politician Boris Nemtsov as a tragedy that has brought disgrace on Russia, and warned against a rise in "extremist crime".
2"We must pay the greatest attention to crimes that prompt a major reaction, including those that are politically charged," he told interior ministry officials at a televised meeting on Wednesday.
3"We must rid Russia of disgrace and tragedies like the one we just saw... the audacious murder of Boris Nemtsov right in the centre of the capital."
4Mr Nemtsov, a longtime Putin critic and anti-corruption crusader, was gunned down near the Kremlin on Friday night in an attack that sent shockwaves through the Russian opposition and the world.
5Mr Putin has previously described Mr Nemtsov's murder as a "provocation" and vowed that everything would be done to bring to justice those who committed a "vile and cynical murder".
6Investigators have said they are examining possible motives for the murder, including Mr Nemtsov's opposition to Russian military actions in Ukraine and a link to his condemnation of the attack against the Charlie Hebdo satirical weekly in Paris in January.
7Mr Putin also warned against a rise in "extremist crime", without directly linking it to Mr Nemtsov's murder.
8"Extremists poison society with militant nationalism, intolerance and aggression," he said, adding that unrest in Ukraine was a consequence of such extremism.
9"It's important to react instantly to any signs of extremist action."
10The Kremlin has denied any involvement, saying that the killing was a "provocation" designed to discredit Mr Putin and strengthen his opponents.
11But Mr Nemtsov's friends say the Kremlin is to blame for fomenting an atmosphere of hatred towards its opponents.
12Who was Boris Nemtsov?
13Look back at the career of Boris Nemtsov who served as a deputy prime minister under Boris Yeltsin before becoming one of the most prominent opponents of Russian president Vladimir Putin.
14Earlier on Wednesday, the director of Russia's federal security service said that an investigation had identified several suspects, without giving details.
15Russian news agencies reported that investigators were seeking a car in connection to the killing that may be connected to the finance ministry.
16The ministry said in a statement sent to Reuters that the car, a Ford, did not belong to the ministry itself but was owned by a state enterprise responsible for providing security to government institutions, including the finance ministry.
17The vehicle drove past the spot where Mr Nemtsov was shot after the killing and when police patrol cars had already arrived at the scene, the statement said.

12(CNN)Slovenia's star is rising in 2015.
13The lakes and meadowlands of this small central European country have long been under-visited, but this year's anniversaries and cultural events are luring more travelers than ever before.
14The capital city Ljubljana is toasting a double anniversary: two millennia since its foundation and 120 years of renewal after a huge earthquake.
15A rare performance of Slovenia's oldest literary work will bring drama to the town of Skofja Loka, while carnivals and wine festivities unfold in Ptuj and Maribor.
16This rich calendar, as well as the timelessly beautiful landscapes, makes Slovenia almost unmissable in 2015.
17At about 300,000 inhabitants, Ljubljana is small for a capital city.
18But its history is epic: Ljubljana is celebrating 2,000 years since its foundation as a Roman city.
19This year sees an additional anniversary, as locals toast 120 years of reinvention since Ljubljana was rocked by a huge earthquake.
20The 1895 tremors destroyed 10% of the city's buildings.
21During its rebuilding, the Vienna Secession period brought new architectural styles to this baroque city.
22Modern Ljubljana is beautiful enough to rival Vienna or Prague.
23Its geranium-pink Franciscan Church in Preseren Square overlooks the interlacing Triple Bridge, with a backdrop of the Julian Alps.
24But Ljubljana's dragon emblem truly sets it apart.
25Some folklore links the dragon to a monster subdued by Jason and the Argonauts.
26A more likely source is the dragon-slaying Saint George, patron of Ljubljana Castle's chapel.
27These fire-breathing beasts glower from Dragon Bridge, adorn the city's coat of arms and top the castle.
28Saint George also points his lance in the northeasterly city of Ptuj.
29Near the entrance to the saint's namesake church there is a 14th-century statue of the saint slaying the dragon, which represents paganism.
30So it's intriguing to see the strong pagan influence of Kurentovanje, a Shrove carnival and Slovenia's most popular springtime folklore event.
31In the days before Shrove Tuesday, locals clad in sheepskin, masks and grotesque prosthetic tongues symbolically scare away winter by processing through Ptuj.
32Children dive out of the way of a devil with a soul-catching net who leads the procession.
33When the remarkable creatures of Postojna Caves were first recorded in the 17th century, locals called them baby dragons.
34Today, we know them as proteus, ghostly pale amphibians native only to this region of Slovenia.
35In Postojna, they're a huge tourism draw.
36An underground train takes visitors through caverns dripping with stalactites to a vivarium full of these strange animals.
37Scientists are discovering that the proteus is more bizarre than any creature of myth.
38The proteus can survive without food for eight to 10 years, and live for up to a century.
39They're blind, navigating via light-sensitive receptors and by perceiving weak electrical fields.
40"Although proteus may be one of the best-researched cave animals, it still holds many secrets," say Dr. Liljana Bizjak Mali and Dr. Boris Bulog of the University of Ljubljana.
41"We do not yet know if proteus can regenerate like other salamanders, but if they do we could learn much about stem cells, tissue repair and perhaps even cancer."
42Just 15 kilometers shy of Slovenia's border with Austria, the Julian Alps and miles of forest halt abruptly for Lake Bled.
43This small alpine lake, overlooked by a towering Romanesque castle, is startling.
44The lake's bright jade waters lap against dainty Bled Island (Blejski otok).
45Its most prominent building is the 17th-century Church of the Assumption, which is steeped in romantic tradition.
46According to regional lore, a happy marriage is assured to a husband who can carry his bride all 99 paces from the dock into the church.
47Some 55 wedding ceremonies took place in the island church last year, with many more couples visiting to toll its "wishing bell."
48Boat rentals ply the lake for much of the year.
49Summer visitors can just as easily swim to the island -- thermal springs keep the water a toasty 26 C (79 F).
50Come winter, when the trees are dusted with snow, ice skaters swirl around the frozen surface of the lake.
51In 2015, visitors to Skofja Loka have a rare chance to experience Slovenia's vibrant literary history.
52The medieval town is home to the oldest complete dramatic text ever written in the Slovene language, the Skofja Loka Passion Play (Skofjeloski pasijon).
53The original manuscript is closely guarded by the local monastery and only irregularly performed.
54This year 32,000 visitors are expected to watch a cast of 640 actors bring the play to life as a procession around multiple locations in the medieval town.
55"Local involvement is intense," says the play's project manager Matej Mohoric Peternelj.
56"We call it the Passion spirit."
57Performances will take place in the town from March 21 to April 12, 2015.
58Tickets can be booked at pasijon.si.
59Mercury and lace seem to bear little relation, but in Idrija they're cornerstones of local tradition.
60Mercury mines brought Idrija wealth.
61They're the reason for Idrija's spot on UNESCO's World Heritage list.
62But it's the centuries-old tradition of lace making that's given area a thriving new voice.
63More than 400 children and 150 adult students have come to try their hand at crafting intricate bobbin lace in Idrija over the past half-decade.
64Today, visitors are as likely to buy lace-embellished garters and jeans as more traditional souvenirs.
65"Fashion was, and still is, important for lace making," says Metka Fortuna.
66With 12 years as a lace-making teacher and now a head of the Idrija Lace School for nine years, Fortuna has watched the development of this intricate craft.
67"Older lace makers now cooperate with students to create products interesting for younger generations."
68You can experience both sides of Idrija's local pride with a descent of Anthony's Shaft and a visit to the lace making school.
69Slovenia's second largest city acquired polish as 2012's European Capital of Culture.
70But Maribor's enduring attraction dangles at the end of a vine.
71The Old Vine in Maribor's Lent neighborhood is said to be the world's oldest living grapevine.
72Maribor grew to prominence through a wine-growing trade that began in the Middle Ages, with a flourishing Jewish community at its heart.
73One famous vine has lasted throughout Maribor's vinicultural history.
74Wine pressed from the fruit of this 400-year-old vine is gifted to dignitaries as a symbolic key to Maribor.
75A ceremony adds pomp to the harvesting of the vine annually in early October.
76Year-round, the Old Vine House welcomes visitors to savor wine and history in equal measure.
77Triglav National Park in Slovenia's northwest covers nearly 3% of the country's area.
78Two million visitors arrive annually to hike around Bohinj, Slovenia's largest glacial lake, and to waterfalls like 78-meter-high Savica.
79The crowning attraction is Mount Triglav.
80This 2,864-meter mountain in Slovenia's only national park is a recurring motif in Slovene art, film and music, and a cherished emblem of national identity.
81As Slovenia's highest point, Mount Triglav remains a symbolic lightning rod for national identity.
82It's from Triglav's summit that the Slovene flag was unfurled at the country's 1991 independence.
83The country's former president, Milan Kucan, even declared climbing the mountain a duty for every Slovene national.
84There are trails around Triglav National Park to suit all abilities, but long hikes should only be undertaken with experienced guides.
85The Slovenian kozolec has grown from being a simple agrarian tool into a symbol of national pride.
86Visitors are sometimes bemused by the local fondness for the kozolec, a distinctive hay rack found throughout Slovenia's meadowlands.
87In 2013 a dedicated open-air museum in Sentrupert began to showcase the kozolec and its history.
88To most local people they're much more than a farming tool: they're a tangible reminder of expert craftsmanship, eliciting powerful nostalgia for Slovenia's past.
89Squeezed between Italy and Croatia, Slovene Istria has the vibe of the Italian Riviera.
90The star attraction of this 43-kilometer stretch of coast is Piran, a grand coral-roofed city lapped by the Adriatic's navy blue waters.
91Here in Slovenia's most southwesterly tip, Italian and Slovene are both official languages.
92Istria is known for rich olive oils and sweet Malvasia wine.
93Italian flavors come through in pastas and risottos scattered with shellfish.
94Croatian staples are also popular, such as octopus salads and gulas (stew).
95In this same region, salt is panned from the Mediterranean's most northerly salt flats in Secovlje in a method unchanged for seven centuries.
96As in so many places in Slovenia, tradition reigns supreme.
97Midsummer brings most of Piran's visitors, but shoulder season (April-May and September-October) has lovely weather without crowds.
