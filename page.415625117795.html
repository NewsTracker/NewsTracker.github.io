For liberal Israelis, Netanyahu's victory is a reality check from Israel's hawkish majority

Under ISIS, Life In Mosul Takes A Turn For The Bleak

1There had been a sense of urgency among moderate Israelis, and even an ounce of hope, that widespread frustration with Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's six straight years in office would lead voters to pull Israel away from what they perceive as its rightward march toward international isolation, economic inequality, and a dead end for peace with the Palestinians.
2But as the results trickled in on Wednesday, they showed Likud with a shocking lead that has all but guaranteed Netanyahu a third consecutive term.
3Netanyahu called it a victory "against all odds."
4The liberals' optimism has been replaced with devastation - an infuriating belief that the masses may never understand that logic shows the current path is suicidal.
5"Drink cyanide, bloody Neanderthals. You won," award-winning Israeli author and actress Alona Kimhi wrote on her Facebook page, before erasing it as her comments became the talk of the town.
6"Only death with save you from yourselves."
7Such rage rippled through liberal Israel this week.
8Social media was full of embittered Israelis accusing Netanyahu's supporters of racism, and some vowed to stop donating charity to the underprivileged whom they perceived as being automatic supporters of the right.
9The prime minister's main rival denounced such attacks.
10"Attempts to divide, vilify and spread hate in Israeli society disgust me, and it doesn't matter whether it comes from the right or the left," wrote Zionist Union leader Isaac Herzog on Facebook.
11The anger was about far more than the election, reflecting a larger and more dramatic battle for the face of the country.
12Israel's founding fathers were Jews of Ashkenazi, or eastern European, descent and the ideological predecessors of the Labor party, the main faction in the rebranded Zionist Union.
13The left led the country for its first three decades until Likud - heavily backed by working class Jews of Mizrahi, or Middle Eastern, descent - gained power in 1977.
14The Labor Party returned to the helm in the 1990s, leading the first efforts at peace with the Palestinians.
15But the Palestinian uprising in the early 2000s saw the return of hawkish rule, which in one form or another has lasted till today.
16The divisions between right and left largely revolve around the question of what do with territories captured by Israel in the 1967 Mideast war - and the millions of Palestinians who live there.
17Parties on the left would trade the land for peace and allow the creation of a Palestinian state.
18They also argue that the lands are a liability, since incorporating the Palestinians as citizens would destroy Israel as a Jewish-majority state.
19The right emphasizes the lands' strategic value and biblical symbolism and pushes constantly for settling them with Jews.
20Its success in this endeavor has, paradoxically, put the country on a path toward being a place where Jews may no longer be a strong majority.
21With more than 550,000 Israeli settlers now living in territories claimed by the Palestinians, Israeli liberals - along with the Palestinians - believe time is running out for the "two-state solution."
22So compelling is this "demographic argument" that Netanyahu himself has adopted its language, claiming at various times since 2009 that he, too, wants to end the occupation; but his party opposes this and Netanyahu continues to support the settlements, leading opponents to believe he is bamboozling them and adding to the sense of urgency.
23Activists at the headquarters of V-15, an initiative that called to unseat Netanyahu, silently bundled dozens of banners on Thursday, and one activist asked a journalist to leave.
24A whole floor of the Zionist Union's campaign headquarters was empty, and party leaders gazed up from crumpled posters next to a vacuum cleaner.
25"It's a big disappointment. There was a lot of energy for change here," said Zev Laderman, an investor in start-up companies, sitting in a boulevard cafe.
26"I woke up this morning to realize that I'm a minority in this country."
27The center-left's Zionist Union won 24 seats - somewhat higher than the combined previous total of the two parties that form it - but Likud won 30.
28Another 37 seats were captured by parties believed to be willing to support Likud for a solid majority in the 120-member parliament.
29And the left-wing Meretz party will now be the smallest party in the upcoming government.
30The looming coalition will likely feature right-wing pro-settler and ultra-Orthodox Jewish religious parties.
31In fundamental ways, they represent the opposite of the defiantly secular Israeli liberals who are fed up with taxpayer money being pumped to West Bank Jewish settlements and ultra-Orthodox Jewish communities.
32The prime minister's sudden turnaround toward victory took place after an 11th-hour effort to appeal to nationalist Israelis by pledging not to support an independent Palestinian state, and by warning voters of Arab citizens being bused to the polls in "droves" by left-wing organizations - comments that drew rebukes from Israeli Arabs and the White House.
33Netanyahu has since tried to contain the damage from his statements - saying he remains committed to Palestinian statehood if conditions throughout the region improve - and insisting he is not a racist.
34But it seems unlikely that peace negotiations with the Palestinians will be high on his agenda.
35And the Jewish settlement of the West Bank, which enrages liberal Israelis and cements the country's entanglement there, will likely march on.
36Liberal voters perceived this week's defeat less as the result of a poorly fought campaign than as a reflection of demographic trends and genuine public opinion in the country of 8 million.
37After years of failed peace efforts, including two Israeli offers for statehood that were rejected or ignored by the Palestinians, few think a deal is likely.
38Even the Zionist Union seemed to hide from the issue during the campaign, focusing instead on bread-and-butter issues like the country's high cost of living.
39"It doesn't matter what kind of campaign (the left) ran," said political blogger Tal Schneider.
40"There is a reality in the field. You can't change it. It's a nationalist public that is afraid of the Arabs."
41Sitting at a bustling cafe in a hipster neighborhood of Tel Aviv, a 26-year-old campaign activist for the Zionist Union broke down in tears about the party's defeat.
42"It's devastating," said Lior Shalish, the activist.
43She said the election results shouldn't be a surprise, just months after left-wing Israelis were attacked on the streets of Tel Aviv by nationalists during Israel's war against Hamas militants.
44"You don't get a left-wing government after that. Like, that doesn't change so quickly," Shalish said.
45"We were stupid to believe that it does."
46Some liberal Israelis said there were rays of light: A joint list unifying various Arab parties emerged as the country's third-largest party, re-energizing a disaffected Israeli Arab minority, and the V-15 initiative claims it increased turnout by centrist and left-wing voters.
47In the lead up to the election, the left's momentum reached its peak at a major rally this month, when tens of thousands of Israelis packed a Tel Aviv square demanding a change of government.
48The rally was seen as a victory.
49Many focused on the keynote speech by Meir Dagan, a former head of the Mossad intelligence agency, who issued an emotional appeal for change.
50But in retrospect, it seems a tipping point in favor of Netanyahu occurred when artist Yair Garbuz took to the podium and railed against the "amulet kissers" who support Netanyahu.
51His comments were perceived as a condescending swipe at the country's conservative working class of religious Jews of Sephardi, or Middle Eastern, lineage who have longstanding gripes with the country's European-descended Ashkenazi elite and lean heavily toward Likud.
52The day after elections, columnist Ben Caspit wrote an article in the Yediot Ahronot daily titled "Two States."
53He was not referring to the left's two-state solution for the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, but to Israel's own cultural divide.
54"Israel is split - between left and right, between Bibi and anti-Bibi, between aspirations for normalcy and aspirations for territory," Caspit wrote, using Netanyahu's nickname.
55"Two states, two styles, two world views, split once again."
1Thousands of Sunni Arabs from Mosul, Iraq's second largest city, escaped to Erbil at the end of the summer when the militants of the self-proclaimed Islamic State first overran the city and imposed a draconian social code.
2Among them is a man we'll call the professor - he, his wife and their children fled Mosul in August.
3He doesn't want his name published because his extended family still lives there under ISIS control.
4The professor now lives in Erbil, at the Motel Delicious, a seedy place despite the enticing name, with parakeets in the lobby and every room packed with his neighbors from Mosul.
5"I think ISIS is losing now," says the professor.
6He and others from Mosul are all watching for signs that their city will be the next target of an Iraqi government assault to oust ISIS from major urban areas seized by the militants last summer.
7The professor says that ISIS is more paranoid than they were when the militant group first entered the city in June.
8Six months ago, they blew up the cell phone towers around the city.
9Now, his relatives can only call late at night, standing on roof tops to catch distant signals.
10Recently, ISIS shut down all the escape routes out of the city.
11"So dangerous to try to get of Mosul now," he says, based on reports from his sisters still in Mosul.
12Kurdish peshmerga fighters rest on the outskirts of Mosul on Jan. 30.
13"Nowadays, they forbid everything. Everyone cannot get out of Mosul," he says.
14The only way is by paying a smuggler, but even those routes are dangerous due to coalition bombings.
15It's a city where beheadings and floggings became routine, and people believed to be heretics, like the Yazidis, a religious minority in Iraq, could be sold as slaves.
16Smoking is forbidden and women must be completely covered from head to toe.
17Lately, even their eyes must be covered.
18Despite the harsh social rules, the markets remain full.
19Produce is brought in from Syria and sellers set up shop from the back of the trucks, he says.
20"Everything is available there, and people are still taking salaries from the government," he says.
21"Maybe at least 30, 40 percent of people are still taking salaries."
22Iraqi officials confirm Baghdad spends up to $16 million a month on the government payroll in Mosul.
23But ISIS taxes the wages and takes a cut, say Western diplomats.
24Daily life is increasingly grim.
25Fighters are on edge as coalition airstrikes hit ISIS military bases and convoys.
26Some ISIS fighters have retreated to Mosul from the nearby battlefront in Tikrit, where the government launched the first major assault against ISIS.
27Other fighters are pulling out of Mosul to head for the relative safety of Syria.
28Other ISIS fighters are pulling out of Mosul to head for the relative safety of Syria.
29The professor's relatives report there is tension between the local Iraqis and the foreign fighters.
30"I saw them fighting - Iraqi's local and the foreigners," he says.
31"Some of the foreigners started to take their families and travel outside - and the local fighters reject."
32On Tuesday, across Mosul, Iraqi government planes dropped 2 million leaflets promising liberation soon.
33"Your armed forces are close to you, and they are ready to participate with you in defeating ISIS," was the message on the floating papers.
34But that seems unlikely.
35The Iraqi army is far from ready for an assault on Iraqi's second largest city.
36The first assault on ISIS in Tikrit has stalled for more than a week.
37The forces leading that military campaign are primarily Iraq's Shiite militias, backed and trained by Iran.
38"You bring in the military force, and you fight the terrorist there, you evict them. And then what?" asks Qubad Talabani, vice president of the Kurdish regional government.
39In other words, Iraq's militia may be able to take Tikrit, but it's unclear they will be able to hold it.
40Kurdish peshmerga fighters walk with their weapons as they take control of the area, on the outskirts of Mosul on Feb. 6.
41Mosul will be even a more difficult and sensitive operation, Talabani says.
42The city is five times larger than Tikrit, with more than 1 million civilians, mostly Sunni Arabs, who welcomed ISIS when they first arrived, relieved to be rid of an oppressive Shiite-dominated government and army.
43Now, the Sunnis of Mosul are watching Shiite forces battling ISIS in Tikrit.
44"That's the problem with the Tikrit operation, that it is a purely Shiite-led military operation against a heavily Sunni place of the country," Talabani says.
45"This is Saddam's birthplace here, with no political endgame anywhere in sight. Not for the people of Tikrit, not for the Sunnis of Iraq."
46The professor says attitudes in Mosul have changed after eight months of ISIS rule.
47Compared to those early months, ISIS is beginning to lose support day by day.
48"Every day, rejection is increasing," he says.
49But it's still not clear if a loss for ISIS is a win for the government of Baghdad.
50There is no political plan for broad reconciliation between Iraqi's Sunnis and Shiites as the push against ISIS in Tikrit and Mosul is on hold.
