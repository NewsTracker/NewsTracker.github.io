The Jinx, Serial: Robert Durst murder case highlights journalists' changing role as crime sleuths

1LOS ANGELES (AP) -- Robert Durst was a rich man living free despite police efforts to link him to murder.
2Adnan Syed was a young man imprisoned for life for killing an ex-girlfriend.
3Media scrutiny changed their fortunes, pushing both back into the courts: Durst is facing trial on a murder charge, and Syed awaits an appeal of his conviction.
4Observers say it's what journalists, or others taking on the role of investigative reporters, can and should do -- but not simply, or heedlessly, to play faux detective.
5"We are holding law enforcement accountable," said Kelly McBride, an expert on ethics for the Poynter Institute journalism think tank.
6"Our job is not to prove people innocent or guilty. But we very much are part of the checks and balances that ensure that democracy is working."
7Robert Durst after his arraignment in New Orleans, March 17, 2015.
8Durst, heard dramatically muttering "killed them all" in the finale of HBO's six-part docuseries "The Jinx: The Life and Deaths of Robert Durst," was charged Monday with first-degree murder in the 2000 shooting of his confidante, Susan Berman.
9Police had planned to question Berman as part of their renewed probe into the 1982 disappearance of Durst's wife.
10In 2003, the multimillionaire real estate heir was acquitted of murder in the death of a Texas neighbor.
11Syed, who has maintained his innocence in the strangulation of Hae Min Lee in 1999, when both were teenagers, was granted a request for review by Maryland's Court of Special Appeals after the popular National Public Radio podcast "Serial" dissected the evidence against him last year.
12News and entertainment programs spotlighting criminal cases aren't new: "America's Most Wanted" began hunting fugitives in 1988.
13But the HBO and radio projects, along with a number of TV series, are full-blown investigations.
14CBS' long-running crime-focused newsmagazine "48 Hours" has had an impact on a number of cases.
15The family of Ryan Ferguson, a Missouri man imprisoned in the killing of a newspaper sports editor, credited the attention of "48 Hours" for drawing attention to his case.
16He was released after a court determined police fabricated evidence against him.
17"Sometimes people feel that nothing will happen to them if they talk to us," said Susan Zirinsky, the show's senior executive producer.
18"Obviously that can be incorrect if it goes public. Any exposure can end up coming back to haunt them. We've had killers who think they can outsmart us and talk to us, and then the authorities get them."
19Criminals can be braggarts, said Rebecca Lonergan, a University of Southern California Gould School of Law professor and a former federal prosecutor.
20"There's a certain thrill in talking to media," she said.
21But the suggestion that media's recent record puts law enforcement's competence in doubt deserves scrutiny, she and others said.
22Filmmakers worked on "The Jinx" for seven years, according to HBO.
23The Orange County Cold Case Task Force, formed last year with 12 investigators, was handed some 800 unsolved murder cases dating back to 1961, said task force member Santa Ana police Sgt. Richard Gatto.
24"We have access to certain databases that (journalists) might not have access to," Gatto said.
25"But as far as the law is concerned, there are certain things that reporters can do that we can't do as agents of the government."
26Police must heed constitutional safeguards including the Sixth Amendment, which protects the right to counsel.
27If a suspect gives any indication that he may have killed someone, all law enforcement interviews must stop, Lonergan said.
28Not so with news reporting, which sets its own ethical standards.
29"As journalists, we don't have legal restrictions on how we gather information. We have civil penalties when we do a bad job, but we can pretty much do whatever we want," Poynter's McBride said, adding "most of us believe we have an obligation to be transparent and honest."
30With "The Jinx," she said, Durst's vague and rambling utterances -- captured on a body microphone he kept on during a restroom visit -- required further scrutiny.
31"Your obligation is to find out what the context is and not assume you know what the context is," McBride said, suggesting Durst should have been given the chance to address what he meant.
32Filmmaker Andrew Jarecki has said Durst knew he was being recorded and signed a contract giving the project free rein in using the material it gathered.
33Any evidence was shared with police well before the series aired, he said.
34Durst's longtime Houston lawyer, Chip Lewis, called Jarecki "duplicitous" for not making it clear to Durst that he would be giving footage to police.
35Bob Steele, a Poynter fellow and recently retired DePauw University professor, said he was unfamiliar with details of the Durst case or "The Jinx" but had a strong caution for reporters in general.
