In Nepal, the devout see divine anger in the devastation wrought by massive earthquake

1PUJARI, Nepal - Some blame the corrupt politicians for bringing devastation to this Himalayan nation.
2Others fault Western tourists with tight T-shirts and loose morals.
3But among many of Nepal's most devoutly religious, the chaos and destruction wrought a week ago by a massive earthquake makes one thing clear: The gods are angry.
4Now, in the shadow of some of Nepal's holiest mountaintop temples, many find comfort in the notion that there was a divine and just cause behind the tragedy that has killed thousands and left hundreds of thousands more homeless and destitute.
5"It's a warning from the gods to the politicians: Change yourselves, or who knows what will happen," said Ishwar Nath Yogi, the 51-year-old high priest at Gorkha Kalika, a revered temple to the goddess Kali and the birthplace of Nepal's first king.
6Yogi was certain the Hindu goddess of death and destruction had saved him and the many others who were praying in the 17th century temple when the Earth heaved and the red bricks of its walls began crashing down in the magnitude-7.8 quake on April 25.
7Those less fortunate were unlucky, the yellow-robed holy man said, or maybe even undeserving.
8Perhaps they did not pray enough, or did not revere their sacred cattle.
9Maybe they embraced Western cultures and lost their own traditions.
10"We are a Hindu nation," he explained, a place where dressing conservatively is simply a given.
11"Look at the Americans, the Japanese, they run around half naked. That's why the gods were mad."
12Three-fourths of Nepal's 27 million people consider themselves Hindu, and the religion has a deep connection to this rugged Himalayan land dotted with countless temples and shrines.
13The king, for example, has long been worshipped by many as an incarnation of the Hindu god Vishnu.
14Even as Nepal modernized in recent decades, many of its traditional beliefs remain deeply felt.
15There is also a practical side to the devotion; most Nepalese are desperately poor and rely heavily on agriculture and cattle-rearing to survive.
16Appeasing the gods who control nature is just common sense.
17The rivers, the trees, the raptors that sweep the peaks on the horizon - all can be considered holy.
18Prayer is a daily habit, and religious idols as ubiquitous as home-brewed milk tea.
19Pilgrimages to mountaintop temples that skim the sky offer spiritual relief and a path to salvation.
20"My god is up there," said 23-year-old Sushma Kanwar, cradling a squalling baby in her lap as she pointed toward the hilltop temple to Kali from a valley below.
21"Somebody must have done something very bad. There is so much that could have angered the gods: theft, corruption, murder. These things are only getting worse."
22Nepal is deeply divided along ethnic and political lines, and is still struggling to recover from a bloody Maoist insurgency that finally ended in 2006.
23It ranks as one of the world's more corrupt nations, and among the poorest, with an annual per capita income of just $712.
24Some regret that the country, officially a Hindu kingdom for more than 200 years, was declared a secular republic in 2007.
25Others see lingering past evils inviting retribution, including the 2001 massacre of the royal family, apparently by the crown prince.
26The same punishing gods, though, are also seen as benevolent - sparing lives, for example, with an earthquake that came on a weekend when children were not in school, and at midday when many villagers were outside in their fields and not in their now-destroyed homes.
27Though Kanwar and her family lost their home and everything in it, she considers herself blessed, and prays to Kali these days with an extra measure of penitence.
28"Everyone in this village, our houses are destroyed but no one has died. Of course, this is the work of the goddess," she said.
29The same spirit of gratitude spread throughout the temple town of Manakamana in the quake's aftermath.
30Another pilgrimage site in the quake-wracked district of Gorkha, it suffered no casualties among its 7,200 residents or among the thousands of tourists crammed into the alleys and the temple square when the quake turned serene scenes of prayer into a nightmare of screaming and chaos.
31Concrete buildings collapsed.
32The temple itself lost a brick wall and fell sharply askew.
33Locals saw the lack of casualties as a gift from the 17th century temple's goddess, Bhagwati, a granter of wishes visited by some 10,000 people a day.
