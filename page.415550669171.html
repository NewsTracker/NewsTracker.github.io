Microsoft to kill Internet Explorer name, ship Windows 10 with 2 browsers

Netanyahu's shock re-election leaves Israel's pollsters red-faced

1Microsoft is trying on some new names for its yet-to-be-released successor to the infamous Internet Explorer Web browser.
2The Verge reported Microsoft's marketing chief Chris Capossela announced the move Monday at the Convergence Conference in Atlanta.
3The company previously announced that Windows 10 would ship with two browsers: Internet Explorer to keep enterprise users with compatibility needs happy, and something codenamed "Project Spartan" for average users.
4The Spartan browser, which will get a new name, will come packed with new features like an integrated Cortana personal assistant and the ability to annotate Web pages.
5Internet Explorer has come as part of Microsoft's popular Windows operating system for nearly two decades.
6It is - by far - the most-used Web browser, consistently gobbling up 58 percent of the market, according to analyst group NetMarketShare.com.
7But despite its dominant market share, IE continues to be the black sheep of Internet browsers.
8Competitors Firefox and Google Chrome are generally considered faster, easier to work with and more compatible with different kinds of Web content.
9Microsoft has addressed IE's poor reputation head on, but it has so far been unable to shake the image that it lags behind competitors.
10So when it came time to name its new browser, the company decided to do some market research.
11Attaching the Microsoft moniker in front of a secret new name increased appeal, while "IE" and "Internet Explorer" in front of a secret name were at the bottom of the list, according The Verge.
12Capossela also reiterated that Windows 10 will ship with Internet Explorer as well as this new browser, though there's no telling how long that will last.
13One thing seems certain: IE's days are numbered.
1Netanyahu's Likud won 30 of parliament's 120 seats in Tuesday's ballot, against 24 for the center-left Zionist Union - upsetting opinion surveys that as recently as Friday gave the challenger a four-seat lead.
2Exit polls also proved unreliable.
3Israel's top three television stations, airing first returns as voting booths closed, found the parties close or tied.
4Had that been borne out, either could have potentially headed the next government.
5Instead, during overnight counting, Likud's tally went from 27 seats to 30 and the Zionist Union's from 27 to 24.
6Grilled on the discrepancy, Channel 2 TV's veteran pollster Mina Tzemach said many Likud voters declined to take part in replicating their vote in the dummy ballot boxes set up by survey-taking companies outside voting stations.
7Even though exit polls are anonymous, she suggested such reticence might have cultural roots for Israelis originally from countries with different political regimes in which they worry about sharing their private voting choices.
8"In certain voting stations, voting stations in places where there are a lot of new immigrants, pro-Likud ballot boxes, the percent of those who voted (in the exit polls) was especially low," Tzemach told Israel's Army Radio.
9Fellow survey-taker Camil Fuchs agreed, saying final counts from voting stations he had monitored showed that a significant number of Likud supporters had not participated in exit polls.
10If they did participate, they may also not have been honest about the way they voted and as exit polls close earlier than the real polls, a last-minute surge in Likud votes, in response to a call from Netanyahu, may have been missed, he said.
11"Some people don't say (in exit polls) what they really voted, and the exit polls close about two hours before the voting booths," Fuchs told Israel Radio.
12Israeli election forecasts have been wrong before - in 1981, when the Likud narrowly won; in 1992 about the return to left-wing Labor party rule; and in 1996, when Netanyahu toppled Labor incumbent Shimon Peres for his first term in office.
13Recent reliance on Internet-based studies has thrown another spanner in the works, according to Avi Degani, an Israeli pollster who says he conducted telephone surveys exclusively.
14Since last month, he has been the only one consistently predicting a victory for Netanyahu.
15Degani said Web-based "panels," made up of tens of thousands of pre-selected respondents, rarely reflect Israeli society accurately as they favor the tech-savvy, educated and urbane.
16"The Internet does not represent the State of Israel or the people of Israel. (It is) biased strongly toward Tel Aviv," Degani told reporters in a conference call arranged by the Israel Project advocacy group, referring to Israel's second largest city and financial capital.
17"People who are in the periphery ... and have a stronger tendency to vote Likud are, I think, very poorly represented."
18In separate remarks to Reuters, Degani said Israeli pollsters were always bedevilled by some 30 percent of citizens whose votes are unknowable - either because they waver until the last minute or end up backing fringe parties that do not muster enough support to enter parliament and are nixed from the tally.
19"We are talking about 20 parliament seats that could go either way. It is almost impossible to tackle statistically."
20Still, Degani said he anticipated Netanyahu's win by finding that at least half of wavering voters would choose Likud, adding that some of those respondents viewed themselves as rallying against Zionist Union's strong showing in opinion polls.
21"It is a highly emotional matter in Israel, and the Likud had the added advantage of being the last party, with the possible exception of (liberal) Meretz, of having a defined ideology. The rest are just about personalities," Degani said.
