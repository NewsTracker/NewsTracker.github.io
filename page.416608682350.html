Nepal quake toll tops 5,000 as aid reaches area near epicenter

1GUMDA, Nepal (AP) - Hands pressed together in supplication, the Nepalese women pleaded for food, shelter and anything else the helicopter might have brought on an in-and-out run Wednesday to this smashed mountain village near the epicenter of last weekend's mammoth earthquake that killed more than 5,000 people.
2Unlike in Nepal's capital, where most buildings were spared complete collapse, the tiny hamlets clinging to the remote mountainsides of Gorkha District have been ravaged.
3Entire clusters of homes were reduced to piles of stone and splintered wood.
4Orange plastic tarps used for shelter now dot the cliff sides and terraced rice paddies carved into the land.
5But food is not the only necessity in short supply out here beyond the reaches of paved roads, electricity poles and other benefits of the modern world.
6These days, even water is scarce.
7Communication is a challenge.
8And modern medical care is a luxury many have never received.
9Gumda is one of a handful of villages identified as the worst hit by Saturday's 7.8-magnitude earthquake, from which it will almost certainly take years to recover.
10As in many villages, though, the death toll in Gumda was far lower than feared, since many villagers were working outdoors when the quake struck at midday.
11Of Gumda's 1,300 people, five were killed in the quake and 20 more were injured.
12As the helicopter landed Wednesday with 40-kilogram (90-pound) sacks of rice, wind and rain whipped across the crest of the mountain.
13Seeing the conditions, the U.N. World Food Program's Geoff Pinnock shouted over the roar of the propellers, "the next shipment has to be plastic sheets. These people need shelter more than they need food."
14About 200 villagers huddled under a few umbrellas and plastic tarps as they waited to receive the aid, some with runny noses and chattering teeth.
15With the erratic Himalayan weather, aid workers are worried about keeping people warm, fed and safe.
16"More helicopters, more personnel and certainly more relief supplies including medical teams, shelter, tents, water and sanitation and food are obviously needed," said Pinnock, who was coordinating the aid relief flights.
17With 8 million Nepalese affected by the earthquake, including 1.4 million needing immediate food assistance, Pinnock said the relief effort would stretch on for months.
18Nepalese police said Wednesday the death toll from the quake had reached 5,027.
19Another 18 were killed on the slopes of Mount Everest, while 61 died in neighboring India, and China's official Xinhua News Agency reported 25 dead in Tibet.
20The disaster also injured more than 10,000, police said, and rendered thousands more homeless.
21The U.N. says the disaster has affected 8.1 million people - more than a fourth of Nepal's population of 27.8 million - and that 1.4 million needed food assistance.
22Planes carrying food and other supplies have been steadily arriving at Kathmandu's small airport, but the aid distribution process remains fairly chaotic, with Nepalese officials having difficulty directing the flow of emergency supplies.
23About 200 people blocked traffic in the capital Wednesday to protest the slow pace of aid delivery.
24The protesters faced off with police and there were minor scuffles but no arrests were made.
25Police arrested dozens of people on suspicion of looting abandoned homes as well as causing panic by spreading rumors of another big quake.
26Police official Bigyan Raj Sharma said 27 people were detained for stealing.
27But in a sign that life was inching back to normal, banks in Kathmandu opened for a few hours Wednesday and stuffed their ATMs with cash, giving people access to money.
28Thousands of people lined up at bus stations in the capital, hoping to reach their hometowns in rural areas.
29Some have had little news of family and loved ones since Saturday's quake.
30Others are scared of staying close to the epicenter, northwest of Kathmandu.
31"I am hoping to get on a bus, any bus heading out of Kathmandu. I am too scared to be staying in Kathmandu," said Raja Gurung, who wanted to get to his home in western Nepal.
32"The house near my rented apartment collapsed. It was horrible. I have not gone indoors in many days. I would rather leave than a live a life of fear in Kathmandu."
33In some heartening news, French rescuers freed a man from the ruins of a three-story Kathmandu hotel more than three days after the quake.
34Rishi Khanal, 27, said he drank his own urine to survive.
35Khanal had just finished lunch at a hotel on Saturday and had gone up to the second floor when everything suddenly started moving and falling.
36He was struck by falling masonry and trapped with his foot crushed under rubble.
37"I had some hope but by yesterday I'd given up. My nails went all white and my lips cracked ... I was sure no one was coming for me. I was certain I was going to die," he told The Associated Press from his hospital bed on Wednesday.
38Khanal said he was surrounded by dead people and a terrible smell.
39But he kept banging on the rubble all around him and eventually this brought a French rescue team that extracted him after being trapped for 82 hours.
40"I am thankful," he said.
41Associated Press writers Binaj Gurubacharya, Jerry Harmer and Rishi Lekhi in Kathmandu contributed to this report.
