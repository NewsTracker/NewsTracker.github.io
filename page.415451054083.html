10Another argument from the pro-homework camp is that parents can track what their kids are doing at school.
11Well, I get plenty of information from parent teacher meetings, and folders stuffed full of work are sent home regularly.
12I simply don't want school time to bleed into home time.
13For me school is school, home is home.
14There's too much else going on.
15My daughter is in the school band and two different choirs - it's her choice and she loves it.
16She also does swimming.
17Added up, that's almost a whole extra school day.
18And more importantly, every minute she spends scratching her head over a matrix literacy task, is a minute less that she could be playing in the backyard.
19Free play is usually the first activity to be pushed down the priority list.
20It's infuriatingly unstructured.
21It can't be scheduled within neat half-hour blocks.
22It relies on the alchemy of a couple of kids, space and make-believe.
23It doesn't seem to achieve anything.
24But it does, and I will fight to protect that precious time.
25It's also important that my kids help set the table for dinner and entertain their baby brother.
26I think this falls under the key learning area of "life skills".
27And the most valuable literacy 'homework' of all?
28Reading a book for sheer pleasure - preferably flopped on the lounge.
29Now that's a lifetime habit to encourage.
30What's surprising is how little I have to press my case with the teachers during my annual homework meeting.
31They nod in sympathy at my nervously delivered spiel.
32They know how much kids need to rest and reboot.
33But they all say the same line: "The other parents would complain if we didn't give homework."
34This is a very curious situation.
35Well-intentioned schools give homework to satisfy the parents who demand it.
36Well-intentioned parents make their kids to do homework because they think it's the right thing to do.
37And where are the kids in all this?
38There's no consensus that homework lifts academic performance in the primary years.
39There's conflicting research about whether it has any other benefits at all.
40This is probably why the Department's homework policy leaves a lot of wriggle room.
41Schools can opt out, and parents can "discuss alternative strategies".
