AP ANALYSIS: US on sidelines of key Iraqi battle against IS

Bob Evans Reports Earnings Miss

1The campaign for Tikrit is a dress rehearsal for the real contest: The fight to recapture Mosul, Iraq's second-largest city and the extremists' biggest stronghold.
2But can a large-scale ground offensive alone succeed, without U.S.-led air support?
3The Tikrit operation is aimed at stopping Islamic State fighters from closing in on Samarra, a Shiite holy city just to the south that tens of thousands of Shiite militiamen rushed to defend during the extremists' blitz across northern Iraq last June.
4One of the biggest campaigns in the heart of militant-controlled Iraq, the battle for Tikrit involves a complex mix of several Iraqi military brigades and thousands of Shiite militiamen and Sunni tribal fighters.
5Directing the offensive with the aid of dozens of Iranian military advisers is a powerful Iranian general, Ghasem Soleimani, commander of the elite Revolutionary Guard's Quds Force.
6Glaringly absent are the U.S.-led coalition forces whose air campaign since last summer has nearly halted the Islamic State rampage across Iraq.
7Pentagon spokesman Army Col.
8Steve Warren said this week that the U.S. is not providing air power in the Tikrit operation "simply because the Iraqis haven't requested us to."
9Liberating the city without the backing of coalition airstrikes will put Iraq's security forces to the ultimate battle-readiness test since any operation to recapture Iraq's densely-populated cities - including Mosul and Fallujah - will have to rely almost entirely on ground forces to minimize civilian casualties.
10However, more of a concern for the U.S.-led coalition is Iran's prominent role in the fight against the Islamic State militants.
11Iran has long been influential in Iraq, but never so much so as over the past year, when the Iraqi military collapsed in the face of the Sunni extremists' onslaught.
12Iraqi officials have noted Iran's quick response to their urgent requests for weapons and frontline assistance even as they accuse the coalition of falling short on commitments on the ground.
13Embedding coalition advisers and forward air controllers - the officers who call in airstrikes - with Iraqi military units presents a twofold challenge.
14Frontline positions would put their lives in danger at a time when risk aversion is at its peak.
15But it also raises the potential for coalition forces and Iranian soldiers to share a battlefield, a politically untenable prospect for the U.S. given the uncertain future of ongoing nuclear talks with Tehran.
16However, the U.S.-led mission has hit a roadblock in its efforts to support the Iraqi government.
17Both the Iraqi and U.S. government agree that airstrikes have pushed the militants back and the group has struggled to gain territory since airstrikes began.
18But there will come a point where airstrikes alone will not be enough.
19Most of the battlefield successes in Iraq have been coordinated efforts, with Iraqi and Kurdish forces and Shiite militias fighting on the ground and the U.S.-led coalition providing air power.
20The siege on the village of Amirli just north of Baghdad, when many feared the capital itself might fall, was broken last year with the help of U.S.-led airstrikes and a fighting force of mainly Shiite militias.
21Shiite militiamen backed by a coalition air campaign also retook the town of Jurf al-Sukhr, on Baghdad's outskirts, from the militants in October.
22Soleimani, the Iranian general leading the Tikrit operation, was a key player in both of those campaigns.
23But Iraqi and Kurdish officials and Shiite militia fighters all acknowledge the crucial role the coalition airstrikes played in their modest victories.
24With the military operation to retake Mosul planned for as early as April, many are skeptical about whether the Iraqi military will be ready for the fight.
25Efforts to recruit Sunni tribes - seen as crucial for rooting out the militants from their strongholds - have yielded few results.
26Training and arming of Iraqi soldiers have also stalled.
27The failure so far to make headway in Tikrit does not bode well.
28Iraqi forces are bogged down on the outskirts of the city, unable to penetrate the extremists' defenses.
29On Tuesday, a suicide bomber drove a military vehicle into a checkpoint manned by government forces and Shiite fighters south of Tikrit, killing four troops and wounding 12, authorities said.
30The stalemate persists despite assurances from Iraqi Prime Minister Haider al-Abadi that momentum is on Iraq's side after recent successes in the oil refinery town of Beiji - an operation that received heavy aerial support from the U.S.-led coalition.
31The Islamic State group, which controls a third of Iraq and neighboring Syria in its self-declared caliphate, has littered major roadways with mines that slow any ground advance and require painstaking clearance operations before troops can safely move through.
32Kurdish and Iraqi forces frequently cite bomb-detecting equipment as one of their biggest shortfalls on the battlefield.
33The battle for Tikrit is likely to involve Iraq's first serious urban warfare challenge, involving street battles that Iraqi security forces are not trained for.
34Concerns are also mounting over the Shiite militias' battlefield conduct in a largely Sunni region where some residents are said to have initially welcomed the Islamic State militants as a better alternative to the Shiite-led government in Baghdad.
35In their earlier battlefield successes, the Shiite fighters often flattened towns and villages so as to prevent Sunni residents from returning home.
36Winning over the Sunni tribes is essential because they know the terrain of the Sunni-majority areas under Islamic State control and can fight with and advise the Shiite fighters.
37But animosity has reached a peak and fragile battlefield alliances could be short-lived once the common enemy is eliminated.
38And heavy Iranian involvement only threatens to exacerbate sectarian tensions.
