EU tells Greece time, patience running out

1BRUSSELS/BERLIN, March 19 (Reuters) - Euro zone leaders told Greece on Thursday its leftist-led government must implement agreed reforms to avert a looming cash crunch that could force it out of the single currency.
2Greece has been kept from bankruptcy by two international bailouts but now risks running out of money within weeks if it does not receive more funds.
3Greek banks reported the largest deposit withdrawals in a month, a sign savers are worried about the outlook for the country's finances and institutions.
4Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras requested a meeting with the leaders of Germany, France and the main EU institutions on the sidelines of a European Union summit to press for Athens to receive short-term funds to keep itself afloat.
5German Chancellor Angela Merkel doused any expectation of a deal at the late-night session, saying decisions were up to the Eurogroup of finance ministers of the 19-nation euro area.
6"I want to say: don't expect a solution, don't expect a breakthrough. It's not the right setting," she told reporters on arrival at the summit.
7"Decisions are made in the Eurogroup and that's how it will remain."
8French President Francois Hollande said the message to Tsipras would be that all sides must stick to their commitments.
9"Time is ticking away, so we don't have a lot of time," he said.
10"So we have really got to get to work."
11A person familiar with ECB thinking said European Central Bank President Mario Draghi would make clear the bank would not lift its limit on Greek short-term debt issuance, which Greece's finance minister has said is "asphyxiating" his country.
12"It's up to Greece to meet its commitments in order to get money from its creditors," said the person.
13"The ECB doesn't do bridge finance."
14Tsipras did not respond to questions about his country's funding problems, saying only: "The EU needs more political initiatives that respect both democracy and its treaties so that we leave behind the crisis and move to growth."
15A Greek official said Athens had enough cash to pay a final 350 million euro instalment of a loan repayment to the International Monetary Fund on Friday.
16Merkel earlier told the German parliament in Berlin the crisis could only be resolved if Greece stuck to reform commitments it made when it agreed with the euro zone on a four-month extension of its bailout programme.
17A political meeting of a small group of leaders could not be used to circumvent the formal agreement Greece concluded on Feb. 20 with the Eurogroup, she told the lawmakers.
18"There remains a very tough way ahead," Merkel said.
19Greece must understand that international aid brought with it an obligation "to reform its budget and work towards one day no longer needing help".
20European Council President Donald Tusk, chairing the summit, said he arranged a side-meeting with Tsipras to take the heat out of exchanges around the table.
21Belgian Prime Minister Charles Michel said he would complain about the private session among a few players.
22"It's a bad method," he said.
23Luxembourg's Xavier Bettel said he would have liked to have been present.
24Tusk met the Benelux leaders to defuse their irritation, promising that no decision would be taken behind their backs.
25He asked whether any leader wanted a full euro zone summit on Greece but no one took him on the offer, an aide said.
26Two EU/IMF bailouts totalling 240 billion euros have kept Greece from bankruptcy since 2010 but its economy has shrunk by 25 percent, partly due to austerity measures imposed by the lenders.
27It risks running out of cash without more aid or permission to issue more short-term debt.
28European Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker has been trying to build bridges between Tsipras and Greece's creditors.
29His exasperated tone suggested even Athens' friends are losing patience with its belligerent rhetoric and procrastination.
30EU sources said Greece had refused to provide any update on public finances or reform plans in a conference call of senior euro zone officials on Tuesday and had denied EU, IMF and European Central Bank experts access to government buildings in Athens, insisting all meetings take place in a hotel.
31The discussions had not gone beyond procedural issues of who would be allowed to talk to whom, the sources said.
32Asked whether the experts had been kicked out, an EU official said: "The talks in Athens were paused yesterday. This is normal procedure and can be helpful to take stock. There is willingness to talk but the Greeks must deliver."
33Deputy Prime Minister Yannis Dragasakis, in a television talk show early on Thursday, accused the creditors' team of exceeding their authority.
34"The technical teams came to collect facts, but they then requested things which went beyond their jurisdiction. For example, they wanted to review the government as a whole, every ministry's programme and the reforms," he told Alpha TV.
35Dragasakis acknowledged Greece faced a liquidity problem and needed the cooperation of its European lenders to keep paying salaries, pensions and debt repayments: "We haven't received any (bailout) tranches since August 2014 but we have been meeting all of our obligations," he said.
36"This has its limits."
37The ECB agreed late on Wednesday to raise the limit on emergency lending to Greek banks by 400 million euros to 69.8 billion, banking sources said.
38Bankers said savers withdrew about 300 million euros in deposits on Wednesday.
39Greece has asked to receive some 1.9 billion euros in ECB profits on Greek bond holdings, which finance ministers have linked to progress in implementing the programme.
40It also wants ECB permission to issue more short-term treasury bills, which only Greek banks are willing to buy.
41Tsipras' Syriza party won a general election in January on a platform of scrapping the bailouts, ending austerity and refusing to cooperate with the "troika" of institutions -- EU, ECB and IMF -- supervising its rescue programme.
42The prime minister lambasted EU "technocrats" on Wednesday for demanding prior consultations on the cost of a "humanitarian bill" adopted by parliament to provide food stamps and free electricity to the poorest Greeks worst hit by austerity.
43Athens has made no move in the month since the Brussels agreement to bring forward legislation to meet its commitments under the bailout agreement.
