3WASHINGTON - The Supreme Court struck another blow for free speech Monday, ruling that threats made over the Internet should include at least some measure of intent.
4The decision was a temporary victory for Anthony Elonis and those like him whose threatening words on Facebook or similar social media sites may instill fear in their targets.
5It was a defeat for the government and groups that defend victims of domestic violence.
6Chief Justice John Roberts wrote the 8-1 decision for a near-unanimous court.
7It was based only on the court's interpretation of a federal statute, rather than more broadly under the First Amendment.
8The ruling also left open the possibility -- indeed, perhaps even the likelihood -- that Elonis will be found guilty under the tougher standard.
9While Roberts said "negligence is not sufficient to support a conviction," he made clear that the threat would be a crime if it was intended as a threat or if Elonis knew it would be viewed as one.
10"The jury was instructed that the government need prove only that a reasonable person would regard Elonis's communications as threats, and that was error," Roberts said.
11"Federal criminal liability generally does not turn solely on the results of an act without considering the defendant's mental state."
12Justice Clarence Thomas dissented, and Justice Samuel Alito dissented in part.
13Both would have said mere recklessness on Elonis' part would be sufficient for conviction; Roberts left it up to the lower court to decide that question first.
14"The court's disposition of this case is certain to cause confusion and serious problems," Alito wrote.
15While he, too, would have reversed Elonis' conviction and sent the case back for further review, he said the court should have defined what does and does not constitute a true threat.
16"The court declines to say," he lamented.
17"Attorneys and judges are left to guess. This will have regrettable consequences."
18Elonis was 27 and recently unemployed in Pennsylvania five years ago when he began posting threats against his estranged wife and others, from a generic kindergarten class to the FBI agents who came to his door.
19He was convicted on four counts of transmitting threats and sentenced to 44 months in prison.
20He completed his term a year ago.
21The question that has split federal appeals courts is whether the threats must be intentional, or whether they are illegal just because a "reasonable person" -- such as those on the receiving end -- takes them seriously.
22Elonis was convicted under the latter standard; a majority of justices ruled that's not sufficient.
23Elonis might not be off the hook, however.
24The high court's ruling means his case will be sent back to a lower court to determine whether he meant what he posted or was at least reckless in posting it.
25If the court rules that his posts were intentionally or recklessly threatening, his conviction would stand.
26The case represents a critical test of free speech in the Internet age, when words that seem threatening emanate from violent spouses and video game-players alike.
27The justices were seeking a rule that could result in locking up the former and letting the latter off the hook.
28Elonis' case offered a perfect test.
29After his wife, Tara, left him and took their two children, he lost his job at an Allentown, Pa., amusement park and began a series of dark posts containing explicit references to violence against his wife, co-workers, kindergartners, police and the FBI.
30Sometimes, he imitated rap lyrics.
31Other times, he referred to his First Amendment rights.
32His lawyers said it was a form of therapy as well as art.
33"Did you know that it's illegal for me to say I want to kill my wife?"
34Elonis wrote in one of many posts.
35"It's illegal. It's indirect criminal contempt. It's one of the only sentences that I'm not allowed to say."
36The lengthy diatribe copied nearly word-for-word a satirical sketch by The Whitest Kids U' Know comedy troupe, concluding with Elonis' own summation: "Art is about pushing limits. I'm willing to go to jail for my constitutional rights. Are you?"
37Elonis' attorneys said he never intended to carry out his violent threats.
38On the Internet, they said, context is lost and words can be misinterpreted.
39The government said the standard used by lower courts - that Elonis' words on Facebook could be viewed as threats by a reasonable person reading them - was sufficient, and his intent did not have to be proved.
40"Juries are fully capable of distinguishing between metaphorical expression of strong emotions and statements that have the clear sinister meaning of a threat," its brief said.
41Rap music has thrived under the "reasonable person" standard, it noted, without ensnaring popular rappers such as Eminem.
42The government was backed by groups such as the National Network to End Domestic Violence, which cited a survey of 759 victims' service agencies that found nearly 90% of them had cases of threats delivered via technology.
43Text messages were the most prevalent form, followed by social media and e-mail.
44Women between the ages of 18 and 24 were the most frequent targets.
45The current Supreme Court has been a strong defender of free speech rights, going so far as to permit distasteful protests at military funerals and online videos depicting animal torture.
46But it also has drawn lines, ruling last term against the free speech rights of a previously convicted military protester and opponents of then-President George W. Bush who were moved from their protest site by the Secret Service.
