Execution of Georgia woman postponed; problem with drug

1(AP) - Georgia postponed its first execution of a woman in 70 years late Monday because of concerns about the drug to be used in the lethal injection.
2The pentobarbital was sent to an independent lab to check its potency and the test came back at an acceptable level, but during subsequent checks it appeared cloudy, Georgia Department of Corrections spokeswoman Gwendolyn Hogan said.
3Corrections officials called the pharmacist and decided to postpone the execution "out of an abundance of caution," she said.
4No new date was given.
5Pentobarbital is the only drug used in Georgia executions.
6For other recent executions, the state has gotten the drug from a compounding pharmacy, but officials did not immediately respond late Monday when asked if that was the source in this case.
7Georgia law prohibits the release of any identifying information about the source of execution drugs or any entity involved in an execution.
8Kelly Renee Gissendaner was scheduled to be executed at 7 p.m. at the prison in Jackson for the February 1997 slaying of her husband, Douglas Gissendaner.
9The execution was put on hold while officials waited for the U.S. Supreme Court to either grant or deny a stay requested by her lawyers.
10The court had still not ruled more than five hours later.
11Her lawyers were seeking a delay pending a U.S. Supreme Court ruling in another case out of Oklahoma, and an appellate court had rejected that request.
12Late Monday, the lawyers added additional arguments for the high court: that it should consider a stay because Gissendaner didn't kill her husband, Douglas Gissendaner, herself in February 1997.
13They also argued that she had been thoroughly rehabilitated.
14Previously, courts had found Gissendaner had plotted the stabbing death of her husband by her boyfriend, Gregory Owen, who will be up for parole in eight years after accepting a life sentence and testifying against her.
15Gissendaner would have been only the 16th woman put to death nationwide since the Supreme Court allowed the death penalty to resume in 1976.
16About 1,400 men have been executed since then, according to the Death Penalty Information Center.
17The Georgia Board of Pardons and Paroles, the only entity authorized to commute a death sentence, denied clemency last week and upheld that decision late Monday.
18Gissendaner's lawyers had urged the board to reconsider and "bestow mercy" by commuting her sentence to life without parole.
19The board said it voted to abide by its earlier decision after "careful consideration" of the request.
20Kelly and Douglas Gissendaner had a troubled relationship, repeatedly splitting up and getting back together, divorcing and remarrying.
21At the time of her husband's death, Gissendaner was a 28-year-old mother of three children, 12, 7 and 5 years old.
22And she had an on-again, off-again lover in Owen.
23Rather than divorcing her husband again, Gissendaner repeatedly pushed Owen to kill him, prosecutors said.
24Acting on her instructions, Owen ambushed her husband while she went out with friends, and forced him to drive to a remote area.
25Then he marched him into the woods and stabbed him multiple times, prosecutors said.
26Owen and Gissendaner then met up and set fire to the dead man's car in an attempted cover-up.
27Both initially denied involvement, but Owen eventually confessed and testified against his former girlfriend.
28Her lawyers challenged the constitutionality of her sentence as disproportionate, given that she wasn't there when Owen killed her husband, and yet Owen will eventually be eligible for parole.
29But Georgia's Supreme Court voted 5-2 Monday to deny her motion, citing Owen's testimony that she pushed for murder rather than divorce so that she could get her husband's insurance money.
30In their request Monday for reconsideration, Gissendaner's lawyers said the parole board did not have a chance to hear the overwhelmingly positive testimony of many corrections employees who declined to speak up for fear of retaliation.
31Her clemency petition already included testimonials from dozens of spiritual advisers, inmates and prison staff who described a seriously damaged woman transformed through faith behind bars.
32She has shown remorse and provided hope to struggling inmates while helping guards maintain control, they said.
33"The spiritual transformation and depth of faith that Ms. Gissendaner demonstrates and practices is a deep and sincere expression of a personal relationship with God," Prison chaplain Susan Bishop wrote.
34"It is not a superficial religious experience."
35Two of Gissendaner's three children also asked the board to spare their mother's life, describing their own emotional journey from anger and bitterness to forgiveness.
36"The impact of losing my mother would be devastating. I can't fathom losing another parent," wrote her daughter, Kayla Gissendaner.
37"My mom has touched so many lives. Executing her doesn't bring justice or peace to me or to anyone."
38But it also has been "a long, hard, heartbreaking road" for Douglas Gissendaner's parents and sister, according to a statement from them issued through the Gwinnett County district attorney's office.
39The family made it clear they wanted the execution to go forward.
40Several dozen people gathered outside the prison in support of Gissendaner, including some women who served time with her.
41Kara Tragesser recalled Gissendaner telling her "You can do better!" when she was put on lockdown while serving a 10-year sentence for armed robbery.
42"We're here because Kelly's made a difference in our lives," Tragesser said.
43Michelle Collins, who did time for forgery, remembered Gissendaner persuading her to stop misbehaving and start caring about her future.
44"She looked around at us and said, 'At least y'all are going to get out of here again. Who are you to throw your lives away when I'm never going to get out of here?'" said Collins.
45"She gave me the will to do something good when I got out," said Collins, adding that she now makes good money working for a Fortune 500 company.
46"She told me to make sure I never came back and I never have."
47A loud cheer came up from the crowd gathered outside the prison when they heard the execution had been postponed.
