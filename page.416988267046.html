Nepal's Prime Minister: 'We Were Not Prepared' for 2nd Quake

1Nepal has been overwhelmed by its second powerful earthquake in less than three weeks, its prime minister said Thursday as he visited this normally placid foothills town, now filling up with frightened villagers desperate for government help.
2Thousands of people coming from surrounding areas to seek help crowd the streets of Charikot, the administrative center of the isolated district hit hardest by Tuesday's magnitude-7.3 quake, which killed at least 96 people and injured more than 2,300.
3The magnitude-7.8 earthquake that hit April 25 killed more than 8,150 people, injured tens of thousands more and left hundreds of thousands homeless.
4"After the first quake, we were not prepared for a second one so big," Prime Minister Sushil Koirala told reporters after arriving in Charikot by helicopter.
5He said the coming monsoon rains loomed large, with hundreds of thousands of people left homeless.
6"We need tents. Our people need shelter. With the rainy season, it will be difficult for people to survive in the open," he said.
7Nearly everyone is too afraid to sleep indoors and aftershocks are keeping people on edge in the town 140 kilometers (85 miles) north of Nepal's capital, Kathmandu.
8Food has been handed out occasionally here, but nowhere near enough for the people who keep arriving.
9Many simply waited at the locked gates of the army's small aid distribution center, shaking the fence angrily when their frustration got the better of them.
10"We came here with such hopes and such difficulty, but now we're just waiting and waiting," said Navraj Nama, 25, who came to Charikot with his brother and elderly uncle after the second earthquake.
11He said 90 percent of their home village, Danda Khorka, had been damaged in the April 25 quake, and about 50 buildings collapsed when the second one hit.
12Nama's village is among those in desperate need of shelter, and the young farmer came here hoping to get tents or tarpaulins to carry back with him.
13None was available.
14Sabita Debi, who ran a tea shop with her teenage son, said she has been living in the open with her children since the first April 25 earthquake.
15Her damaged house leaning to one side, Devi said she had received one tarp sheet and some rice in the past two weeks.
16Her face is red and chapped from the long days spent in the sun.
17"The government is promising but nothing has come to us ... We keep running here and there when we hear food is being distributed," said 35-year-old Debi, dressed in a crumpled sari since she left her home two weeks ago.
18Ganga Karki Jungu, a social worker in Charikot, said the people were angry as "they have no roof, no food."
19He said the government was failing to coordinate the distribution of relief material.
20"In many villages, there is not a single house that has not developed cracks or not collapsed altogether. The buildings that remain are shaky."
21Jungu was among a group of social workers and political activists who met Prime Minister Koirala to press for urgent help.
22"The government has to build some permanent structures to provide housing on a large scale before the monsoon rains begin, " he told the prime minister.
23Koirala promised help.
24"We all have to work with a new determination to build a new Nepal. All our efforts will be to reconstruct homes and rebuild the lives."
25Sabita Devi said "if we don't get help soon, I don't know what is going to happen to us."
