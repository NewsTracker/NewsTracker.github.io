Fifteen-minute Ebola test approved

1It should allow patients to be identified, isolated and cared for as quickly as possible in an attempt to bring an end to the outbreak that has killed more than 9,300 people.
2It is less accurate than conventional tests, but takes minutes rather than hours to get a result.
3The test also works without electricity so it can be used in remote regions.
4Current Ebola testing requires a laboratory to analyse the blood for fragments of the virus's genetic material.
5It can take between 12 and 24 hours to get a definitive answer.
6The ReEBOV Antigen Rapid Test, developed by US company Corgenix, searches the blood for a different part of the virus.
7Trials in West Africa suggest it correctly identifies about 92% of people who have Ebola.
8The World Health Organization said: "While less accurate, the antigen test is rapid, easy to perform and does not require electricity.
9"It can therefore be used at lower health care facilities or in mobile units for patients in remote settings."
10It added that, where possible, the results from the rapid test should be confirmed by the more precise conventional testing.
11There were 128 new cases of Ebola in the three affected countries - Liberia, Sierra Leone and Guinea - last week.
12More than 23,250 people have been infected in the outbreak and 9,380 have died.
13Dr Ben Neuman, a lecturer in virology at the University of Reading, said: "The new test could help to quickly confirm outbreaks in remote areas without the need to send samples to a testing clinic and wait for results.
14"The new test isn't about saving the lives of infected people, but it can help in the long run by making it easier and quicker to detect Ebola outbreaks."
