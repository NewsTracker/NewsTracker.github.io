Omar Sharif, of "Doctor Zhivago," "Lawrence of Arabia," dies

1CAIRO (AP) " In "Lawrence of Arabia," Omar Sharif is first seen in the distance, a speck in the swirling desert sand. As he draws closer, he emerges first as a black figure on a galloping camel, slowly transforming into a handsome, dark-eyed figure with a gap-tooth smile. It wasn't unlike the Egyptian-born actor's debut in Hollywood.
2Sharif was Egypt's biggest box-office star when David Lean cast him in 1962's "Lawrence of Arabia," but he was a virtual unknown elsewhere.
3He wasn't even the director's first choice to play Sherif Ali, the tribal leader with whom the enigmatic T.E. Lawrence teams up to help lead the Arab revolt against the Ottoman Empire.
4Lean had hired another actor but dropped him because his eyes weren't the right color.
5The film's producer, Sam Spiegel, went to Cairo to search for a replacement and found Sharif, already a heartthrob in his own country playing brooding romantic heroes.
6After passing a screen test that proved he was fluent in English, he got the job.
7The film brought him a supporting-actor Oscar nomination and international stardom.
8Three years later, Sharif demonstrated his versatility, playing the leading role of a doctor-poet who endures decades of Russian history, including World War I and the Bolshevik Revolution, surviving on his art and his eternal love of Lara, in "Dr. Zhivago."
9Lean's adaptation of the Boris Pasternak novel had a rocky beginning in its first U.S. release.
10Attendance was sparse and reviews were negative.
11After MGM removed it from theaters and Lean re-edited the sprawling tale, it was re-released and became a box-office hit.
12Still, Sharif never thought it was as good as it could have been.
13"It's sentimental. Too much of that music," he once said, referring to Maurice Jarre's luscious Oscar-winning score.
14Although Sharif never achieved that level of success again, he remained a sought-after actor for many years, partly because of his proficiency at playing different nationalities.
15He was Argentine-born revolutionary Ernesto "Che" Guevara in "Che!", Italian Marco Polo in "Marco the Magnificent" and Mongol leader Genghis Khan in "Genghis Khan."
16He was a German officer in "The Night of the Generals," an Austrian prince in "Mayerling" and a Mexican outlaw in "Mackenna's Gold."
17He was also the Jewish gambler Nick Arnstein opposite Barbra Streisand's Fanny Brice in "Funny Girl."
18The 1968 film was banned in his native Egypt because he was cast as a Jew.
19In his middle years Sharif began appearing in such films as "The Pink Panther Strikes Again," ''Oh Heavenly Dog!," ''The Baltimore Bullet" and others he dismissed as "rubbish."
20The drought lasted so long that finally, beginning in the late 1990s, Sharif began declining all film offers.
21"I lost my self-respect and dignity," he told a reporter in 2004.
22In 2003 he accepted a role in the French film "Monsieur Ibrahim," portraying a Muslim shopkeeper in Paris who adopts a Jewish boy.
23The role won him the Cesar, the French equivalent of the Oscar, and he followed with "Hidalgo," a lively western starring Viggo Mortensen.
24In that one he was a desert sheikh who duels 11 assailants with a sword.
25His career was back on track.
26Born Michael Shalhoub in Egypt's Mediterranean coastal city of Alexandria, Sharif was the son of Christian Syrian-Lebanese parents.
27After working three years at his father's lumber company, he fulfilled his longtime ambition to become a movie actor, going on to appear in nearly two dozen Egyptian films under the name Omar el Sharif.
28His fame only increased when he married Egypt's then-reining movie queen and screen beauty, Faten Hamama, in 1955.
29Some of Egypt's most iconic film posters are of Hamama and Sharif.
30Sharif converted to Islam to marry her, and they had a son, Tarek.
31Hamama died in January.
32They divorced in 1974, and Sharif never remarried.
33In a 2003 interview with the AP, Sharif struck a wistful note about how "Lawrence of Arabia" vaulted him to fame.
34"It separated me from my wife, from my family ... We didn't see each other anymore and that was it, the end of our wedding," he said.
35"I might have been happier having stayed an Egyptian film star."
36Yousra, Egypt's biggest actress for much of the past 30 years and a close friend of Sharif, compared Sharif to a "clean-cut" diamond.
37"He was a phenomena; a one of a kind. Everyone had a dream to be like Omar Sharif. No one will be like him," she told the AP on Friday after his death was announced.
38"I don't think we are going to ever have someone like him."
39Sharif's son Tarek revealed in May that his father had been suffering Alzheimer's.
40Zaki, the Egyptian Theatrical Arts Guild president, told the AP Friday that Sharif had stopped eating and drinking in the last three days.
41Sharif was romantically linked with a number of Hollywood co-stars over the years.
42In 2004, he acknowledged that he also had another son, who was born after a one-night stand with an interviewer.
43Away from the movies, Sharif was a world-class bridge player who for many years wrote a newspaper column on bridge.
44He quit the game in later years, however, when he gave up gambling.
45He had been a prodigious gambler, reportedly once winning a million dollars at an Italian casino.
46After losing a substantial amount at a Paris casino in 2003, he insulted a croupier and was ordered to leave.
47When he refused, he was thrown out and head-butted a policeman during an ensuing scuffle.
48He was fined $1,700 and given a one-month suspended sentence.
49Sharif spent much of his later years in Cairo and at the Royal Moncean Hotel in Paris.
50"When you live alone and you're not young, it's good to live in a hotel," he told a reporter in 2005.
51"If you feel lonely, you can go down to the bar. I know all the people who work here and who come here regularly. The room is done for you, and you don't have to worry about anything," he said.
52"If you feel anything, health-wise, you can call the concierge and tell them to bring all the ambulances in Paris."
