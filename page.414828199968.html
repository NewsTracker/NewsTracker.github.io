Bali nine case a 'law enforcement' issue says, Indonesia's Foreign Minister Retno Marsudi

At Division III St. Thomas, a Coach Applies His Psychology Doctorate

1Bali: Indonesia's Foreign Minister Retno Marsudi has acknowledged the international attention on the fate of Bali nine prisoners Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran but said that ultimately the case is "law enforcement" issue.
2Ms Marsudi told reporters in Jakarta that Indonesia had reached a critical point in its drugs emergency.
3Indonesian Foreign Minister Retno Marsudi lays out the government's position.
4"Although we understand the position of the Australian government, it should be underlined that this issue is purely a law enforcement issue," she said.
5"A law enforcement against an extraordinary crime. A law enforcement by a sovereign country, Indonesia," she said.
6The minister's comments follow a delay in the transfer of Australian citizens and convicted drug offenders Chan and Sukumaran to an Indonesian island for their execution.
7Australians Andrew Chan, left, and Myuran Sukumaran in 2006.
8Ms Marsudi stressed the impartiality of the case from an Indonesian perspective.
9"The decision to enforce the death penalty by the Indonesian courts is not directed toward any country nor national of certain countries, but it is directed to an extraordinary crime," she said.
10Ms Marsudi noted the broad ranging bilateral co-operation, but stressed "relations between two countries should be based on mutual respect and mutual benefit".
11Bali authorities on Monday declared they would this week take the Australian drug smugglers to Nusakambangan for their execution, amid confusion around whether the prison island could accommodate so many executions at once.
12Tony Spontana, spokesman for Attorney-General HM Prasetyo, on Tuesday admitted the Central Java island wasn't prepared to handle the execution of so many people.
13"This week, I think we can't do the transfers because the isolation cells and other things are not ready," he told reporters.
14But his comments were contradicted by a senior prison official, who told News Ltd the island was prepared for the executions.
15Lawyers for the Bali nine pair had been hoping for a break, as they ready to appear in court next Tuesday.
16In a rare move, they are challenging in the administrative court President Joko Widodo's blanket ban on clemency for Chan, Sukumaran and all death row drug offenders.
17Barrister Julian McMahon welcomed the delay, however brief.
18"It takes away a sense of urgency," he told reporters.
19Mr Spontana insists there's no alteration or delay in the plan to execute the drug felons.
20He said it would take some time to renovate the cells to accommodate more prisoners.
21"And at the same time, we can facilitate the Australian government and convicts' families' request to give more time for them together before they have to go to Nusakambangan."

6The psychology class, Motivation and Emotion, explores the motives behind human behavior.
7Over the next 65 minutes, Tauer, a tenured professor with a doctorate in social psychology from Wisconsin, spoke to his students about what drives them to perform the important activities in their lives.
8"I'm biased about why motivation is the most fascinating topic," he said.
9Most successful coaches are amateur psychologists in one way or another, but trained ones are rare in the N.C.A.A. Equally rare these days are men's basketball coaches who teach courses, even in Division III.
10Recruiting and other obligations leave little time for academic pursuits.
11But Tauer, an all-conference forward for St. Thomas in the 1990s and a member of its athletic Hall of Fame, enjoys teaching so much that he cannot give it up.
12He taught three courses a semester during 11 seasons as a St. Thomas assistant coach.
13He cut back to one per semester when he succeeded Steve Fritz upon his retirement as head coach four years ago.
14Tauer, 42, wrote his doctoral dissertation on intrinsic motivation: doing something simply for enjoyment, without regard for money or fame or popularity.
15Tauer is so well regarded in his field that he contributed regularly to a Psychology Today blog until he became a head coach.
16He often appears on Twin Cities television news programs as a behavioral expert.
17In recruiting, Tauer seeks selfless players who are intrinsically motivated.
18That can be hard to discern, but the results speak for themselves.
19The 21-2 Tommies, winners of 16 straight before falling to Concordia-Moorhead on Saturday, are ranked No.
203 nationally and have secured at least a tie for the Minnesota Intercollegiate Athletic Conference regular-season championship, giving them a record 10 consecutive titles.
21St. Thomas is 95-17 under Tauer, who took over after Fritz coached the Tommies to the 2011 N.C.A.A. Division III title.
22"In Division III, we have a limit on how much time we can and should spend with players, so they can truly balance academics and athletics," Tauer said.
23"For the seven-plus months that we're not in season, we can't do anything with them. If we get intrinsically motivated players, they work incredibly hard in the off-season. They come back as better players. If we get extrinsically motivated players, we're probably not going to see anything near the skill development."
24Teamwork and discipline were staples of Fritz's teams.
25Tauer has maintained that.
26Four Tommies average at least 10 points a game, and two more are at 9.8 and 9.3, in a Princeton-style offense that relies on spacing and precise movement.
27St. Thomas went more than seven years without someone scoring 30 points in a game until the senior guard Marcus Alipate had 30 at Bethel University on Jan. 19.
28Through Sunday's games, the Tommies led Division III in 3-point shooting at 45.2 percent while ranking second in assist-to-turnover ratio (1.62 to 1).
29Though playing almost exclusively man-to-man defense, the Tommies had committed the third-fewest fouls in Division III (288).
30St. Thomas's 178 victories since 2008 are the most at that level.
31The teamwork notion is even reflected in the team's uniforms, which spell out the school initials, U.S.T., on the back where last names usually go.
32This month, Tauer rejected a suggestion to list individual point totals on the Schoenecker Arena scoreboard, saying it would send the wrong message to his players.
33"John is very, very bright, and motivation is his specialty," said Fritz, the university's longtime athletic director.
34"That's part of the package when you get a guy like John."
35One of Tauer's mentors, John Buri, a St. Thomas psychology professor who taught Tauer as an undergraduate, was not sure Tauer's methods would work.
36Buri himself coached high school basketball for 16 years.
37"Some coaches motivate through intimidation; some coaches motivate through inspiration," Buri said.
38"They're responding. And the record shows it."
39The N.C.A.A. does not keep data on coaches serving in other capacities.
40Tauer and Brendan Stern, a presidential fellow in government and public affairs at Gallaudet University, a Division III institution in Washington, are among the small group of men's basketball coaches nationally who teach.
41Neither of the two Division I men's coaches who have a Ph.D., La Salle's John Giannini and Lehigh's Brett Reed, teach at their institutions, though Giannini did previously at Maine and Rowan.
42In Division II, Tom Kropp, who is a co-coach at Nebraska-Kearney, teaches physical education.
43None of Tauer's current players are taking his class.
44Alipate, the team's leading scorer, audited a few of Tauer's class sessions but decided against signing up.
45"I wouldn't say it's too hard," he said.
46"I'd get called on a little more than I wanted to in his class."
47Then he laughed.
48Playing for a trained psychologist took getting used to, Tauer's players said.
49Forward Conner Nord said that whenever Tauer spoke to him in his first two seasons, he wondered what was behind it.
50"In the beginning you were almost afraid to answer," Nord said.
51"You were trying to think, what is the most neutral answer I can get?
52"But as you go, you just answer honestly. It will help him evaluate you. It will help him teach you better and help him change his teaching style to match what my learning style is, almost like he would do with a student. But it's translating over to basketball. Basically, it's like this is his classroom."
53Alipate, the son of the former Jets linebacker Tuineau Alipate, has his own clothing line, springing from a project in an entrepreneurship class.
54He, too, felt self-conscious.
55"Some of the questions, and even some of the methods, were a lot different than I was used to in high school," Alipate said.
56"He hit different parts of your brain. You could tell he knew something different than the other coaches - getting us to be motivated and doing things in the way that pleases us and also pleases the team, I think he does a phenomenal job doing that."
57For Tauer, a divorced father of two boys, juggling time is a constant struggle.
58Besides coaching and teaching, he runs a summer basketball camp and is completing his first book, on youth sports, parenting and motivation.
59Tauer delegates some responsibility to his assistants, all part time, but other things he must do himself.
60Two weeks ago, Tauer graded papers on the team bus heading for a road game.
61"I feel fortunate that my work epitomizes the intrinsic motivation that I study," he said.
62"I love the various jobs I have, ranging from coaching to teaching to research to running camps to raising my sons. As a result, it doesn't feel busy. It feels like I am doing exactly what I am called to do."
