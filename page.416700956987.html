A week after disaster, Nepal rules out finding more quake survivors

1KATHMANDU - One week on from an earthquake that killed more than 7,000 people, officials in Nepal's capital on Saturday ruled out finding more survivors buried in the ruins of Kathmandu despite relatives refusing to give up hope.
2Two days after any signs of life had been detected among the mountains of rubble that litter the capital, the focus was shifting to reaching survivors in far-flung areas who have yet to receive relief supplies.
3UNICEF, the U.N. children's fund, warned of a race against time to avert an outbreak of disease among the 1.7 million youngsters estimated to be living in the worst-hit areas, with monsoon rains just a few weeks away.
4The magnitude-7.8 quake wreaked a trail of death and destruction when it erupted around midday on April 25, reducing much of Kathmandu to rubble and even triggering a deadly avalanche on Mount Everest.
5"It has already been one week since the disaster," Home Ministry spokesman Laxmi Prasad Dhakal said.
6"We are trying our best in rescue and relief work but now I don't think that there is any possibility of survivors under the rubble."
7National police officer Babu Kanji Giri said Sunday that the death toll had reached 7,040 as more bodies were found among the debris.
8The Home Ministry said 14,123 people were injured in the April 25 quake.
9Of them, 6,512 are being treated in hospitals.
10While multiple teams of rescuers from more than 20 countries have been using sniffer dogs and heat-seeking equipment to find survivors in the rubble, no one has been pulled out alive since Thursday evening.
11Nevertheless, relatives of those missing have refused to abandon hope.
12"I believe he must still be trapped and will be rescued alive," said Suntali Tamang, whose husband, Langte, 41, was believed to be in the neighborhood of Gongabu, where the last survivors were found.
13"I reached here three days ago after he went missing," she said after traveling to Kathmandu from the family's home in the northeastern Dolakha district.
14"He was the family's breadwinner and I am praying for him to be brought back safely."
15The exact scale of the disaster has still to emerge, with the mountainous terrain in the vast Himalayan nation complicating relief efforts.
16With relief workers still to reach many areas, it is likely to be some time before authorities come up with a comprehensive list of people missing but police said the task of compiling names has already started.
17The list so far only contained 204 names, national spokesman Kamal Singh Bam said, as relatives have only just started approaching authorities.
18"We have only just begun to draw up the list, the number will obviously go up," added the spokesman.
19The numbers of foreigners who died is also unclear, with around 1,000 EU citizens still unaccounted for in Nepal, according to diplomats.
20Tens of thousands of survivors have been living out in the open in Kathmandu in the week since the quake, having either lost their homes or fearful that aftershocks could bring teetering buildings to the ground.
21"We are not living in this tent out of choice. We are here because we have nowhere to go," said Dhiraj Thakur, who has been camped out for the last week in Tundikhel Maidan, an open area in the center of the city.
22"I have seven family members with me, which includes my wife, my sister, nephew and my parents. The rented room where we were living in Kathmandu is now in ruins.
23"Most of our stuff is lost and even the person for whom I used to work as a driver is dead, so I don't know where I will get the money for renting another room," said the 24-year-old.
24Shambhu Thapa, who worked as a cook in a hotel before it was wrecked in the quake, had decided to move back to his home village with his wife and three children now that he has no means of supporting them in Kathmandu.
25"Even our village home is damaged, but at least it is our own land," he said after another night out in the open.
26Rameshwor Dangal, of Nepal's National Disaster Management Division, said many people are waiting to receive aid supplies or else be airlifted to safety.
27"In many areas people are not getting relief and it is natural that they are unhappy about it," he said.
28On Saturday U.N. aid chief Valerie Amos said she is extremely concerned that Nepal's customs authorities are slowing the delivery of aid.
29Amos said she reminded Prime Minister Sushil Koirala that Nepal signed an agreement with the United Nations in 2007 that provides for simpler and faster customs clearance for relief aid in the event of a disaster.
30"He has undertaken to ensure that happens, so I hope that from now we will see an improvement in those administrative issues," she said.
31But the government, complaining it has received such unneeded supplies as tuna and mayonnaise, insisted its customs agents had to check all emergency shipments.
32Nepal lifted import taxes on tarpaulins and tents on Friday but Home Ministry spokesman Dhakal said all goods coming in from overseas have to be inspected.
33In Sri Lanka, visiting U.S. Secretary of State John Kerry said the United States has provided $22.5 million in aid relief to Nepal.
