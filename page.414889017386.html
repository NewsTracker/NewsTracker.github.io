Drugs killed my brother but I dont want the Bali Nine pair to be executed

Rebel fire continues, says Ukraine

1'I know first-hand the pain and trauma that drug trafficking causes families,' Kaili Behan says in a statement posted to Facebook.
2'I have felt the powerlessness of trying to help someone get past grief and watching them slowly succumb.
3'Does that mean I don't stand for mercy towards Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran?
4When she was just 19, Ms Behan lost her older brother Adam to a lethal cocktail of heroin and Oxycodone.
5Adam was autistic.
6It was his 22nd birthday party and someone gave him a heroin 'hot shot' which reacted with his medication.
7His heart failed.
8'My brother was preyed upon by thoughtless d***heads who thought it would be funny to get the "retard" wasted,' Behan writes.
9In her open letter, Behan says she is telling her brother's story because she is 'tired of waking up with sadness reading the hatred and the blood-lust' towards Chan and Sukumaran.
10She was tired of 'indignant' calls for the death penalty supposedly made on behalf of families who have lost loved ones to drugs, she told Daily Mail Australia.
11'You don't stand for me,' she writes.
12'To all of those baying for blood today.
13Although those responsible for her brother's death were never held to account, Behan says she found peace in compassion.
14'Those of us who have been through the loss of a son, brother, daughter, sister or other loved one through drugs seem to be the clearest and articulate voices for mercy towards Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran,' she writes.
15The pair have 'become the benchmark for what rehabilitation should like worldwide,' she says.
16On Wednesday evening Ms Behan and her daughter Lily joined 200 people at the Perth Mercy vigil for the Bali Nine pair on death row.
17Her 'most compassionate' brother would have chosen to be there too, she says.
18'I doubt I would have been so blessed had I stayed in a place of anger' says Kaili Behan pictured here with her children (from left) Lily-Bliss, Harley and Eden.
19'Despite what happened to my brother and the terrible aftermath of it all - because I was able to be merciful and show forgiveness, even though the people who killed my brother were never brought to justice - I have since then lived an awesome life - and raised three compassionate and merciful (also feisty) children.
20To all of those baying for blood today.
21To all of those saying that they would cheerfully partake in aiming a loaded gun at two men tethered to a post on a deserted beach, and shoot at them until their blood runs into the sand, and their hearts explode in their chests; making their final living minutes (yes death is not usually instant in a firing squad situation) excruciating - YOU DON'T SPEAK FOR ME.
22When you inarticulately spout your trash talk on social media like the gutless and grammatically challenged keyboard warriors that you are - YOU DON'T SPEAK FOR ME.
23When you repeatedly try and bludgeon the internet by peddling barbaric and offensive verbal diarrhoea, purporting to be a voice for those who have lost loved ones due to drugs - YOU DON'T SPEAK FOR ME.
24As it so happens, today, 17 February marks the 23rd anniversary of my beautiful and vulnerable autistic brother Adam's death - who died from a heroin overdose.
25In case you hounds of death are still reading, and not hovering viciously over your keyboard to give me your unfounded, uninformed opinion that he made his choice and somehow was 'to blame' for his death - ironic isn't how you people both blame the trafficker on behalf of 'the families', but still find space in your hardened hearts to blame the deceased person - I invite you to consider your position in light of my story.
26In case you are still reading, and your eyes have not clouded over with self-righteous indignation, this is the story of what happened to my big beautiful brother Adam.
27This is also the story of how I learned that compassion is the only way forward.
28This is the story of why I STAND FOR MERCY.
29My darling brother Adam was at a party ostensibly to celebrate turning 22.
30In reality the 'party' was just another excuse for those who should have been caring for him to indulge their own vices and he was left alone.
31My darling brother was not a drug user, in fact he was so anti-drugs that he actually called Crime Stoppers when he found a joint in my backpack when I was 13!
32My brother was preyed upon by thoughtless d***heads who thought it would be funny to get the 'retard' wasted.
33I am sure they never meant to kill him, however among other things, once he was vulnerable and could not defend himself after having been fed a cocktail of prescription medications and alcohol, the perpetrators decided that as he was still lucid to administer him a shot of heroin mixed with Oxycodone - a 'hot shot'.
34The drugs reacted with his existing medication and began to slow his heart.
35My 6'4" big beautiful brother regained consciousness long enough to try and make his way home - a short walk up the beach in the dark of night. Sadly, he collapsed and the incoming tide washed over him sealing what was already inevitable. Adam was found at dawn by a woman walking her dog. My brother died on his 22nd birthday, 23 years ago.
36Due to many factors, which as an 18 year old girl, I found hard to fathom - and still do as a 41 year old woman, the investigation was a myriad of cover-ups, corruption and shear ineptitude, and the four perpetrators were never held accountable for their crime, and given the long jail sentences they deserved.
37We will never know if two of them would have ever atoned as they subsequently met their own untimely premature demise due to their toxic lifestyles.
38Of the two who remain alive, I understand that they have been in and out of jail in Queensland - however never for what they did to my brother.
39It is not the premise of my writing today to make comment on the flawed justice system that has not provided the mechanism for these two people to atone and become better citizens.
40I have spoken previously on the impact that my brother's unnecessary death had on our family.
41It was heartbreaking for me to deliver the news to my Dad.
42It was exhausting and draining to watch my Mum slowly drink and drug herself to death from the guilt of leaving Adam alone that night.
43Even though I was living away from Adam's home and could not have 'saved him' if I wanted to, for many years I too struggled with enormous guilt that has at times manifested itself in substance abuse and certainly self-sabotage.
44I have been very blessed to have attracted caring beautiful people into my life who have steered me back to the path of peace and success.
45I am blessed to have raised three compassionate, driven and healthy children who are making their way in the world as fine young humans.
46Over the years, in my struggle to understand and come to terms with what happened to my beautiful brother, I have interacted intimately with many families who have lost a loved one through the use of drugs.
47We have shared our stories, consoled each other in grief and been towers of strength at times when the sadness threatens to overwhelm - birthdays, anniversaries sometimes it can be just a song that evokes emotions that cause your chest to tighten and the tears to fall.
48I have found that all of these families are profoundly compassionate, and although we all at some deeper level hold some anger either towards those who caused (in my brother's case) our loved one to die, or even at our loved one for making stupid choices.
49It is certainly true that many of us are angry at a society which allows drug trafficking to flourish and drug traffickers at the highest level to grow fat and wealthy on the back of so much misery.
50Of course we are angry at the traffickers down the line, like Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran.
51We have every right to be.
52Over the years, when the subject of the Bali 9 came up, it certainly brought some of that anger to the surface.
53However, like grief - anger can overwhelm you and certainly cloud your decision making abilities.
54I believe that Tanya Plibersek said recently when speaking in Parliament about the issue of the looming execution of Andrew and Myuran, and relating it to her own brother's violent death in Port Moresby in 1997.
55'I know that if I had been the one making the decision about the punishment of the person who did that crime, I couldn't have thought of a punishment bad enough,' she said.
56As an evolved society we must hold fast to this premise.
57We must continue to strive towards a rehabilitative and restorative criminal justice system that provides a mechanism for those willing to atone for their crimes and become better citizens.
58In the case of Andrew and Myuran, whether or not you believe that the death penalty is warranted in certain cases (I don't but that is a discussion for another day), surely you cannot refute the fact that these men - despite being on death row for nearly a decade, in a prison system which provides for minimal rehabilitative opportunities, these young men have made it their mission to not only rehabilitate themselves, but through their work in Kerobokan Jail, which I have seen - they have become the benchmark for what rehabilitation should like worldwide.
59To those of you who are vociferous (look it up if you need to) in your baying for blood, the blood of Andrew and Myuran, I wonder what you think is to be gained.
60However, the administrators of Kerobokan jail themselves have been outspoken in their praise of Andrew and Myuran as not only model prisoners, but for their work in reducing the drug taking within the prison itself.
61Neither Andrew or Myuran or their families, or their growing army of supporters are asking for anything more than that they are allowed to serve their lives in prison in way that allows them to continue these good works.
62The punishment is that they are to spend their lives in prison, away from their families, in a developing country where creature comforts such as space, working toilets and even basic safety are not the norm.
63For me, that seems enough.
64I don't need to have their blood on my hands to feel less angry about what was done to my beautiful brother Adam - that is my journey, and thankfully through my writing, my interactions with extraordinary people and my friends and family, that anger is something I have largely dealt with.
65So, to all you people who think you speak 'on behalf of the families' - YOU DON'T SPEAK FOR ME.
66I invite you to pause and consider what I have written, because although at times, I have been harsher and less polite than I usually am, it is because I am tired.
67I am tired of going to sleep at night, only to wake up sad because of something awful I have read about the need to kill these men.
68Those of us who been through the loss of a son, brother, daughter, sister or other loved one through drugs seem to be the clearest and articulate voices for mercy towards Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran.
69For me, I could no more wish the Chan or the Sukumaran families to suffer the pain of losing their loved one than I could load the gun and stand in line to shoot these men through their hearts.
70If after reading this, you still (and I accept that many of you will) don't see a need to be merciful towards Andrew and Myu, please at least refrain from saying you speak for the 'families' of drug victims.
71You DO NOT speak for us.
72You do not speak for me.
73We stand for mercy.
74I stand for mercy.
