Greece under fire from creditors as bailout talks drag

1A man watches the city of Athens at Filopalous hill, on Friday, April 24, 2015.
2Time is running out for Greece to reach a deal with bailout creditors and prevent the country going bankrupt, the eurozone's top official said Friday as he headed into discussions with his colleagues from the 19-country single currency zone.
3RIGA, Latvia (AP) - European creditors turned up the heat on Greece Friday to deliver an economic reform program that it needs to avoid a possible default and even an exit from the euro.
4At a meeting in the Latvian capital of Riga, Greece's finance minister faced a series of rebukes from his peers in the 19-country eurozone for failing to come up with a comprehensive list of economic reforms after weeks of slow progress.
5For some normally restrained finance ministers, now was the time to express discontent at what they perceive to be the delaying tactics of the new left-wing Greek government.
6"It was a very critical discussion," said Jeroen Dijsselbloem, the eurozone's top official, after the meeting.
7Others spoke of being "tired" and "annoyed" with the way the talks are going.
8The points were made "very vigorously," according to Austria's finance minister, Johann Schelling.
9Just two months ago, Greece secured an agreement from the eurozone to get the remaining money in its bailout fund - 7.2 billion euros ($7.7 billion) - but only if it came up with a mutually agreed set of reforms.
10An end-of-April deadline was considered achievable and markets breathed a sigh of relief that the threat of bankruptcy had been averted.
11But with days to go until that deadline, Athens has yet to present a full list.
12European officials are now talking of a new deadline of the end of June, when the European part of Greece's bailout program officially ends.
13How Greece pays wages and salaries and meets its debt commitments, notably to the IMF, in that period remains sketchy.
14The decision this week by the Greek government to scrape together spare cash from municipalities and state enterprises like hospitals and the national gallery is likely to buy some time.
15Dijsselbloem conceded there had been a step-change in Athens' appetite for discussion over the past few days but that "significantly more progress" was required.
16He spoke of "wide differences" without specifying where the problems lay.
17He also ruled out that the creditors might consider a half-way deal that could give Greece part of the pending rescue loans.
18Though acknowledging the "anxiety" around the negotiating table, Greece's Varoufakis sought to portray the discussions in a more positive light.
19"We look at the last few weeks and what we see is convergence," he said, noting progress on issues such as privatization, reforming the tax system, the judiciary, the bureaucracy and product markets.
20A deal will happen, he insisted, and "will happen quickly as it's the only option we have."
21Varoufakis said the main sticking points related to pensions and the level of the budget surplus Athens has to post after debt and interest payments are stripped out - a higher level would effectively mean the government has less money to spend on its priorities.
22All sides agreed they are running out of time.
23The next possible date for a deal could be May 11, when eurozone finance ministers will meet next and just one day before Greece owes a big payment to the IMF.
24Greece has relied on 240 billion euros in bailout loans since May 2010 after it was effectively locked out of international bond markets amid concerns it was insolvent.
25In return for the cash, successive governments have had to make savage spending cuts and economic reforms.
26But while the measures have focused on improving public finances, they have also hurt the economy and caused unemployment to skyrocket.
27The current government was elected in January on a promise to end such so-called austerity.
28Its focus is on fighting corruption, reforming the public sector, and improving the porous tax system.
