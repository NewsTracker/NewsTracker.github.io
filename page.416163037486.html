Kenya orders UN to move massive Somali refugee camp

1Kenya has in the past accused fighters of hiding out in Dadaab camp which it now wants the UN refugee agency UNHCR to move across the border to inside Somalia.
2"We have asked the UNHCR to relocate the refugees in three months, failure to which we shall relocate them ourselves," Deputy President William Ruto said in a statement on Saturday.
3"The way America changed after 9/11 is the way Kenya will change after Garissa," he said, referring to the university that was attacked on April 2.
4Al Jazeera's Mohamed Adow, reporting from Garissa in Kenya, said that Ruto warned that if the UN fails to remove the camp, the government would do so itself.
5He also quoted Ruto as saying that the move was necessary to secure its border with Somalia, which Kenya has failed to do so because of the camp's impediments.
6The complex of camps hosts more than 600,000 Somali refugees, according to Ruto, in a remote, dry corner in northeast Kenya, about an hour's drive from Garissa town.
7The camp was first established in 1991 when civil war broke out in neighbouring Somalia, and over subsequent years has received waves of refugees fleeing conflict and drought.
8The United Nations puts the number of registered refugees in the chronically overcrowded settlements of permanent structures, mud shanties and tents, at around 335,000.
9The camp houses schools, clinics and community centres.
10Macharia Munene, professor of international relations at USIU-Africa, said the logistics of moving hundreds of thousands of refugees across the border would be "a tall order".
11But he said there were now safe areas within Somalia from where al Shabaab armed group had been chased out by African Union forces in recent years.
12"Kenya is in an emergency situation... Each country has an obligation to look after its people first," he told Reuters.
13Funerals of the students killed in the campus attack were taking place across the country.
14Pictures of their grieving families dominated the media, reminding Kenyans of the attack.
15Ruto said Kenya had started building a 700-km (440-mile) wall along the entire length of the border with Somalia to keep out members of al-Shabab.
16"We must secure this country at whatever cost, even if we lose business with Somalia, so be it," he said.
17On Tuesday, Kenya closed 13 informal money remittance firms, hawalas, to cut off funding to suspected fighters.
18Ruto said any business that collaborated with al-Shabab would be shut down.
19Al-Shabab has killed more than 400 people on Kenyan soil in the last two years, including 67 during a siege at Nairobi's Westgate mall in 2013, damaging tourism and inward investment.
20On Monday, the Kenyan air force launched air strikes against al Shabab targets in Somalia, a country where it has been militarily engaged against the armed group for several years.
