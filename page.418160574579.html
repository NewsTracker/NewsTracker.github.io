Asia stocks bounce back but Greece uncertainty still looms

1Trader Greg Mulligan works on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange, Monday, June 29, 2015.
2Stocks are falling in early trading in the U.S., but not as much as in Europe as Greece's debt woes deepen.
3Trader Gordon Charlop works on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange, Monday, June 29, 2015.
4Stocks closed with their biggest losses of the year as investors worry about fallout from Greece's worsening debt crisis.
5The Dow Jones industrial average dropped 350 points, or 2 percent, to 17,596.
6Asian stock markets bounced back Tuesday, recouping some of the previous day's sharp losses, but investors remained worried the crisis in Greece could spread to other financially weak countries.
7Japan's benchmark Nikkei 225 gained 0.4 percent to 20,189.02 after dropping to its lowest point for the year Monday.
8South Korea's Kospi was up 0.2 percent to 2,065.34.
9Hong Kong's Hang Seng rose 1.2 percent to 26,280.22.
10China's Shanghai Composite was down 0.6 percent at 4,052.47 while Australia's S&P/ASX 200 was little changed at 5,423.70.
11In currency trading, the euro dropped to $1.1189 from $1.1216 in the previous global trading session.
12The dollar fell to 122.40 yen from 122.62 yen.
13"Most traders are well prepped over the possible fallout of Greece. It's guessing which card will fall next that's the difficult bit," said Nicholas Teo, analyst at CMC Markets in Singapore.
14"The Brazilian real was knocked back hard these past days as have the Turkish lira and most other emerging Asian currencies. These associations are made to Greece as they fall under the labeling of global emerging markets."
15A series of events over the weekend has left Greece perilously close to defaulting on its debts.
16Greek leaders pulled out of talks with creditors that include European nations and the International Monetary Fund and called a referendum that's likely to determine if Greece stays in the euro common currency.
17In the vote set for Sunday, the government is urging Greeks to vote against its creditors' proposals, arguing that they are humiliating and that they would prolong the country's financial woes.
18Investors fled from stocks worldwide on Monday and retreated to the safety of government bonds.
19U.S. stocks had their worst day of the year.
20The Standard & Poor's 500 index dropped 43.85 points, or 2.1 percent, to 2,057.64.
21The Dow Jones industrial average lost 350.33 points, or 2 percent, to 17,596.35, and the Nasdaq composite fell 122.04 points, or 2.4 percent, to 4,958.47.
22The losses wiped out all the gains for the Dow and S&P 500 indexes this year.
23In Europe, Germany's DAX lost 3.6 percent while France's CAC-40 lost 3.7 percent.
24Britain's FTSE 100 fell 2 percent.
25Greece's stock market was closed.
26Investors bought German and British government bonds, which are seen as safe havens, and sold bonds issued by Greece's government, sending those yields sharply higher.
27Investors were concerned that the crisis in Greece could spread to other euro countries that are struggling with high debt and austerity policies or to developing nations in Asia and South America.
28Over the weekend, the European Central Bank refused to extend its emergency support for Greece's banking system.
29That prompted the Greek government to close banks and announce limits on withdrawals.
30Pictures of long lines at bank machines in Athens appeared on television screens around the world.
31"Whenever you see any kind of bank line, there is in the back of investors' mind the thought: 'What if it spreads? What if people panic?'" said Karyn Cavanaugh, senior market strategist at Voya Investment Management.
32"What's going on in Europe, of course it's going to roil markets in the short term," But for U.S. investors, she said, "the long-term impact is not that big of a deal."
33If Greece defaults and switches to a new currency, it's sure to shake global financial markets.
34But the world is unlikely to see anything like the full-blown crisis of 2008.
35A few years ago, banks across Europe were loaded down with loans to the Greek government, corporations and banks.
36Things have changed since then.
37The European Central Bank is also ready to swing into action to prevent a panic.
38The ECB has already committed to buying 60 billion euros a month in corporate and government bonds to push down interest rates and help the European economy.
39It could buy even more, and flood financial markets with cash, to calm jittery European investors.
40In energy markets, benchmark U.S. crude was down 14 cents at $58.19 a barrel in electronic trading on the New York Mercantile Exchange.
41The contract dropped $1.30 to close at $58.33 in Nymex floor trading on Monday.
42Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils, was down 10 cents at $62.50 a barrel in London.
