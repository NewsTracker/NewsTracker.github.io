5LOS ANGELES (AP) - Apple's new music service is a valiant effort to catch up in the emerging business of offering unlimited music on demand for a monthly price.
6But Apple Music feels like an attempt to take on every competitive app from Pandora to Spotify to YouTube and advance the idea of online radio at the same time.
7As a result, Apple Music feels crowded with features and buttons - unusual for a company known for promoting simplicity and ease of use.
8And as much as Apple Music tries to suit my personal tastes, I find many of its recommendations off-base.
9Apple Music is several services in one.
10- The core is an on-demand music streaming service like Spotify with a similar price tag - $10 a month.
11You can pick any of millions of songs to play over Wi-Fi or your cellular connection.
12You don't own the songs, though.
13Although you can download them for offline playback, access disappears once you stop paying (of course, after the generous 90-day trial period ends).
14It's like renting rather than owning music.
15- Apple Music is also a giant song recommendation machine.
16A "For You" section offers playlists created by staff acquired when Apple bought Beats last year for $3 billion.
17It's meant to reflect your iTunes purchases and preferences you make using animated bouncing bubbles, though I found the choices limiting.
18A "New" tab features new music, videos and yet more playlists.
19- Then there's the "Radio" tab, which debuts not only Apple's new 24/7 online radio station, Beats1, but contains several stations specializing in various genres.
20These are like playlists, but you can't see all the songs that are up next.
21- A "Connect" channel offers a running stream of photos, videos and other content provided by artists for fans.
22The area feels empty, probably because not many musicians have participated yet.
23- One is Beats1.
24The concept of a radio station taken online and delivered to mobile devices in 100 countries around the world is refreshingly simple.
25Hosted by Zane Lowe in Los Angeles, Julie Adenuga in London and Ebro Darden in New York, Apple is presenting three tastemakers whom I'm just getting to know.
26They play music and conduct interviews.
27Live listening is fun, and I can switch when I want more control over listening.
28- Siri, Apple's ever-evolving digital assistant, can now take voice commands and launch music immediately, even responding to relatively complex commands.
29Ask it to "Play the top songs from 1973," and you'll get that year's top 25, including "Tie a Yellow Ribbon Round the Ole Oak Tree."
30In fact, if Apple Music had only these two features, I'd feel satisfied.
31Recommendations sound great, but they get overwhelming.
32Presented with dozens of options, the app turns back into a record store.
33I just want the app to say, "You like this? Then listen to this."
34Pandora does this well.
35There's value to a playlist like "Bruce Springsteen: Deep Cuts," compiled of worthwhile Boss songs that somehow never get played on the radio.
36But the human compilers are presented as faceless entities like "Apple Music Rock."
37This feels impersonal and, to me, gets in the way of connecting with the artists.
38Apple Music launched June 30 on iPhones and iPads as part of the free iOS 8.4 update.
39It ships with the new iPod Touch released Wednesday, though the previous model can get the update.
40A new Music app replaces Apple's online radio service, iTunes Radio.
41Apple Music is on Mac and Windows computers through the iTunes app.
42An Android version and integration with Sonos wireless speakers are coming this year.
43The service benefits from being featured prominently on Apple's mobile devices, but I'm not sure that will be enough for people to switch from another service.
44If you've already spent time creating playlists on Spotify, for instance, you're not going to want to start over on Apple Music.
45Apple has a better chance at luring people new to music streaming, especially those who regularly buy songs on iTunes.
46Apple Music is now the default organizer of all tunes people have saved to iPhones.
47When people venture away from their personal purchases in "My Music" and explore playlists and other offerings, they might save some songs to personal libraries, where subscription-only music is commingled with purchased downloads.
48Keeping up with the subscription could be worth it to keep what's in one's personal library intact.
49Apple Music is mainly a deal for people who spend more than $120 on music annually, which is about double the average.
50However, there are also plenty of things Apple Music offers for free such as Beats1, genre-based radio stations and the ability to follow artists on Connect.
51It's no doubt difficult to encapsulate the world of music in a product that aims to mold itself to millions of different tastes.
52But we've come to rely on Apple to address these very vexing problems.
53Usually it does so in a way that feels less cluttered.
