Greek Leader Defends Bailout Plan in Talks With Creditors

1Greece's prime minister Alexis Tsipras said early Thursday he found a constructive approach during a showdown with creditors in Brussels and all sides insisted talks to unlock crucial bailout loans and save the country from financial disaster would continue within days.
2Even though Greece is running out of cash and faces more debt repayments as soon as Friday, Tsipras still found a lifeline to keep the momentum for more negotiations going.
3"I believe that, in any case, agreement is in sight but we need to conclude the discussions with a realistic point of view," Tsipras said.
4French President Francois Hollande said ahead of the Brussels talks that end around midnight Wednesday that the negotiations were at least heading in the right direction: "We are some days, not to say some hours away from a possible agreement."
5Greece has been negotiating for four months with its creditors over what budget reforms it should make to get the 7.2 billion euros ($8.1 billion) in loans that are left over in its bailout fund.
6Wednesday's meetings are part of a string of high-level diplomatic efforts to bring the negotiations to a successful end.
7Asked whether he would be able to make the next repayment to the International Monetary Fund next Friday, Tsipras said: "Don't worry about it."
8After his dinner with EU Commission chief Jean-Claude Juncker, Tsipras stressed the positive.
9The Commission showed a constructive intention to reach common ground," he said. "
10At the same time, Tsipras maintained he would continue to defend the poor and needy in the face of calls for more austerity reforms to unlock bailout funds.
11Tsipras said "proposals such as cutting supplementary payments to people with very low pensions, or increasing VAT on power bills by ten percentage points are proposals that, naturally, lack any basis for us to discuss them."
12Tsipras also spoke by teleconference with Hollande and German Chancellor Angela Merkel ahead of his meeting with Juncker, a Greek government official said.
13The three agreed on the need for Greece to have lower primary surpluses - the budget balance without taking into account debt servicing.
14"We are very close in an agreement in the primary surpluses, that means that all the sides agreed to go further without the tough austerity measures of the past," Tsipras said.
15Lower primary surpluses than those initially demanded under Greece's five-year bailout have been one of Athens' main requests, although it appears to have been the easiest to overcome in the negotiations.
16Sticking points appear to have been labor and pension reforms, as well as some changes to consumer tax.
17The eurozone's top financial official, Jeroen Dijsselbloem, was also in Brussels and met with Juncker and Tsipras.
18He was less upbeat than others going into the meeting, saying: "We still have a lot of work to do."
19But he also came out saying the talks would continue within days.
20Greek markets were rising on hopes of a deal, with the main stock index closing up 4.1 percent.
21Time is pressing.
22Greece must repay 1.6 billion euros to the IMF this month alone.
23The first installment of just over 300 million euros is due Friday, with other installments on June 12, 16 and 19.
24Although Athens insists it intends to repay its debts, it is unclear how much longer it will be able to do so without outside help.
