Germanwings Investigation: How Will They Figure It Out?

1Accident investigators tough task; how they'll determine why Germanwings Flight 9525 crashed.
2NEW YORK (AP) - Cockpit voice recordings and, hopefully, flight data will provide the main clues to investigators trying to understand what caused Germanwings Flight 9525 to crash.
3But that is just the beginning.
4Drawing on decades of experience with crashes around the globe, French officials will analyze thousands of pieces of wreckage, maintenance log books and other clues to determine what led the Airbus A320 to crash into a mountain, killing all 150 passengers and crew.
5"Mapping the accident site is a very detailed process that can take days and sometimes weeks," warns Jim Hall, former chairman of the National Transportation Safety Board.
6Investigators lead by the France's BEA, an agency that investigates air accidents, will photograph, chart and document each piece of wreckage as well as the human remains.
7While there is no indication of terrorist activity, the crash site is treated like a crime scene until proven otherwise.
8Other members of the team will start to listen to the cockpit voice recorder for any clues.
9The recorder takes audio feeds from four microphones within the cockpit, documenting all the conversations between the pilots, air traffic controllers as well as any noises in the cockpit.
10In this case, the entire flight, starting with taxi and takeoff, should be recorded.
11Investigators will look for clues in the pilots' voice tones, words and listen for sounds of explosion or alarms in the background.
12Psychologists will also study the recording to listen for voice inflexions to see if the pilots were drunk or impaired, tired or angry with each other.
13"The cockpit voice recorder will be very interesting, perhaps by its silence," says Steve Hull, an accident-investigation consultant with RTI Forensics.
14If that is the case, it could indicate that the pilots were unconscious.
15The flight data recorder captures 25 hours' worth of information on the position and condition of almost every major part in a plane.
16Investigators will initially focus on the engines, flaps, rudders and cockpit instruments.
17Even if the two data recorders - often called black boxes, despite being bright orange - don't offer clues, experts are confident that authorities will still piece together the chain of events.
18"The wreckage itself will provide a lot of information," Hall says.
19If there is usable data on the recorders, that will initially lead the direction of the investigations, says Alan E. Diehl, a former air safety investigator with the NTSB and a former scientist for human performance at the Federal Aviation Administration.
20"Both will point you in directions of what is critical," Diehl says.
21"Based on what you learn from the recorders, you might focus on key pieces of wreckage."
22The four possible causes of any crash are human error, mechanical problems, weather, criminal activity or a combination of two or more.
23Diehl says investigators will work backward, starting by eliminating what didn't happen.
24"You're usually dealing with a jigsaw puzzle with many of the pieces missing," he says.
25"You start eliminating things that didn't happen."
26As the debris field is mapped and photographed, Diehl says, a key question there will be: What's missing?
27If engine parts or bits of the tail, for instance, are missing from the main crash site then investigators can presume they broke off earlier in the flight and might have been part of the problem.
