Car bombing at Italian Consulate in Egypt's capital kills 1

1Workers clear rubble at the site of an explosion near the Italian Consulate in downtown, Cairo, Egypt, Saturday, July 11, 2015.
2Italy's foreign minister vowed that his country would not be intimidated after a deadly explosion Saturday morning killed one person and heavily damaged the Italian Consulate in the Egyptian capital.
3CAIRO (AP) - A car bomb exploded early Saturday outside the Italian Consulate in Cairo, destroying several floors of the historic building in a powerful blast that killed one Egyptian, the latest casualty in an ongoing Islamic militant campaign targeting the country's security forces.
4No group immediately claimed responsibility for the blast, though Egypt faces threats from insurgent factions including an Islamic State affiliate in its restive Sinai Peninsula that the military says killed at least 17 soldiers in a recent assault there.
5Security service officials who spoke to The Associated Press said that attack killed dozens more.
6The Italian Consulate bombing struck at around 6:30 a.m. (0430 GMT, 12:30 a.m. EDT), exploding in a side street in downtown Cairo near the building's back entrance and a busy highway overpass.
7Italian authorities said the consulate was closed at the time and none of its workers were wounded.
8The blast killed a passer-by and wounded eight, only one of whom is still in the hospital, the Interior Ministry said in a statement.
9Egyptian Heath Ministry official Hossam Abdel-Ghaffar told the AP that the man killed had some of his limbs blown off.
10Italian Premier Matteo Renzi spoke with Egyptian President Abdel Fattah el-Sissi after the attack, saying in a statement: "We will not leave Egypt alone: Italy and Egypt are and will always be together in the fight against terrorism."
11Italian Foreign Minister Paolo Gentiloni said that Italy would increase security at Italian sites in Cairo and greater Egypt.
12"This is not a challenge that the West will win by itself," he said.
13"It is a challenge that we will win together with the large majority of the Islamic community and of the Arab governments."
14An Egyptian security official said investigators were reviewing closed-circuit video recordings from the area, noting that one vehicle that disintegrated in the explosion had license plates from the canal city of Suez.
15He spoke on condition of anonymity as he was not authorized to brief journalists.
16The blast heavily damaged the distinctive early 20th century building that once housed a school and became the Italian Consulate after World War II.
17Charred car parts littered the street, which flooded in some areas from ruptured water pipes.
18Several floors of the consulate were destroyed on one side, leaving a gaping hole.
19"I was sleeping when the explosion went off; it blew in my window and when I went outside the air was full of dust," said Ahmed Hasan, 20, a neighbor whose leg suffered minor cuts from flying glass.
20Civilians gathered around the area of the explosion, as police cordoned off the streets leading to the consulate.
21Several foreign journalists were briefly detained on the scene by police but later released.
22Interior Minister Magdy Abd El-Ghaffar later inspected the blast site.
23The consulate sits on one of the busiest intersections in downtown Cairo, along a major artery that connects Ramsis Square to the heart of the capital.
24The surrounding area includes a large hospital, a major police station surrounded by blast walls, a central ambulance dispatching station and the state-owned flagship newspaper Al-Ahram.
25The blast comes less than two weeks after the country's chief prosecutor was killed in an explosion near his home in Cairo as he was heading to work.
