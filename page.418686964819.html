Immigration complaint filed against Oregon imam

1Portland Imam Mohamed Sheikh Abdirahman Kariye leaves the United Sates Court of Appeals following oral arguments on the ACLU No Fly List challenge Friday, May 11, 2012, in Portland, Oregon.
2On Monday, July 20, 2015 U.S. authorities sought to revoke his citizenship.
3U.S. authorities are seeking to revoke the citizenship of an imam in Oregon who they say tried to conceal past associations with groups such as Al Qaeda.
4Mohamed Sheikh Abdirahman Kariye raised money, recruited fighters and provided training for armed groups battling Soviet forces in Afghanistan in the 1980s, the U.S. Department of Justice says in a complaint filed Monday in U.S. District Court in Portland.
5Kariye was one of more than a dozen people who filed a lawsuit challenging the no-fly list, winning last year a court order saying the government must provide information about why people are on the list.
6Government lawyers say Kariye for a time "dealt directly" with Osama bin Laden and Abdullah Azzam, the founders of Al Qaeda, and he recruited sympathizers in the United States and Pakistan for an Al-Qaeda precursor known as Maktab Al-Khidamat.
7Federal authorities say Kariye failed to reveal those details in his application for citizenship, which was granted in 1998.
8Attempts to reach Kariye through his Portland mosque and a former attorney were not immediately successful.
9Born in Somalia, Kariye came to the United States on a student visa in 1982, according to the complaint.
10Between 1985 and 1988, he traveled to Afghanistan, where he went to a training camp and fought with the Afghan mujahedeen against the Soviets.
11He helped process foreign fighters arriving in Pakistan for travel to training camps, authorities say, working directly with bin Laden and Azzam.
12At some point, he was arrested for his involvement with the mujahedeen and spent four months in a Pakistani prison.
13After returning to the United States in 1988, he applied for asylum and swore under oath that he hadn't left the country or been arrested.
14The Soviets pulled out of Afghanistan in 1989, but the complaint alleges that Kariye returned to Pakistan in 1990 and worked for three years to recruit sympathizers and raise funds for Maktab Al-Khidamat, the precursor of Al-Qaeda.
15As the longtime leader at Portland's largest mosque, Masjed As-Saber, Kariye is a well-known figure in the city's Islamic community.
16It was not immediately clear Monday whether Kariye is still the mosque's imam.
17Kariye was arrested at Portland International Airport in 2002 by an FBI-led anti-terrorism task force.
18He pleaded guilty to using a fraudulent Social Security number and defrauding the state Medicaid program by lying about his income to receive state-funded health insurance.
19A federal judge sentenced him to five years on probation.
20In a 2003 affidavit, the FBI said it believed Kariye provided support to seven Muslims who tried unsuccessfully to join the Taliban in fighting U.S. forces in Afghanistan.
21He was never charged.
22Kariye was one of a number of people who in 2010 sued the federal government over their placement on the no-fly list.
23Eight others have since joined the case.
24A federal judge in Portland ruled last year that The U.S. government's no-fly list banning individuals it accuses of links to terrorism from boarding commercial flights is unconstitutional as it gives people affected no meaningful way to contest the decision.
25The decision handed a major victory to the 13 Muslim American plaintiffs - who had all been denied boarding due to their placement on the list, and said they have never been charged with a terrorism-related offense, The Oregonian, a local news website, reported.
26Lawyers for the American Civil Liberties Union, which is representing Kariye and other plaintiffs in the case, filed a motion in April saying the government still hadn't provided them with enough information about their placement on the no-fly list.
