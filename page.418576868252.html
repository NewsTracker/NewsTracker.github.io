On the brink, EU leaders clinched bailout deal _ with an SMS

1Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras speaks with the media after a meeting of eurozone heads of state at the EU Council building in Brussels on Monday, July 13, 2015.
2A summit of eurozone leaders reached a tentative agreement with Greece on Monday for a bailout program that includes "serious reforms" and aid, removing an immediate threat that Greece could collapse financially and leave the euro.
3BRUSSELS (AP) - As dawn broke in Brussels on Monday, Europe's frazzled leaders gazed into an abyss: the looming exit of Greece from a common currency that symbolized European unity.
4Hours past a midnight deadline to clinch a Greek bailout deal, German Chancellor Angela Merkel and Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras were about to walk away from the table - exhausted from all-night talks filled with threats, shouting and ultimatums.
5A single text message rescued the talks - and possibly the euro.
6Dutch Premier Mark Rutte, a hard-liner on Greek reform waiting outside with other leaders, sent European Union President Donald Tusk an SMS proposing a compromise on the last sticking point keeping the two sides apart.
7For three hours, Merkel, Tsipras, Tusk and French President Francois Hollande had been stuck in dead-end closed-door talks, and the message suddenly made something click.
8Within an hour, the deal was clinched.
9This account of the dramatic events that led to a last-minute bailout agreement for Greece has been pieced together from interviews with a half-dozen officials from EU nations and institutions who attended the negotiations over the past months.
10They spoke on condition of anonymity because the talks were not public.
11Announcing the deal later that morning, Tusk, the summit host - who had been instrumental in keeping the opposing sides together before the text message landed - tried to lighten the mood with corny humor.
12"We have an a-Greek-ment," he declared.
13It was not enough to sweep away the months of bitterness leading to that moment.
14The negotiations, watched with trepidation around the world, swung from despair to hope and back again as leaders groped for a pact that would keep Greece in the euro and keep global markets steady.
15The Greek crisis, roiling Europe since the election in January of Tsipras - a radical leftist who vowed to free Greece from debilitating austerity - reached a terminal point at the end of June, when the bailout that was keeping Greece afloat was set to expire.
16Without a deal, Greece would effectively go bankrupt, likely forcing it out of the euro.
17Markets were sanguine in the run-up to that deadline, amid signs that the two sides were close to an agreement.
18But no leader could vouch for what would happen in case of failure.
19Deep into the night of June 26, after two promising days of summit talks, technical negotiators sat down at EU headquarters to fine-tune the details of what was expected to be the final breakthrough.
20So everyone was stunned when the Greek negotiators took cellphone calls, then simply got up and left.
21Tsipras returned to the Greek capital that night, and hastily convened his Cabinet.
22Hours later, he made a televised address that shocked Europe: He was calling a referendum for the following weekend on whether to accept the European bailout plan - and advocating a 'No' vote.
23"From an economic, financial and social point of view it was an absolute disaster," said Irish Finance Minister Michael Noonan.
24Within hours, lines had formed at ATM machines across Greece.
25The bank run continued throughout the weekend, and the government was left with little choice by Sunday night but to declare that banks would be shuttered the following week.
26It imposed capital controls, restricting cash withdrawals to 60 euros ($67) per day.
27The overwhelming "No" vote in the July 5 referendum was a triumph for Tsipras.
28Emboldened by what he saw as a strong mandate to bargain further with Greece's creditors, he returned to the negotiating table - but not before asking for the resignation of Yanis Varoufakis, his brash finance minister.
