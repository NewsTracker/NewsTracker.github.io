5 years of bailouts: the grim legacy of Greece's crisis

1The sculpture shows a shabbily-dressed man slipping off what appears to be a graph, an economic index perhaps, that is crumbling under his feet.
2His mouth is distended in something less than a scream, more than a cry.
3It's clearly not one of Greece's classical marbles depicting the wars and gods of old - in fact, it's the first public monument about the country's harrowing experience of economic depression.
4The resin-and-fiberglass piece, by 22-year-old Tasos Nyfadopoulos, is named "Crisis" and speaks of a country trying to cope with a collapsing economy.
5"It is extremely important to provide a voice for the human beings behind the numbers, who have a real story to tell that goes beyond statistics," Nyfadopoulos told The Associated Press.
6The sculptor worked on the project for four years and donated it to a southern Athens municipality, where it stands beside a busy highway linking the city center with the southern beach front.
7"Art must turn the collective experience of the crisis into an image, so that people can remember what has happened, and question every decision that has been taken in their name without their having being consulted," Nyfadopoulos said.
8Wednesday marks five years since Greece voted in its first bailout deal, a day after violent popular protests left three dead.
9The act was followed by years of turmoil in which the country tried to overhaul its economy in the midst of a downturn as brutal as the Great Depression.
10But after a myriad budget cuts, a million lost jobs, 250,000 closed businesses and nearly 240 billion euros ($267 billion) in rescue loans, the country is once again on the brink of default and relations with its creditors are worse than ever.
11Moody's rating agency said Monday that Greece is, despite all its savings efforts, still the fourth riskiest sovereign bond issuer, behind Ukraine, Venezuela and Argentina, all which have either defaulted on their debt or are perilously close to doing so.
12-In 2010, the bailout lenders had forecast Greece's economy would return to growth in 2012.
13It only emerged from recession late last year, and is predicted to fall back into it this year.
14-They had predicted unemployment would ease to around 15 percent by 2012.
15Not only did it not fall, but it kept rising, peaking at around 28 percent in 2014 and currently at 26 percent.
16-Debt was scheduled to fall to 141 percent of GDP in 2014, but it actually rose to 177 percent, even after inflicting severe losses on private bondholders in 2012.
17-An initial plan to sell off state assets aimed to raise 50 billion euros ($55.6 billion) by 2015.
18It has so far raised just 3.1 billion euros.
19One cause for the bad performance is that it is hard to cut debt at a time when the economy is shrinking, as Greece's creditors had asked it to do.
20But successive Greek governments have also done too little, experts say.
21They were too slow to change the way the economy and political system work.
22Because the public sector was spared cost cuts, the private sector suffered the burden of recession.
23Greece's Foundation for Economic and Industrial Research think tank noted in a report that the country made significant progress in healing public finances between 2010 and 2013.
24But that progress petered out late last year, when early elections were called for January.
25The report said state revenues undershot targets in early 2015 and if the trend continues, "it is very likely that significant fiscal problems" will emerge.
26In a nutshell: despite all the sacrifices of the past five years, the pain is not over for Greeks.
27The new radical left-led government was elected in January with a pledge to protect Greeks from more budget cuts.
28But creditors have not taken kindly to its hard line and insist they will pay no more rescue money unless Greece promises to make more reforms.
29The uncertainty is again hurting Greece's economy.
30The EU this week slashed its growth forecast for Greece this year from 2.5 percent to just 0.5 percent.
31If Greece gets no more rescue loans, the country may soon have to choose between paying pensions and state salaries or servicing state debt.
32Defaulting on debt could launch a chain reaction culminating in its exit from the eurozone and a Greek financial Stone Age, where the import-reliant country could barely afford key goods and commodities with a new, devalued currency.
33Berenberg bank analyst Holger Schmieding said that after "three months of pointless posturing, the new Greek government has finally entered into serious negotiations with its creditors."
34He said the debate is no longer mostly about debt, but rather reforms that will boost growth, allowing the country to service its debt in the long term.
35"Accordingly, labor and pension reforms are now among the major bones of contention," he said in a note Monday.
36He sees a 30 percent chance of "Grexit" - of Greece exiting the eurozone.
37The Economist Intelligence Unit placed the risk of Grexit at 40 percent.
38The EIU's Joan Hoey said a deal may still be possible, because the eurozone is averse to taking incalculable risks and Greek public opinion strongly favors staying in the euro.
39Hoey warned, however, that even a last-ditch agreement by the end of June, when Greece's bailout ends, would not necessarily resolve the problems underlying Greece's membership in the eurozone.
40The currency union lacks a common fiscal policy, and weaker members are unable to improve competitiveness through the traditional tool of devaluing their currency.
41Back on the beach-bound highway, Nyfadopoulos rejected the notion that his sculpture of a man slipping off an economic index was necessarily gloomy.
