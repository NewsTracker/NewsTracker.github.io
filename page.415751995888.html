24Airlines rushed to change rules on Thursday to require a second crew member in the cockpit at all times, hours after French prosecutors suggested a co-pilot who barricaded himself alone at the controls of a jetliner had crashed it on purpose.
25The United States already requires two crew members to be in the cabin at all times, but many other countries do not, allowing pilots to leave the flight deck, for example to use the toilet, as long as one pilot is at the controls.
26That is precisely what French prosecutors suspect happened on the Germanwings flight on Tuesday.
27They say Andreas Lubitz, 27, locked the captain out and appears to have set the controls to crash into a mountain, killing all 150 people on board.
28Airlines including Norwegian Air Shuttle, Britain's easyJet, Air Canada and Air Berlin all said within hours that they had introduced a requirement that two crew members be in the cockpit at all times.
29Canada said it would immediately impose such a rule on all its airlines.
30"We had a lot of concerned customers," an Air Berlin spokesman said.
31Airlines including Ryanair that already had such rules in place rushed to reassure customers.
32Among the companies that did not announce such a policy change was Germanwings parent Lufthansa, whose CEO Carsten Spohr said he believed it was unnecessary.
33"I don't see any need to change our procedures here," Spohr told journalists.
34"It was a one-off case. But we will look at it with the various experts at Lufthansa and the authorities. We shouldn't lose ourselves in short-term measures."
