Kenya shuts money transfer firms

1The bank accounts of 86 individuals and "entities" had also been frozen, Joseph Nkaissery added.
2Those targeted are believed to include a Somali-linked bus company and hotel.
3An assault by the Somalia-headquartered al-Shabab group killed 148 people at Kenya's Garissa University last week.
4Kenyan officials have repeatedly accused Somalis in the country of colluding with the militants.
5Nearly 500,000 refugees are in the country - many of whom fled decades of conflict and drought in Somalia.
6Kenya also has its own Somali population - most of whom live in the capital, Nairobi, and in the north-east, where the university attack took place.
7Somalis say most of them are law-abiding citizens of Kenya, and they have also bore the brunt of al-Shabab's insurgency.
8They have condemned the government's crackdown as "blanket punishment" of the community.
9BBC Africa's Abdullahi Abdi in Nairobi says a leaked document shows that hotel and transport companies, as well as Muslim clerics and human rights organisations, risk having their bank accounts frozen.
10Somalis around the world rely heavily on the informal money transfer firms, known as "hawalas", to do business and to send cash to relatives because of the almost non-existent banking sector in Somalia.
11There have been a similar crackdown on the companies in the UK and US.
12A spokesman for the Somali money transfer agencies in Kenya, Abdi Ali, told the BBC they would oppose moves to shut them down.
13"To us, it is only a ministerial directive which is sent to the central bank. We will talk to the Kenyan government about this issue and use its justice system to settle matter," he said.
14The United Nations estimates Somalis in the diaspora send home about $1.6bn ( 1.1bn) annually, significantly more than foreign aid.
15More than 40% of Somalis receive remittances, the bulk of which are used for basic needs, including food, clothes, medicine and education, according to a UN survey.
16Somalia has not had a functioning government since the fall of Siad Barre's government in 1991, and has been hit by religious and clan conflicts.
