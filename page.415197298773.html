In Ferguson, scathing U.S. report brings pressure for change

1City officials said Tuesday they were reviewing the report, which they expect to be released Wednesday.
2With scathing findings of a months-long investigation being released, attention now turns to Ferguson as the city confronts how to fix racial biases that the federal government says are rooted in the police department, court and jail.
3The full report could serve as a road map for significant changes by the department, which commanded national attention after one of its officers shot and killed an unarmed black man, 18-year-old Michael Brown, last summer.
4Similar federal investigations of troubled police departments have led to the appointment of independent monitors and mandated overhauls in the most fundamental of police practices.
5The Justice Department maintains the right to sue a police department if officials balk at making changes, though many investigations resolve the issue with both sides negotiating a blueprint for change known as a consent decree.
6"It's quite evident that change is coming down the pike. This is encouraging," said John Gaskin III, a St. Louis community activist.
7"It's so unfortunate that Michael Brown had to be killed. But in spite of that, I feel justice is coming."
8Brown's killing set off weeks of protests and initiated a national dialogue about police use of force and their relations with minority communities.
9A separate report being issued soon is expected to clear the officer, Darren Wilson, of federal civil rights charges.
10A state grand jury already declined to indict Wilson, who has since resigned.
11The findings of the investigation, which began weeks after Brown's killing last August, are being released as Attorney General Eric Holder prepares to leave his job following a six-year tenure that focused largely on civil rights.
12The report is based on interviews with police leaders and residents, a review of more than 35,000 pages of police records and analysis of data on stops, searches and arrests.
13An image of 18-year-old Michael Brown is seen on the tie of his father Michael Brown Sr. as he attends church in Ferguson, Mo., last year.
14Ferguson became the focal point of a U.S. debate on race relations after police officer Darren Wilson shot and killed Michael Brown.
15A summary provided Tuesday reveals patterns of bias across the criminal justice system, from encounters with patrol officers to treatment in the municipal court and jail.
16It says black drivers are far more likely to be searched than white motorists even though they're less likely to be found with contraband.
17Nearly all people kept at the city jail for more than two days are black, and of the cases in which the police department recorded instances of use of force, the overwhelming majority involved force used against blacks.
18Overall, African-Americans make up 67 percent of the population of Ferguson, about 16 kilometres from downtown St. Louis.
19The police department has been criticized as racially imbalanced and not reflective of the community's demographic makeup.
20At the time of the shooting, only three of 53 officers were black, though Mayor James Knowles III has said the city is attracting a large pool of applicants to police jobs, including minority candidates.
21Benjamin Crump, the attorney for the Brown family, said the report's findings "confirm what Michael Brown's family has believed all along, and that is that the tragic killing of an unarmed 18-year-old black teenager was part of a systemic pattern of inappropriate policing of African-American citizens in the Ferguson community."
22Besides identifying discriminatory police practices, the report alleges a culture of distrust between the police and community fuelled by the reliance on fines for revenues.
23It says blacks are overwhelmingly exposed to citations for minor infractions such as walking in the street or disturbing the peace.
24The physical tussle that led to Brown's death began after Wilson told him and a friend to move from the street to the sidewalk.
25Activist Cornel West is knocked over during a scuffle with police last year during protests over the shooting of Michael Brown.
26The practice hits poor people especially hard, sometimes leading to jail time when they can't pay, the report says, and has contributed to a cynicism about the police on the part of citizens.
27The department has conducted roughly 20 civil rights investigations of police departments during Holder's tenure.
28It's common for federal officials to issue starkly critical findings about police department practices and to demand extensive changes.
29"I'm confident," Holder said of the Ferguson report last month, "that people will be satisfied with the results that we announce."
