UN: After 2nd quake, many in Nepal cut off by blocked roads

1KATHMANDU, Nepal (AP) - Many survivors of Nepal's most recent earthquake remain cut off by blocked roads in isolated villages, a U.N. official said Wednesday, after this Himalayan nation suffered through its second major quake in less than three weeks.
2The magnitude-7.3 earthquake shook the impoverished country Tuesday, killing at least 79 people and injuring more than 2,300, just as it was beginning to rebuild from a devastating April 25 earthquake.
3The most recent quake hit hardest in deeply rural parts of the Himalayan foothills, hammering many villages reached only by hiking trails and causing road-blocking landslides.
4A woman points towards cracks on the wall of the Bir Hospital that appeared after s earthquake in Kathmandu, Nepal, Wednesday, May 13, 2015.
5Thousands of fear-stricken people spent the night outdoors after a new earthquake killed dozens of people and spread more misery in Nepal, which is still reeling from a devastating quake that killed thousands nearly three weeks ago.
6"Damaged houses were further damaged or destroyed. Houses and schools building spared before were affected yesterday, roads were damaged," said Jamie McGoldrick, a top U.N. official in Nepal.
7Among 14 quake-hit districts, some are barely accessible, and a large part of the affected population could not be reached easily because of damaged roads.
8"Some are even difficult to reach by helicopter. We are facing monumental challenges here to support the government in these districts to have a credible response," McGoldrick said.
9On Wednesday, officials with bullhorns walked through the worst-damaged streets of Chautara, a small town northeast of Kathmandu, calling for people to leave buildings in danger of collapsing after Tuesday's quake.
10"There is danger!" they said over the bullhorns.
11"Leave the buildings!"
12Most people, though, had fled into the open the day before and had spent the night in tents or under plastic tarps.
13Chautara, a foothills town, became a hub for rescuers and humanitarian aid after the first earthquake.
14Officials there said at least three people had died and more than 60 were injured in the new quake.
15People salvaged whatever they could from their toppled homes.
16Most houses appeared to be damaged; some were leveled.
17Others tilted and rested on adjacent homes.
18"We were in the shop. All of a sudden the building shook. I jumped out of the store and the next second it fell down. It was already tilted by the last month's earthquake. I watched it just slide and fall on its side," said Devi Acharya, a convenience store owner.
19Meanwhile, a U.S. Marine Corps helicopter carrying six Marines and two Nepalese soldiers remained missing.
20It had been delivering disaster aid Tuesday in the country's northeast, U.S. officials said.
21There have been no indications the aircraft crashed.
22Home ministry official Laxmi Dhakal said Wednesday that army helicopters were scouring the Sunkhani area, nearly 80 kilometers (50 miles) northeast of Kathmandu, for the missing helicopter.
23Most of the people confirmed dead in the second quake by Wednesday afternoon were in Dolakha district, northeast of Kathmandu, said the district's chief administrator, Prem Lal Lamichane.
24"People are terrorized. Everyone is scared here. They spent the night out in the open," Lamichane said, adding the administration was running out of relief materials.
25He asked the government to send more helicopters and supplies and said many injured people were stranded in villages.
26Tuesday's quake killed 16 people in northern India and one person in Tibet.
27The magnitude-7.8 earthquake that hit April 25 killed more than 8,150 people and flattened entire villages, leaving hundreds of thousands homeless in the country's worst quake since 1934.
28The U.S. Geological Survey said Tuesday's earthquake was the largest aftershock of the April quake.
29But it was significantly less powerful and occurred deeper in the Earth.
30Working from USGS data, calculations done by University of Michigan earthquake geophysicist Eric Hetland indicated that about 65,000 people were exposed to "violent" shaking Tuesday, compared to 1.5 million on April 25.
31The first quake also drove many people to leave damaged homes, which were empty when the new quake caused more damage and collapses.
32On Wednesday, McGoldrick said the U.N. revised its donor appeal to provide $423 million.
33The response to the earlier appeal of $415 million has been low, with about 15 percent of the sum received.
34Associated Press writers Bernat Armangue in Chautara, Tim Sullivan in New Delhi and Lolita C. Baldor in Washington contributed to this report.
