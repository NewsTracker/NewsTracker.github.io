Google poaches Morgan Stanley's CFO

1The corporate logo of financial firm Morgan Stanley is pictured on the company's world headquarters in the Manhattan borough of New York City, January 20, 2015.
2SAN FRANCISCO/NEW YORK (Reuters) - Google Inc hired Morgan Stanley Chief Financial Officer Ruth Porat as its own finance chief, a sign Google is aiming to rein in costs as it invests in new businesses such as self-driving cars and internet-connected eyeglasses.
3Porat has helped execute a sweeping cost-cutting strategy across several business lines at Morgan Stanley.
4The bank cut its expenses, excluding compensation, to 29 percent of its revenue last year, down from 34 percent in 2012.
5Google's costs have jumped as the company embarked on an increasing number of ambitious projects.
6Last year, the company's revenue grew 19 percent, while total expenses rose 23.4 percent, a trend that alarmed some analysts.
7"You want someone to come in there and push back against the free spenders," said Colin Gillis, an equity research analyst at BGC Partners, a brokerage, adding that investors hope Porat will be that person.
8Google's shares rose as much as 2.5 percent on Tuesday after Porat's appointment was announced by both companies.
9She will start her new job on May 26.
10Porat is the latest among a string of Wall Street executives to leave an industry that is increasingly regulated to move into the more free-wheeling technology sector, where fortunes can be built fast but businesses can also become irrelevant overnight.
11Total compensation to Google's departing CFO was twice as much as Porat's for the three years through 2013 - $62.2 million vs $29.6 million, according to public filings by the companies.
12Google has not disclosed how much it expects to pay Porat.
13She joined Morgan Stanley in 1987, and led Morgan Stanley's investment banking business for tech companies during the Internet boom, working with Amazon.com Inc and eBay Inc, among others.
14Some analysts took her appointment as a sign Google acquisitions could pick up.
15Porat ran the investment banking business for financial companies including banks, and during the financial crisis she became Morgan Stanley's point person with regulators and other government officials in Washington.
16Porat, 57, was considered a potential candidate to become chief executive whenever current CEO James Gorman steps down.
17But several high-level Morgan Stanley sources who have spoken to Reuters about succession planning over the past two years said she was not seen as a top contender.
18Instead, they pointed to two other executives - Gregory Fleming, 52, who runs wealth management and asset management, and Colm Kelleher, 57, who runs investment banking and trading - as more likely contenders.
19Some observers saw gender bias at play, given her qualifications and Wall Street's history as a male-dominated industry.
20At an event last year, Porat criticized the lack of female leadership in corporate America as "an embarrassment."
21"If a woman 'leans in,' but is leaning against a door that is nailed shut, no amount of leaning will bust down the door," she said.
22"So I think we must hold our organizations accountable where they control the doors by demanding clarity and transparency around succession planning."
23Critics have also accused Silicon Valley's culture of being hostile to women.
24Ellen Pao, a former partner at Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers, is suing her firm for discrimination.
25The trial heads into final arguments on Tuesday.
26Porat joins an array of bankers, analysts, and other executives that have left Wall Street as strict post-crisis regulations have made the business safer and stodgier.
27Some of these executives have headed into the technology sector, including Anthony Noto, once an investment banker at Goldman Sachs Group Inc, who last year became CFO at Twitter Inc. In 2010, Mary Meeker, a widely followed tech analyst at Morgan Stanley, left the bank to join Kleiner Perkins.
28Porat has ties to Silicon Valley, having spent much of her childhood in California and attended Stanford University, where she serves on the board of trustees.
29She has spent time building her connections with Washington.
30Those ties to Washington could have translated into a government post - she was widely reported to have been a candidate to be the Treasury deputy secretary, but said she was not interested.
31Porat will replace Patrick Pichette at Google, who said on March 10 that he planned to retire.
32At Morgan Stanley, Jonathan Pruzan, co-head of the bank's global financial institutions group in investment banking, will become CFO.
33Pruzan, 46, joined Morgan Stanley in 1994.
