Latest on Americas Summit: Mexico backs US-Cuba talks

1Barack Obama and Raul Castro are having the first sit-down between US, Cuban leaders in more than half century.
2President Enrique Pena Nieto of Mexico is expressing support for negotiations to improve relations between Cuba and the United States, countries he describes as "two great friends of Mexico."
3Pena Nieto says, "The leaders of both nations have reminded the world that openness to dialogue is charged with promise and possibilities."
4Pena Nieto comments came at a plenary session of the Summit of the Americas in Panama.
5U.S. President Barack Obama and President Juan Manuel Santos of Colombia have concluded a meeting on the margins of the Summit of the Americas.
6The White House says the leaders noted there has been progress since a 2012 summit on the Colombia peace process.
7Obama also expressed his continued support for Colombia as it works through victims' rights and other issues related to the peace talks.
8He told Santos the appointment of a U.S. special envoy to the peace process symbolizes shared hope for a stable and lasting peace in Colombia.
9Venezuela President Nicolas Maduro is holding out an olive branch of sorts to President Barack Obama, saying he's willing to forgive a long history of aggressions against his socialist government and work to repair relations.
10The two countries have seen relations fall to a new low since the White House slapped sanctions on senior officials for alleged human rights abuses.
11In a 40-minute speech at the Summit of the Americas, Maduro said any effort to normalize relations depends on the U.S repealing the financial and travel sanctions.
12Maduro said he's been waiting for more than a year for the Obama administration to respond to his invitation to exchange ambassadors for the first time since 2010.
13Obama wasn't present for Maduro's remarks at the Summit of the Americas.
14He had stepped away from the plenary session earlier to attend a bilateral meeting with Colombia's President Juan Manuel Santos.
15Cuba is dedicating a special TV channel to live coverage of the Summit of the Americas, but relatively few in Havana seemed to be watching when Raul Castro made his impassioned speech.
16Many capital residents said they had no idea it was going to be on.
17Sixty-nine-year-old retired lawyer Paco Soler was one of the few who caught Castro's remarks, watching from a bar in the Vedado district.
18Soler was struck by Castro's remark absolving President Obama of blame for the past.
19Soler says that comment is "an elegant way to create a positive atmosphere for the possible meeting between the two presidents."
20Seventy-five-year-old retiree Diego Reyes saw footage of last night's historic handshake between the two presidents on TV but was unimpressed.
21He complained that since the Dec. 17 announcement that the U.S. and Cuba would move to restore diplomatic relations, "nothing has changed for regular Cubans. And what we're waiting for is an improvement."
22-Associated Press writer Anne-Marie Garcia in Havana.
23Brazilian President Dilma Rousseff is one of several leaders criticizing recent U.S. sanctions against seven Venezuelan officials whom Washington accuses of human rights abuses related to anti-government protests last year.
24"This good moment for hemispheric relations can no longer allow unilateral measures and policies of isolation in general, and they are always counterproductive and ineffective," Rousseff says, according to a translation of her remarks into Spanish.
25"For that reason we reject the adoption of sanctions against Venezuela."
