Anti-Muslim Film: US Court Sides With Google

1A federal appeals court overturns an earlier ruling that ordered Google to remove Innocence Of Muslims from YouTube.
2YouTube should not have been forced to remove an anti-Muslim film that sparked violence in the Middle East, a US federal appeals court has ruled.
3The decision, seen as a victory for free speech advocates, overturned an earlier 2-1 ruling that ordered YouTube to scrub Innocence Of Muslims from its site.
4The film inspired rioting in 2012 by those who considered it blasphemous to the Prophet Mohammed.
5It was initially cited by the Obama administration as the cause of an attack on a US mission in Benghazi, Libya, where four Americans, including Ambassador Chris Stevens, were killed.
6President Barack Obama and other world leaders asked Google, which owns YouTube, to take the film down.
7The low-budget movie also prompted death threats against actors who took part in it.
8Cindy Lee Garcia, who received threats, argued she had a copyright claim to the film because she believed she was acting in a different production.
9Garcia said she had been paid $500 ( 319) to perform in a movie called Desert Warrior that she thought had nothing to do with religion.
10She ended up appearing in a five-second scene in Innocence Of Muslims, during which her character's dubbed-over voice asked if Mohammed was a child molester.
11Google said Garcia had no claim because the filmmaker wrote the dialogue, managed the production and even dubbed over her lines.
12The tech giant also argued that requests to pull the film down amounted to censorship.
13The film's creator, Mark Basseley Youssef, was imprisoned in 2012 for violating probation stemming from a 2010 cheque fraud case.
14He was released on probation in September 2013.
15Google has not said whether the film would be reposted on YouTube.
