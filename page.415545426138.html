Put the skatearounds to bed? Evidence suggests more sleep may be better than morning practice

Crimea exiles fight to keep Ukraine unity message alive

1He silences his cellphone and sometimes reads a relaxing book to drift off.
2Although he's an 18-year veteran, the Colorado Avalanche forward only recently happened upon this simple eye opener: Quality sleep really does matter.
3Especially to athletes.
4The closer Iginla comes to nine hours of slumber on any given night, the better his production seems to be on the ice.
5That is why Iginla is not a big fan of early morning practices before games, even though those skates are as much a part of hockey tradition as playoff beards and the handshake line.
6There is evidence that sleep restriction curtails adequate rest and recovery for athletes, which can impact performance.
7Some NBA teams have even dabbled with eliminating the traditional shootaround.
8Cheri Mah, a researcher at the Stanford Sleep Disorders Clinic and Research Laboratory, tracked the sleep habits of the men's basketball team at Stanford for a study released in 2011.
9By extending their sleep, the Cardinal players increased their 3-point and free throw shooting by 9 percent.
10Iginla sees how the evidence can come into play on the ice, too.
11"When you're tired, it doesn't seem like the game comes as quick," said Iginla, who began working with sleep coaches while with the Calgary Flames a few years ago.
12"You want to keep things fresh, kind of like a kid, where you can't wait to get on the ice, can't wait to go out there and expend that energy. The more you capture that the better."
13Nashville Predators coach Peter Laviolette actually scrubbed the morning skatearound when he was with Carolina in 2008-09.
14But he was fired that December and didn't get to see the experiment through.
15The Hurricanes went to the Eastern Conference final after he was let go, and Laviolette is convinced the lack of a skatearound early in the season played a role.
16"I think it's because they had a lot of energy left," Laviolette said, smiling.
17"I think right now (skatearounds) are more habit than anything else. I don't necessarily think it's good for you."
18Still, players are creatures of habits.
19Maybe decades ago, skating or shooting around before games was a necessary ploy - a way for players to loosen up after a late night.
20Nowadays, players are more conditioned.
21"I'm all for that, getting rid of those," Anaheim forward Ryan Getzlaf said.
22"It's just kind of been instilled in some guys that they need to go on the ice."
23Dr. Charles Czeisler is known around NBA circles as the "sleep doctor."
24As the chief of the sleep and circadian disorders division at Brigham and Women's Hospital in Boston, Czeisler has worked with several professional teams and believes more emphasis should be placed on monitoring the body's internal clock.
25A few seasons ago, Czeisler consulted with the Portland Trail Blazers to curb their trend of struggling on East Coast trips.
26His biggest advice?
27Keep everyone's schedule on Oregon time and not, say, New York time.
28That three-hour difference is huge to the body.
29"So the coach told them, 'OK, your curfew is 2 a.m.,'" said Czeisler, who's worked with the Boston Celtics and Red Sox.
30"The players were all very excited about that. Trying to adjust is just going to degrade your performance."
31Czeisler would like to see sleep taken just as seriously as strength programs or nutrition.
32"Would you starve yourself before a game and say, 'Ok, I'm not going to eat for 24 hours?' That would be crazy," Czeisler said.
33"There's no reason to sleep-starve yourself before a game."
34In Mah's opinion, elite athletes need between 8-10 hours of sleep.
35"So often sleep is the first thing to go when there's a busy schedule and a lot on the plate," said Mah, who recently offered sleep guidance to the Western Conference-leading Golden State Warriors.
36"Making it a priority is essential for players and coaches."
37NBA teams such as the Minnesota Timberwolves and Denver Nuggets have experimented with cutting down on the traditional shootaround approach.
38Instead of having their players show up at the gym early in the morning, go home around lunch and then return that night for the game, they're having them arrive closer to game time.
39Recently, though, and by a 6-5 player vote, the struggling Nuggets decided to go back to the early morning shootaround just before coach Brian Shaw was fired.
40Not everyone is a big fan of getting up early.
41"I notice when we had no shootaround, everyone is more upbeat when they got to the gym," Denver guard Ty Lawson said.
42"It sticks better and it's fresher (later in the day)."
43Lawson prefers to break his sleep into chunks - five hours at night and then a three-hour nap during the day.
44"When I get back from shootarournd now, that's all I do is nap," Lawson said.
45"If I don't get that nap, I'm done."
46Iginla certainly wouldn't mind being done with morning skates.
47But it might take a Stanley Cup champion to usher in that sort of change.
48"That's what it will probably take to get it to catch on big time," Iginla acknowledged.
49"I'd rather get a little more sleep."

6SHCHASLYVE, Ukraine (AP) - The tormentors stuck scotch tape to his eyes and locked him in a dank room.
7For 11 days, Andrei Schekun said, they beat him, shocked him with electrodes and scalded him with burning metallic plates.
8Schekun's crusade for Ukrainian unity, which he continues today in exile, began as pro-Moscow sentiment among the majority ethnic Russian population reached fever pitch on the peninsula.
9Demands for a split from Ukraine spiked within days of the February ouster of Moscow-friendly Ukrainian President Viktor Yanukovych.
10Although most Ukrainians resented the president with feverish intensity for his predatory rule, people in regions such as Crimea and eastern Ukraine, with dominant ethnic Russian populations, viewed him with more sympathy.
11Russian state television stations already fed them a steady diet of stories about unrest being led by rabid Ukrainian nationalists bent on oppression of the Russian population.
12Ill-intentioned Ukrainians, the Russian networks warned, were poised to descend upon Crimea.
13Schekun's activism in drumming up support in Crimea for the anti-government protests that toppled Yanukovych was enough to make him suspect.
14Soon flyers appeared in the courtyard outside Schekun's home in the Crimean town of Bakhchysarai describing him as a traitor.
15"Everywhere in the district where we lived, they stuck up leaflets with his face, calling him a bloodthirsty traitor and a fascist, and all kinds of other things," said Schekun's wife, Lyudmila, speaking from their new home outside Kiev.
16Schekun, along with his wife and three sons, are among the roughly 20,000 people who Ukrainian officials say have left Crimea for the mainland since the region was annexed by Russia.
17On March 9, one week before the referendum, plain-clothed policemen detained Schekun at a train station in the Crimean capital of Simferopol, as he awaited the delivery of Ukrainian flags and symbols.
18The group was from a self-described militia charged with ensuring the referendum on joining Russia proceeded smoothly.
19"They thought we wanted to derail their referendum," he said.
20"They thought we were some kind of terrorist group or something."
21Pro-Ukrainian activism inside Crimea has become an even more dangerous proposition since then.
22Last week, a small group of Crimea residents marked the day of Ukraine's national poet Taras Shevchenko by gathering outside his statue in Simferopol with Ukrainian flags.
23That minor act of defiance earned them 40 hours hard labor.
24The peninsula's Tatar minority, whose leadership resisted Russian annexation, says it too has been subjected to intimidation that has ranged from petty official harassment to beatings, kidnappings and murder.
25Speaking at a United Nations conference in Geneva on Monday, the human rights envoy for Ukraine's parliament, Valeriya Lutkovskaya, described Crimea as a "peninsula of terror."
26"People are afraid to express their opinion," Lutkovskaya said.
27"They feel fear for their life and future, fear to espouse their faith and speak in their native language."
28According to the most recent census, carried out in 2001, ethnic Ukrainians make up one-quarter of Crimea's population.
29From a distance, exiles feel at a loss to help and say they are disappointed with the Ukrainian government's failure to keep Crimea near the top of its agenda.
30"Unfortunately, there is at the moment no single government executive body that deals with Crimean matters," said Eskender Bariyev, a member of the Mejlis, a self-governing body of Crimean Tatars.
31With government initiative lacking, Schekun says it is up to activists like him to shore up the morale of Ukrainians still in Crimea: "They need our moral support," he said.
32It's hard to keep up the fight in a rented apartment outside the capital.
33But Schekun remains upbeat about his mission.
34"We will not let the government forget," Schekun said, joined by his wife in emphatically finishing the thought, "that Crimea is Ukraine."
