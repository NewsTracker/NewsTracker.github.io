FIFA: Blatter resignation won't lead many to quit

10RIO DE JANEIRO (AP) - A consistent ally of Sepp Blatter, Danny Jordaan already has a new job.
11Jordaan, the head of the South African Football Association, was credited with bringing the 2010 World Cup to South Africa.
12He is also being investigated for $10 million in bribes paid to land the event, part of the corruption scandal that pushed FIFA President Blatter to resign.
13A day after the corruption and bribery scandal broke last week, Jordaan was appointed the new mayor of his South African hometown.
14He plans to stay on as the soccer federation's head, a job with no salary.
15But he's hedging his bets.
16Call it the domino effect, or a chain reaction, or simply bracing for change coming to FIFA.
17Blatter is going, so can federation presidents who run world soccer - some like feudal lords in Africa, Asia and Latin America - hang on for long?
18Peter Alegi, a historian at Michigan State University and author of "Africa's World Cup," said a 'mild reformer" - not a radical - was likely to succeed Blatter.
19"I do not see much of a chance of a domino effect from Blatter's resignation," he said in an email.
20"I believe it's more likely that the diverse members of the global football establishment will do whatever it takes to hold on to power, privilege, and prestige."
21Former Barcelona and Brazil star Romario hopes not.
22An outspoken Brazilian federal senator, Romario termed Blatter's downfall "a big wave that can sweep away" bribery and impunity.
23"This is the best news we've had in a long time," Romario said through his social media accounts.
24"His fall will come as a tsunami to every corrupt leader in confederations around the world."
25Romario singled out Brazil's authoritarian football leadership, often described as more powerful than the country's president.
26Former Brazilian Football Confederation president Jose Maria Marin is in a Swiss jail awaiting extradition to the United States, and current president Marco Polo Del Nero is keeping a low profile.
27Another former president Ricardo Teixeira is under investigation in Brazil for corruption related to bank transfers during preparations for the 2014 World Cup.
28"We need the corrupt ones in prison," Romario said.
29Blatter had automatic support in Brazil, home to his 99-year-old predecessor Joao Havelange and host for last year's World Cup.
30His real power came from guaranteeing that tiny Caribbean islands, or countries distant from Europe, receive millions in FIFA development money.
31FIFA spent about $1 billion on development projects in the last three years - from Zambia to Andorra - and Blatter got the credit, even if not all the money reached the grassroots.
32"Sad day for Futbol," Zambian Football Association President Kalusha Bwalya said on Twitter.
33"You will always have my support."
34Ibrahim Sanni Darra, a spokesman for the Ghana Football Association, said Blatter's 17-year reign would be viewed "with nostalgia" and said Africans "will not forget."
35"Before his tenure, Africa had only two slots in the World Cup," Darra said.
36"He not only increased it, he also brought the World Cup to Africa."
37New Zealand Football chief executive Andy Martin called Blatter's resignation "a great day for football."
38"I think we can all get together now and start repairing the tarnished brand of football, and that's what everybody wants."
39Wilmar Valdez of Uruguay, a vice president of football's South American governing body CONMEBOL, called Blatter departure a "shock to world football."
40Uruguay, Argentina and Chile were among the South American federations that voted against Blatter on Friday.
41In previous elections, South America was solid Blatter territory.
42"What we were sure of is that sooner or later Blatter's reign would end," Valdez said.
43"FIFA needed change, and now we have to see how the (new) pieces fit."
44Jules Boykoff, who studies sports politics at Pacific University in Portland, Oregon, called Blatter's resignation an "unexpected shift in the tectonic plates at FIFA."
45"This is a remarkable opportunity to move world football in a very different direction," said Boykoff, a former professional soccer player, who is lobbying for changes in women's soccer.
46"For decades FIFA has represented a privileged sliver of the global 1 percent; a sexist bastion of power. If there's ever been a chance to dent that reality, this is it."
47Gerald Imray in Cape Town, South Africa; Francis Kokutse in Accra, Ghana; Deborah Rey in Buenos Aires, Argentina; Steve McMorran in Wellington, New Zealand, contributed to this report.
