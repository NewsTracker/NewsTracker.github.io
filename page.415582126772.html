Greece looms over EU summit

Tag Heuer to make smartwatches with Google and Intel

1In many ways, this EU summit is a haunting reminder for the participants of the worst days of the Greek debt crisis several years ago.
2After two bailout programs and a partial debt relief deal, Greece once again is threatened by bankruptcy.
3It could be only weeks away.
4The big difference between then and now is that today the leftist Greek government under Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras is doing all it can to put off its creditors.
5Greek politicians have not only reviled the German government and the Troika team of auditors from the EU, the European Central Bank and the International Monetary Fund.
6Tsipras is also implementing pieces of legislation that run contrary to agreements.
7With his "humanitarian crisis" bill he is offering free electricity and food stamps to the needy.
8That may be understandable from a social point of view, and it is certainly popular in Greece.
9The EU, however, wants to have a say in the way its money is spent.
10No wonder tensions are high.
11EU Council President Donald Tusk fears they might dominate the Brussels summit completely, and therefore he has relegated to the sidelines of the summit a special meeting between Tsipras, German Chancellor Angela Merkel, French President Francois Hollande and top EU officials.
12For some, holding this exclusive meeting outside the normal summit schedule is a dangerous split.
13The Belgian Prime Minister Charles Michel has complained that small countries like his own were not invited, although they have contributed to the bailout programs.
14Tusk, as summit host, has a difficult job on his hands and has tried to downplay the importance of the talks on Greece.
15Chancellor Merkel meanwhile - who will also meet Tsipras in Berlin on Monday - has made it clear that neither the Brussels nor the Berlin meeting will bring a decision on Greece's acute financial problems.
16For her, as well as for practically everybody else except Mr Tsipras, it is now up to the Greeks to fulfill the conditions.
17The Greek leader had some sympathy initially when he came to power, but has now become totally isolated on the Brussels stage.
18As this Greek tragedy unfolds - some would even call it a parody - it is already overshadowing other issues at the summit, like dealing with Russia and Ukraine.
19EU leaders will probably keep existing sanctions against Russia in place as long as the Kyiv ceasefire agreement is fully working.
20They also want to create an energy union to reduce dependence on Russian energy imports, and work on details of a planned massive EU investment program.
21All this, however, could move to second place now that the Greek crisis is getting more dramatic by the day.
22Chancellor Angela Merkel has warned German parliamentarians not to expect a quick solution to Greece's dispute with international creditors over its debt.
23She was speaking to the Bundestag ahead of an EU summit.
24Greek premier Alexis Tsipras has asked for a meeting with counterparts on the sidelines of an EU leaders' meeting.
25However, Tsipras sounded a note of defiance in advance, vowing that his government would end austerity.
26Greek lawmakers have adopted a "humanitarian crisis" bill.
27It's the biggest package of social measures put forward by the new government so far.
28Instead of looking at plans for a European Energy Union, an EU summit later this week is set to deal with Greece and its financial woes yet again.
29Bernd Riegert filed this report from Brussels.
30Greek prime minister Alexis Tsipras defended his so-called humanitarian law on Thursday as the first bill drawn up by Athens in five years, rather than ordered by E.U. technocrats.
31European Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker urged all parties involved in solving the crisis to "get a grip".
32Following emergency talks with German and French leaders, Greece has agreed to submit a reform list "within days."
33Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras has urged the two leaders to help tackle a looming financial crisis.
1NEW YORK -- Watch out Apple: Swiss company Tag Heuer is developing its own smartwatch with tech rivals Google and Intel.
2The announcement Thursday comes a month before Apple Inc. starts selling its smartwatch.
3Tag Heuer's version is expected to be ready at the end of the year.
4The companies did not say what it will look like or how much it will cost, but watches from the luxury timepiece maker typically cost $1,000 and up.
5The Apple Watch will start at $350 when it goes on sale April 24, but ones made with 18-karat gold begin at $10,000.
6The Tag Heuer watch won't be the only fashionable smartwatch competing with Apple.
7Clothing maker Guess worked with a startup, Martian Watches, to create a line of fashionable smartwatches modeled on Rigor, a traditional watch line from Guess.
8Another small company, Burg, has a model with Swarovski crystals.
9The Tag Heuer smartwatch will use technology from Intel Corp., whose Basis subsidiary already makes a fitness tracker called Peak.
10It will run Google Inc.'s Android Wear system, which is already used in smartwatches by Motorola, Sony, LG and others.
11Android Wear emphasizes voice controls, although some selections can be made through buttons and touch screens.
12Like most smartwatches, Tag Heuer's version will require that a wearer keep a smartphone nearby for all the watch's functions to work.
13Tag Heuer, Google and Intel made the announcement at Baselworld, a watch and jewelry trade show in Switzerland.
14Tag Heuer is a part of French luxury goods group LVMH.
