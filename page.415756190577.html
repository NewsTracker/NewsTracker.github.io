Germanwings plane crash: co-pilot Andreas Lubitz was 'happy he had the job' with airline

1Montabaur, Germany: Co-pilot Andreas Lubitz was silent as Germanwings Flight 9525 from Barcelona to Dusseldorf descended for eight minutes before crashing in the French Alps on Tuesday, authorities said.
2Lubitz, identified by a French prosecutor as the co-pilot of the Airbus A320, appeared to want to "destroy the plane" in the deadly Alpine crash that killed 150 people.
3Marseille Public Prosecutor Brice Robin told reporters that there was "absolute silence in the cockpit," which the pilot had exited, as the plane descended.
4The cockpit voice recorder indicates that the pilot was locked out of the cockpit while Lubitz remained inside.
5"This action can only be done deliberately," Robin added.
6A sign reading'Crash landing path' stands next to field at the LSC Westerwald flight club in Montabaur where the Germanwings co-pilot Andreas Lubitz had been a member.
7"The theory of a deliberate crash is plausible," the German transport minister said in a press conference after Robin's announcement.
8German authorities are expected to release information about Lubitz later this week, the AFP reported, but details are beginning to emerge about the pilot.
9Lubitz started working for Lufthansa's budget carrier Germanwings in September 2013, immediately after completing training at Lufthansa's Bremen facility.
10He had 630 hours of flight experience, a Lufthansa spokesperson said.
11Klaus Radke, chairman of the aviation club Westerwald, talks to journalists in Montabaur, Germany.
12Club member Andreas Lubitz was the co-pilot on flight Germanwings 9525 that crashed with 150 people on board on Tuesday in the French Alps.
13Andreas Lubitz first took to the skies as a teenage glider pilot flying from a grass runway in western Germany and never showed any sign he was anything but thrilled to have landed a job with Germanwings, those who taught him the trade say.
14On Thursday, French prosecutors said Lubitz, the co-pilot of Germanwings Flight 9525, "intentionally" crashed the jet into the side of a mountain in the French Alps.
15Members of the hometown flight club in Montabaur, where he renewed his glider licence only last year, said the 28-year-old appeared to be happy with his job at the airline, a low-cost carrier in the Lufthansa Group.
16A picture from Facebook of a man believed to be 28-year-old co-pilot Andreas Lubitz.
17"He was happy he had the job with Germanwings and he was doing well," longtime club member Peter Ruecker said.
18"He was very happy, he gave off a good feeling."
19Club chairman Klaus Radke said he rejected French prosecutors' conclusion that Lubitz put the Germanwings flight intentionally into a descent when the pilot had left the cockpit.
20At the house believed to be his parents', the curtains were drawn and four police cars were parked outside.
21Police kept the media away from the door of the Montabaur home.
22Neighbours refused to comment, and police told journalists to stay away.
23Lubitz learned to fly at the glider club in a sleek white ASK-21 two-seat glider, which sits in a small hangar today on the side of the facility's grass runway.
24Ruecker said that he remembers Lubitz as "rather quiet but friendly" when he first showed up at the club as a 14 or 15-year-old.
25According to the airline, he trained in Bremen before starting to fly for Germanwings in September 2013.
26Ruecker said Lubitz also trained in Phoenix, Arizona.
27Ruecker said Lubitz had a girlfriend but did not have many more details about his life.
28Lubitz's family could not immediately be reached, but a recently deleted Facebook page bearing Lubitz's name showed him as a smiling man posing in front of the Golden Gate Bridge in California.
