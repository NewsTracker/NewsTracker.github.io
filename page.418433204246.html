Greek debt crisis: Greeks muse on future path after Alexis Tsipras' abrupt reversal

1Greek restaurant workers say they feel betrayed by the government's proposed reforms to increase VAT by as much as the country's creditors' had demanded.
2Athens: Did Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras betray his principles and cave in to Greece's creditors?
3Or did he fight valiantly and succeed in establishing that his nation's debt load is not sustainable and has to be reduced?
4Those are the questions being asked across Greece in the wake of Mr Tsipras' abrupt decision to give the creditors nearly all of what they have sought throughout the long and contentious negotiations over keeping Greece afloat.
5Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras reacts during a parliament meeting in Athens, on Saturday.
6From the halls of Parliament to streets filled with people who have suffered through five years of severe economic hardship, Greeks were struggling to process the news, gird themselves for further budget cuts and assess what they had gotten for their efforts to stand against the European orthodoxy.
7Some Greeks said they felt betrayed by what they saw as a quick about-face.
8Hundreds of people marched in Athens on Friday night in a rally organised by the Communist Party to protest the reversal of the "no" vote in Sunday's referendum.
9A Twitter trend was formed under the hashtag #ExplainNoToTsipras, with some wondering what the point of the referendum was.
10"And this whole time I thought it was Merkel who was bluffing," one user wrote, referring to the expectation that Chancellor Angela Merkel of Germany would blink first in the showdown with Mr Tsipras.
11Anti-Euro protesters attend an anti-austerity rally in central Athens, Greece, on Friday.
12In Parliament, opposition parties took to the floor to excoriate Mr Tsipras' latest proposals as even worse than those Greeks rejected in the referendum.
13They chided him as naive for thinking that he would get a better deal from the creditors than the opposition parties had when they ran the country - though most opposition members also made it clear that they would approve his new package of proposals.
14"We have to vote yes," said Harry Theoharis, a member of the new To Potami centrist party.
15"It's a bad deal, but it's this deal or catastrophe."
16Evangelos Venizelos, former head of Greece's socialist party, even asked Mr Tsipras' finance minister whether he would, in effect, apologise for past statements suggesting that the previous government had been incompetent.
17Far-right Golden Dawn party lawmakers hold placards that read 'no' in Greek as party spokesman Christos Pappas speaks during a parliamentary session in Athenson Saturday.
18Early in the day, the government released a photograph of Mr Tsipras smiling and receiving a standing ovation after briefing members of his Syriza party on the state of his negotiations with European officials.
19But later, on the chamber floor, one Syriza member, Rachel Makri, took the microphone to criticise the new deal and announce that she would not vote for it.
20"Under no circumstances will I approve proposals that align with the lenders by 80 percent," she said.
21Ms Makri added that the proposals were what 60 percent of voters on Sunday had rejected.
22The mandate from the referendum, she said, would not allow reductions in pensions, one of the concessions made by Mr Tsipras.
23Even as they fumed, some unhappy Syriza members were expected to vote for the deal, which is likely to pass with support from many opposition parties.
24A lone protester stands on the steps in front of the Greek parliament as demonstrators march through Athens during an anti-austerity rally on Friday.
25The Tsipras proposal, which the Greek government submitted under a tight deadline before a weekend of meetings that could determine whether Greece remains in the euro currency union, is part of an effort to win approval of a new three-year bailout loan of 53.5 billion euros, or about $59 billion.
26Many Greeks said that despite Mr Tsipras' apparent reversal, his fight with European leaders had given Greece back a measure of dignity and forced its creditors to admit in public that debt relief was necessary for the country to prosper.
27That more hardship was on the way was not really a surprise, even though the country had voted overwhelmingly on Sunday to reject a deal with almost precisely the terms their government now appeared to be agreeing to.
28A Syrian immigrant waves Greek and Syrian flags during an anti-austerity protest in front of the parliament in central Athens on Friday.
29Ioannis Alexis, 44, owner of a small shop selling gum and cigarettes, was glued to the television, watching the debate in Parliament.
30Tears sprang to his eyes as he described how he had been forced to close his snack bar four years ago because of debts and open this smaller business, which was not doing well, either.
31Yet he did not for a second regret his "no" vote on Sunday and he guessed Mr Tsipras would get 60 percent of the vote if a new election were held now.
32"We will have new measures, which is not good," he said.
33But he said he was convinced that Mr Tsipras, by putting the earlier deal to a referendum, had forced the Europeans' hand and made leaders like Jean-Claude Juncker, president of the European Commission, soften their stances.
34"Before, it was Mr Juncker against the government," Mr Alexis said.
35"Now the people have spoken and it's completely different."
36Some said the biggest challenge now for the Greek people would be to remain patient and let the political and economic situation play out.
37Two friends, enjoying a beer together at a sidewalk cafe in central Athens after a moving job at the Cypriot Embassy, said they had both voted "no" in the referendum and thought the will of the Greek people had been heard but they were prepared to live with the new cutbacks Mr Tsipras had proposed.
38"I think the measures will be a little more harsh," said one of them, Ivan Ivanov, a Bulgarian immigrant who said he had been in Greece for decades.
39"But Germany can't write off 60 percent of the people."
