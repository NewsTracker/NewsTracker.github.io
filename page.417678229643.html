Remains of exchange students killed in Germanwings crash arrive home

1Students from the Joseph-Koenig-Gymnasium watch as hearses carrying the remains of 16 of their fellow students and two teachers who were killed in the Germanwings plane crash in March drive slowly past on Wednesday.
2Haltern am See, Germany: As their friends held white roses, a group of students returned home from a language exchange program that ended in tragedy when their Germanwings flight crashed in the French Alps more than two months ago.
3The German teens, their remains borne in white hearses, were among the first 44 of the 150 victims to be repatriated.
4Hundreds of people in this town, about 50 miles from Duesseldorf and known before the crash mostly for its blue lake and web of hiking trails and cycling paths, pressed along the road leading to the students' school Wednesday, awaiting the procession of hearses bearing the coffins.
5Hours before the cars arrived, a woman who would only give her name as Kerstin, "a fellow mama," had scrambled to organize several hundred white candles and roses that were later held high by townspeople and classmates in a silent tribute.
6A woman holds a white rose as the hearses transporting coffins with the bodies of victims pass.
7"It should look nice when the children finally come home," she said, as she begged bystanders to help keep the candles lit against the breeze.
8A Lufthansa cargo plane carrying the coffins of many of the German victims landed late on Tuesday at Duesseldorf Airport from Marseille, France.
9French investigators have spent the past 11 weeks sifting through evidence recovered from the mountainside where the co-pilot, Andreas Lubitz, is believed to have deliberately steered the Airbus A320, headed to Duesseldorf from Barcelona, Spain.
10Messages are posted on an airport wall in Duesseldorf, Germany to commemorate the victims of the March 24 Germanwings plane crash.
11The 16 high school students were among the first of the 72 German dead to be repatriated.
12Along with two teachers, they had been on their way home from an exchange with a partner school near Barcelona.
13All together, the 150 victims were from at least 18 different countries, with several holding two passports.
14"It is a difficult day because we are again intensively confronted with this terrible tragedy, but it is also a good day," said Bodo Klimpel, the town's mayor.
15"There is a certain relief that the families, after waiting so long, now have their children back and are able to lay them to rest."
16Heinz Joachim Schoettes, a spokesman for Germanwings, said that all the crew members, except Lubitz, were among those repatriated on Wednesday.
17Lubitz's family had arranged for his remains to be returned separately, he said.
18Hearses carrying coffins with remains of victims of the Germanwings flight 4U 9525 on highway A52 on their way to Haltern, Germany.
19After a brief ceremony at a hangar at the airport, when the families had time alone at the coffins, each marked with a photograph, they departed for their home cities.
20Most of the Haltern families took part in the organised procession, under police escort, that ended in front of the school, Joseph-Koenig-Gymnasium.
21Elmar Giemulla, a lawyer who is representing many of the victims' families, including those from Haltern, said the repatriation was an important psychological step.
22"For a lot of these people, they still have the last image in their mind of their children waving goodbye from the airport" on their way to Spain, Mr Giemulla said.
23"This is why getting back the remains is so important. Now they see the coffins and they know their children are inside. They are confronted with reality."
24Coffins of the victims are loaded onto a Lufthansa plane at Marseille airport France.
25After the final hearse rounded the corner beyond the school, sobs could be heard as parents comforted their children and small groups of teens supported one another in tightly wound hugs.
26Many brought their roses and placed them before a row of 18 saplings planted in memory of the classmates and teachers who died in the crash.
27A plaque bearing their names will be added to the memorial, said Ulrich Wessel, the school principal.
28Mr Klimpel said the city was planning a separate memorial at the town's main cemetery in the fall.
29In the meantime, some students have expressed their grief on a German memorial website.
30"You were such a great friend! You were always so happy, no matter how much the others annoyed you," wrote one person, who gave her name only as Melina, beside the image of a green candle, to her friend, Elena Bless.
31Annette and Martin Bless, the parents of Elena who died a day before her 16th birthday, have set up a foundation in their daughter's memory with the aim of helping other students take part in language exchanges.
32"According to Elena's wishes, the foundation shall support other pupils participating in school exchange programs and attending work placements abroad," the foundation's website says.
33Many of the Haltern victims were set to be buried this week, although some families rescheduled their funeral plans after Lufthansa, the parent company of Germanwings, told them that the repatriation would be delayed because of a bureaucratic error on some of the death certificates.
34The error was rectified in two days, however, allowing the initial repatriations to take place as originally planned.
35Lufthansa said the remains of the other victims would be returned for burial in the coming weeks.
