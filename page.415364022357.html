Ferguson city manager leaving in response to DOJ report

Utah lawmakers vote to become only state to allow firing squad

1The Justice Department on Wednesday cleared a white former Ferguson police officer in the fatal shooting of an unarmed black 18-year-old, but also issued a scathing report calling for sweeping changes in city law enforcement practices it called discriminatory and unconstitutional.
2The city manager of Ferguson, Missouri, has resigned in the wake of a U.S. Justice Department probe that found a range of systemic racially biased practices by the police and municipal court, the city said on Tuesday.
3Ferguson City Council voted unanimously at a meeting on Tuesday to accept the resignation of City Manager John Shaw effective immediately.
4The city manager is the highest-ranking, non-elected municipal official and oversees the police department.
5Shaw was among several individuals heavily criticized in the Justice Department report issued last Wednesday.
6Shaw had held the position in the St. Louis suburb since 2007.
7The Justice Department launched its investigation into Ferguson's police department and municipal court after the Aug. 9 shooting death of unarmed black teenager Michael Brown by a white police officer.
8The officer, Darren Wilson, was not charged by the DOJ in the shooting that triggered nationwide protests and widespread complaints of mistreatment of blacks by police, both at the time of the killing and the decision of a St. Louis grand jury not to charge Wilson.
9Shaw's resignation from the post that paid $120,000 a year came after Ferguson Municipal Judge Ronald Brockmeyer, also criticized in the Justice Department report, resigned on Monday.
10The Justice Department said it found that the mostly white police force in the mostly black community routinely targeted African-Americans for arrests and ticketing, in part to raise revenue for the city through fines and fees.
11The department also found officers had a pattern of using excessive force, illegally arresting people without cause, deploying attack dogs and tasers on unarmed people.
12The federal report said the city made it clear to the police chief and municipal judge that revenue generation must be a priority.
13Central to Shaw's resignation was his support for Brockmeyer, who was singled out in the DOJ report for his role in turning the courts into a money-making enterprise that heightened tensions among residents.
14The DOJ report, in one instance, showed that Shaw responded to an email from Police Chief Tom Jackson about a record-setting month for court revenue - nearly $180,000 in February 2011 alone - with the exclamation, "Wonderful!"
15And when Jackson told Shaw in January 2013 that municipal court revenue had exceeded $2 million the previous year, the city manager was similarly excited.
16"Awesome!" he said, according to the federal inquiry.
17But in a statement issued after the announcement of his departure, Shaw said his office "never instructed the police department to target African Americans, nor falsify charges to administer fines, nor heap abuses on the backs of the poor. Any inferences of that kind from the report are simply false."
18Some protesters, business leaders and state and federal officials have also called for the ouster of Police Chief Tom Jackson.
19"We have had all the eyes of the world on us in this tragedy," said Joe Reagan, chief executive of the St. Louis Regional Chamber and Growth Association.
20"We have to focus on restoring trust in the justice system. Leadership changes are needed."
21A news release from the city on Tuesday evening said the Council had reached a "mutual separation agreement" with Shaw, effective immediately.
22The statement also said a nationwide search for Shaw's replacement would begin right away.
23"I believe that the city of Ferguson has the resolve to overcome the challenges it faces in the coming months and emerge as a stronger community for it," Shaw, 39, said in a statement included with the release.
24Since Brown's death seven months ago, Mayor James Knowles, a part-time officeholder who earns less than $5,000 annually, has been the public face and voice of Ferguson's city government.
25Shaw was Ferguson's city manager for eight years.
26He had previously worked as city clerk and assistant to the city administrator in Shrewsbury, another town in St. Louis County.
27Shaw has not spoken publicly about the shooting, protests, grand jury inquiry or Justice Department investigation.
1The state's lawmakers voted to allow firing squads as a backup execution method.
2Utah s governor hasn t said whether he would sign bill allowing firing squads.
3Utah lawmakers approve firing squads if there is shortage of lethal drugs.
4Utah - the only state in the past 40 years to carry out a death sentence by firing squad - is poised to bring back the Old West-style executions if the state cannot track down drugs used in lethal injections.
5The Republican-controlled state Legislature gave final approval to the proposal Tuesday night, with lawmakers billing it as a backup plan as states struggle to find execution drugs amid a nationwide shortage.
6Utah Gov. Gary Herbert, a Republican, has declined to say if he'll approve or veto the bill, a decision that's not expected to come for a week or so.
7If he approves the measure, Utah would become the only state to allow an execution by firing squads if there's a drug shortage.
8The bill's sponsor, Republican Rep.
9Paul Ray of Clearfield, said it will give the state options.
10"We would love to get the lethal injection worked out so we can continue with that. But if not, now we have a backup plan," Ray told The Associated Press.
11Utah is one of several states to seek out new forms of capital punishment after a botched lethal injection in Oklahoma last year and one in Arizona that took nearly two hours for the condemned man to die.
12Legislation to allow firing squads has been introduced in Arkansas this year.
13In Wyoming, a measure to allow firing squads if the lethal drugs aren't available died.
14In Oklahoma, lawmakers are considering legislation that would allow the state to use nitrogen gas to execute inmates.
15In Utah, Ray has touted the measure as being a more humane form of execution.
16He argued that a team of trained marksmen is faster than the drawn-out deaths that have occurred in botched lethal injections.
17Opponents, however, say firing squads are a cruel holdover from the state's wild West days and will earn the state international condemnation.
18"I think Utah took a giant step backward," said Ralph Dellapiana, Director of Utahns for Alternatives to the Death Penalty.
19He called firing squads "a relic of a more barbaric past."
20Dellapiana said the legislature should be discussing whether, not how, to execute citizens.
21That same concern was raised by Salt Lake City Democrat Gene Davis, the only Senator to speak on the bill Tuesday night.
22Davis said he was voting against the bill because it "only puts another alternative on the table" instead of doing away with executions altogether.
23The state Senate did not debate the idea before passing the bill on an 18-10 vote, with four Republicans joining Democrats in opposition.
24Last month, the measure narrowly passed the state House of Representatives after lawmakers were called in from back hallways and informal meetings throughout the Capitol in order to break a tie vote.
25Anna Brower, a representative for the American Civil Liberties Union of Utah, said the legislation would make Utah "look backwards and backwoods."
26The organization is still holding out hope that Herbert will not sign the bill, she said.
27The governor's spokesman, Marty Carpenter, issued a statement Tuesday acknowledging that the method would give Utah a legitimate backup method if execution drugs are unavailable.
28Like Utah, states across the country have struggled to keep up their drug inventories as European manufacturers have refused to sell the lethal concoctions to prisons and corrections departments because of their opposition to the death penalty.
29Texas' supply will be used up if the state goes forward with two lethal injections in the next two weeks.
30The Texas deadline is the most imminent, but other states are struggling, as well.
31The head of Utah's prison system has said the state does not have any lethal injection drugs on hand and would have to obtain some in the years ahead if an execution were to be scheduled.
32States turning to alternative drugs have been faced with legal challenges from inmates, something Ray has said Utah could sidestep if it has a backup execution plan in place.
33Herbert's approval would reinstate the use of firing squads more than a decade after the conservative state abandoned the practice.
34Utah lawmakers stopped offering inmates the choice of a death by firing squad in 2004, saying the method created a media frenzy around murderers and took attention away from victims.
35A handful of inmates on Utah's death row were sentenced before the law changed and still have the option of going before a firing squad after their court appeals are exhausted in a few years.
36Utah's last execution was by a firing squad in 2010, when Ronnie Lee Gardner was executed by five police officers with .30-caliber Winchester rifles.
37The state has carried out three executions by firing squad since the U.S. Supreme Court reinstated the death penalty in 1976.
38The first U.S. inmate executed after that ruling was Gary Gilmore, who was in Utah killed by firing squad in early 1977.
39The Washington, D.C.-based Death Penalty Information Center, which opposes capital punishment, says a firing squad is not a foolproof execution method because the inmate could move or shooters could miss the heart, causing a slower, more painful death.
40One such case appears to have happened in Utah's territorial days back in 1879, when a firing squad missed Wallace Wilkerson's heart and it took him 27 minutes to die, according to newspaper accounts.
