Baku World of Mugham Festival celebrates traditional music from Azerbaijan

1One of Azerbaijan's many forms of folk music, Mugham is a traditional art form that combines classical poetry and musical improvisation, drawing on popular bard melodies, rhythms and performance techniques.
2The week-long event drew musicians from some 40 different countries.
3In 2003, Mugham was recognised as a Masterpiece of Oral and Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity by UNESCO.
4The festival's director, Firengiz Alizade, is a UNESCO Goodwill Ambassador.
5"This is our 4th edition, the festival takes place every two years and public interest for the event is growing. Musicians from all over the world get in touch with us to take part in the festival," she told euronews.
6One of the stars of this edition was Iranian traditional vocalist Alireza Ghorbani.
7Mugham, he explains, is an art form that transcends borders: "Mugham can be found throughout the whole region, from Azerbaijan, to Turkey and Iraq. Mugham is transmitted orally and we just pass on the lyrics from generation to generation."
8Together with Iranian composer Saman Samimi, Ghorbani recently published a new album 'Lost in Love', featuring lyrics by both classical and contemporary Persian poets: "This album is all about love and poetical dialogue with God," said Samimi.
9Mugham musicians begin their musical education at a very early age.
10Even before they learn to play traditional European instruments like the guitar or the violin, they have to learn the oud or the qanun, a middle eastern string instrument.
11Twenty-three-year old Azeri musician Natevan Hasenova doesn't even remember when she took her first qanun lesson: "I started playing this instrument when I was very young, much earlier than when I started playing the piano at the age of 7 or 8," she said.
12This year's festival paid special homage to famous jazz pianist Vaqif Musafazadeh on what would have been his 75th birthday with a special jazz concert.
13An acclaimed Mugham musician, Mustafazadeh was also a jazz pianist and composer, and is famous for merging the two musical forms in a pioneering movement that emerged in the late Sixties and Seventies in Baku.
14As the Mugham Festival came to an end, musicians from all walks of life took to the stage for a jam session in a joyful celebration of the marriage between Mugham and jazz.
15The next edition of the International World of Mugham Festival is scheduled in Baku in 2017.

1The internal documents obtained by the Associated Press in Geneva reveal that WHO's Geneva headquarters was receiving emails by mid-April 2014 from staffers on the ground in Guinea calling for help with an epidemic that had already killed 100 people but was recognised to be largely hidden and spreading.
2One of the emails was from an experienced Ebola expert with WHO's Africa office, who wrote to a Geneva official saying the situation had taken a critical turn because many health workers at the Donka hospital in Guinea's capital, Conakry, had been exposed to the virus.
3"What we see is the tip of an iceberg," wrote Jean-Bosco Ndihokubwayo.
4The scientist requested the help of half a dozen veteran outbreak responders, writing in all capitals in the email's subject line: "WE NEED SUPPORT."
5WHO official Stella Chungong said she was very worried, warning in an email that terrified health workers might abandon Donka Hospital and that new Ebola cases were coming out of nowhere.
6"We need a drastic ... change [of] course if we hope to control this outbreak," she said.
7WHO sent a top Ebola expert, Pierre Formenty, to the region.
8But many of the other managers sent to Conakry "had no idea how to manage an Ebola epidemic," according to Marc Poncin, who was mission chief in Guinea for Medecins Sans Frontieres, the volunteer doctors who bore the brunt of the epidemic until after WHO declared a global public health emergency in early August.
9That, together with the publicity around the infection of two American health workers who were repatriated for treatment, brought the US, UK and other countries together in the fight against the disease.
10But in early April, WHO was downplaying concerns.
11Spokesman Gregory Hartl told reporters that "this outbreak isn't different from previous outbreaks".
12In a Twitter message sent by Hartl and preserved by ITV News, he is shown asking: "You want to disrupt the economic life of a country, a region, [because] of 130 suspect and confirmed cases?"
13The news worsened throughout April.
14Formenty said teams in Conakry had seen patients pop up all over the city with no known links to other cases.
15"This means there is one part of the epidemic that is hidden," he later wrote in an internal report.
16"The Ebola outbreak could restart at any time."
17In early June, there were discussions at WHO over whether to call a global health emergency.
18An internal document says such a declaration "ramps up political pressure in the countries affected" and "mobilises foreign aid and action."
19But one director viewed it as a "last resort".
20WHO was having to contend with other outbreaks, including polio, which has a high political priority.
21There were also issues with the government of Guinea, which, according to WHO documents, was reporting only confirmed Ebola cases and not those suspected or probable, in a bid to downplay the dangers and avoid alarming foreigners working in the mining industry.
22Dr Sylvie Briand, head of the pandemic and epidemic diseases department at WHO, acknowledged that her agency made wrong decisions, but said postponing the alert made sense at the time because it could have had catastrophic economic consequences.
23"What I've seen in general is that for developing countries, it's sort of a death warrant you're signing," she told AP.
24On 10 June, Briand, her boss, Dr Keiji Fukuda, and others sent a memo to WHO chief Dr Margaret Chan, noting that cases might soon pop up in Mali, Ivory Coast and Guinea-Bissau.
25But the memo went on to say that declaring an international emergency or even convening an emergency committee to discuss the issue "could be seen as a hostile act".
26But others argue that although declaring an international emergency is no guarantee of ending an outbreak, it functions as a kind of a global distress call.
27"It's important because it gives a clear signal that nobody can ignore the epidemic any more," said Dr Joanne Liu, MSF's international president.
28In a meeting at WHO headquarters on 30 July, Liu said she told Chan: "You have the legitimacy and the authority to label it an emergency ... You need to step up to the plate."
29After WHO declared an international emergency on 8 August, Barack Obama sent 3,000 troops to west Africa and promised to build more than a dozen 100-bed field hospitals.
30Britain and France also pledged to build Ebola clinics, China sent a 59-person lab team, and Cuba sent more than 400 health workers.
31Dr Bruce Aylward, WHO's top Ebola official, maintains however that labelling the Ebola outbreak a global emergency would have been no magic bullet.
32"What you would expect is the whole world wakes up and goes: 'Oh my gosh, this is a terrible problem, we have to deploy additional people and send money,'" he said.
