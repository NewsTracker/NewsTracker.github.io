In Benjamin Netanyahu's coming speech to Congress, a message for Israel

North Korea fires two missiles toward Sea of Japan, Seoul reports

1The setting will be a joint meeting of the U.S. Congress, but Benjamin Netanyahu's most crucial audience may be the one back home: the Israeli electorate.
2The Israeli prime minister's planned speech on Tuesday, two weeks before Israel's general elections, is dominating the country's political discourse, eclipsing other campaign issues.
3Because Netanyahu openly defied the White House by accepting a Republican invitation to address Congress and will use the appearance to lobby against Obama administration efforts to reach a nuclear accord with Iran, there have been weeks of hand-wringing about damage to Israel's relationship with the United States.
4But the Israeli leader has embraced the controversy, which has kept him and his security-heavy agenda in the public eye, overshadowing voter discontent over other hot-button questions such as the country's soaring cost of living.
5Netanyahu's backers have worked hard to portray him as a prescient and courageous figure, likening his warnings on Iran to those of Winston Churchill on encroaching Nazism.
6His office released video of him in pensive, statesmanlike mode, writing out his speech in longhand.
7On the eve of his departure for Washington, the prime minister, whose knack for high drama is acknowledged even by critics, employed a powerful piece of imagery: praying at the Western Wall, hands pressed to the ancient stones as reporters looked on.
8With Judaism's most sacred prayer site as a backdrop, Netanyahu reiterated the message he has been delivering for weeks: that the dangers of a nuclear Iran trump what his campaign has characterized as concern over diplomatic niceties.
9"I respect President Barack Obama; I believe in the strength of the relationship between Israel and the U.S., and in their strength to overcome differences of opinion," he said.
10"As prime minister of Israel, it is my obligation to see to the security of Israel."
11Even as Netanyahu professed respect for the U.S. president, however, his camp put out a slick new video that pointedly compares his position to that of an earlier prime minister, Levi Eshkol, who in 1967 defied U.S. objections and ordered attacks on Arab forces menacing Israel.
12The surprise strikes are generally regarded by military historians as having prevented the young state from being overwhelmed by the Jordanian, Egyptian and Syrian militaries.
13"In 1967, Arab armies threatened Israel's existence," the Hebrew-language narration intones, with English subtitles lest anyone miss the point.
14"Levi Eshkol faced an existential decision: Should he launch a preemptive strike? The White House strongly objected.... Would we still be here today if Levi Eshkol had not done the right thing?"
15Secretary of State John F. Kerry sought Sunday to tamp down the controversy, saying the administration does not want to see Netanyahu's visit turned into a "political football."
16Kerry said he speaks regularly to Netanyahu, including a phone call Saturday, and he said Israel is safer today because of the 2013 interim agreement with Tehran that saw Iran freeze or scale back most of its nuclear development.
17"We want to recognize the main goal here is to prevent Iran from getting a nuclear weapon," Kerry said on ABC's "This Week."
18As the prime minister boarded a flight to Washington on Sunday, critics in Israel continued to excoriate his judgment, saying the trip amounted to little more than political grandstanding.
19"Netanyahu's speech is a dangerous mistake," Isaac Herzog, a co-leader of the opposition Zionist Union, told Israel Radio.
20Tzipi Livni, a senior opposition figure running alongside Herzog, was quoted by the Ynet website as saying the prime minister was "destroying relations with the U.S. for the sake of a few ballots."
21Others took a last opportunity to urge the prime minister to cancel the speech.
22A group representing 180 former senior military and security officials held a news conference in Tel Aviv on Sunday to publicly call on him to reconsider.
23One of them was once Netanyahu's army commander.
24"I taught Bibi how to reach the target," said retired Gen. Amiram Levine, using Netanyahu's nickname.
25Still unknown, of course, is how this will all play out at the polls.
26Although Netanyahu is doing all he can to keep the spotlight on the Iranian threat, a weekend poll on Israel's Channel 10 said 56% of voters described the cost of living and social-welfare issues as their greatest concerns.
27Security issues were a distant second, cited by 27%.
28Last month, the state comptroller published a highly anticipated and damning report on the country's housing crisis, which took aim at Netanyahu's government, as well as previous ones, for missteps that had triggered skyrocketing housing costs.
29In a response that was widely mocked on social media, the prime minister said the issue was an important one, "but when we speak about housing prices and the cost of living, I do not forget for a moment the matter of life itself. And the biggest challenge we face & is the threat of Iran obtaining nuclear weapons."
30That led to dozens of Internet memes with the phrase "Don't forget life itself," in which wedding congratulations and university exams, among other things, are appended with mock concern about the Iranian nuclear program.
31One showed the late "Star Trek" actor Leonard Nimoy offering his trademark Vulcan salute and admonishing: "Live the long life itself & and remember the Iranian threat."
32As the flap has dragged on, Netanyahu's camp appears to have concluded that the U.S. president is so unpopular in Israel that his evident displeasure is an electoral boost.
33The newspaper Haaretz quoted an anonymous Netanyahu strategist as saying, "Obama is our best campaigner."
34Other reports suggested that the prime minister's Likud bloc believes the speech controversy will deliver two additional seats in the 120-seat parliament, or Knesset, and the race is close enough that such a margin matters.
35In departing remarks Sunday, Netanyahu referred to the upcoming Jewish holiday of Purim, which commemorates the biblical Esther acting as envoy to a powerful king on behalf of the Jews.
36"I am leaving for Washington on a fateful, even historic mission," he said.
37"I feel I am the emissary of all Israelis, even those who disagree with me - of the entire Jewish people."
38At least one Jewish member of Congress, Sen. Dianne Feinstein (D-Calif.)
39, found that language troubling.
40Feinstein said Sunday that she would attend the talk, but that it was "arrogant" for Netanyahu to assert that he speaks for all Jews.
41"He doesn't speak for me on this," Feinstein said on CNN's "State of the Union."
42"The Jewish community is like any other community. There are different points of view."
43Several Jewish lawmakers have said they will not attend Tuesday's address.
44House Speaker John A. Boehner (R-Ohio), speaking on CBS' "Face the Nation," stood by his decision to invite Netanyahu without consulting the White House and said interest in tickets to attend the speech has been robust.
45"Everybody wants to be there," Boehner said.
46"The United States Congress wants to hear from him, and so do the American people."
1The firing came hours before the U.S.-South Korean military exercises were scheduled to begin, drills which the secretive North denounces as a preparation for war.
2The missiles hit the sea early on Monday morning after traveling for about 490 km (305 miles), according to South Korea's Defense Ministry.
3Ministry spokesman Kim Min-seok said North Korea fired the missiles without designating any no-sail zones, which was regarded as a provocation.
4"If North Korea takes provocative actions, our military will react firmly and strongly so North Korea will regret it in its bones," Kim told a news briefing.
5Pyongyang has escalated its rhetoric against the drills, with a spokesman for its army general staff saying Washington and Seoul "should be dealt with only by merciless strikes".
6Japan quickly lodged a protest with North Korea over the latest missile launches, saying they posed a serious threat to safety at sea and in the sky.
7"The ballistic missile launches by North Korea are extremely problematic conduct in terms of aviation and navigation safety," Chief Cabinet Secretary Yoshihide Suga told a news conference.
8"We swiftly lodged a stern protest with North Korea."
9Speaking to reporters, Foreign Minister Fumio Kishida denounced Pyongyang's move as infringing on U.N. Security Council resolutions that ban it from any launch using ballistic missile technology.
10It also runs counter to accords at the six-party talks aimed at ending the North's nuclear ambitions, Kishida said.
11Japan needs to tread a fine line between conveying its condemnation to Pyongyang while not derailing bilateral talks aimed at resolving the issue of Japanese citizens abducted by North Korean agents decades ago.
12In July, Japan eased some sanctions on North Korea in return for the North reopening its investigation into the fate of Japanese abductees.
13Little progress has been made so far.
14The annual joint exercises between South Korea and the U.S. always trigger a surge in military tensions and warlike rhetoric on the divided peninsula, and analysts saw the North's missile tests as a prelude to a concerted campaign of saber-rattling.
15"And if there is a particularly sharp escalation, we could see the North orchestrating some kind of clash on the maritime border," said Jeung Young-tae, an analyst at the Korea Institute for National Unification in Seoul.
16North Korea frequently tests short-range missiles off its coast as part of military drills.
17The launches this time came with a stern warning from the nuclear-armed North Korean People's Army that this year's military drills would bring the peninsula "toward the brink of war."
18North Korea has carried out three nuclear tests - in 2006, 2009 and 2013.
19In January, the North offered a moratorium on further tests if this year's joint drills were cancelled - a proposal rejected by Washington as an "implicit threat" to carry out a fourth atomic detonation.
20Analyst Jeung said Pyongyang was unlikely to conduct a fourth test just to protest against the exercises.
21"Nuclear tests carry more significance than that," he said, noting that the North's testing schedule was primarily driven by technical development.
22"On the other hand, there is the chance of a mid- or long-range missile test," Jeung said.
23"I would say that a demonstration that it could deliver a nuclear warhead would be more threatening to the world than an actual nuclear test," he added.
24Although its nuclear program remains shrouded in uncertainty, Pyongyang is currently believed to have a stockpile of some 10 to 16 nuclear weapons fashioned from either plutonium or weapons-grade uranium.
25A new research report by U.S. experts published this week estimated that North Korea could be on track to have an arsenal of 100 nuclear weapons by 2020.
