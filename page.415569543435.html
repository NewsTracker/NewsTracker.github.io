Cyclone survivors desperate for aid

1With power, phones and Internet still down across much of the country, officials faced logistical headaches in sorting out where to send supplies, particularly in Tanna Island, which was hit hard by Cyclone Pam's 168mph winds.
2Tanna's roads remained blocked by debris, forcing aid workers to hike across the island to inspect damage to schools and other buildings, UNICEF said.
3Though the death toll from the disaster stood at 11, officials and relief teams were growing increasingly concerned about islanders' long-term survival, with food and water scarce in the worst-hit areas and access to some of the archipelago's more remote islands remaining difficult.
4More than 100 residents on Tanna, including the elderly and a baby, have been sheltering in a store since Friday.
5With no relief supplies yet to reach them, they have taken to eating fruit they find on the ground and drinking water from a creek to survive.
6Planes have been carrying food, water and medical supplies to Tanna and neighbouring Erromango Island for two days.
7And a boat stocked with canned goods, biscuits and water was expected to head to Tanna today or tomorrow, but distributing it to the island's villages remains difficult.
8UNICEF estimates that nearly 5,000 people across Vanuatu have no access to drinking water.
9The problem is especially concerning on Tanna, which suffers from water shortages in the best of times.
10Tanna's water towers were knocked over by the storm and wells built close to the shore were contaminated, said Sebastian Rhodes Stampa, disaster coordinator for the UN's humanitarian affairs office.
11Several more flights carrying water and water purification tablets were heading to the island today, and officials were hoping to set up water purification units, he said.
12Lack of food was also a rising worry for those who survived the storm, which demolished gardens and fruit trees that locals depend on to survive.
13"Everyone in Tanna and other islands in the south, they really live subsistence lives, so they grow what they need for a short period," said Tom Perry, spokesman for CARE Australia.
14"And the reality is that much of that would have been washed away by this storm. That's a grave concern because we desperately need to get food to people soon."
15Thousands of people remained homeless, with more than 3,300 still sheltering in evacuation centres on the main island of Efate and in the provinces of Torba and Penama, according to the UN.
16Though officials were still conducting damage assessments and had yet to reach many of the outer islands, the nation appeared to have avoided mass casualties.
17Many locals rode out the cyclone in larger buildings such as schools and churches - a practice that relief groups have impressed upon Vanuatuans as a life-saving measure during storms.
18Vanuatu is frequently battered by cyclones in the southern hemisphere's summer months and lies along the Pacific "Ring of Fire," where earthquakes and volcanic activities are common.
19Most communities have buildings designated as evacuation centres.
20"A lot of people did evacuate," said Hanna Butler, an aid worker with the Red Cross in Vanuatu.
