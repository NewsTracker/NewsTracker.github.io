Mexico's deportations of Central American migrants up 79 percent in first 4 months of 2015

1FILE - In this Aug. 27, 2014, file photo, an immigration official checks a bus for Central American migrants, at a roadblock north of Arriaga, Chiapas state, Mexico.
2According to data from Mexico's National Immigration Institute, Mexico has deported 79 percent more Central Americans in the first four months of 2015 than it did during the same period a year earlier.
3MEXICO CITY - Mexico deported 79 percent more people from Central America's northern triangle in the first four months of 2015 than it did during the same period a year earlier, according to government statistics.
4Data from Mexico's National Immigration Institute say that 51,565 immigrants from Guatemala, Honduras and El Salvador were deported between January and April, up from 28,736, during that period in 2014.
5Deportation of Guatemalans rose 124 percent, followed by Salvadorans at 79 percent and Hondurans at 40 percent.
6The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights on Wednesday expressed its "concern over stepped-up actions reportedly being taken against migrant persons" that were put in place after Mexico initiated its Southern Border Plan last year under pressure from the United States.
7In 2014, more than 46,000 unaccompanied minors from Central America crossed into the United States, leading the U.S. government to turn to the governments in Mexico and Central America to try to stanch the flow.
8Mexico responded with an initiative that included sending 5,000 federal police gendarmes to Chiapas, a Mexican state bordering Guatemala.
9More border checkpoints were opened, raids on migrants increased and authorities focused on keeping migrants off the northbound freight train known as "the Beast."
10On Thursday, Adam Isacson, head of regional security at the Washington Office on Latin America, a human rights organization, said in a statement that the wave of immigration seen from Central America in 2014 continues.
11"Enormous numbers of Central Americans are still fleeing, but most of them are now getting caught in Mexico instead of the United States," he said.
12The countries the migrants are returned to remain extremely dangerous.
13Honduras and El Salvador have the highest per-capita homicide rates in the world.
14El Salvador is averaging 20 killings a day as the conflict between gangs, police and soldiers intensifies.
15The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights said it was also worried about reports of attacks on migrant advocates in southern Mexico.
16Migrant activist Ruben Figueroa in Tenosique, a town in Mexico's state of Tabasco, said police are waging a violent campaign against migrants.
17"Masked officers with rifles run operations on the train to keep (migrants) off and to remove migrants from the train," Figueroa said.
18"They set up checkpoints on the highways, above all in the southern states of Tabasco, Oaxaca, Chiapas and Veracruz. They enter hotels in the areas where migrants take shelter waiting for rides."
19Figueroa said the flow of migrants has not decreased because conditions in their countries have not improved.
20He said Mexico's crackdown is only exposing migrants to greater risks of human trafficking, extortion, assault and other crimes.
21"Every day there are more people who walk, every time more exposed," he said.
22"Women with children walk hundreds of kilometers at night in big groups."
23In Guatemala, Ursula Roldan, migration coordinator at Rafael Landivar University in Guatemala City, also raised concerns about the crackdown leading migrants to take bigger risks.
