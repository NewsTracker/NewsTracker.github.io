Islamic extremists threaten more attacks after massacre

1GARISSA, Kenya -- Somalia's Islamic extremist group al-Shabaab on Saturday warned of more attacks in Kenya like the assault on Garissa University College in which 148 people were killed.
2"Kenyan cities will run red with blood," said al-Shabaab according to the SITE intelligence monitoring group.
3The Islamic militants said the attack on Garissa college was in retaliation for killings carried out by Kenyan troops fighting the rebels in Somalia.
4"No amount of precaution or safety measures will be able to guarantee your safety, thwart another attack or prevent another bloodbath," said the al-Shabaab statement.
5Daisy Achieng repeatedly heard the attackers single out Christians in Thursday's attack.
6"They were being asked a few questions from the Quran," she told CBS News' Debora Patta.
7"If you get it correct, you were spared. If you get it wrong, you were shot on the spot."
8CBS News found Achieng at a makeshift shelter set up for the shell-shocked victims of the siege.
9At one point, she said she heard a phone ring.
10It was the mother of one of the students taken hostage.
11"He took the phone from her and told the lady that 'I'm going to kill your child right now, so know it's the last time you're going to hear her voice, so hear me kill her,' and I heard a gunshot," Achieng said.
12Five people have been arrested on suspicion of involvement in the Garissa attack, a Kenyan official said.
13Kenyan security agencies arrested three people trying to cross into Somalia, said Interior Ministry spokesman Mwenda Njoka in a Twitter post.
14He said the three are associates of Mohamed Mohamud, also known as Dulyadin Gamadhere, a former teacher at a Kenyan Madrassa Islamic school who authorities say coordinated the Garissa attack.
15Kenyan authorities have put a $220,000 bounty for information leading to Gamadhere's arrest.
16Two other suspects were arrested at Garissa college.
17A survivor of the killings at Garissa University College was found on Saturday, two days after the attack by Islamic extremists killed 148 people.
18Cynthia Cheroitich, 19, told The Associated Press from her hospital gurney that she hid in a wardrobe and covered herself with clothes, refusing to emerge even when some of her classmates came out of hiding at the demands of the gunmen from the al-Shabaab group.
19She was rescued shortly before 10 a.m., according to Kenyan officials.
20Cheroitich said she didn't believe that rescuers urging her to come out of her hiding place were there to help, suspecting at first that they were militants.
21"How do I know that you are the Kenyan police?" she said she asked them.
22Only when Kenyan security forces had one of her teachers appeal to her did she come out, she said.
23"I was just praying to my God," Cheroitich, a Christian, said of her ordeal.
24Cheroitich appeared tired and thirsty, sipping on yoghurt and a soft drink, but otherwise seemed in good health.
25She said she drank a body lotion because she was so thirsty and hungry while in hiding.
26Authorities displayed the bodies of the alleged attackers before about 2,000 people in a large open area in central Garissa.
27The bodies lay on the bed of a pickup truck that drove slowly past the crowd, which broke into a run in pursuit.
28Soldiers monitored the crowd.
29There was shouting and clouds of dust rose as the vehicle left the area.
30Kenyan authorities initially said the attackers had been strapped with explosives that went off like bombs when they were shot, but investigators later said there were no suicide vests.
31The four bodies shown Saturday had wounds but were intact.
32Spectator Yusuf Mohamed applauded the display, saying authorities wanted to "win the hearts of the people" and clear any doubts that the attackers had been killed.
33The bodies of many of those killed in Garissa have been transported to the capital, Nairobi, where grieving Kenyans gathered to view the remains of family members.
