Madrid find may be Cervantes' remains

1Cervantes, often lauded as having written the first modern novel, died in 1616 after requesting burial in a convent in Madrid where, for almost a year, investigators have been searching the subsoil for bones that they now believe to include some of the author's.
2"Everything coincides to lead us to believe that Cervantes is there," a forensics expert, Francisco Etxeberria, said at a news conference.
3Separating and identifying his badly damaged bones from other fragments will be difficult, researchers said.
4Almudena Garcia-Rubio, an archaeologist, said that there was "no confirmed genetic identification," although DNA tests were being performed.
5During their excavations, the investigators found decayed wood from a coffin with the letters M and C marked out in tacks.
6Cervantes' given name was Miguel.
7The discovery of the coffin was initially announced in January.
8The whereabouts of Cervantes' tomb had been a mystery since the Convent of the Barefoot Trinitarians was rebuilt in the late 17th century.
9Etxeberria said the fragments had been "discovered in the ground of the crypt of the current church," Agence France-Presse reported.
10"His end was that of a poor man. A war veteran with his battle wounds," said Pedro Corral, head of art and tourism for Madrid's city council.
11The team of 30 investigators used infrared cameras, 3-D scanners and radar to identify 33 alcoves where bones could be stored, AFP said.
12Inside one of niches found against a far wall, archaeologists discovered a number of adult bones matching a group of people with whom Cervantes had been buried, before their tombs were disturbed and moved into the crypt.
13"The remains are in a bad state of conservation and do not allow us to do an individual identification," said Garcia-Rubio said.
14Cervantes is most often remembered for "Don Quixote," which gave the world the word quixotic in acknowledgment of its central character and his adventures, including his joust with windmills.
15The expression "tilting at windmills" also denotes people who take on imaginary adversaries.
