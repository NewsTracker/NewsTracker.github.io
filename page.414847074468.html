Fierce crackdown on dissent no laughing matter in Egypt

Journalist's resignation after HSBC leaks makes waves

1Gags like this are in short supply in Egypt.
2Humor was one of the only channels for protest under successive Egyptian autocrats accused of mismanagement, repression and corruption.
3But Sisi's tightening grip on power is leaving little space for critics, comics or cartoonists.
4Thousands of government opponents are languishing in prison, a new law effectively bans protests and poking fun at the government is not remotely funny for the authorities.
5"Whoever opposes Sisi is seen as someone who opposes the nation, so the political joke has little place in society and people reject it," said comedian Mahmoud al-Ghoul.
6"Political jokes are not dead but they are frozen."
7At a cafe near Tahrir Square, epicenter of the 2011 revolt that led to the downfall of old leader Hosni Mubarak, a group of comedians discuss the glum state of their business.
8Ghoul says that post-2011, he had high hopes that he would one day widely sell his paperback, which takes a critical look at the nature of Egyptian leaders.
9But "The President is a Donkey" is a tough sell in modern-day Egypt, where both state-run and private media have rallied behind Sisi and portray his opponents as traitors.
10Only a handful of shops are willing to display the book even though it does not name names.
11Comedians who once saw themselves as agents of change have realized the joke is on them.
12By comparison with the current climate, comedians describe the rule of the previous president -- Egypt's first freely-elected leader Mohamed Mursi of the Muslim Brotherhood -- as the golden age of humor.
13Police and intelligence services that thrived under Mubarak and were opposed to Mursi did not interfere, meaning comic writers found a wide reception for their work in newspapers.
14"The joke solved political, social and economic problems and jokes helped get rid of Mursi," said cartoonist Al Salam al-Qawsi.
15But the laughter did not last and the rise of Sisi, who as army chief toppled Mursi following mass protests against his rule in 2013, coincided with growing fear of economic collapse and the sort of violence that has torn apart Iraq and Syria.
16"Now people are scared. They are obsessed with the need for stability," he said.
17Egyptians are famous across the Arab world for their humor.
18Egyptian comedy movies have cracked up living rooms from Baghdad to Beirut for decades.
19Jokes helped Egyptians grapple with poverty, poor sanitation and slums where many families live on top of each other.
20But Egyptians rarely poke fun at Sisi, who gazes out inscrutably behind dark glasses from omni-present portraits hanging in offices, shops, homes and lifts across the country.
21That might be partly because Sisi has delivered a degree of stability and raised hopes that several mega-projects, such as a second Suez Canal, will deliver jobs and repair the economy.
22It is also because no one wants to be branded a traitor, not at a time when the army is battling Islamist militants in the Sinai peninsula, or when the airforce is bombing Libya following the mass beheading of Egyptian laborers by the Islamic State.
23"There used to be guys churning out one-liners here," said a man at the run-down Metropole coffee shop in downtown Cairo as dispassionate customers puffed on water pipes.
24"No one is willing to joke anymore."
25The fact that Egypt's most famous satirist was forced off the air last year when his show was canceled because of the oppressive political climate sums up the situation neatly.
26Bassem Youssef repeatedly poked fun at Mursi and became known abroad as Egypt's very own Jon Stewart -- the renown American political satirist.
27Despite a massive following at home -- including almost four million followers on Twitter -- he has decided to move to the United States to become a resident fellow at Harvard's Institute of Politics at the John F. Kennedy School of Government.
28"We are living in the most glorious years of democracy in Egypt, and may the tongue of the person who does not agree with that be cut off," he said last June after he was yanked off the airwaves, deploying the biting sarcasm he was famous for.
29Reflecting the parlous state of freedom of speech in Egypt, the country came in 159th out of 180 countries in the 2014 Reporters Without Borders World Press Freedom index.
30Other comics try to fill the void -- but on social media such as Twitter or on Facebook, rather than mainstream media, limiting their reach in a country where the Internet is largely the preserve of city elites.
31Video blogger Ahmed Anwar said he received a six-month jail sentence in January 2014 for posting a facetious item on YouTube, mocking the Interior Ministry.
32"Jokes are present on social media. Television shows borrow comedy from social media. The younger generations tell jokes, but the older people don't like to challenge the leaders because they supported them," said Anwar.
33Aside from the cautious mood among Egyptians, some comedians say they find it hard to get material when it comes to Sisi, who served as chief of military intelligence under Mubarak.
34"Sisi rarely makes mistakes that we can jump on," said comic writer Mohamed Ashour, 32, who like others had a field day under the veteran Mubarak, who was known for blunders.
35"Sisi is a cautious military man. He watches every step."
36With new material hard to come across, old jokes are simply being recycled -- such as this one from the time of President Gamal Abdel Nasser in the 1960s, which show that the problems of yesterday still resonate today.
1Chief political commentator Peter Oborne announced his resignation from the Daily Telegraph on Tuesday in a searing attack on the newspaper published online.
2Oborne accused the broadsheet of suppressing negative stories about banking giant HSBC to keep the valuable advertiser happy in comments widely shared on social media as a defence of journalistic freedom.
3In the wake of his claims, The Guardian, which published the leaks, alleged on Wednesday that HSBC had put its advertising with the newspaper "on pause".
4HSBC declined to comment on the accusations.
5In a 3,000-word article describing waves of redundancies and falling standards during his five years at the Daily Telegraph, Oborne said the separation between the editorial and advertising departments had "collapsed".
6"After a lot of agony I have come to the conclusion that I have a duty to make all this public," Oborne wrote on website openDemocracy.
7"The Telegraph's recent coverage of HSBC amounts to a form of fraud on its readers."
8He repeated his claims in an interview with BBC radio's Today programme, saying: "There has been a series of stories which look very much as if poor editorial judgement has been exercised and they tend to affect significant advertisers."
9Oborne wrote that he had informed the Telegraph leadership of his resignation in December due to concerns that financial interests were influencing coverage.
10He said he had changed his mind about leaving quietly because of the newspaper's February coverage of allegations that HSBC helped wealthy clients evade tax, a story that made headlines around the world last week.
11"You needed a microscope to find the Telegraph coverage," Oborne wrote.
12In response, the Telegraph issued a statement denying the accusations.
13"The distinction between advertising and our award-winning editorial operation has always been fundamental to our business. We utterly refute any allegation to the contrary," the statement read.
14"It is a matter of huge regret that Peter Oborne, for nearly five years a contributor to the Telegraph, should have launched such an astonishing and unfounded attack, full of inaccuracy and innuendo, on his own paper."
