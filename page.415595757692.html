Plans for leisurely visit in Tunisia turn to horror

1Tunisia's deadliest terror attack in more than a decade jolted locals out of their daily routines and took the lives of visitors to this democracy-minded North African country of 11 million, according to Associated Press interviews with witnesses, victims and a police official.
2Wednesday's attack at Tunis' National Bardo Museum, claimed by the Islamic State group that has sought to spread its bloodthirsty influence beyond its base in Syria and Iraq, drew immediate condemnation from many world leaders and promises from Tunisian officials and citizens that they won't bow to terror.
3Miami resident Giovana Gonzalez had just finished a brief bathroom break at the end of a museum tour when the attackers "started shooting everybody."
4"Everyone ran in different directions," Gonzalez told reporters after disembarking from her cruise ship Friday in Barcelona.
5Gonzalez, 46, and her husband were on a cruise to celebrate their 25th wedding anniversary.
6For Mohamed Ali, an unemployed 42-year-old father of four who lives in the Bardo district, the episode upended a family stroll to the zoo.
7Looking in through a high fence around the museum grounds next to parliament, he saw men pull automatic weapons out of bags and calmly walk inside, opening fire on tourists either inside a bus or emerging from it.
8"There were police on one side of the bus that they set upon, and the terrorists were hiding on the other side," he told The Associated Press in an interview in the popular La Gazelle cafe across the street a day after the attack.
9"There was an exchange of fire, and the gunmen took refuge in the museum."
10Tunisian President Beji Caid Essebsi said counterterrorism forces arrived within 10 minutes and prevented the carnage from being far bloodier.
11"Their intervention stopped the terrorists from setting off their explosive belts, which would have caused more than 100 deaths if they had detonated," he said Friday in a televised address.
12A police captain, speaking to the AP, recalled how at 12:17 p.m. he heard the rat-a-tat of repeated shots and rushed to the museum entrance, where a woman lay injured on the stone-tile floor and two other people were strewn on an adjacent lawn.
13Inside, many museumgoers had little or no place to hide.
14Some tucked behind thin columns, or cowered on the floor.
15One French visitor with a bandaged knee, speaking afterward to France 2 Television, said she'd only survived because a person in front of her took a bullet to the head.
16Giovana and Hector Gonzalez hid behind pillars in the labyrinth-like museum for three to four hours, cringing at the sound of gunfire.
17"When we had the feeling that the bullets were coming closer, we tried to hide ourselves in different places and we were running from one place to another," she said.
18"We changed our position a lot."
19The 50-year-old police captain, in a police ball cap and three shiny stars on the breastplate of his bulletproof vest, said at least one assailant made his way to the roof.
20Authorities said they were looking for at least two or three accomplices of the two gunmen who were killed, and later made several arrests.
21The captain and a colleague fired upward to give cover to a police car that hurriedly took away a visitor who had been shot in the hip.
22Collecting himself with a cigarette as he recounted the attack, the officer's eyes welled up when he thought of helping four injured visitors into emergency vehicles, leaving blood on his hands.
23The officer said he couldn't aim his gun well because his main concern was protecting the injured.
24He declined to provide his name, insisting he was doing his duty and did not want to be publicly associated with the incident.
25Police cars and ambulances rushed in, and a helicopter soared overhead.
26Special forces " with their armored vehicle crunching two track-like welts into the floor tiles " were next.
27Aside from the attackers, the only other Tunisian killed was a special forces officer that the captain said had recently become a father.
28By varying accounts, the end of the siege lasted no more than an hour or so, and special forces gunned down the attackers inside.
29Scores of tourists " some parents carting small children " scurried up the steps of a nearby building to take shelter.
30But Gonzalez, who had become separated from her husband, didn't know if the armed men dressed in plainclothes were police or hostage-takers as she ran with them, crouching to take cover behind cars along the way.
31"I thought to myself we weren't going to get out of this," Gonzalez said.
32"I didn't think we were going to live so I called my daughter to say goodbye to her."
33One Spanish couple, Juan Carlos Sanchez and Cristina Rubio, had holed up in a small room in the museum, where they had hid after he spotted a fleeing man, and spent the night there because they thought the gunmen might still be around.
34Rubio, who is pregnant, was uninjured and told Tunisia's health minister at a hospital maternity ward that tests showed that the baby girl on the way was OK.
35Seventeen of the 20 foreigners who were killed had been cruise-ship passengers.
36Health Ministry officials said Japanese, French, Italian, British, Polish, Spanish, Australian, Colombian and Belgian citizens were among the dead, and several remained unidentified.
37It was the deadliest terror attack in Tunisia since an al-Qaida militant set off a truck bomb outside a historic synagogue on the resort island of Djerba in 2002, killing 21 people " mostly German tourists.
38Tunis resident Mohamed Aziz Smiri said he saw the evacuation from the museum, and heard police assurances that the situation was under control.
39He noted that extremists had generally attacked security forces in Tunisia's rural west near Algeria in recent years.
40"Every time they kill Tunisian security forces, and now tourists," he said.
41"It's haram (a sin), they came as guests to our country. I didn't want that for them, it makes me feel sorry, we're sad ... What did they do to us?"
42Gonzalez didn't learn until she was back on the cruise ship that seven of the 40 people on her tour excursion bus had died and seven were injured.
