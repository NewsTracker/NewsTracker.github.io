Euro falls near $1, but European goods remain pricey

1NEW YORK (AP) " Americans hunting for a bargain on a new Beemer, bottle of Chateau Margaux or Hermes handbag thanks to the sliding euro should put away their gold cards. European luxury goods sold in the U.S. still carry luxurious price tags.
2The European currency has fallen 10 percent against the dollar this year.
3In theory, car dealerships, department stores and other companies that sell goods from Europe could pass on the savings to their American customers, said Ira Kalish, chief global economist at Deloitte, the consulting and accounting firm.
4"But what would be the point of doing that?"
5Kalish asked.
6Demand for anything made in Europe is so strong that they have little trouble moving merchandise.
7"From their perspective, it's better to leave the price unchanged and pocket the profit."
8The euro has been in a long tailspin.
9Last April, it was trading just shy of $1.40.
10Since then, it sank as low as $1.04 in March before bouncing back to $1.10 on Monday.
11In other words, you used to have to pay $1.40 to buy a single euro; today, you pay just $1.10.
12So where are the bargains?
13There's no reason to think that prices for Italian shoes and French red wines will fall along with the euro, analysts said.
14Part of the reason is that most European consumer products sold in the U.S. aren't aimed at most consumers.
15Armani, Hermes, and Prada cater to the affluent, selling well-made products as well as the perception of prestige and status.
16They have an image to maintain and slashing prices isn't part of it, Kalish said.
17Loyal customers might consider it, well, gauche.
18"If a luxury product becomes really cheap, they might think, 'Why am I buying it, then?'" Kalish said.
19"The high price makes it attractive for some people."
20Bill Earle, president of the National Association of Beverage Importers, said customers shouldn't expect to see cheaper prices for French and Italian wines anytime soon.
21Part of the explanation, Earle said, is that U.S. importers pay well in advance for wines that often take years to age.
22With Brunello di Montalcino from Italy, for instance, the wine sits in an oak barrel for about four years before it's ready.
23"One way to look at it is, the earliest you're going to see cheaper Brunello is in 2019," Earle said.
24But even then, there's no guarantee that businesses will pass on savings to customers.
25Earle said a bottle of wine has to pass through layers of businesses before it shows up on the shelves " importers, distributors and retailers " and each business has its own costs.
26Because currency markets can be volatile, businesses are slow to cut prices because any savings could quickly vanish with a sudden swing in currency trading.
27Doug Bell, a wine buyer for Whole Foods Market, also said he doesn't think people will see a significant fall in prices for European wines.
28Any drop would start showing up with the 2015 vintage, and even then, he said, other factors, such as bad weather, could easily offset a currency move.
29The only way people might benefit from cheaper European wine, Earle said, is if they're "bringing it over on an airplane."
30Similarly, anyone looking to score a Chanel handbag on the cheap is in for a letdown.
31Chanel's classic handbag carried a $4,900 price tag last year, according to Robert Burke and Associates, a luxury consulting firm.
32That's up from $2,250 in 2007.
33Chanel is reportedly tweaking prices in other parts of the globe while leaving them alone at its U.S. stores.
34Robert Burke said he expects other luxury retailers to follow Chanel's cue.
35Demand remains so solid for these products in the U.S. that high-end retailers have no reason to pare prices.
36It would only tarnish their elite image.
37Nate Herman, vice president of international trade for the American Apparel & Footwear Association, said higher manufacturing costs give these companies another reason to keep prices high.
38Over the past decade, luxury brands such as Louis Vuitton and Prada shifted some of their production to Asia, as manufacturers in the region improved their ability to craft high-end goods.
39As a result, the euro's fall against Asian currencies has driven up their costs.
40It's the same story with German luxury cars.
41Americans with a taste for fancy rides are already able to afford them, so there's no need for Mercedes-Benz, BMW or Audi to pass along the benefit of a falling euro to customers by slashing prices.
42Demand for these German cars remains solid.
43Audi's U.S. sales climbed 15 percent last year, while Mercedes's sales rose nearly 10 percent and BMW's 6.5 percent.
44The exception might be Volkswagen, whose U.S. sales sank 3 percent last year.
45The company behind the Beetle, Golf and Touareg could use the weak euro to cut prices and lure more buyers.
