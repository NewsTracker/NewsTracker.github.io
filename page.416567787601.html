Prosecutor: 2 exams found James Holmes to be sane

1CENTENNIAL, Colo.
2(AP) - A prosecutor declared Monday that two psychiatric exams found Colorado theater gunman James Holmes to be sane as he meticulously plotted a mass murder, considering a bomb or biological warfare before settling on a shooting so that he could inflict more "collateral damage."
3"Meticulous" was the word Holmes used twice during the exams, District Attorney George Brauchler said, marking the start of a long-awaited, lengthy and emotionally wrenching trial to determine if he'll be executed, spend his life in prison, or be committed to an institution as criminally insane.
4"Boom!"
5Brauchler said repeatedly as he showed pictures of the victims and the weapons that killed them on a screen, describing in detail how bullets pierced their organs.
6Colorado theater shooter James Holmes, far left, sits at the defense table at the opening of his trial in Centennial, Colo., Monday, April 27, 2015.
7The trial will determine if he'll be executed, spend his life in prison, or be committed to an institution as criminally insane.
8"I shot at people trying to get away. I shot at people trying to leave the theater ... to make sure others wouldn't follow," Holmes later told a psychiatrist, the prosecutor said.
9The former neuroscience student has pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity to murdering 12 people and wounding 70 at a midnight "Batman" premiere nearly three years ago.
10Many more people would have died, but a magazine on his AR-15 assault rifle jammed, leaving 218 bullets unfired, Brauchler said.
11Jurors must eventually decide whether he was unable to know right from wrong because of a mental illness or defect when he slipped into the theater, unleashed tear gas and marched up and down the aisles, firing at people who tried to flee.
12"Through this door is horror. Through this door are bullets, blood, brains and bodies. Through this door, one guy who thought as if he had lost his career, lost his love life, lost his purpose, came to execute a plan," said Brauchler, standing before a scale model of the theater.
13"Four-hundred people came into a boxlike theater to be entertained, and one person came to slaughter them," the prosecutor said.
14Holmes is accused of 166 counts of first-degree murder, attempted murder and an explosives offense for the mayhem he caused in suburban Denver on July 20, 2012.
15It remains one of America's deadliest shootings, and that Holmes was the lone gunman has never been in doubt.
16He was arrested at the scene, along with an arsenal of weapons on his body and in his car.
17Holmes sat quietly as Brauchler described his emotional rise and fall, interspersing facts about the defendant's life with poignant details about his victims.
18He said the once-promising doctoral candidate told his ex-girlfriend that he had an "evil" plan "to kill people" but that she dismissed what he said as "theoretical."
19Told by school authorities to find a new career, he instead remained in suburban Denver and fumed, Brauchler said.
20For weeks thereafter, while turning away offers of help from his parents, friends and a therapist on campus, Holmes bought guns, protective gear and a detonation system to blow up his apartment with, along with earphones to blast techno music so that he wouldn't hear the screams of his victims, Brauchler said.
21Dying his long hair a clown-like red "has nothing at all to do with the movie or being the joker," he said.
22Holmes did that because he wanted to stand out and "be remembered."
23"By the time he gets into that theater ... there is not a millimeter of flesh that is not covered by armor or some protective material," the prosecutor said.
24"He was wearing a kill suit."
25Defense lawyers say Holmes was in the grips of a psychotic episode and could not tell right from wrong when he went on the rampage.
26His parents, Robert and Arlene Holmes, in pleading for his life, have called their son a "human being gripped by a severe mental illness."
27Under Colorado law, the burden falls on the state to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that he was "NOT insane," Judge Carlos A. Samour Jr., told the jury.
28And that depends in part on "a culpable state of mind:" If Holmes acted with deliberation and intent - willfully taking actions that he knew would kill people -- then even if he had mental problems, he should be found guilty of murder, the judge said.
29Prosecutors allege that Holmes planned the violence for months, buying a rifle, a shotgun, two pistols, tear gas canisters, body armor, thousands of rounds of ammunition and a chemical stockpile that turned his 800-square-foot apartment into a booby trap that might have caused a conflagration.
30"He tried to murder a theater full of people to make himself feel better and because he thought it would increase his self-worth," Brauchler said.
31"I would like to focus on the victims," he said, but instead he must prove that Holmes was not insane.
32The state has already spent millions seeking that verdict, managing an outsized number of victims, witnesses and more than 85,000 pages of evidence.
33Nearly three years passed hundreds of motions were filed in legal debates over capital punishment and insanity pleas.
34Insanity defenses are successful in only 25 percent of felony trials nationally, even less so in homicides.
35"Lay people tend to think of people with mental illness as extremely dangerous, and that also influences jurors, especially if someone has killed someone," said Christopher Slobogin, who teaches law and psychiatry at Vanderbilt Law School.
36"Usually there's evidence of intent and planning that seems to be counterintuitive to the lay view of mental illness."
37Winning a trial on mental-health grounds is rare, but then again, so are jury trials for mass shooters.
38Most are killed by police, kill themselves or plead guilty.
39A review of 160 mass shootings found killers went to trial 74 times, and just three were found insane, according to Grant Duwe, a Minnesota corrections official who wrote the book "Mass Murder in the United States: A History."
40Just one has won a mental-health case in the last two decades, Duwe said: Michael Hayes, who shot nine people, killing four, in North Carolina in 1988.
41Based on that, Holmes "faces some pretty long odds," he said.
42Brauchler began laying out how the once-promising doctoral candidate in neuroscience plotted and planned for months, amassing guns, ammunition, tear gas grenades and enough chemicals to turn his dingy apartment into a potentially lethal booby trap that could have caused even more carnage.
43Holmes was arrested almost immediately, while stripping off his body armor in the parking lot outside the Century 16 movie theater where he replaced Hollywood violence with real human carnage.
44His victims included two active-duty servicemen, a single mom, a man celebrating his 27th birthday, and an aspiring broadcaster who had survived a mall shooting in Toronto.
45Several died shielding friends or loved ones.
46At 6 years old, the youngest to die was Veronica Moser-Sullivan.
47Her mother, Ashley Moser, was left paralyzed and lost her unborn child.
