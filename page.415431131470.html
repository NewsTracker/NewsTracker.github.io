7The conflict that erupted in Ukraine last year between government troops and Russian-backed separatists has claimed at least 6,000 lives and displaced nearly 1.8 million people.
8The United Nations Children's Fund estimates that 1.7 million children on both sides of the front line have been harmed through lack of proper shelter, nutrition, medicine or schooling.
9Children struggle to understand what is going on around them, and why.
10Fighting has abated drastically since a new cease-fire came into effect last month, but the suffering, loneliness and terror remain.
11In the government-held town of Popasna, 70 kilometers (45 miles) north of the children's home, only a few people walk along deserted streets between apartments gutted by rocket fire, a grim contrast to the days when the town bustled with 30,000 people.
12One of those destroyed homes belongs to Tatyana Belash, who has now taken shelter in a basement with her 3-year old daughter, Zlata.
13As the adults talk politics, Zlata, a shy girl with blonde pigtails, darts around the basement cluttered with battered mattresses.
14Asked about the shelling, Zlata shies away and seeks comfort in stroking her cat.
15"When we first came here, she kept saying: 'Let's go home! "
16Belash says.
17"I couldn't explain to her that we couldn't go home because there was fighting going on."
18Children in the Ukraine-controlled village of Chermalyk play war and scuttle in and out of the craters made by falling Grad rockets.
19As 11-year old Tolik Tokar shimmies into one crater, his head disappears from view.
20Then he raises his head and pretends to shoot at the baddies: the separatists.
21The boys take aim with make-believe guns fashioned from sticks - but Tokar has seen shooting first-hand, not just in play.
22"When shelling was raging, we went to check it out, and then they opened fire on me," Tokar says, stammering as he tells his tale.
23"The bullets tore through the cloth on my shoulder here and flew past."
24A few dozen kilometers away on the rebel side, children play the same games - but with roles reversed.
25There, Ukrainian soldiers are the bogeymen, with "Nazi" one of the favorite slurs.
26The 22 children under Nikulenko's tutelage, in Khartsyzk, are some of the most vulnerable anywhere in the war-wracked region, and she asks that they be identified only by their first names.
27Before fighting began, the home served as a shelter for children rescued from the streets, or seeking respite from dysfunctional families.
28More have been abandoned in recent months.
29Veronika, a freckled and gap-toothed 6-year-old, smiles and pulls down on her red-checked dress as she recalls life before the war - the visits to the amusement park and zoo, her mother's home cooking.
30She even has fond memories of her father, who returned home after a stint in prison for slashing her mother's shoulder with a knife.
31Since then, her father enlisted with the rebel army and her mother left her at the children's home.
32Veronika relies on her own inner strength to ward off the terror brought on by war.
33"When they were doing boom-boom, it was so scary," she says, recalling a recent bout of shelling.
34"Once when they were shooting at night, I fell off the bed."
35Veronika's mother visits Khartsyzk from time to time.
36The young girl hopes to go home when summer comes.
37Nikulenko says children in her care have come under the spell of Russian television.
38They love watching programs that cast the rebels as valiant heroes of a popular uprising.
39Ukrainian government troops are treated as vicious occupiers.
40"It's very dangerous, this black-and-white perception," Nikulenko says.
41"These children get information only from one side. They see that (government troops) shoot at us and that their fathers and brothers take arms and go to protect us."
42As a group of girls huddles on a carpet in the games room, Yulya, a tall and unsmiling 12-year old, stands to one side.
43Before the war began, Yulya lived with her grandparents in Rusko-Orlivka, a village that changed hands several times as fighting raged last summer for the nearby town of Ilovaysk.
44Yulya's grandfather told her that when the rebel fighters captured Rusko-Orlivka, fighters found nine Ukrainian soldiers hiding out in a farm, marched them out to a forest and shot them.
45Yulya says she felt little pity.
46"I understand they are people too, but they kill other people," she says in a whisper.
47"I know that, because my grandfather told me so."
48For the very young, little is truly understood about who is fighting or why.
49Drawing his tank in the children's home, Seryozha, in a moment of confusion, gets it into his head to decorate it with a blue-and-yellow Ukrainian flag.
50"It was born in Ukraine. I was born in Ukraine, too," he says.
51Above the Ukrainian flag that sticks out from the side, he then draws the black, blue and red flag of the separatists.
52Seryozha's own past is a blur even to those around him.
53A scar on his back shows where he was shot with an air-powered pistol before the war.
54Nobody quite knows what happened, beyond that his parents died of tuberculosis, leaving his sister and two brothers orphaned.
55A bureaucratic oversight separated Seryozha from his siblings.
56As they were evacuated by Ukrainian authorities to a neighboring region, nobody remembered that Seryozha was lying in a hospital recovering from tuberculosis.
57He landed in the Khartsyzk home in January.
58Children may have a hazy understanding of the events around them, but forgiveness appears to come more easily than to the adults.
59Could Seryozha ever be friends with children from the other side?

2WASHINGTON (AP) - Unscrupulous tax preparers are using President Barack Obama's health care law as a ploy to pocket bogus fines from unsuspecting taxpayers, including some immigrants not bound by the law's requirements, the IRS warned Friday.
3In an advisory, the tax agency said consumers can be sure something is wrong when a tax preparer says they collect the health law fines that may be due the government.
4The law requires virtually everybody in the country to have coverage or risk fines.
5"The payment should never be made directly to an individual or return preparer," the IRS said.
6"Most people don't owe the (fine) at all because they have health coverage or qualify for a coverage exemption."
7Sometimes the con artists promise to lower the purported fine if the consumer pays them directly.
8The IRS said it has received reports from around the country that fraudsters are targeting immigrants, particularly Spanish speakers with a limited understanding of English.
9In some cases, people who don't owe a fine because they have Medicaid or some other form of health insurance have been told they need to pay anyway, because supposedly they don't have the right kind of coverage.
10In other cases, immigrants who are not bound by the law's coverage requirements are being told they must pay a fine.
11Young immigrants protected from deportation by the Obama administration fall into a legal gray area that may be prime territory for exploitation.
12While they do have authorization to work and therefore pay taxes, they are not considered "lawfully present" for purposes of the health care law.
13They are not entitled to coverage under the law, and the mandate to get coverage does not apply to them.
14The health care law uses the income tax system to subsidize coverage and also to collect fines from people who remain uninsured.
15This is the first tax-filing season that the connections between health insurance and income taxes are becoming visible to average consumers.
16Two of the most complicated areas for consumers - taxes and health care - are now intertwined.
