Tunisia's neglected youth find their voice in hip hop, rap

1In this photo taken Jan.10, 2015, a break dancer performs in Sousse, Tunisia.
2Tunisian youth are getting out their message of rage about life on the fringes in post-revolution Tunisia through a perhaps surprising channel: hip hop.
3In this photo taken Jan.10, 2015, female break dancer Safa Meftahi, right, dances to a beat mixed by DJ Gamra (background) at a youth-organized hip hop conference in Sousse, Tunisia.
4In this photo taken on April 9, 2014 break dancer BBoy Chtak, from the poor suburb of Bardo in Tunis, takes the stage solo at a hip hop festival organized in Hammamet, Tunisia.
5TUNIS, Tunisia (AP) - On the roof of a concrete building in an impoverished Tunis neighborhood, hip hop beats pound out from a PC hooked up to cheap speakers.
6Under graffiti-daubed cloth, young men in sweatpants and baseball caps breakdance, popping and locking robotically to the rhythm thumping around them.
7Rappers from local hip hop group Zone 5 snarl back and forth lines they've just written about police, poverty and smoking pot.
8Zone 5 rapper Mohamed Ayari and other Tunisian youth are getting out their message of rage about life on the fringes in post-revolution Tunisia through a perhaps surprising channel: hip hop.
9"You see what the system does? We write a graffiti message up on the wall and they call it 'provocation' and the police come after us. But why do they call it provocation?" the 23-year-old said during a break in rehearsals for an upcoming show.
10"It's because we're pointing out their faults, their weaknesses. No one wants to hear about their weaknesses."
11Since overthrowing its long-ruling dictator in 2011, Tunisia has had a string of elections and is being hailed as "the success story" of the region.
12But the new men in charge look very much like the old ones, with an 88-year-old president and ministers that all cut their teeth in previous administrations.
13Despite spearheading the revolution, Tunisia's youth are still feeling sidelined, and one of the few ways they are getting their voices heard is through rap - shouting to anyone who will listen that all is not well in Tunisia.
14An attack on the national museum on March 18 by two young Tunisians from working-class neighborhoods that killed 22 people, mostly tourists, has once more sounded the alarm about the future of young people in the country.
15Tunisia's parliamentary elections last fall saw reasonably high voting rates.
16But the youth turnout was abysmal, with over 80 percent of Tunisians between 18 and 25 boycotting the ballot.
17Unemployment, already high at 15.5 percent, soars to 42.3 percent for young people, according to Eurostat figures from 2011.
18The most sinister indication of youth disillusion with the system: the 3,000 Tunisians, nearly all in their 20s, who the Interior Ministry says have left to fight with the Islamic State in Syria, Iraq and lately, Libya.
19Nakasaki Dali, a member of Zone 5, said in his neighborhood, youths either become rappers or take refuge in ultra-conservative Islam.
20Just down the hill from Tunis' seat of government, where young people rallied for change four years ago, is the rundown neighborhood of rapper Ahmed Ben Ahmed, known as Klay BBJ.
21His lyrics of resistance and rage are hummed by kids walking through streets choked with mopeds and garbage.
22In his raps - which jump nimbly between literary Arabic and Tunisian street dialect - Ben Ahmed talks about the issues that concern young people the most: police oppression, the lack of jobs and being scapegoated by the wealthy for the country's ills.
23It is the police - called "the ruler" in his neighborhood - who bear the brunt of his raps.
24Police under the country's former dictator were reviled in Tunisia as the oppressive arm of a corrupt system - a role activists and urban youth say they continue to play in poorer neighborhoods.
25The movement to use rap to protest against the system started during the dictatorship, largely as a reaction to police brutality.
26Around the corner from Ben Ahmed's house lives 16-year-old Zied Sellimi, who said he was recently picked up with a group of young people after one of them insulted a police officer.
27"I have two brothers who are always in jail," said Sellimi, adding that his father is dead and mother has no work.
28"It was only a month ago that I myself was taken into the police station."
29There, he said, he and his friends were slapped around and insulted.
30Not far away, Adel Bouhamdi works under the light of a single bare bulb in a closet-sized shop repairing mopeds.
31On the sidewalk lay two half-assembled scooters.
32"Rent's gotten expensive, you know. And these days the cops won't leave me alone, always coming and demanding I show them the papers I have for the shop," he said, gesturing at his grim surroundings with disgust.
33"There's no money to make from a shop like this."
34International human rights groups say police abuses include arbitrary detentions, beatings and torture, overcrowded prisons and the denial of due process.
35"I blast the police because they're the stick that the system beats us with," said Ben Ahmed, sitting in his mother's home in front of a half-built computer blasting music, as he peered out from under a baseball hat with "Chicago" splayed across the front.
36"Here, even with a lawyer, you get beaten by the cops and mistreated. If you've got money or know people inside the administration, you don't get hit."
37Ben Ahmed has had his own brushes with police.
38He was arrested in August 2013 after he performed the anti-police rap song "Boulicia Kleb" (The Police are Dogs) with fellow rapper Weld El 15 at a concert in the resort town of Hammamet.
39While Weld El 15 went into hiding after the initial arrest, Ben Ahmed was jailed for several weeks for insulting police.
40He was released only when authorities felt the pressure of a campaign for his liberation.
41In the minds of older Tunisians, however, the police are seen as the guardians of order after the years of post-revolution turmoil.
42The support of most voters for veteran politician Beji Caid Essebsi as president was clearly motivated by a desire for stability.
43The one thing all Tunisians can agree on is the perilous economic situation, with annual inflation of 5.5 percent and few jobs.
44Once again, it has been worst for young people, who exist on the margins of an economic system with few opportunities.
45In his 2012 song "Work, Freedom, National Dignity," Ben Ahmed highlighted how the youth are frustrated in their search for jobs and respect from authorities.
46The song's video is a grim depiction of riot police attacking protesters with dogs, as a chained-up Ben Ahmed raps feverishly in front of a list of political parties.
47So far, the solution hasn't come for the young people through elections.
48The choice is between Islamists and nationalists who generally have similar policies that have never served the urban poor.
49Teenagers like Sellimi see little hope in the country's politics, especially with the election of an octogenarian as president.
50"Beji won't help us, I've seen him on TV three times already and he hasn't said anything about the youth," he said.
51"We need a president that takes from the rich, not steals from the poor."
