Greek debt crisis: Prices for everyday items shoot up in Greece

1Sunday working and pension cuts: Greece prepares to reopen banks but its people are divided over the terms of an aid package.
2Athens: Greeks, many already struggling with the cost of living, will on Monday begin to pay substantially more for everyday items.
3Prices have been pushed up by increases in value-added tax, one of the conditions imposed on the country by its creditors before talks began on Greece's third bailout in five years.
4The taxes have pushed up the prices of a long list of items, including fresh and frozen meat, fish, coffee, tea, juices, eggs, sugar, cocoa, rice, flour, dairy products such as ice cream and yoghurt, fertiliser, condoms and toilet paper and even funerals.
5Shopping in Athens before the prices shoot up on Monday.
6The tax increases, from 13 to 23 per cent, will also apply to products and services such as processed foods and drinks and dishes served in restaurants and bars.
7A sharp increase in consumer taxes on many Greek islands is also set to take effect on Monday.
8The tax increases are forecast to bring in $US867 million ($1.1billion) by year-end, Greek financial media said.
9A customer, right, buys fresh fish from a vendor at Varvakeios fish and meat market in Athens last week.
10Parliament approved the increases on Thursday as part of a package of austerity and reform measures Greece's international creditors required before talks would begin on a new, three-year bailout package the country needs to stave off bankruptcy.
11Once the new austerity program was approved, the European Central Bank agreed to allow more emergency funding to flow to Greek banks, which were nearly out of cash.
12As a result, they will reopen Monday after being closed for three weeks.
13Transactions, however, will still be limited.
14Customers will not be able to withdraw money at bank counters but must do so from ATMs as in the previous three weeks, according to a government decree.
15Value-added tax increases will push up the cost of living in Greece from Monday.
16Account holders will be allowed to withdraw up to 420 per week ($616).
17For the past three weeks, depositors have been allowed to take out 60 ($88) a day from ATMs.
18Restrictions on transfers abroad and other capital controls remain in place because the banks' financial health remains critical.
19Transfers of money to other countries are allowed only with permission from the Greek central bank and the finance ministry.
20Greek credit cards will be able to be used abroad again, but only for purchases and payments of bills, not for cash withdrawals.
21Eurozone finance ministers also provided Greece with a $US7.6 billion bridge loan so it could meet a $US3.8 billion loan repayment due on Monday to the European Central Bank and limp through the next few weeks until the new bailout is approved.
