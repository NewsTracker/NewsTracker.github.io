Suruc suicide bomber identified as Turk with possible Islamic State ties

1Hundreds protest a deadly bombing in Turkey by Islamic State militants that left more than 30 people dead.
2Istanbul: The suicide bomber who killed at least 32 people at a cultural centre in southeast Turkey has been identified as a Turkish citizen who was believed to have had ties to the Islamic State militant group, a senior government official said Wednesday.
3The bomber, Seyh Abdurrahman Alagoz, a 20-year-old university student who had recently returned from Syria, was identified through DNA testing, according to reports in the Turkish news media.
4"The investigation is ongoing, but we have evidence that the suspect was linked to Daesh," the official said, speaking on a condition of anonymity in line with government protocol, using an Arabic acronym for the Islamic State.
5The government was criticised after the attack, and access to Twitter was temporarily blocked on Wednesday after a court imposed a ban on the circulation of images and videos from Monday's suicide bombing in the town of Suruc.
6The service was restored a few hours later, after Twitter took down the images.
7The bomber struck the Amara Cultural Centre in Suruc as a group of young activists gathered in the garden to discuss rebuilding the predominantly Kurdish town of Kobane, just across the nearby border with Syria.
8Kobane was recently the scene of fierce fighting between the Islamic State and Kurdish fighters, and the militants lost control of the town in January.
9Some members of the opposition in Turkey have accused government leaders of failing to take sufficient measures to curb the rise of the Islamic State in the country.
10Demonstrators took to the streets of Istanbul on Monday, with some accusing President Recep Tayyip Erdogan of collaborating with the Islamic State.
11Others charged that the focus of the governing Justice and Development Party, or AKP, on toppling President Bashar Assad of Syria facilitated the growth of Islamist militants in the region.
12Prime Minister Ahmet Davutoglu vehemently denied the accusations and vowed to increase security measures along the 800-km border between Syria and Turkey.
13"Turkey and the AKP government have never had any direct or indirect links with any terrorist group and have never showed tolerance to any terrorist group."
14Mr Davutoglu said after visiting people wounded in the Suruc attack on Tuesday.
15He warned that Turkish security could be compromised by a spillover from the war in Syria.
16Separately, two police officers were shot and killed in their homes in the border town of Ceylanpinar on Wednesday, the district governor said.
17Responsibility for the attack was claimed by a military organisation affiliated with the Kurdistan Workers' Party, or PKK, a Kurdish separatist group that is in a fragile peace process with the Turkish government after a 30-year conflict that claimed over 40,000 lives.
18The group, the People's Defence Forces., said on its website that the attack was in retaliation for "the massacre in Suruc".
19It also accused the police of cooperating with jihadis.
