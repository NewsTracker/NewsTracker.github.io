Cyclone Pam devastates island nation of Vanuatu [Photos] [Video]

1The United Nations was preparing a major relief operation and Australia said it was ready to offer its neighbour whatever help it could.
2With winds up to 340 kph, Cyclone Pam left Vanuatu cut off, with little power, poor communications and a looming threat of hunger and thirst.
3Unconfirmed reports said the number of dead could run into dozens but aid workers said it would be days or weeks before the full impact was known.
4"It felt like the world was going to end," Alice Clements, a spokeswoman for the UN Children's Fund (UNICEF), said from Vanuatu.
5"It's like a bomb has gone off in the centre of the town. There is no power. There is no water."
6Tom Skirrow, country director for the Save the Children aid group, told Reuters that Vanuatu's National Disaster Management Office had confirmed eight dead and 20 injured.
7He said he expected those figures to rise substantially.
8Aid workers in Papua New Guinea said at least one person had been killed by the storm there.
9Satellite photographs showed the storm covering virtually all of Vanuatu, a sprawling country of 83 islands and 260,000 people 2,000 km (1,250 miles) northeast of the Australian city of Brisbane.
10The president of Vanuatu, Baldwin Lonsdale, told a disaster risk conference in Japan he had no confirmed report of the impact of the storm but he appealed to the world to "give a lending hand".
11Australian Foreign Minister Julie Bishop said Canberra would be willing to offer Vanuatu whatever help it could.
12Formerly known as the New Hebrides, Vanuatu was jointly ruled by France and Britain until independence in 1980.
13It is among the world's poorest countries and highly prone to natural disasters, such as earthquakes, tsunamis and storms.
14Witnesses described sea surges of up to 26 feet and flooding throughout the capital, Port Vila, after the category 5 cyclone hit late on Friday.
15Aid officials said the storm could be unprecedented in the island's history and one of the worst natural disasters the Pacific region has ever experienced.
16They said the storm was comparable in strength to Typhoon Haiyan, which hit the Philippines in 2013 and killed more than 6,000 people.
17Chloe Morrison a spokeswoman for the World Vision aid group said the storm had been terrifying.
18"Trees are across the roads. Some of them are piled up so you can barely see over them," she said.
19"There are reports that there have been casualties across all of the islands.
20"This is going to need a long and sustained response. People in Vanuatu are subsistence farmers. They grow food for their own consumption. Crops will be absolutely wiped out from this."
21Outlying islands may take weeks to reach, aid officials said, while a lack of clean water and widespread damage to crops meant the situation could deteriorate sharply in coming days.
22There were no reports of looting but Skirrow described men whose homes had been destroyed walking the streets of Port Vila with machetes and families huddling without shelter after their flimsy homes of thatch were torn away by the wind and rain.
23Many residents were in evacuation centres, he said, but the authorities were ill prepared.
24"These people are homeless now. These people are going to be there for probably six weeks," Skirrow said.
25As darkness fell on Saturday, the storm was moving off to the south but the wind was still strong.
26U.N. relief workers were gearing up for a rapid response on Sunday, with members drawn from as far away as Europe.
27However, with the airport closed and high wind still blowing it was not clear how they could reach Vanuatu.
28"We fear the worst."
