1The White House is mulling ways to undercut Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's upcoming trip to Washington and blunt his message that a potential nuclear deal with Iran is bad for Israel and the world in what has become a nasty grudge match.
2Administration officials have discarded the idea of President Barack Obama himself giving an Iran-related address to rebut the two speeches Netanyahu is to deliver during his early March visit.
3But options remain on the table.
4The administration has already ruled out meetings between Netanyahu and Obama, saying it would be inappropriate for the two to meet so close to Israel's March 17 elections.
5However, the White House is now doubling down on a cold-shoulder strategy, including dispatching cabinet members out of the country and sending a lower-ranking official than normal to represent the administration at the annual policy conference of the American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC), the officials said.
6Vice President Joe Biden will be away, his absence behind Netanyahu conspicuous in coverage of the speech to Congress.
7Other options were described by officials, who spoke only on condition of anonymity because they were not authorized to discuss internal deliberations.
8Netanyahu's plan for a March 3 address to a joint meeting of Congress has further strained already tense ties between the United States and Israel.
9Congressional Republicans orchestrated Netanyahu's visit without consulting the White House or State Department, a move the Obama administration blasted as a break in diplomatic protocol.
10Some Democratic lawmakers say they will boycott the speech.
11U.S. officials believe Netanyahu's trip to Washington is aimed primarily at derailing a nuclear deal with Iran, Obama's signature foreign policy objective.
12While Netanyahu has long been skeptical of the negotiations, his opposition has increased over what he sees as Obama's willingness to make concessions he says would leave Iran on the brink of being able to build a nuclear weapon.
13His opposition has intensified as negotiations go into overdrive with an end-of-March deadline for a framework deal.
14"I think this is a bad agreement that is dangerous for the state of Israel, and not just for it," Netanyahu said Thursday.
15The difference of opinion over the deal has become unusually rancorous.
16The White House and State Department have both publicly accused Israeli officials of leaking "cherry-picked" details of the negotiations to try to discredit the administration.
17And, in extraordinary admissions this week, the administration acknowledged the U.S. is withholding sensitive details of the talks from Israel, its main Middle East ally, to prevent such leaks.
18The rebukes have only emboldened the leader of Israel.
19He has a double-barrelled attack on the Iran talks ready for when he arrives in Washington.
20Besides adressing Congress, he will also deliver similar remarks at the AIPAC conference, an event to which administrations past and present have traditionally sent top foreign policy officials - but maybe not this year.
21An AIPAC official said Friday that the group has not yet received any reply to its invitation for senior administration figures to attend the meeting that starts March 1.
22The official stressed that last-minute RSVPs are not unusual, but the White House has been signalling for some time that a cabinet-level guest may not coming.
23Instead, the administration is toying with the idea of sending Antony Blinken, the newly installed deputy secretary of state, to speak to the conference, according to officials familiar with internal discussions on the matter.
24But it's possible Treasury Secretary Jack Lew could attend.
