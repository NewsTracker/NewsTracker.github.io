Will the Apple Watch Eclipse the Classics?

1BRAINY SEASON | The era of the luxe smartwatch is dawning.
2Apple Watch Edition 42MM 18K Gold Case with Classic Buckle, $15,000, preorder at apple.com Photo: Photo Illustration by F.
3Lately, I've been thinking about how my daughter will react when I eventually give her one of my impressively fancy mechanical watches, the first of which I received from my parents when I graduated from college.
4She's only two-and-a-half.
5So we've got some time before she hits a milestone deserving of an heirloom Cartier or an IWC.
6But the Apple Watch, which after several months of buildup and anticipation will finally be available to the public on April 24, feels like a looming force that may well change her generation's idea of what a luxury watch is.
7Like many smartwatches, Apple's is designed to let you quickly dictate responses to text messages, check the weather, find a child-friendly Ethiopian restaurant-things you'd normally rely on your smartphone to do.
8(Caveat: It doesn't have cellular capabilities so many of its functions aren't available if it isn't paired wirelessly with an iPhone.)
9But while several smartwatches-also known as "connected watches" and "wearables"-have entered the market, none has so directly and deftly taken aim at both the male and female luxury and fashion consumer.
10To wit, the company invited an international crew of fashion editors out to its headquarters in Cupertino, Calif., for the announcement of the watch last September.
11Soon afterward, the watch made an appearance at Paris fashion week, where Apple's design gurus Jonathan Ive and Marc Newson showed it to the likes of Vogue editor Anna Wintour and Chanel creative director Karl Lagerfeld.
12Strapped to a model in a Celine dress, the watch graced the cover of Vogue China's November issue, its first major fashion-magazine outing.
13But perhaps most important is that, while entry-level Apple Watches start at $349 and go up to $1,099, with an array of bands that range from sporty rubber to steel links, the company is offering a provocatively high-end version called the Edition, which starts at $10,000 and goes up to $17,000.
14These are made of 18 karat rose gold or yellow gold with a variety of straps.
15To try one on, you have to make an appointment at an Apple store that carries them (not all do).
16And appointments are currently nearly impossible to land.
17(Should you find owning a rose-gold model too pedestrian, a number of fine jewelers have been devising methods to pave the case with diamonds and engrave it, among other upgrades.)
18In other words, the Apple Watch is clearly being positioned as a luxury good.
19This all raises the question-what do the boys in Geneva and Paris think?
20Will Apple Watch dominance some day force them to abandon their craft and jump into the smartwatch business to stay afloat?
21In many ways, their reaction amounts to little more than the brushing away of a mildly bothersome fly.
22"I don't believe [the Apple Watch] will affect the Hublots and Breitlings and Pateks," said Jean-Claude Biver, who is president of LVMH's watch division and CEO of its brand Tag Heuer.
23"You don't buy a $20,000 watch to tell you what time it is. Time is everywhere. You buy it because it is a piece of art. And art is eternity."
24Which brings up the big ticking elephant in the room: Apple Watches will almost certainly never be heirloom pieces, no matter how much gold they are carved from, because, like your iPhone and MacBook, they will inevitably become obsolete.
25Meanwhile, mechanical watches can, with regular maintenance, last for decades if not centuries.
26This is a sentiment echoed by nearly everyone I talked to.
27(Apple declined to comment on the longevity of its watches.)
28But the excitement around the Apple Watch and smartwatches in general can't be ignored; people are talking again about watches, which at one point seemed at risk of total irrelevance.
29It's no coincidence that last month at Baselworld, the luxury-watch world's biggest convention, Tag Heuer announced a partnership with Silicon Valley stalwarts Google and Intel to collaborate on a smartwatch that will be unveiled later this year.
30Mr. Biver said that Tag Heuer decided to make a connected timepiece to offer consumers a luxe option that will be significantly less expensive than the Apple Edition.
31"If I were to sell you a $10,000 or $20,000 smartwatch, my conscience would have a problem," he said.
32"Maybe it should have a label, like on cigarettes. Warning: This watch is not eternal."
33Tag's as-yet-unnamed smartwatch will likely start at just under $1,000, said Mr. Biver, while models in ultralight magnesium or titanium could go up to $2,500.
34Tag isn't alone among traditional watchmakers in getting into the smart game.
35In January, Montblanc announced the Montblanc TimeWalker Urban Speed e-Strap, which comes out in June and will be around $400.
36It's an innovative solution: All of the gadgetry is in the strap itself (you can connect it to almost any timepiece, not just a Montblanc), meaning that the watch won't end up in the recycling bin once the technology becomes outdated or the next big thing comes along.
37Still, Alexander Schmiedt, director of watches at Montblanc, sees this move as a small nod to smart that won't really affect their bread and butter.
38"The more we have interconnected technologies, the more people will appreciate exactly the opposite," he said.
39So perhaps smartwatches and traditional luxury can ultimately coexist peacefully.
40Aurel Bacs, head of the new watch department at Phillips auction house thinks so.
41"Luxury smartwatches could be like a Porsche with a hybrid engine," he said.
42"We can have the modern and sensible with the traditional and luxurious. But if there are two camps of watches, that's OK. The guy who drinks a $1,000 Bordeaux at dinner can still have an energy drink the next morning."
43There's only space on a man's left wrist for one watch at a time; everyone will have choices to make.
44Club Monaco menswear designer Aaron Levine falls into the mechanical-only camp.
45"When I wear a mechanical watch, I think of the precision required to make it work on such a micro scale. That's pretty awesome as opposed to just a chip," said Mr. Levine, who owns several vintage Rolexes and a Bulova Tank inherited from his father.
46"[A watch] is the only piece of jewelry I wear besides a wedding ring. I'm not going to give that up easily."
47Many are simply going with the two-watch solution Mr. Bacs proposes-a little Chateau Margaux, a little Red Bull.
48"I'm probably going to wear an Apple Watch during the day, but if I'm going out at night, I'll switch to a more classic watch," said Tony King, CEO of King & Partners, a digital agency whose clients are mainly fashion and luxury brands like Bentley and Akris.
49To accompany the Panerai Radiomir he owns, Mr. King said he'd most likely buy the steel-case Apple Watch with a black leather band.
50Christopher O'Neill, a New York-based banker and avid collector of Rolexes (he owns about 20) intends to buy the Apple Watch Sport to use at the gym.
51"I come from a pretty archaic group of guys who love collecting mechanical watches-nothing is going to disrupt that," he said.
52"The Apple Watch is a gimmicky thing on the side that's kind of useful."
53For the more digitally savvy watch wearer who grew up with smartphones or is entrenched in the digital world, however, the gold Apple Watch Edition represents a different kind of luxury that's more than a gimmick.
54Sky Gellatly, a managing partner of consumer marketing agency Team Epiphany, said he'd consider buying an Edition watch, even if it couldn't ever replace his treasured Audemars Piguet Royal Oak Offshore.
55But what about the prospect of his $10,000 smartwatch becoming obsolete in a few years?
56"It doesn't bother me too much," he said.
57"That's the opportunity cost for living at the speed of innovation, isn't it? It's like paying tens of thousands to sit on the first SpaceX flight. Those who buy the watch are choosing to be at the front of the pack where technology, utility and design all converge."
58He added, "After all, we're here for a good time, not a long time, right?"
59Unlike my old Casio Data Bank and other cheap plastic electronics, the cases and bands of the higher end watches should have a much longer useful life than the electronics.
60I wonder if Apple might deviate from the historical approach to obsolescence for electronics by making the watch internals replaceable.
61Even if it required a round trip to a repair depot, that would be a way to ensure the electronics could evolve, without tossing the more durable parts of the watch away.
