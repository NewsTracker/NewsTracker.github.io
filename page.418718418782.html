Premarket: Global stocks sour as Apple results leave bitter aftertaste

1Disappointment over U.S. corporate earnings, most notably at Apple Inc., the world's largest company, pushed stocks lower on Wednesday and investors towards the shelter of bonds.
2Retrenchment and caution dominated trading in other assets, with the dollar slipping a little further from its recent highs and commodities such as gold and oil resuming their downturn.
3Stocks will look to corporate earnings on both sides of the Atlantic for direction.
4Meanwhile, the Greek parliament will vote on reforms it must undertake for talks on a multibillion-euro bailout to start this week, which will help determine broader sentiment in Europe.
5The FTSEuroFirst 300 index of leading European shares fell 0.5 per cent in early trade to 1,588 points.
6Germany's DAX fell 0.6 per cent to 11,534 points and France's CAC 40 was down 0.5 per cent at 5,079 points.
7Britain's FTSE 100, which is more exposed to the mining and energy sectors, was down 0.7 per cent.
8British investors are also awaiting the latest Bank of England policy minutes, which may show a more hawkish tilt towards raising rates.
9"Despite a beat at the earnings level and a surge in revenue from China, the market latched onto disappointing overall iPhone shipments relative to street expectations," said Jim Reid at Deutsche Bank in London, of Apple's results.
10"Combined with a near-4 per cent fall for Microsoft in extended trading after a similarly disappointing report, (U.S.) futures have fallen this morning."
11S&P futures showed Wall Street expected to open down around 0.5 per cent.
12European bourses were also weighed down by a 4 per cent fall in chip maker Arm Holdings.
13Although ARM posted a 32 per cent rise in second-quarter profit, the results from Apple, a major customer, hit hard.
14Earlier in Asia, MSCI's broadest index of Asia-Pacific shares outside Japan slid 1 per cent, its biggest decline in two weeks.
15Japan's Nikkei stock index ended down 1.2 per cent, snapping its six-day rising streak and pulling away from Tuesday's nearly four-week closing high as the Apple news reverberated on related tech shares.
16Following its tumble to a five-year low on Monday, investors remained wary of gold.
17It reversed Tuesday's rebound to trade down 0.7 per cent on the day to $1,090.95 per ounce.
18"We believe gold should range trade around current levels, but do not dismiss the possibility of further price falls, given the lack of safe-haven interest," Barclays commodities analysts wrote in a note to clients.
19Crude oil futures were still under pressure too, as investors worried about ample supply.
20U.S. crude was down 1.5 per cent at $50.10 and Brent shed more than 1 per cent to $56.43.
21In currencies, the euro edged up to $1.0950, continuing its rebound from Monday's three-month low of $1.0808, and the dollar index slipped 0.1 per cent to 97.218.
22On Tuesday, it rose as high as 98.151, a level not seen since late April.
23Sterling was the biggest mover among major currencies.
24It was up 0.4 per cent against the dollar at $1.5615, supported by expectations the Bank of England will raise interest rates around the turn of the year.
25On Greece, Standard & Poor's upgraded its sovereign credit rating on Tuesday by two notches and revised its outlook to stable from negative, citing euro zone countries' initial agreement to start negotiations with Athens on a third bailout.
26European bond markets awaited the Greek parliament vote, sticking to narrow ranges.
27Benchmark 10-year Bund yields fell a basis point to 0.77 per cent, and Italian yields were steady at 1.96 per cent.
28"The newsflow since last week's deal does not fill us with great hope about the design of the pending deal, which is one reason we retain a fairly pessimistic view about what has been solved in Greece," said Michael Michaelides at RBS.
29The yield on 10-year U.S. Treasuries slipped a basis point to 2.33 per cent.
