Greek prime minister puts bailout deal to referendum July 5

3ATHENS, Greece (AP) - Greece's fraught bailout talks with its creditors took a dramatic turn early Saturday, with the radical left government announcing a referendum in just over a week on the latest proposed deal - and urging voters to reject it.
4Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras announced the July 5 referendum in a televised address to the nation, following an emergency meeting of his cabinet.
5"The Greek government has been asked to accept a proposal that places new unbearable burdens on the Greek people," Tsipras said.
6"Right now, we bear an historic responsibility concerning ... the future of our country. And this responsibility obliges us to answer (the bailout creditors') ultimatum based on the sovereign will of the Greek people."
7The move radically raises the stakes in Greece's confrontation with its increasingly irate creditors, whom Tsipras accused of seeking to "humiliate the country," demanding new pension cuts, sales tax hikes and labor market reforms.
8Worried Greeks have been pulling their money out of banks for months, and an estimated 4 billion euro left Greek banks last week.
9Queues were seen forming outside several Athens cash machines and fuel stations late Friday and early Saturday.
10Giorgos Pistevos, a retired bank employee, was in a queue withdrawing money at 3 am in the northern suburb Vrilissia, where many cash machines had 5 to 25 people waiting.
11Pistevos said he went home after midnight and switched on the TV.
12"As soon as I saw (the news), I went straight to the bank because we don't know what will happen," he said.
13Tsipras said he would ask creditors Saturday for an extension "of a few days" to Greece's bailout program, which expires on Tuesday.
14In theory, without an extension, the country will lose access to any remaining bailout funds.
15The referendum announcement also raises severe questions over whether the debt-crippled country will be able to remain solvent and in the 19-state eurozone.
16Greece desperately needs a deal with its creditors.
17Without a 7.2 billion euro ($8.07 billion) bailout loan installment - which would only be available if there is a deal - the country will be unable to make a 1.55 billion euro payment to the International Monetary Fund on Tuesday, and even bigger payments later July.
18A Greek official close to the bailout negotiations said the country was unlikely to pay the IMF on Tuesday, adding that IMF rules allow a certain period during which a country is considered to be in arrears.
19By essentially defaulting on its debt mountain, Greece would likely see its banks collapse, as they depend on emergency European Central Bank funding.
20The government could soon run out of cash, face huge difficulties in paying pensions and civil servant salaries - and that could force it to leave the eurozone and adopt a weak national currency.
21But the country imports most key consumer goods, whose cost would rocket beyond most Greeks' reach under a new currency.
22Teneo Intelligence analyst Wolfgango Piccoli said Tsipras' move places Greece in "entirely uncharted waters."
23In a note, he said it also raises the risk of the country leaving the euro to "at least 50 percent," more than double the previous level.
24Greek opposition parties - except for the Nazi-inspired Golden Dawn - expressed horror at the referendum.
25Conservative main opposition leader Antonis Samaras accused Tsipras' radical left government of advocating an exit from the eurozone and the European Union.
26"Mr Tsipras has led the country to an absolute impasse," he said.
27"Between an unacceptable agreement and leaving Europe."
28But government officials insisted that the referendum would not be about currency change.
