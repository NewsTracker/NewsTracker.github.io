AP sources: Middle-class economics a focus of Clinton's bid

1Boosting economic security for the middle class and expanding opportunities for working families, key issues that her campaign says will be heralded by a results-oriented "tenacious fighter."
2The former first lady, U.S. senator and secretary of state is expected to make her 2016 effort official Sunday with an online video followed by small events with residents of early-voting states over the days ahead.
3The campaign's opening strategy was described ahead of the announcement by two senior advisers who requested anonymity to discuss her plans.
4If Clinton's strategy sounds familiar, it might be because President Barack Obama framed the choice for voters in 2012 as between Democrats focused on the middle class and Republicans wanting to protect the wealthy and return to policies that led to the Great Recession.
5Clinton intends to sell herself as being able to work with Congress, businesses and world leaders, the advisers said Saturday.
6That approach could be perceived as a critique of Obama, who has largely been unable to fulfill his pledge to end Washington's intense partisanship and found much of his presidency stymied by gridlock with Congress.
7In New York on Saturday, at the final event put on by "Ready for Hillary," a group not connected with her campaign that's worked for the past few years to stoke excitement for it, enthusiastic supporters joined elected officials and local party leaders to celebrate the launch to come.
8"After she left the State Department she could have slipped into grandmother-hood, but people want to call her back into public service," said Jarret Berg, 29, a Democratic staffer in the New York legislature.
9"It's time for her."
10As her official announcement loomed, the Republican National Committee linked Clinton to Obama, a regular focus of criticism from the GOP.
11"All Hillary Clinton is offering is a continuation of the same big government ideas that have grown Washington instead of the middle class," RNC spokesman Michael Short said in a statement Saturday.
12"That's why voters want fresh leadership and a new direction, not four more years of Obama's failed policies."
13Clinton is not expected to roll out detailed policy positions in the first weeks of her campaign.
14Advisers said she planned to talk about ways families can increase take-home pay, the importance of expanding early childhood education and making higher education more affordable.
15It's not yet clear whether that will include a noticeable break with Obama on economic policy.
16The GOP has hammered Obama's approach as anti-business and insufficient in the wake of the recession.
17The White House says the economy has improved significantly in recent years.
18The unemployment rate fell to 5.5 percent in March, but manufacturing and new home construction slowed, cheaper gas has yet to ignite consumer spending and participation in the labor force remains sluggish.
19Clinton will enter the race as the overwhelming favorite for her party's nomination.
20Still, her team has said her early strategy is designed to avoid appearing to take that nomination for granted.
21The early events will include discussions at colleges, day care centers and private homes, and stops at coffee shops and diners.
22After about a month of such events, Clinton will give a broader speech outlining more specifics about her rationale for running.
23Clinton's husband, Bill, and daughter, Chelsea, are unlikely to appear at her early events.
24Bill Clinton, the former two-term president, said recently that he wanted to play a role as a "backstage adviser" in his wife's campaign.
25Advisers said Bill Clinton has been engaged with his wife in some of the policy discussions leading up to this weekend's rollout.
26To prepare for the campaign, Clinton has spent months meeting with economic policy experts, including Heather Boushey, whose research focuses on inequality, and Teresa Ghilarducci, a labor economist and retirement policy expert.
27The policy development process has been overseen by aides Jake Sullivan and Dan Schwerin.
28In the days before her announcement, Clinton has been holding lengthy meetings with her staff, sometimes joining them at her crowded personal offices in midtown Manhattan and other times participating by phone.
29She is expected to reach out to donors in the coming weeks, but does not plan to headline many fundraising events over the next month.
30Clinton's growing team of staffers began working Friday out of a new campaign headquarters in Brooklyn.
31They gathered Saturday to hear from campaign manager-in-waiting Robby Mook, who told them the campaign would value teamwork, respect, diversity, discipline and humility.
32A memo distributed by Mook, "We are Hillary for America," said the campaign "is not about Hillary Clinton and not about us - it's about the everyday Americans who are trying to build a better life for themselves and their families."
33A Democratic official in attendance described the meeting on condition of anonymity because it was a private strategy session.
34The memo was first reported by Politico.
