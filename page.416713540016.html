Google embeds engineers as professors

1Google software engineer and Google In Residence Sabrina Williams, center, talks with students including freshman Alanna Walton, left, during a Google Student Development class on Impostor Syndrome, at Howard University in Washington, Tuesday, April 14, 2015.
2In ongoing efforts to diversify Silicon Valley's tech sector, Google is embedding engineers at a handful of Historically Black Colleges and Universities where they teach, mentor and advise on curriculum.
3Google software engineer and Google In Residence Sabrina Williams, right, talks with students including, from left, freshmen Lucretia Williams, Alanna Walton, and Christopher Hocutt, during a Google Student Development class on Impostor Syndrome at Howard University in Washington, Tuesday, April 14, 2015.
4Howard University freshman Alanna Walton knew something was different about the professor teaching her introduction to computer science course.
5First, there was her name: Professor Sabrina.
6She was an African American woman, kept office hours until 2 a.m. if that's what it took to see everyone, and had an additional title: Google In Residence.
7"It was an awesome class," said Alanna who has already chosen her major at the Washington D.C.-based university: computer science.
8Today 35 percent of African Americans receiving computer science degrees come from those schools, but they don't make their way to Silicon Valley's top tech firms.
9Google is typical - about 1 percent of its technical staffers are black.
10Last year a push by civil rights advocate Jesse Jackson prompted several dozen tech firms to release workforce diversity data which showed under-representation of African Americans, Latinos and women in the field.
11In response, businesses, universities and community leaders have launched initiatives aimed at diversifying their ranks, both ethnically and by gender.
12The Anita Borg Institute and the National Center for Women and Information Technology have partnered with many companies to support female engineers.
13Facebook offers "Facebook University," an internship for low income minority college freshmen interested in computer science.
14Intel has committed $300 million over the next five years toward diversifying its workforce, while Apple has a $50 million partnership with nonprofits to support women and minority computer science majors.
15Google decided to go to the source, sending a handful of software engineers to teach at Howard, Hampton University in Hampton, Virginia, Fisk University in Nashville, and Spelman and Morehouse colleges in Atlanta.
16They taught introductory courses, but they also trained students on everything from how to send a professional email to how to make it through a software engineering job interview, which can involve a lot of time solving coding questions at a white board.
17This summer, 30 of those students will be Google interns.
18And Howard University graduating senior Christopher Hocutt, 21, whose friends jokingly call him Mr. Google, will be starting at the company full-time.
19Hocutt said the Google In Residence professors convinced him to apply.
20"What they discovered was a lot of people weren't even applying to Google because we didn't believe we were skilled enough to do it," he said.
21"Once we realized we have the skills, we just needed mentorship to make our resume look good, get through the interview, have confidence to try."
22Google software engineer Sabrina Williams, who took a semester away from her Mountain View campus this year to mentor and teach at Howard, is thrilled to see her student becoming a colleague.
23"I'm inspired," she said.
24"Change is slow, this is going to take time, but I think what's interesting about this program is that it's a different way of attacking the problem of lack of diversity in tech."
25Fifteen years ago, Williams was the only female African American computer science major at Stanford University.
26"I kind of felt awkward so I kind of hid a lot," she said.
27"It was very difficult."
28She said that while "teaching is hard" and the hours at Howard were grueling, she welcomed the opportunity to offer students an experience different from her own.
29This included taking female computer scientists aside early in the semester, telling them she was available for any questions, and encouraging them to support each other.
30Legrand Burge, who chairs Howard University's computer science department, welcomes the temporary addition of Google engineers to his faculty.
31"They're not academics but they have domain expertise that students could definitely learn a lot from," he said.
32"The word got out and it actually got a lot of students interested in computer science who didn't initially plan to study it."
33Indeed, class sizes have doubled in intro courses.
34Williams had 70 students in one class; about 250 were taught so far this year by Google engineers at all five schools.
35In the 1970s and 80s a similar program partnered the university with AT&T, Bell and Hewlett Packard, Burge said.
36This time, he said, Google is bringing a disruptive, Silicon Valley mentality to their campus.
37"The issue with the East Coast and West Coast ecosystem, is that in the West there's a fail fast, fail often mentality," Burge said.
38"And there's a disconnect between academia, which does not have that try things out, dare to experiment, fail and learn about why you failed, come back and do better, culture."
