CLINTON'S IN! Hillary announces 2016 White House bid

1Former Secretary of State Hillary Clinton on Sunday officially announced her 2016 presidential campaign, ending months of speculation over her political plans and immediately elevating her as a target for the field of Republican contenders.
2Clinton made the announcement in a YouTube video in which she says: "Everybody needs a champion. And I want to be that champion."
3The roughly two-minute video begins by showing a cross-section of Americans working to get ahead before Clinton says: "I'm doing something, too. I'm running for president."
4The 2016 White House winner will assuredly need to appeal to middle-class Americans and win their votes.
5And Clinton wasted little time making her pitch.
6"Americans have fought back from tough economic times," she says in the video.
7"But the deck is still stacked in favor of those at the top."
8Clinton later released a similar, more condensed message on Twitter.
9The former first lady is seeking the presidency for a second time, after losing the party nomination to then-Sen. Barack Obama in 2008.
10The 67-year-old Clinton has been the clear Democratic front-runner since speculation started last year about her potential candidacy, and her announcement makes her the only Democrat so far to officially start a 2016 White House campaign.
11A victory next year would make Clinton the United States' first female president.
12Clinton in 2000 won a U.S. Senate seat in New York, and in 2009 was appointed secretary of state, serving four years.
13On Saturday, the group We Are Hillary for America circulated a memo to supporters that organizers called "guiding principles."
14But the memo also provided some insight into to the underpinnings of a Clinton campaign.
15"Give every family, every small business and every American a path to lasting prosperity by electing Hillary Clinton the next president of the United States," the memo in part stated.
16Clinton now heads this week to key, early-voting states, starting with Iowa on Tuesday, then onto New Hampshire.
17She has signaled that she intends to make a major push in the Iowa caucuses, won by Obama in 2008.
18Her team has hired a former top aide to Agriculture Secretary Tom Vilsack to lead her Iowa campaign.
19Her ties to New Hampshire are much stronger.
20State Democrats remember Bill Clinton's surprising second-place finish in the 1992 primary that helped him overcome charges of draft dodging and womanizing.
21Hillary Clinton surprised Obama by winning the 2008 New Hampshire primary.
22Clinton has faced sharp criticism in recent weeks, after news reports that as secretary of state she used a private server and emails for official business, then made available only about half of the roughly 60,000 messages, permanently deleting those she considered personal.
23Still, a Fox News poll released March 26 shows Clinton with support from 49 percent of early voters, numbers similar to those in other polls for roughly the past five months.
24The response to Clinton's announcement was quick and widespread with Democratic and Republican lawmakers, candidates and political groups eager to get in on the biggest news so far in the early 2016 election cycle.
25"Hillary Clinton's tenure as secretary of state resulted in an America that is less safe and trusted abroad," said Our American Revival, a political action committee for Wisconsin Gov. Scott Walker, a potential 2016 GOP presidential candidate.
26"We must stand together and speak out against Hillary Clinton's dangerous liberal record."
27Carly Fiorina, another potential 2016 GOP candidate and a former Hewlett-Packard chief executive, release her own video.
28"Hillary Clinton's a highly intelligent woman, hard-working, she's dedicated her life to public service," Fiorina says the video that she posted on Facebook.
29"But unfortunately, she does not have a track record of accomplishment or transparency. She's not the woman for the White House."
30Kentucky GOP Sen. Rand Paul, who last week officially announced his 2016 bid, became the first candidate to release an attack ad on another official candidate with a video that in part says: "Hillary Clinton represents the worst of the Washington machine."
31Former Maryland Gov. Martin O'Malley, a Clinton supporter who is seriously considering a bid for the Democratic nomination, said through a spokeswoman that he will make his decision "regardless of what other people decide."
32"He's heard from Democrats that they are looking for someone who offers strong progressive values, new leadership and the experience of getting real results," spokeswoman Lis Smith said.
33"The Democratic Party will benefit from a robust issues debate should Governor O'Malley decide to enter the race."
34Democratic National Committee Chairman and Florida Rep.
35Debbie Wasserman Schultz welcomed Clinton as the first official candidate seeking the party's 2016 presidential nomination.
36But she also suggested Clinton would have to work for the mantle.
37"We expect a competitive primary for the Democratic nomination," Wasserman Schultz said.
38"I look forward to the contributions that Secretary Clinton and all of our eventual candidates will bring to this debate."
