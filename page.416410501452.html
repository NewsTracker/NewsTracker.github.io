Egypt's ousted president Morsi jailed for 20 years

1Cairo (AFP) - An Egyptian court Tuesday sentenced ousted president Mohamed Morsi to 20 years in prison for abuses against protesters but acquitted the Islamist leader of charges carrying a possible death penalty.
2Morsi was convicted of ordering the arrest and torture of demonstrators involved in clashes in 2012 when he was president, in a verdict Amnesty International denounced as a "travesty of justice".
3Fourteen others were convicted of the same charges, with most also sentenced by the Cairo court to 20 years in jail.
4It acquitted the defendants of inciting murder in connection with the deaths of a journalist and two protesters during the December 5, 2012 clashes outside the presidential palace in Cairo.
5Morsi, dressed in a white prison uniform and standing in a soundproof cage, raised his fists when the verdict was announced, an AFP correspondent reported from the courtroom.
6Defence lawyers said they would appeal the convictions while rights groups voiced alarm at the ruling, the first in a series of trials Morsi is facing.
7"This verdict shatters any remaining illusion of independence and impartiality in Egypt's criminal justice system," said Hassiba Hadj Sahraoui, Amnesty's deputy Middle East and North Africa director.
8She called for a full retrial or the ex-president's release.
9Egypt's first freely elected leader, Morsi came to power following the 2011 ouster of longtime autocrat Hosni Mubarak in a popular uprising.
10But after just a year in power, Morsi was himself toppled by then-army chief and now President Abdel Fattah al-Sisi following mass street protests.
11Morsi's Muslim Brotherhood has been blacklisted and targeted in a government crackdown that has seen hundreds killed and thousands thrown in jail.
12Other Brotherhood leaders have been sentenced to death and Tuesday's decision to acquit Morsi on the incitement to murder charge left some surprised.
13"We were expecting them to be convicted of murder," Ramy Ghanem, a lawyer for an anti-Morsi protester who was wounded in the clashes, told AFP.
14"But the sentences are not bad. We were expecting life in prison, but then 20 years is not very different," he said.
15A life sentence in Egypt is 25 years.
16Hundreds of Morsi supporters have been sentenced to death in speedy mass trials which the United Nations has called "unprecedented in recent history".
17The Muslim Brotherhood condemned Tuesday's verdict.
18"The illegitimate powers are trying to impose their tyranny through invalid sentences. This oppression will backfire as the revolution and public anger continue," it said in a statement.
19The Brotherhood had called for pro-Morsi protests on Tuesday but there were no reports of major rallies.
20The movement has failed to mobilise supporters for several months following the government crackdown.
21Morsi is facing two other trials in which he could face the death penalty, including on a charge of spying for foreign powers.
22Sisi has vowed to "eradicate" the Brotherhood, an 85-year-old movement that topped successive polls between Mubarak's fall and Morsi's presidential election victory in May 2012.
23The authorities designated it a "terrorist group" in December 2013, making even verbal expressions of support punishable by stiff jail terms.
24The crackdown has sparked a fierce backlash including from jihadist groups, who have claimed a string of deadly attacks on security forces.
25Sisi was elected president in May last year, but he faced no serious challengers and -- in a country where the army has held the reins of power for decades -- his victory dashed the hopes of pro-democracy protesters who ousted Mubarak.
26Sisi's regime is popular among many Egyptians tired of political turmoil and seeking stability and economic growth.
27But Sisi has been accused of repression and his crackdown has also targeted secular and liberal activists who spearheaded the uprising against Mubarak.
28In November, a court dropped murder charges against Mubarak in his own trial over the deaths of hundreds of protesters in 2011.
