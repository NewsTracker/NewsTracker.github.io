Nintendo President Satoru Iwata dies after leading Japan game company through success, woes

1TOKYO - Satoru Iwata, who led Japanese video game company Nintendo Co. through years of growth with its Pokemon and Super Mario franchises, died July 11 of a bile duct tumor, the company said Monday.
2He was 55.
3Iwata, president from 2002, died in a Kyoto University hospital.
4He had not been seen recently at game events, such as E3 in Los Angeles, where he was usually a participant.
5Iwata led Nintendo's development into a global company, with its hit Wii home console and DS handheld, and also through its recent woes caused by the popularity of smartphones.
6His replacement was not immediately announced, but the company said star game designer Shigeru Miyamaoto will remain in the leadership team along with Genyo Takeda.
7Iwata had been poised to lead Nintendo through another stage after it recently did an about-face and said it will start making games for smartphones - meaning that Super Mario the plumber will start arriving on cellphones and tablets.
8The falloff in appetite for game machines in the past few years was partly because people are increasingly playing games or doing social media and other activities on smartphones.
9Nintendo has repeatedly had to lower prices on gadgets to woo buyers.
10Until the recent shift in strategy, company officials including Iwata had repeatedly rejected the idea of developing games for mobile devices, a market that they brushed off for years as irrelevant.
11In March, Nintendo announced an alliance with Japanese mobile game company DeNA Co. to develop games for mobile devices.
12The company returned to profit in the fiscal year ended March 2015 after several years of losses.
13Nintendo pioneered game machines since the 1980s, developing one of the first machines and the hit Game Boy hand-held machine.
14Its main rivals in the business are Sony Corp. with the PlayStation machines and Microsoft Corp. with the Xbox One machine.
15Both companies have done better in adapting to the era of online and mobile games.
16Iwata succeeded Hiroshi Yamauchi, who ruled over the Kyoto-based company for half a century, transforming it from a traditional playing-card company to a technological powerhouse.
17Yamauchi died in 2013 at 85.
18Iwata was picked, with Yamauchi's blessing, and Yamauchi remained adviser for many years.
19Iwata was a respected and popular figure in the game industry, partly because he was relatively more approachable than executives at other Japanese companies, who tend to be aloof and rigid in demeanor.
20A funeral service will be held on July 17.
