US Treasury secretary urges Greece to reach debt deal

1WASHINGTON - U.S. Treasury Secretary Jacob Lew is urging Greece to reach a deal with its creditors, warning that a default would "create immediate hardship for Greece" and damage the world economy.
2Greece is in negotiations with the International Monetary Fund and European authorities to receive the final 7.2 billion euro ($7.8 billion) installment of its financial bailout.
3Creditors are demanding that Greece produce credible reforms before releasing the money.
4The country has relied on loans from the international community since 2010.
5Without more bailout money, Greece could miss two debt payments due to the IMF in May and run out of cash to pay government salaries and pensions.
6"It's something that the European and global economies don't need - to have another crisis," Lew told reporters Friday.
7Fears that Greece could default and abandon the euro currency group sent shockwaves through global markets Friday.
8After being down nearly 360 points, the Dow Jones industrial average recovered a bit to finish down 279.47, a drop of 1.5 percent.
9Lew said he stressed in a series of one-on-one meetings, including discussions with Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis, that it was urgent that the debt negotiations be resolved quickly.
10The status of the Greek debt talks was a prime topic on the sidelines at the spring meetings of the 188-nation International Monetary Fund and its sister lending organization, the World Bank.
11Those meetings were scheduled to conclude Saturday with financial officials addressing a variety of issues - from the weak global economy to a stalled effort to overhaul IMF voting procedures.
12The negotiations over Greece's debt have proved contentious.
13Greek officials said they planned to meet with creditors Saturday in a search for "common ground."
14Some finance officials expressed their frustration with Greece's new left-wing government, elected in January.
15Luis de Guindos, Spain's economy minister, said the Greek government had sent "contradictory" signals regarding the negotiations.
16"We have wasted very precious time over the last three or four weeks," de Guindos said in an interview with The Associated Press.
17"The communication of the Greek government has not been great. They have not made a lot of friends," he said, while adding, "I expect and I hope that the communication will improve."
18Greece wasn't the only country that drew criticism at the finance meetings: China and Japan complained that the United States has still not approved the legislation needed to put into effect IMF reforms that have been stalled for the past five years.
19The reforms would boost the IMF's resources to deal with financial crises and increase the voting power of fast-emerging economies such as China, Brazil and India.
20Lew said he had assured his fellow finance ministers that the Obama administration still believed it would soon overcome objections from Republican lawmakers and win congressional approval for the IMF reforms.
21The United States is the IMF's largest shareholder and congressional approval is the last hurdle to putting the reforms into effect.
22Chinese Vice Finance Minister Zhu Guangyao said in a speech Friday that the failure to pass the IMF quota reforms had damaged America's image.
23Growing frustration has caused other major countries to examine alternatives to break the stalemate in ways that might reduce America's global influence.
24Japanese Finance Minister Taro Aso, speaking to reporters after the G-20 meeting, noted the growing dissatisfaction: "There are other camps who are saying we can leave the U.S. behind and do something else. However, there's a voting power being held by the United States, so it's not realistic to think about other ways."
25As the largest shareholder, the United States is the only country that can veto key IMF actions.
