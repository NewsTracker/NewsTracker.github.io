Hillary Clinton's long road back into presidential politics

1Clinton didn't make a final call until the holidays.
2She spent her annual Christmas vacation last year in the Dominican Republic wading through 500 pages of memos and polling analysis, and discussing the material with her husband, former two-term President Bill Clinton.
3When Clinton returned to New York in the new year, there was no meeting with staff or email to friends to announce her candidacy.
4The former senator and secretary of state simply started telling advisers to move forward with hiring and find a campaign headquarters.
5Clinton formally launched her campaign Sunday in an online video.
6The announcement cements her status as the front-runner for the Democratic presidential nomination, back within sight of an elusive ambition and a history-making turn as the nation's first female commander in chief.
7Already one of the world's most analyzed figures, the 67-year-old will be subjected to months more scrutiny of her wealth, her appearance and health, and her personal relationships.
8To Clinton's critics, her campaign is the calculated next act in a master plan she's crafted with her husband.
9Clinton friends bristle at that depiction.
10Interviews with nearly 20 people who have spent significant time with Clinton in recent years reveal a decision-making process that was slow, almost painstakingly deliberate, a reflection of Clinton's methodical and cautious nature.
11Some of those interviewed requested anonymity in order to speak candidly about their private conversations with Clinton.
12Clinton didn't shut down talk of another White House run after she left the State Department in early 2013, but she wasn't ready to jump back into presidential politics either.
13After returning to private life for the first time in more than two decades, Clinton told people that her main goals were to "walk, sleep and eat."
14Last summer, Democratic officials told Clinton that if she was even considering running for president, she needed to hit the campaign trail for her party during the midterm elections.
15President Barack Obama was considered politically toxic, but candidates were clamoring to appear alongside the woman they hoped would be their party's next leader.
16Clinton would headline 45 events for 26 candidates.
17More than half lost.
18The Democratic defeats proved clarifying for Clinton.
19She concluded that the party had run scared, done little to defend its accomplishments on the economy and health care, and failed to articulate what it wanted to do in the future.
20Clinton plunged into discussions about her own potential campaign and sought to answer the questions she felt Democrats had ignored during the midterms.
21She convened long economic discussions that centered on income inequality and the widening achievement gap.
22Foreign policy experts gave her their diagnosis of pressing problems in the Middle East and with Russia.
23Clinton took hand-written notes and stockpiled multi-page memos.
24"She wanted to be satisfied that she had a set of policies and ideas that could really make a difference for people and she had a theory of change to get them done," said John Podesta, her new campaign's chairman.
25"She went pretty deep."
26The political data Clinton consumed underscored the demographic advantages the Democratic Party has built up among Hispanics, African-Americans and women.
27Clinton seeks to pick off one or two percent more of the white vote and women than Obama.
28What resonated with Clinton more than the policy papers and the polling were appeals to her sense of duty, a hallmark of her Methodist upbringing in the Midwest.
29She was by far the most qualified Democrat to run, supporters argued.
30If she didn't run, what other Democrat could win?
31"If a young Bill Clinton was out there today and she saw that and believed in that person's ideas, character and competence, I am absolutely convinced she would not run," said Lynn Forester de Rothschild, a longtime friend and Clinton financial backer.
32There was plenty that gave Clinton pause.
33She grimaced at the thought of giving up her privacy again.
34She worried about putting Chelsea, husband Marc and their baby in the spotlight.
35She felt bad that her candidacy would crowd out Vice President Joe Biden, a longtime friend who was also toying with a White House run.
36Clinton was also mindful of chatter that she could be challenged from her party's liberal left flank.
37She set up a meeting with Sen. Elizabeth Warren, whose anti-Wall Street positions were galvanizing progressives and stoking talk that she could launch her own 2016 campaign.
38The meeting was as much an opportunity for Clinton to gauge the mood of progressives as it was a chance to size up the seriousness of Warren's repeated denials of a White House run.
39By December, most of Clinton's anxieties had been resolved.
40Her private deliberations over the holidays would put to rest her internal debate.
