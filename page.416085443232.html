Apple Watch Review: The Smartwatch Finally Makes Sense

1The Apple Watch screen is only big enough for the important things.
2WSJ Personal Tech columnist Geoffrey A. Fowler explains why this means less time on devices, more time in real life.
3The Apple Watch puts the iPhone in its proper place?your pocket.
4I?ve got a utilitarian view of the year?s most-hyped piece of bling.
5Sapphire crystal and $10,000 gold alloy aren?t what make the Apple Watch the first smartwatch worth buying.
6What?s valuable is your time.
7The Apple Watch is a computer built to spend it better.
8And if you can tolerate single-day battery life, half-baked apps and inevitable obsolescence, you can now wear the future on your wrist.
9Smartphones gave us the wondrous ability to take the Internet anywhere.
10But they?re not always productive.
11In fact, they?ve become like cigarettes, leaving us itching for the latest affirmation from Instagram or Twitter.
12I found I spend 4.3 hours each day looking at my phone?good grief, even on vacation.
13So the company that invented the iPhone has a solution: Buy another gadget!
14That irony didn?t escape me a week ago when I began wearing an Apple Watch, on loan before they become available on April 24.
15Do I really need another connected screen blinking, beeping and buzzing all day?
16I?ve found the Apple Watch isn?t a replacement for the iPhone, but it?s the right screen for many important things.
17I only look at it in blips, for rarely more than five seconds.
18It shows me the weather with one finger swipe.
19It gets physical, gently tapping my wrist when something important needs my attention and lighting up when I lift my arm to look.
20It nudges when I?ve been sitting too long.
21With an Apple Watch, you can answer incoming calls from your iPhone right on your wrist.
22A graph of brightly colored concentric circles reports how much you?ve moved, exercised and stayed on your feet.
23The watch allows you to buy things with Apple Pay, even if your phone isn't around.
24Wrist-top navigation could be great, though the Apple Watch can?t load maps fast enough.
25The Apple Watch doubles as a selfie machine, giving you a remote viewfinder and shutter for the iPhone camera.
26The 42 mm stainless steel Apple Watch with Link Bracelet?here showing its app launch screen?costs $1,000.
27On the underside of all Apple Watches is an array of sensors for detecting heart rate and presence.
28If you?re not wearing the watch, it locks down.
29This description may either strike you as helpful or oppressive.
30It has made me more present.
31I?m less likely to absent-mindedly reach for my phone, or feel compelled to leave it on the table during supper.
32With the Apple Watch, smartwatches finally make sense.
33The measure of their success shouldn?t be how well they suck you in, but how efficiently they help you get things done.
34Living on your arm is part of that efficiency?as a convenient display, but also a way to measure your heart rate or pay at a cash register.
35This is a big idea about how we use technology, the kind of idea we expect from Apple.
36Which isn?t to say that this first Apple Watch will appeal to all iPhone owners, or even a significant fraction of them.
37Shrinking a computer onto a wrist required many compromises.
38The Apple Watch turns some of these into opportunities for smart ideas.
39Others still need refinement?and a reason for many to wait for an Apple Watch 2.
40One big challenge Apple conquered is making its wrist computer small and stylish enough to wear without a nerdy pocket protector.
41My colleague Joanna Stern and I agree the Apple Watch is a fine watch for both men and women?a standard previous smartwatches couldn?t meet.
42Yet the $1,000 steel 42mm version I tested is still a bit thicker than I?d want, as tall as a stack of six quarters.
43And you can?t soap it up in a shower, though a little rain won?t hurt it.
44The battery lives up to its all-day billing, but sometimes just barely.
45It?s often nearly drained at bedtime, especially if I?ve used the watch for exercise.
46There?s a power-reserve mode that can make it last a few hours longer, but then it only shows the time.
47The Apple Watch?s screen does an adequate job outdoors, but less so in the direct sun.
48Most of the Apple Watch?s screens feature white text on a black background, which helps some.
49The Apple Watch doesn?t have cellular connection, so you?ll need a companion iPhone nearby for many of its functions to work.
50Sometimes, though, that arrangement is painfully slow: The maps app, surely the answer to wandering pedestrians?
51dreams, is so slow it makes me want to pull out my paper Rand McNally.
52The watch does work (a little) away from the phone.
53When you?re around a known Wi-Fi network, the watch can tap directly into it.
54When the iPhone?s at home, you can still use Apple Watch to track your heart rate and progress during a run or use it for Apple Pay.
55Perhaps the biggest limitation for a wrist computer is us humans.
56Most of us, save a few yogis, aren?t comfortable holding our arms up for very long.
57It?s here that Apple applies its design expertise for its boldest idea: Minimizing the time you spend touching the Apple Watch.
58Which Watch?
59Cut out the Apple Watch and some of its rivals to try on for size.
60This emphasis on quick interactions requires you to learn a new sign language.
61Alerts and information appear only when you need them, and then disappear on their own?no need to dismiss them.
62Instead of pinching to zoom, there?s a digital crown to turn.
63And there?s two ways to tap on the screen, regular and ?force touch,?
64which shows more options.
65In return, there are new sensations, such as a mechanism that gently taps your wrist.
66It?s far more discreet than the rattle of a vibrating phone.
67You can grok all of this in an hour or two, but you?ll definitely want someone to show it to you.
68? Making sure I don?t miss that email from mom, or another VIP.
69? Communicating an emotion, either through a set of cheesy animated icons?I?m talking jazz hands in mime gloves?or tapping with two fingers to literally share a simulation of your heartbeat.
70Maybe we?ll get to blow a kiss in Version 2.
71? Buying something: Tap twice on the watch?s side button to activate Apple Pay.
72? Checking on the weather or news through ?glances,?
73available by swiping up from the bottom of the screen.
74? Asking virtual assistant Siri a question.
75? Controlling music, and sometimes even video, on my phone, Bluetooth headset or Apple TV.
76? Taking a photo: A wrist app gives a live view of your iPhone?s high-quality rear camera?so you can set the phone up for a better selfie.
77? Answering a call: Talking to your wrist might look funny, but it?s a remarkably good speakerphone, and you can switch over to your iPhone mid-call if you?d like.
78It?s worth noting that doing much of this around other people calls for new rules of etiquette, or at least new tolerance.
79When is it OK to speak to your watch?
80(People thought I was talking to them when I was actually trying to dictate.)
81Is it appropriate to peek at a wrist alert during a meeting with your boss?
82What about on a date?
83The Apple Watch?s design diligence also includes things it doesn?t attempt to do.
84The Apple Watch has no Web browser, no keyboard and no way to reply to emails, though you can flag and delete them.
85Yet the Apple Watch isn?t quite the gatekeeper to my digital life that I wanted.
86Take app alerts?there?s a fine line between being in the know and having your wrist jiggle all day.
87It never got horrible for me, because Apple lets you assign VIP status to individual contacts and specify which apps can trigger alerts.
88But setting up all of this is a tedious?and unfortunately ongoing?chore.
89It?s here that I missed some of the intelligence of Google (and its services, all absent) on the Apple Watch.
90Google?s Android Wear still floods your wrist with annoying alerts and trails the Apple Watch OS in design.
91But Apple should find out what Gmail knows, and suggest contacts who might be alert-worthy based on how much I?ve connected with them before.
92The big reason many people?even many Apple fans?will skip the Apple Watch is that it?s too new.
93There isn?t yet a world of apps and services beyond Apple?s own, with independent developers?
94sparks of genius showing us all the things a smartwatch can be.
95In fact, apps have been the biggest disappointment of my Apple Watch experience.
96Apple says more than 1,000 Watch apps have been submitted, but only about three dozen have been available to test.
97Aside from some apps that deliver fresh news headlines, including the Journal?s, as well as ones from the New York Times, CNN and Flipboard, not enough felt useful.
98Apps?which download to the watch automatically if you?ve installed them on your iPhone?are relegated to a secondary launch screen that?s attractive but harder to use, a cluster of tiny circles that you have to zoom in on and fish out, like some weird game.
99Still, in these early sketches of an experience, I can already imagine so much more.
100I?d like for the Apple Watch to be my train ticket and my office key, for starters.
101For now, the Apple Watch is for pioneers.
102I won?t pay the $1,000 it would cost for the model I tested, only to see a significant improvement roll in before too long.
103But I plan to pay $400 for the 42mm Sport version once it?s on sale.
104That?s worth paying for a front-row seat for what?s next in tech.
