Thousands still missing after deadly Nepal quake

1KATHMANDU - Thousands of people were still missing in Nepal on Friday as food and help began to trickle through to those stranded in remote areas after last week's earthquake, which killed at least 6,250.
2The death toll could rise further.
3Bodies are still being pulled from the debris of ruined buildings, while rescue workers have not been able to reach some remote areas.
4The government put the number of injured at more than 14,350.
5In the capital Kathmandu, many unclaimed bodies were being quickly cremated because of the need to avert disease and reduce the stench of corpses in areas where buildings collapsed.
6"Morgues are full beyond capacity and we have been given instructions to incinerate bodies immediately after they are pulled out," said Raman Lal, an Indian paramilitary force official working in coordination with Nepali forces.
7Many of the dead could be migrant workers from neighboring India, local officials said.
8The head of the European Union delegation in Nepal said up to 1,000 Europeans were still unaccounted for, mostly around popular trekking routes.
9Officials said it was hard to trace the missing because many backpackers do not register with their embassies.
10"It does not mean that they are buried. They could have left the country without telling anyone before the earthquake struck," Ambassador Rensje Teerink told reporters.
11Another EU official, speaking on condition of anonymity, said: "A number of EU citizens are not accounted for, but this could be for many reasons. It is fast moving and very fluid and estimated numbers are only estimates."
12The number of people unaccounted for from France, Italy, Spain and the Netherlands is 371, according to checks made with these governments, while all Irish citizens, Croatians and Romanians have been traced.
13Other European nations have yet to provide an updated figure for how many of their citizens are unaccounted for.
14Aid was slowly reaching remote towns and villages nestled in the Himalayan mountains and foothills of the impoverished nation.
15But government officials said efforts to step up the pace of delivery were frustrated by a shortage of supply trucks and drivers, many of whom had returned to their villages to help their families.
16Army helicopters have air-dropped instant noodles and biscuits to remote communities but people need rice and other ingredients to cook a proper meal, he said.
17Many Nepalis have been sleeping in the open since the 7.8-magnitude quake, with survivors afraid to return to their homes because of powerful aftershocks.
18According to the United Nations, 600,000 houses have been destroyed or damaged.
19Information Minister Minendra Rijal said the government would provide $1,000 in immediate assistance to the families of those killed, as well as $400 for cremation or burial.
20The U.N. said 8 million of Nepal's 28 million people were affected, with at least 2 million needing tents, water, food and medicines over the next three months.
21Finance Minister Ram Sharan Mahat said Nepal would need at least $2 billion to rebuild homes, hospitals, government offices and historic buildings and appealed for international backing.
22"This is just an initial estimate and it will take time to assess the extent of damage and calculate the cost of rebuilding," Mahat said.
23Prime Minister Sushil Koirala said earlier this week the death toll from the quake could reach 10,000.
24That would surpass the 8,500 who died in a 1934 earthquake, the last disaster on this scale to hit the nation, which is sandwiched between India and China.
25Home ministry spokesman Laxmi Prasad Dhakal said that though the 1934 quake was more powerful, fewer people lived in the Kathmandu valley then.
26"The scale of reconstruction will be unprecedented," Dhakal said.
27While international aid has poured in, some Nepalis have accused the government of being too slow to distribute it.
28"There have been cases where villages have pelted stones on trucks carrying aid and food supplies. They must have been really hungry and angry to do so," said Purna Shanker, who works at the government's commodity trading office.
29In Sundarkhula, a village close to the quake's epicenter west of Kathmandu, villagers said they were searching their destroyed homes for food.
30Bharat Regmi, 28, said he jumped out of the first floor as the quake lifted his house from its foundations.
31When he went back a few days later, he and two of his friends found a bag of potatoes in the rubble.
32"We are living on water and whatever we can dig out from the house," he said, standing under steady rain near the highway to Kathmandu.
33Later, he crept back under a thin orange sheet, shared with about a dozen other villagers.
