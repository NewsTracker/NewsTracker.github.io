The crude oil export ban is a relic of flawed policy

Al-Shabab claim attack which killed 4 people, wounded 3 northern Kenya

1It did not ban the export of products like gasoline and diesel oil.
2The ban was in response to the Arab oil embargo and the prevailing perception in scarcity.
3From an economic perspective, as well as an energy perspective, the ban made little sense.
4Now, it makes no sense.
5It was driven by politics and emotion.
6When oil was discovered in Alaska's Prudhoe Bay, it would have been cheaper to export it to Japan and then buy an offsetting quantity on the world market.
7But because of the ban, it was politically radioactive to even raise such a prospect.
8So U.S. consumers, mainly in California, paid a price for an inefficient outcome that was driven by the ban and another bad policy called the Jones Act.
9As long as imports were growing and domestic oil production was declining, there was little interest in the export ban or to running the risk of a political backlash by removing it.
10It is now a serious impediment to our economic self-interest.
11Today, the notions of scarcity and excessive dependence on foreign oil have finally been discarded.
12They have been replaced by abundance from our energy renaissance and the economic benefits it has produced.
13So, why doesn't Congress pass legislation removing the ban and why doesn't the president direct the Commerce Department to freely grant export licenses?
14Politics still trumps good economics.
15Although it is counterintuitive, exporting domestic crude oil is most likely to result in lower gasoline prices, but the explanation of why is not simple.
16Politicians like simple sound bite explanations and removing the ban does not lend it self to simplicity.
17Presently, there is an excess of crude oil in storage, including in tankers leased to increase storage capacity.
18Increasing storage serves no purpose other than letting traders bet on higher future prices that will allow them to profit.
19Allowing the export of crude into the world market would lower the world price of crude oil and that would lead to a lower price for gasoline.
20There is another practical reason that also will benefit consumers.
21Most of our refining capacity has been built to handle high sulfur imports while the increase in shale oil production is lower sulfur.
22As a result, either refiners have to spend billions of dollars to modify their refineries or they have to find other ways of dealing with the increasing volume of shale oil.
23Some are doing exactly that by investing in "splitters" (simpler refineries) that can process the low sulfur crude enough for the output to qualify as products.
24Clearly, either option is more costly than just removing the ban.
25The free trade of any commodity leads to increased efficiency and that always benefits consumers by lower prices, increased investments and jobs.
26Eventually continuing the export will lead to further reductions in exploration and production, undermining the energy renaissance that has been an economic benefit to a struggling economy.
27The U.S. has always taken the lead in promoting free trade because of the global benefits that it produces.
28It is hypocritical to preach a policy and then ignore it as we have done with the export ban.
29Some also claim that removing the ban will result in more exploration and production that will lead to more greenhouse gas emissions.
30The belief that carbon dioxide emissions lead to climate change has become an article of faith even though it is contradicted by climate history, physics and the pause in warming that began in 1998.
31The International Energy Agency projects that for the next several decades, fossil fuels will continue to provide over 80 percent of the world's energy consumption.
32That reality trumps the environmental argument.
33The case for removing the ban is overwhelming.
34Ideology, politics and special interests should not continue to be obstacles to doing the right thing for the economy and global trade.
