Right Call? Orioles Take the Field in Riot-Torn Baltimore

1With rubble and debris looming nearby, and tempers still smoldering in riot-torn Baltimore, the Orioles played a baseball game on Wednesday.
2This wasn't for the fans, because there weren't any at Camden Yards.
3The game was held behind closed doors, and the Orioles and Chicago White Sox played because it was the best time to fill out the schedule for both teams.
4The timing worked for baseball, not so much for Baltimore.
5It was an unusual move by Major League Baseball, which usually errs on the side of caution in the wake of tragedy.
6Baseball games were cancelled after riots ignited in Los Angeles and terrorists attacked New York and Washington.
7Baseball put off the World Series in 1989 after an earthquake hit San Francisco.
8In Baltimore, after a drug store was set on fire about four miles from the ballpark and the National Guard was summoned to restore order, they played a game because this was the only planned visit by this season by the Chicago White Sox.
9The postponed games on Monday and Tuesday were to be made up as part of a doubleheader on May 28, but there was seemingly nowhere to go on the schedule with Wednesday's game.
10So they moved up the starting time by five hours to 2:05 p.m. to beat the 10 o'clock curfew and had the teams go at it before 47,000 empty seats.
11"We have a schedule so we've got to get games in," Chicago second baseman Gordon Beckham said.
12"We can't just miss all three games and expect to make them up down the line. I mean we'll have no off days for the rest of the year. So, we at least have to get this one in."
13Just about everyone who put on a uniform understood the circumstances.
14The city was hurting, and here they were, playing a baseball game to preserve the integrity of the schedule.
15On a scale of what was significant to Baltimore on this day, the Orioles' 20th game of the season wasn't exactly at the top of the list.
16"It makes you realize how unimportant really in a lot of ways this is compared to some things that are going on," Orioles manager Buck Showalter said.
17"You try to keep that mind and look at things realistically, where this fits in the scheme of things. You prioritize what's important and we tried to do that."
18Their intentions may have been in the right place, but seemingly not much else.
19Playing the game without any fans in attendance was both a good and a bad thing.
20The team didn't divert any police from doing their job around the city, but the people of Baltimore didn't get a chance to turn the page by watching the home team play at Camden Yards.
21"Sports brings people together - black, white, or any different," Orioles center fielder Adam Jones said.
22"For those three hours, you can have beers, nachos and some Boog's (barbecue) and forget about our daily lives.
23"But today, we're just going to have to play a Major League Baseball game without fans. I think that's first time in history."
24Must the game go on?
25"We've thought about that, and we've actually talked about that," Orioles first baseman Chris Davis said.
26"Obviously the decision was out of our hands. But the thing that makes it so tough is that this is an out-of-division opponent. We would have had to basically make up three games.
27"We're doing the right thing. I'm not real happy about playing in an empty stadium. That's one of the reasons that we look forward to coming home so much, playing in front of our fans. But we also understand that there's a bigger picture here."
28That's true, but the bigger question is whether they should have been playing at all.
29"There are a lot more important issues going on outside the stadium," Orioles left-hander Zach Britton said.
30"It kind of makes you realize how small baseball is compared to some of the other issues in the U.S. and around the world."
