Ferguson election could reshape City Council

1Ferguson currently has just one black member of its six-person City Council, even though African-Americans make up about two-thirds of residents.
2The lone black member, Dwayne James, is not up for re-election.
3After Tuesday, at least two, and possibly three African-Americans will be on the council.
4Three of the six council seats are at stake in the first Ferguson municipal election since a white police officer last August fatally shot 18-year-old Michael Brown, who was black and unarmed.
5The shooting sparked sometimes violent protests in the St. Louis area, and spawned a national "Black Lives Matter" movement to press for change in how police deal with local minorities.
6It also prompted a review by the U.S. Justice Department, which decided not to prosecute the white police officer.
7But the federal department released a scathing report blasting the city for racial bias and profiling in the police department and a profit-driven municipal court system.
8Several city officials resigned following the review, including the city manager, police chief and municipal judge.
9The municipal court clerk was fired for racist emails.
10The new city council will be tasked with approving hiring of the replacements.
11The scrutiny in the wake of the shooting also found that the city of 21,000 residents had a mostly white police force and city leadership - the mayor also is white.
12The race for the 3rd Ward, which includes the apartment complex where Brown was killed, involves two black men - Lee Smith, 76, and Wesley Bell, 40 - guaranteeing that an additional black resident will join the council.
13The 1st Ward features four candidates, two black and two white.
14The 2nd Ward race involves two white men.
15Still, turnout is expected to be low, which isn't unique for municipal elections.
16Turnout for the local election last April was 12.3 percent, said Eric Fey, director of elections for St. Louis County.
17A strong push was made after the shooting to register more black voters, but Fey said just 562 new voters were added to the rolls.
18In recent weeks, the focus has been on getting those who are registered to vote.
19On Tuesday, severe storms that roiled the region appeared to be dissuading would-be voters from venturing to the polls.
20Fey said it wasn't immediately clear how many ballots had been cast as of mid-day, or how turnout compared to previous spring municipal elections.
21"In general, so far what we've been hearing is (turnout) has been light. The weather has been very bad so far," he said.
22But the weather didn't deter Marty Einig, who has participated in Ferguson protests since August.
23The 3rd Ward voter appeared buoyed by the election's potential.
24"I see there is raw material within this community to demonstrate hope," he said.
25"I see a glass that's half full, and I feel that the people have the will to force change."
26Charrolynn Washington agreed.
27Voting at the First Presbyterian Church of Ferguson, she said the election is where real change will occur.
28"As much change is needed here in Ferguson, this is where we begin - not out there in the streets, doing what they were doing - but, right here," Washington said.
29"They need to be voting and putting people in position to make the change and make the decisions that need to be made."
