Nigeria girls who fled Boko Haram look to brighter future

1On weekends, she studies, swims or just relaxes.
2But the teenager's life now is one that was unimaginable 12 months ago.
3On April 14 last year, she was in a packed dormitory at the Government Girls Secondary School in Chibok, northeastern Nigeria, seeking a night's sleep before writing end-of-term exams.
4Boko Haram fighters stormed the school after sundown, kidnapping 276 girls.
5The mass abduction provoked global outrage and brought unprecedented attention to an insurgency that has devastated northern Nigeria since 2009.
6Deborah was one of 57 girls who escaped within hours of the attack.
7Her life has changed but for the other 219 hostages still being held and for families desperate for news, the nightmare continues.
8Despite promises from the government and military that the release or rescue of the hostages was at hand, there has been no credible information concerning their whereabouts in months.
9Boko Haram leader Abubakar Shekau vowed to sell the girls as "slaves" and later said they had been "married off".
10Experts say both are possible and they are unlikely to still be all together.
11Deborah and 20 other girls from Chibok who escaped Boko Haram captivity are now studying at the American University of Nigeria (AUN) in the northeastern city of Yola.
12The privately-funded AUN does not look like other Nigerian universities and certainly bears little resemblance to Chibok, which even before the Islamist uprising began was a deeply impoverished town with poor roads and limited electricity supply.
13Spread across a vast stretch of land on the outskirts of Yola, the campus includes an immaculate hotel, with a restaurant overlooking a pool that serves burgers and pizza, where faculty, including visiting Western professors, share sodas with their students.
14"It is a beautiful environment," Deborah told AFP via university staff in an email exchange.
15The Chibok girls at AUN are studying a curriculum aimed at preparing them to start a four-year undergraduate programme next year.
16Deborah said her dream is to work at the United Nations "to help my community in Chibok, Nigeria and the world".
17Others talk of becoming doctors or lawyers.
18All stress the importance of education.
19With degrees from the well-regarded AUN those dreams may come true.
20But among the 21, the prospects feel bittersweet, as international attention returns to the plight of those still being held one year on.
21"We feel sad with the advantages we have now because so many from our hometown do not have these advantages," they added.
22They also acknowledged they would almost certainly not be studying at the university had they not been kidnapped.
23Mary put this conflict in starker terms: "When the insurgency struck, I was devastated but little did I know it was going to be a blessing in disguise."
24The Chibok girls at AUN felt united in a common goal to ensure that some good must come from last year's tragedy.
25"It has been a horrible journey yet we believe that coming to AUN is for a purpose, which is to be an instrument of positive change in our hometown," Sarah said.
26"We have not been broken by the attack. We see ourselves as the people who have been chosen to make positive future changes not just in Chibok, but in our country and the world," she added.
27President Goodluck Jonathan's handling of the hostage crisis was heavily criticised, especially over his administration's failure to immediately recognise the severity of the attack and to swiftly launch a major rescue effort.
28Jonathan's defeat in last month's general election to challenger Muhammadu Buhari may have partly been caused by his inability to contain the Islamist violence.
29Boko Haram, whose name loosely translates from the Hausa language widely spoken in northern Nigeria as "Western education is forbidden", had already been suspected of committing crimes against humanity before the Chibok mass abduction focused global outrage.
30But the girls studying at AUN suggested the Islamist foot-soldiers who carried out the kidnappings ultimately deserve mercy.
31Northeastern Nigeria provides few opportunities and little hope of employment for young men, making them vulnerable to radicalisation, they said.
