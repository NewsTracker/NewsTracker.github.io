5 Things to Know About Vanuatu's Devastating Cyclone

1The cyclone tore through the South Pacific archipelago Saturday, destroying homes and killing at least 13 people.
2It has been a week since Cyclone Pam ravaged the tiny South Pacific island nation of Vanuatu, killing at least 13 people and leaving thousands homeless, hungry and desperate for help.
3For days, Associated Press reporters have been on the ground and in the air covering the disaster's impact across the islands, where communications remain patchy to nonexistent and entire villages have been flattened.
4Here is a look at what they've seen as the country struggles to recover from the disaster.
5In a world that has become reliant on instant access to information at any time, it's hard to overstate the impact on people when they lose access to the Internet and cellphone networks.
6While intermittent service has been restored in the capital, Port Vila, outside the city there is almost no way to make or receive phone calls.
7There are many islands still cut off from the outside world, with no ability to communicate.
8People living on hard-hit Tanna and many of the other islands may have survived the cyclone, but they have no way of knowing how their relatives or friends are faring elsewhere.
9People are desperate for information and will ask friends or even complete strangers to pass on messages or make a phone call for them.
10Much of the focus has been on the problems faced by Port Vila and Tanna Island.
11There is some logic behind this; Port Vila is the capital and most populous area in Vanuatu, while the southern island of Tanna suffered particularly severe damage.
12But with runways still out of operation on some of the northern islands in the archipelago, there has been much less access to those areas and hence less media coverage.
13On Friday, The Associated Press joined an aerial reconnaissance by New Zealand's defense force of Epi, Emae and Tongoa islands to the north, which are home to several thousand people.
14There was evidence of extensive destruction of buildings on all the islands, with Tongoa in particular seeming to fare the worst.
15Tongoa and Emae have reported up to 90 percent of buildings were damaged.
16All three islands were denuded of much of their foliage.
17The cyclone has knocked down fruit-bearing trees and destroyed crops across the country.
18The effect has been felt not only by the large numbers of subsistence farmers who rely on the crops to survive, but also in the markets and hotels in the capital.
19Restaurants catering to foreign tourists that would usually offer a bounty of tropical fruits have been forced to offer simple set menus.
20Tanna Island has estimated its food supplies will only last until Wednesday.
21The widespread crop damage has prompted Vanuatu's government to include seed packs for replanting in its aid distributions.
