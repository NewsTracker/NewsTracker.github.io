Coroner: Rat raised to feed family's snakes fatally bites baby, who contracts rat-bite fever

Boko Haram attacks Nigeria's Gamboru, women slaughtered in Bama

1- A rat being raised to feed a family's pet snakes ended up biting a 6-month-old boy and transmitting diseases that killed him, authorities said Thursday.
2An autopsy found that the baby died from meningitis and myocarditis, and a later investigation determined that the infant had been bitten on the finger by a rat, Cumberland County Coroner Charlie Hall said.
3It was difficult determining a cause of death, and lab tests that came back last week showed the baby had rat-bite fever, which caused the meningitis and myocarditis, Hall said.
4"It's bizarre," Hall told the York Dispatch newspaper.
5The infant and the family, who live in Dillsburg, in neighboring York County, were not identified.
6The child was treated at a hospital emergency room Dec. 30 for a fever and a rash.
7Two days later, the lethargic and feverish child was brought back and died in the emergency room, Hall said.
8Police and child welfare caseworkers were investigating the matter.
9Hall said he notified child welfare caseworkers because a 3-year-old is among the extended family members who live in the home where the bite occurred, the coroner told the Dispatch.
10Carroll Township Police Chief Tom Wargo said his department was notified last week.
11The parents are cooperating and it is too early in the investigation to know whether charges will be filed, Wargo told the Dispatch.
1Five witnesses who recounted the massacres to AFP said the Islamist militants feared they would be killed by advancing soldiers or separated from their wives when they fled the town.
2They killed the women to prevent them from subsequently marrying soldiers or other so-called non-believers, they added.
3"The terrorists said they will not allow their wives to be married to infidels," said Sharifatu Bakura, 39, a mother of three.
4Nigeria's military along with forces from neighbouring Cameroon, Chad and Niger have claimed huge victories over the insurgents in recent weeks but defenceless civilians still face serious threats.
5According to Bakura's account, which was supported by others, Boko Haram fighters received word of a military assault on Bama, formerly an Islamist stronghold in Borno state.
6The insurgents had decided to flee to the nearby town of Gwoza before the troops' arrival but first decided "to kill their wives so that nobody will remarry them", she said.
7Bukara's husband was killed by the insurgents four months ago but she was spared from a forced marriage because she was visibly pregnant.
8Boko Haram forcibly married scores of women in Bama after seizing it in September.
9Nigeria's military announced the recapture of the town on Monday.
10Witnesses who were taken under military protection this week to Borno's capital Maiduguri, 73 kilometres (45 miles) away, said the killing of women began 10 days before Bama was liberated.
11The Islamists said "if they kill their wives, they would remain pious until both of them meet again in heaven, where they would re-unite", said Salma Mahmud, another witness.
12A vigilante who fought alongside the military in the battle to retake Bama, Abba Kassim, said he saw "dozens of women corpses" in the town.
13While other witnesses reportedly a similarly high casualty figure the numbers were impossible to verify.
14Fanna Aisami, 52, also in Maiduguri after escaping Bama this week, said the executions followed a warning from Boko Haram's top commander in the town.
15"He informed them of the situation and the consequence of the takeover of the town by the advancing troops.
16"He warned them that when soldiers killed them they would take their wives back to the society where they would be forced to marry and live with infidels," the mother of seven said, speaking by phone to AFP in Kano.
17The commander "said it would be better for them to kill their wives and send them to heaven," Aisami added.
18A number of women were shot dead in front of the commander's house, she further said.
19Yagana Mairambe, 58, reported similar details but told AFP that "some Boko Haram men refused" and fled with their wives towards neighbouring Yobe state.
20Nigeria's national security spokesman Mike Omeri told AFP he would try to verify the reports while the military could not immediately be reached for comment.
21With Boko Haram gunmen facing heavy military pressure across northeast Nigeria, attacks, including suicide bombings, have persisted, even as the government in Abuja tries to assure voters that March 28 elections will be secure.
22The Islamist uprising has claimed more than 13,000 lives since 2009 and President Goodluck Jonathan has faced fierce criticism over failure to contain the violence.
23The latest reported atrocities in Bama recall similar massacres at high schools and colleges across northeast Nigeria, where Boko Haram has executed scores of students learning a so-called infidel curriculum.
