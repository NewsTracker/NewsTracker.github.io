As he heads to Africa, Obama's electricity plan in jeopardy

1FILE - In this Saturday, Aug. 26, 2006 file photo, then U.S. Senator Barack Obama meets with his step-grandmother Sarah Obama at his father's house in Kogelo, western Kenya.
2On Friday, July 24, 2015 Obama is due to arrive in Kenya, the country of his father's birth, for the first time since he was a U.S. senator in 2006, and the first stop on his two-nation African tour in which he will also visit Ethiopia.
3As President Barack Obama returns to Africa this week, his signature program to help the continent double its access to electricity is in jeopardy, undermined by Congress' refusal to reauthorize the Export-Import Bank.
4Of the $7 billion that Obama set aside for Power Africa, $5 billion fell under the auspices of the now-defunct bank, which guarantees loans to foreign companies buying U.S.-made products.
5But out of that $5 billion, just $132 million in transactions had been approved before the bank's charter expired last month, rendering it unable to approve new transactions.
6Although the bank says it had several billion dollars of Power Africa projects in the pipeline, none of them can proceed unless lawmakers revive the bank.
7For American companies, that could mean losing out to competitors like China, which is investing heavily in Africa and would be all too pleased to see its own companies get the contracts to build and equip power plants in Africa.
8Andy Herscowitz, Power Africa's coordinator, said companies with access to financing help from their governments have a competitive advantage.
9"There are plenty of investors interested in investing in Africa, and a lot of great ideas, but you've got to get to bankability," Herscowitz said in an interview.
10For Obama, the snag illustrates how Power Africa, slow to get off the ground, may fall far short of his lofty ambitions.
11Two years after the president unveiled Power Africa during a visit to South Africa, the program has yet to add any megawatts of electricity to Africa's overburdened, underdeveloped grid.
12With little to show for his top Africa initiative, Obama may find it harder to persuade African leaders that he's maximized his opportunity as America's first black president to make Africa's development a U.S. priority.
13Although this week's trip to Kenya and Ethiopia will be Obama's third to sub-Saharan Africa as president, his global agenda has been largely focused on Asia and the Middle East.
14To be sure, building power plants takes time - often years - and Power Africa officials say it's more instructive to look at deals closed rather than the number of power plants already built.
15In an annual report for Power Africa, the U.S. Agency for International Development said last July that 2,792 megawatts of electricity will be generated as a result of deals that have closed - roughly a quarter of Obama's goal.
16USAID also said Power Africa had leveraged more than $18 billion in private sector financing, although it's unclear how much of that resulted directly from U.S. efforts.
17Power Africa declined to provide updated figures on how many megawatts will be created by deals that have closed.
18Officials said they're still on track to meet Obama's goal.
19Yet promises of future power plants are a far cry from power plants up and running - particularly in Africa, where business deals fall through at a notoriously high rate.
20In the meantime, chronic power shortages and rolling blackouts continue to stymie development and make life harder for hundreds of millions of Africans.
21The president's objective is to expand access to power to 20 million households and businesses through new gas, oil, solar, wind and other power plants.
22Vera Songwe, the West and Central Africa regional director for the World Bank Group's International Finance Corp., said Obama's program doesn't set out to fund all of the continent's energy needs, but rather to spur the private sector's interest in taking on that challenge.
23"Many of these U.S. companies are a bit shy about coming onto the continent. With Power Africa, there is a concerted effort by all the U.S. agencies to trying to accompany U.S. businesses into Africa," said Songwe, also a Brookings Institution scholar.
24Yet a key component of that effort ground to a halt on June 30 when Export-Import Bank lapsed for the first time in its 81-year history.
25Conservatives have denounced the bank as corporate welfare.
26"Orders are on hold, business is in danger, potential expansions will stall, fewer employees will be hired if we do not get this done," Obama said on Wednesday, joined by heads of businesses that have benefited from the bank.
27In the Senate, the bank's supporters hope to add reauthorization to a must-pass highway bill, an idea the White House eagerly embraced.
28Although supporters have the votes to prevail, the underlying highway legislation may run into trouble.
29The House has passed its own, shorter highway bill that leaves out the Export-Import Bank, and House Republicans oppose the Senate bill.
30"It certainly slows Power Africa down," said John Campbell, the former U.S. ambassador to Nigeria and a senior fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations.
31"The question becomes what is the likelihood the bank will be reauthorized - and how soon will it happen?"
