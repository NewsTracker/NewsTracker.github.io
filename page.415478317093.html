Three U.K. teens detained in Istanbul trying to join Islamic State

Tests of Cholesterol Drugs Offer Hope of Reducing Heart Attacks and Strokes

1Three male British teenagers suspected of planning to join Islamic State militants in Syria have been arrested by London police after being deported from Turkey, officials said.
2The three, who have not been named, were detained on Friday in the Turkish city of Istanbul, Turkish sources told Reuters, after a tip-off from British authorities that two of them were traveling to Turkey via Spain.
3London police said they had been made aware on Friday that two 17-year-old boys from the city had gone missing and were thought to be traveling to Syria.
4Further inquiries revealed they had traveled with a 19-year-old male, police said.
5They were then flown back to Britain late on Saturday, when they were arrested "on suspicion of preparation of terrorist acts", the Metropolitan Police statement said.
6Turkish authorities confirmed that they had been deported.
7Police said late on Sunday the three had been released on bail and would return for further questioning at a later date.
8Their arrest comes after three London schoolgirls entered Turkey last month and are thought to have joined Islamic State in Syria.
9Security services estimate some 600 Britons have gone to Syria or Iraq to join militant groups, including the man known as "Jihadi John" who has appeared in several Islamic State beheading videos.
10Hundreds of other Europeans have also joined the fight.
11Their involvement has raised fears about the possibility of attacks at home if they return trained and further radicalized.
12Turkey meanwhile has faced criticism for not better controlling its southeastern borders, and has accused European countries of failing to prevent would-be jihadists from traveling in the first place.
13On Thursday, the Turkish foreign minister said the missing girls who had earlier traveled to Syria were helped to cross the border by a spy working for one of the countries in the U.S.-led coalition against the militants.
14Islamic State controls swathes of territory in Syria and Iraq where it has declared an Islamic caliphate.
1The drugs, one being developed by Amgen and the other by Sanofi and Regeneron Pharmaceuticals, are already known to sharply reduce so-called bad cholesterol, sometimes to levels lower than those achieved by statins like Lipitor, the mainstay lipid-lowering medicines.
2What has not been known, however, is whether the drugs do what patients and doctors really care about: protect against heart attacks, strokes and other cardiovascular problems or "events."
3The early results suggest that there might be such a benefit, maybe even a big one.
4In small studies sponsored by the manufacturers, both drugs reduced the rate of such cardiovascular problems by about half.
5"To see a reduction in cardiovascular events already is very encouraging that we're on the right track," Dr. Jennifer G. Robinson, the lead investigator in the trial of the Sanofi drug, said in an interview.
6The studies were published in The New England Journal of Medicine and were being presented at the annual meeting of the American College of Cardiology taking place through Monday in San Diego.
7Researchers cautioned, however, that the studies were small and intended to assess whether the drugs lowered the bad cholesterol and were safe, not whether they staved off heart attacks.
8That could make the conclusions about heart attack and stroke risk less trustworthy.
9Judging those effects will require larger trials involving tens of thousands of people; such studies are underway and are expected to be completed by 2017.
10"I do not think that either study answers the question definitively of cardiovascular benefit," said Dr. Steven E. Nissen, chairman of cardiovascular medicine at the Cleveland Clinic, referring to the drug makers' research.
11He was not involved in either study.
12Researchers said long-term safety still must be assessed, especially since these drugs are reducing LDL cholesterol to levels never achieved by medicines before.
13While the drugs appeared generally safe, there was evidence that they could cause memory problems.
14Still, the findings could help smooth the way for regulatory approval, wider use of the drugs by doctors and possibly reimbursement by insurers.
15The drugs, evolocumab from Amgen and alirocumab from Sanofi and Regeneron, inhibit a protein in the body called PCSK9 that helps regulate cholesterol.
16In the studies detailed on Sunday, both drugs reduced the bad cholesterol by about 60 percent, to about 50 milligrams per deciliter from about 120 at the start of the studies.
17In many cases such big reductions were achieved even though the patients were already taking statins.
18Both drugs could win approval from the Food and Drug Administration by this summer.
19Analysts say the drugs will have billions of dollars in annual sales and will be taken by millions of people who cannot lower their cholesterol enough using statins alone or cannot tolerate statins.
20(However, the PCSK9 drugs are taken by injection every two weeks or four weeks, which could deter some users.)
21Statins reduce cardiovascular risk and scientists believe it is because they decrease low-density lipoprotein, or LDL, the so-called bad cholesterol.
22But merely looking at cholesterol levels can be misleading.
23The drug niacin did not protect against heart attacks and strokes even though it raised so-called good cholesterol and modestly lowered bad cholesterol.
24Insurers in particular might demand proof that the PCSK9 drugs stave off heart attacks, strokes, deaths from coronary disease and procedures to open arteries before agreeing to pay for them for many patients.
25Executives at CVS Health, a leading pharmacy benefits manager, recently said that PCSK9 inhibitors might cost $7,000 to $12,000 a year and would strain health care budgets because so many people might use them.
26"Managed care pharmacy, indeed the health care system, has never seen a challenge like this to our resilience in absorbing costs," they wrote in the Health Affairs blog.
27Whether the results from these two small studies will be persuasive enough remains to be seen.
28The study of Amgen's evolocumab involved 4,465 patients with various degrees of risk, two-thirds of whom were randomly chosen to get the drug in addition to the medication they were already taking.
29After one year, 0.95 percent of those in the group that received the drug had suffered a heart attack, stroke or other cardiovascular problem, compared with 2.18 percent in the group that did not take the drug.
30By a measure known as the hazard ratio, the risk of cardiovascular events was reduced by 53 percent.
31The alirocumab study involved 2,341 patients.
32After one and a half years, the rate of cardiovascular events was 1.7 percent in those who received the drug, versus 3.3 percent in those who received a placebo, a risk reduction of 48 percent.
33Dr. Sanjay Kaul, a cardiologist at Cedars-Sinai Medical Center in Los Angeles, said analyses of one measure from trials meant to assess other things were "notorious for not being reliable."
34He said the results would not be sufficient to support widespread use of and reimbursement for the drugs.
35He noted, for instance, that the alirocumab trial used a narrower definition of cardiovascular events than the evolocumab trial used.
36Using a broader definition, alirocumab did not provide a statistically significant reduction in cardiovascular problems.
37The evolocumab study, for its part, did not use a placebo, so patients and doctors knew who was getting the drug, which could have affected the outcome.
38But Dr. Marc S. Sabatine, a cardiologist at Brigham and Women's Hospital and the lead investigator of the evolocumab study, said the fact that both trials had similar results was reassuring, suggesting the effect was real.
39The results were also plausible, he said, because people who have genetic mutations that reduce their PCSK9 levels have very low rates of heart attacks.
40Dr. Sabatine and Dr. Robinson have been paid consultants to the companies sponsoring the trials they led.
