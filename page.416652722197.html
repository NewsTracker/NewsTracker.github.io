Life crawling back to normal in Nepal's quake-hit capital

1A Nepalese man puts up a barricade to stop locals entering into the damaged Durbar Square in Kathmandu, Nepal, Friday, May 1, 2015.
2The strong magnitude earthquake shook Nepal on Saturday devastating the region and leaving some thousands shell-shocked and displaced.
3KATHMANDU, Nepal (AP) - Life was slowly edging back toward normal in Nepal's quake-hit capital Friday as residents packed up tents and moved indoors, farmers delivered fresh produce and lines disappeared at gasoline stations.
4Fresh croissants even emerged from a popular bakery and were quickly snapped up.
5As rescue workers continued to comb the rubble in Kathmandu for survivors, the government said it was handing out the equivalent of $1,000 to families for each victim killed in Saturday's earthquake, and another $400 for funeral costs, according to state-run Nepal Radio.
6The death toll from the mammoth quake climbed to 6,198, including those who died in an avalanche on Mount Everest, plus more than 60 elsewhere in the region.
7The city got a lift Thursday when two survivors, including a 15-year-old boy, were rescued after being buried in debris for five days.
8Although poorer sections of the city remained strewn with collapsed buildings, there were visibly fewer tents standing in a central part of Kathmandu that had been packed with people in the first few days after the magnitude 7.8 quake hit amid repeated aftershocks.
9Krishna Maharjan, a farmer on the outskirts, brought green onions and cauliflower on his bicycle into the city.
10"We are trying to get as much fresh food to the people as possible," he said.
11"I feel it is our small contribution. But that's what we can do and every little bit helps."
12More than 130,000 houses are reportedly destroyed, according to the U.N. humanitarian office.
13Its chief, Valerie Amos, landed in Nepal for a three-day visit to meet victims and local leaders.
14She plans to visit areas outside the Kathmandu Valley, according to the deputy spokesman for the U.N. secretary-general, Farhan Haq.
15"Inaccessibility to some remote areas, the lack of helicopters, poor communication and security concerns remain the main challenges in delivering relief," Haq said.
16In the past 48 hours, the U.N. Children's Fund, or UNICEF, has delivered nearly 30 metric tons of supplies, including tents, water purification tablets, and first aid and hygiene kits.
17A group of Nepal's Gurkhas serving the British army have rushed back home to help their quake-hit countrymen get some clean drinking water.
18The soldiers from the Queen's Gurkha Engineers Unit on Thursday set up a portable water purification unit on the Kathmandu grounds of the old royal palace.
19"I am just glad I could serve my countrymen when they really needed something so necessary like clean drinking water," said Corporal Bhesh Gurung, 34.
20"I have been away for 13 years serving in a foreign land and finally I can do something for my motherland."
21Gurung and his fellow Gurkha soldiers, helped by some Nepalese soldiers, are pumping ground water, filtering it in a purification unit and storing it in a 5,000-liter tank.
