'Mr. Holmes' meets his match in Mr. McKellen

1"I have been alone, all my life," says 93-year-old Sherlock Holmes (Ian McKellen), and you hear every one of his years in that voice.
2"Mr. Holmes," directed by Bill Condon and based on a novel by Mitch Cullin, isn't really a mystery, though it presents a few puzzles to solve; instead, it's a gentle examining of life near its end.
3And with McKellen at its center, his face as gloriously long and complex as a Sir Arthur Conan Doyle plot, it's also a master class in acting.
4"Mr. Holmes" entwines three stories, with the main action taking place in 1947: Holmes, long retired, now lives in the south of England, in a rural home he shares with his taciturn housekeeper, Mrs. Munro (Laura Linney), and her 10-year-old son, Roger (Milo Parker).
5There, he tends to his beloved bees, and worries about the case that caused him to retire almost 30 years ago.
6We're taken back to 1919 London to learn that story, and also to Japan, where Holmes has traveled just after the war to find a plant that may help him with his memory problems.
7None of these stories by themselves is particularly compelling, and with a lesser actor at its soul "Mr. Holmes" might well seem slight, predictable, even twee.
8But McKellen, who's in nearly every scene, makes every moment count.
9Watch how the 76-year-old actor differentiates, subtly but distinctively, between Holmes of 1947 and of 1919; the younger (though not so young) man stands slightly more erect, speaks with more confidence, seems less haunted.
10The older Holmes, whose memories are slipping away, has a sadness in his eyes, a way of holding a gaze a little longer.
11The mind is still whirring, but occasionally the wheels pause; in those moments, there's a heartbreaking sense of desperation, of someone knowing that his most valued possession may soon be out of reach.
12This Sherlock occasionally makes amused reference to his semi-fictional counterpart; noting that he couldn't smoke a pipe in public after Watson's stories were published ("it seemed vulgar") and observing that, after a mishap, "I look like I've been attacked by the Hound of the Baskervilles."
13In McKellen's hands, he's a unique blend of deduction, gruffness, sincerity, wonder and bass-toned pronouncement; you miss him, once the movie's done.
14Starring Ian McKellen, Laura Linney, Milo Parker, Hiroyuki Sanada, Hattie Morahan, Patrick Kennedy, Nicholas Rowe.
15Directed by Bill Condon, from a screenplay by Jeffrey Hatcher, based on the novel "A Slight Trick of the Mind" by Mitch Cullin.
