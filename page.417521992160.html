15PORT-OF-SPAIN, Trinidad (AP) - In one moment, Jack Warner is on TV telling his countrymen he fears for his life.
16An hour later, he's standing on a packed narrow street at a political rally, boasting that he fears nothing.
17Contradictions are a constant for Warner, who grew up with almost nothing in Trinidad and Tobago, and became a rich and powerful vice president of FIFA, world soccer's governing body.
18He rubbed elbows with world leaders on a regular basis before being forced out in an ethics scandal four years ago.
19Indicted last week by the U.S. on charges of racketeering, wire fraud and money-laundering, Warner scoffs at the accusations, insisting he's done nothing wrong.
20In Trinidad, they've heard it all before, though many residents say if Warner amassed riches without taking it from them, they're fine with the arrangement.
21"He is our Robin Hood," said Eraj Sagewan, a taxi driver in the capital of Port-of- Spain.
22That's how many in Trinidad see the 72-year-old Warner, now a member of Parliament.
23If he stole from the rich and gave to the poor, then they see no harm done.
24He is simultaneously a villain and a hero, known for getting what he wants, but also for personally arranging whatever help - doctors, food, anything - his constituents need.
25"If he didn't live so long, he would have died a hero," said Sunity Maharaj, a journalist who has long followed Warner.
26Warner doesn't hide his hubris and says the world's perception of him is nowhere near the reality.
27"Everybody knows Jack Warner is bad news. Jack Warner has Ebola," he says to an ovation - bolstered by piped-in cheers from an elaborate sound system.
28The rally starts late, with about 50 people.
29The neighborhood's barking dogs are louder than the crowd.
30Locals set up tables to sell crackers and snow cones.
31After an hour or so, Warner arrives, wearing the lime green colors of his Independent Liberal Party.
32The street is jammed, with the crowd having grown fivefold and police help with traffic and crowd control.
33The dogs can't be heard anymore.
34"I do this for you!"
35Warner proclaims.
36Said Ria Bisnath, an observer: "If this was America, you would call Jack Warner a rock star."
37His future is uncertain, something Warner acknowledges.
38The charges that revolve around allegations of bribery are the most serious he has faced, although he insists they are trumped up.
39"Nobody, no contractor, alive or dead, can say he gave me a kickback," Warner said.
40"Everything I have now, I had before politics. But there are some guys in politics now who had nothing before. How come nobody is concerned with that? ... But they're concerned about FIFA's money because they feel that that will make Jack Warner fall."
41U.S. prosecutors allege South Africa paid $10 million in 2008 to Warner and two other FIFA executive committee members as payback for supporting that nation's successful bid to host the 2010 World Cup over Morocco.
42South African officials say the $10 million was paid, though it does not characterize it as a bribe, calling it an "above-board payment" to help soccer development in the Caribbean region.
43Officials also allege he and others, including former FIFA executive committee member Chuck Blazer of the U.S., got rich off accounts they controlled through CONCACAF, which oversees soccer in North and Central America and the Caribbean.
44Blazer has cooperated with authorities.
45Some of that money is believed to have been steered toward a building in Trinidad called the Center of Excellence.
46Warner has denied owning the center - where residents go to play soccer, swim, even have wedding receptions - but records show otherwise.
47Warner said Wednesday night he has compiled reams of documents to expose wrongdoing, adding that when he heard FIFA President Sepp Blatter was planning to resign, he wrote him and urged his immediate departure.
48"Blatter knows why he fell. And if anyone else knows, I do," Warner said.
49Warner's supporters say the charges are meant to embarrass him ahead of elections this year, with Liberal Independent Party chairman Rekha Ramjit calling them "political manipulation to the highest extent."
50Simply saying the name "Jack" to hotel workers, waiters, lawmakers and others elicits a reaction, often accompanied by an eye roll.
51Some, including top soccer officials, refused to talk about him.
52"I take no pleasure in Mr. Warner's sufferings and his family's pain," said opposition party leader Keith Rowley.
53"I trust that he will be the subject of free and fair judicial processes in Trinidad and Tobago and outside. Let the law take its course."
54Warner often speaks out against the press - he made specific reference to the media at his political rally Wednesday night and later said U.S. reporters were unfair to him - yet he publishes a weekly newspaper and has thrown lavish parties for even those journalists who have criticized him.
55He writes commentaries, but doesn't like answering questions.
56He was mocked on the Internet earlier in the week after he tried to defend himself by quoting an article from the satirical outlet The Onion.
57Spending a night in jail after his arrest last week was a good thing, he said, because he got to tell other Trinidadian leaders they should clean up the prisons.
58Maybe there's no better example of the many sides of Warner than what happened at a World Cup qualifying match in Port-of-Spain in 1989.
59The U.S. beat Trinidad and Tobago 1-0 that day inside a packed stadium, winning on a goal from Paul Caligiuri - still regarded as one of the most important goals in American soccer history.
60Two perceptions of Warner have persevered: Is he the ambassador who humbly gave Caligiuri his straw hat as a token of congratulations in the U.S. locker room after the game?
61Or is he the promoter who arranged for the overselling of thousands of tickets, leaving some without a view or stuck outside the stadium?
62"Sometimes I deliberately break my rear-view mirror, because it is not always pleasant to look back," said Raymond Tim Kee, the mayor of Port-of-Spain who also leads the soccer association that Warner once controlled financially.
63"Since I assumed office two years ago, one of the first things I pursued was rebranding because what I realized was the football federation at the time had lost credibility and there were a lot of questions and fears because of all that was going on that time."
64Tim Kee formed an independent reform commission and is keeping a keen eye on the latest Warner scandal.
65To this day, what is best remembered about soccer in Trinidad is the run to the 2006 World Cup, the first and only time the tiny nation has reached the sport's biggest stage.
66What should have been a gigantic celebration is known here for Warner feuding with players over compensation.
67That's where Warner's gift of deflection comes into play.
68When asked what happened at the time, Warner responded with a story about how he mortgaged his home to fund Trinidad's attempt to qualify for the 1994 World Cup.
69"Gandhi once said that all through history, there have been tyrants," Warner said.
70"But in the end, they fall."
