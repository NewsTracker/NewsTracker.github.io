White House: No sign of Islamic State link to Yemen attacks

FBI asks for patience in black man's hanging in Mississippi

1White House spokesman Josh Earnest said the U.S. is still investigating the claim of responsibility by the Yemeni branch of Islamic State.
2He said the U.S. is looking to see whether IS has a command-and-control structure that would enable it to coordinate the attack.
3But Earnest also said IS often claims responsibility for attacks purely for propaganda value.
4Still, he said the attack shows that everyone in the region - including Muslims - is in danger from IS.
5Earnest said there's also no clear evidence backing up the IS claim of responsibility for another deadly attack in Tunisia this week.
6Bernadette Meehan, a spokeswoman for the White House's National Security Council, said the U.S condemns the attacks and also condemns airstrikes Thursday that targeted Yemeni President Abed Rabbo Mansour Hadi.
7The airstrikes by forces loyal to Yemen's former president missed his palace and Hadi was unharmed.
8Meehan said the U.S. is calling on all sides in Yemen to return to political talks to resolve differences.
9She said all of Yemen's diverse communities must take part in a peaceful democratic process.
1(AP) - An FBI agent appealed for patience Friday after a black man was found hanging from a tree in Mississippi, saying 30 federal, state and local agents were working intensively to determine whether he was killed or committed suicide.
2"Everybody wants answers and wants them quickly. We understand that," FBI Special Agent Don Alway told a crowd outside the Claiborne County Courthouse.
3"We are going to hold off on coming to any conclusions until the facts take us to a definitive answer."
4The county coroner confirmed that the man found hanging from a white sheet Thursday was Otis Byrd, an ex-convict reported missing by his family more than two weeks ago.
5Byrd lived just 200 yards from the spot where his body was found, in a wooded area off a dirt road that ran behind his house.
6Alway said investigators are interviewing Byrd's family and friends and searching his rental home and a storage unit for clues, and will not reveal any evidence along the way.
7"We are trying to paint a picture of Byrd's life. We are trying to find out what was going on with him personally and professionally," he said.
8Claiborne County Sheriff Marvin Lucas Sr. told The Associated Press earlier Friday that Byrd did not appear to have stepped off of anything in the area where he was found hanging from a tree limb about 12 feet high.
9His feet were dangling about two feet off the ground, and his hands were not bound, Lucas said.
10"Life matters," Lucas told the crowd.
11"I commit to you, as the sheriff of Claiborne County, that I will not allow the shadows of the past to cast a shadow on the future."
12The results of an autopsy by the Mississippi Crime Lab could take days; investigators expect to have preliminary information about the cause of death next week, Alway said.
13Byrd's body was released Friday to Rollins Funeral Home in Port Gibson, said Mississippi Department of Public Safety spokesman Warren Strain.
14An employee at Rollins said funeral arrangements will be made early next week.
15Byrd worked on offshore oil rigs and enjoyed gambling in casinos in his off time after getting out of prison, where he served 26 years for fatally shooting a woman while robbing $101 from her convenience store in 1980.
16He wasn't the type to commit suicide, friends and family said.
17"He tried to turn his life around. He was going to church every Sunday," said his stepsister, Tracy Wilson.
18"Anybody could have done this. I just don't see him doing it to himself."
19Lora McDaniel, a high school classmate who went to church with Byrd and his family, said "he always had a smile on his face. I just can't see him committing suicide."
20"He was a quiet man. He didn't bother nobody," added Anita Smith, another high school classmate.
21"He had been out nine years and all of the sudden this happens to him? Impossible."
22Smith said she is planning to participate in a march Monday in Port Gibson to protest Byrd's death.
23Mississippi NAACP President Derrick Johnson suggested that it's too early for that.
24"We do not want to rush to judgment. We just want to make sure there's a thorough federal, state and local investigation," Johnson said.
25The hanging is being investigated by the FBI, the Justice Department's Civil Rights Division and the United States Attorney's office as well as the Mississippi Bureau of Investigation.
26These officials are on the scene to determine if it's a potential hate crime or other violation of federal law, U.S. Attorney General Eric Holder said.
27Video surveillance was recovered showing Byrd at the Riverwalk Casino on March 2, Vicksburg Police Chief Walter Armstrong said.
28"We didn't see anything of significance on the tape. He was just walking around the casino. We did not see him after that," Armstrong said.
29Claiborne County sits on the bluffs of the Mississippi River, with Natchez to the south and Vicksburg to the north.
30A sign leading into Port Gibson, the county seat, says Union Gen. Ulysses S. Grant declared the town "too beautiful to burn" during the Civil War.
31About 85 percent of the county's 9,250 residents are African-American.
32Signs hanging above the main street say "Port Gibson. A Real Life Postcard."
33The town still boasts several stately, columned mansions that are either private homes or bed-and-breakfast inns, but is mostly dotted with modest houses and trailers in states of disrepair.
34Port Gibson is in the district of U.S. Rep.
35Bennie Thompson, a Democrat and the only black member of Mississippi's congressional delegation.
36He issued a statement calling on the government to use "every resource available to bring swift justice" if foul play was involved.
37"Given the history of the state, it is unavoidable that the hanging of a black man in Mississippi justifiably engenders deeply raw emotions," Thompson said.
38Emily Wagster Pettus in Jackson, Mississippi, contributed to this report.
