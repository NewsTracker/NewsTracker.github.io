A Fed rate hike is coming?the question is when

Egyptian warplanes strike IS targets in Libya, pushes for joint international intervention

1There's a major debate brewing in the financial markets, and it concerns the most important potential event of the year for stocks and bonds alike: the timing of a Federal Reserve rate hike.
2In one corner are the economists.
3Many of those looking primarily at the state of the recovery say that the Fed will likely raise its key federal funds rate in June.
4On the other side are traders, who say that current market dynamics-and prior experience with the central bank-tell them that a rate hike isn't coming in 2015.
5What the Fed actually chooses to do, of course, will have a profound impact on financial market, and perhaps on the economy as well.
6The federal funds rate, a critical short-term rate at which banks can lend to one other, has been kept ultra-low by the Fed since the financial crisis days of December 2008.
7Now, many economists expect that the Fed is finally set to shift from ultra-low levels, given the strong state of the labor market.
8With the unemployment rate declining and payrolls data showing some 250,000 payroll gains a month, "the U.S. labor market is screaming for policy normalization," as Societe Generale economist Aneta Markowska put it in a recent note.
9If the economists are right, a hint at a June rate hike could come as soon as Wednesday, when the Fed will release the minutes of their last policy meeting.
10If the minutes find them gushing about growth and unbothered by economic and geopolitical problems overseas, it could serve as a reminder for investors that a June hike is still on the table.
11So, too, could the congressional testimony of Fed Chair Janet Yellen in the following week.
12The Fed is "much closer to hiking then putting it off," said Neil Azous of Rareview Macro, a firm that advises large investors.
13After all, "it is hard to argue from an economist's perspective that they shouldn't at least start the process. Their models are telling them to, regardless of the problems abroad in Europe and Asia."
14Strong job creation, especially if February's payrolls top expectations, could also hint at a tightening.
15"If the job market holds anywhere close to what it's been running at, then yeah, we'll get a hike," agreed Deutsche Bank economist Joseph LaVorgna.
16"I don't see why the Fed wouldn't go in June."
17Still, that sentiment is clearly not reflected in the market.
18Fed funds futures are implying just a 20 percent chance of a rate hike in June, according to CME Group's FedWatch tool.
19Indeed, if Yellen does give a hint in the weeks ahead that a June rate hike is possible, "the fixed income market would re-price swiftly and painfully against the consensus long position," Azous said.
20In other words, rates (which move inversely to bond price) could rise dramatically.
21And that, in turn, could have a profoundly negative impact on stock prices.

23Yet traders clearly believe that such a hint isn't coming.
24In addition to what the fed funds futures are saying, the markets overall don't seem to be pricing in a great deal of bad news.
25Stocks continue to find record highs, and the 10-year yield is just above 2 percent, a very low level in historical terms.
26"The economists believe that the Fed will raise rates because they're looking at the economic data and they don't look at the markets," says Michael Block of Rhino Trading Partners.
27He predicts that the fed funds rate will be kept ultra-low for the foreseeable futures.
28Block points to the Fed's old 6.5 percent unemployment rate threshold for potentially raising rates-which was later dropped.
29Given that the Fed didn't raise rates then, "why do the economists believe them now? Because they're economists," he said.
30"If you're asking me who's going to win, the traders or the economists, my money goes with the traders," Block added.
31"They're the guys who live in the real world as opposed the economist who live in the theoretical world, and that world was tossed out the window."
32But LaVorgna retorts that markets have actually tended to do a pretty bad job of pricing in Fed moves.
33"At inflection points in monetary policy, it's not unusual for the market to grossly underestimate where the fed funds rate is going," he said, noting that this was seen during the rate-hiking cycles in 2004 and 1994.
34"So the fact that the market is at odds with where the Fed is is not unusual."
35And ironically, the market's lack of concern could serve as another point in favor of a rate hike, given that optimistic stock valuations and low bond yields would make such a move more palatable from the Fed's perspective.
36"Stocks are going to sell off on it no matter what, so if we're around all-time highs, it will make the Fed feel better," said Brian Stutland of Equity Armor Investments.
37"I, for one, think traders are underpricing the chance of a potential rate hike," he added.

10The bombs were dropped by U.S.-made F-16 fighter jets that left Egyptian bases for targets in the eastern Libyan city of Darna, according to Egyptian and Libyan security officials who spoke on condition of anonymity because they were not authorized to talk the press.
11The strikes came hours after the Islamic State group issued a grisly video of the beheadings of 21 Egyptian Christians, mainly young men from impoverished families who were kidnapped after travelling to Libya for work.
12The video shows them being marched onto what is purported to be a Libyan beach before masked militants with knives carve off their heads.
13Thirteen of the 21 came from Egypt's tiny Christian-majority village of el-Aour, where relatives wept in church and shouted the names of the dead on Monday.
14Babawi Walham, his eyes swollen from crying and barely able to speak, said his brother Samuel, a 30-year-old plumber, was in the video his family saw on the news Sunday night.
15"Our life has been turned upside down," he told The Associated Press.
16"I watched the video. I saw my brother. My heart stopped beating. I felt what he felt."
17Libyan extremists loyal to the Islamic State and some 400 fighters from Yemen and Tunisia have seized control of Darna and the central city of Sirte and have built up a powerful presence in the capital, Tripoli, as well as the second-largest city, Benghazi.
18Libya's internationally recognized government has been driven into the country's far eastern corner.
19Without publicly acknowledging it, Egypt and the United Arab Emirates carried out airstrikes against Islamist-allied militias last year, according to U.S. officials.

21He did not elaborate.
22For now, any foreign intervention should be limited to air strikes, with political and material support from the U.S.-led coalition staging airstrikes in Iraq and Syria, the Egyptian official said.
23Egypt already has been amassing intelligence on extremists in Libya in a joint effort with the Libyan armed forces and West European nations, including France.
24Insurgents in Egypt's strategic Sinai Peninsula who recently declared their allegiance to the Islamic State rely heavily on arms smuggled from Libya, which has slid into chaos since the 2011 uprising that toppled Gadhafi's 41-year rule.
25France, a lead player in the campaign to oust Gadhafi, has campaigned for months for some kind of international action in Libya, and announced a deal Monday to sell fighter jets to Egypt.
