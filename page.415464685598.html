Vanuatu cyclone 'hit like a bomb'

Aid workers say 8 people confirmed dead in Vanuatu cyclone

1Aid workers in the capital of Port Vila described homes and shops flattened, swaths of trees plowed over and residents seeking shelter in schools and churches -- or with neighbors.
2At least six people are dead, but communication with many of the 80-plus islands in the archipelago is impossible, so the fear is that toll will climb.
3The deaths reported by the National Disaster Management Office are just from Port Vila.
4It is unclear how many thousands will be displaced by the massive storm that bore the might of a Category 5 hurricane when it pounded the islands for most of a 24-hour period.
5"It's like a bomb has gone through," said journalist Michael McLennan, who lives in Port Vila.
6"It's really quite apocalyptic."
7Most buildings were destroyed or damaged, he told CNN on Sunday morning.
8Many roads are blocked by fallen trees or power lines.
9Hardly a tree stood straight after Pam bellowed across the South Pacific nation, where some 260,000 people live, many in flimsy homes built of thatch.
10Cleanup efforts are underway, according to Colin Collet van Rooyen, Oxfam country director in Vanuatu.
11It appears Bauerfield International Airport will reopen Sunday afternoon for emergency flights.
12An Australian C-130 took off for Port Vila with emergency personnel from the defense department.
13The Australian air force will send two cargo planes loaded with relief supplies, the government in Canberra announced.
14The main objective now is to get disaster response teams into Vanuatu and kick-start the humanitarian operation, Sune Gudnitz, regional head for U.N. aid agency OCHA, told CNN from Fiji, about 600 miles away.
15His agency has had word of much destruction on Efate, the island that houses the capital, where the violent winds around Pam's eye roared through, and from the southern island of Tanna, he said.
16Only a little information has so far trickled out from beyond the capital, but Gudnitz, said that he fears the worst.
17"Unfortunately, the more that comes out, the worse it looks," said Gudnitz.
18"I should say it's really a case of the worst-case scenario for the country and for the people."
19Vanuatu President Baldwin Lonsdale, who was at a U.N. conference on disaster reduction in Japan on Saturday, appealed for help for his shattered nation.
20Vanuatu President Baldwin Lonsdale has called the storm a "monster," saying it has set back the development of his country, already one of the poorest in the region, by years.

56"I stand to appeal on the behalf of the government and people of Vanuatu to the global community to give a lending hand in responding this very current calamities that have struck us."
57Pam was the equivalent of a Category 5 hurricane before notching down to Category 4 after landfall at 11:23 p.m. Friday local time.
58CNN's weather center said 155 mph (250 kph) winds blasted the island nation, with gusts up to 200 mph (325 kph).
59By overnight Saturday, the damage was done.
60CNN meteorologist Ivan Cabrera noted that, by then, Pam had passed all the nation's islands and was moving over open water.
61As it moved over cooler ocean waters away from Vanuatu, the storm further weakened Sunday.
62It had sustained winds of 132 mph (213 kph), said the Joint Typhoon Warning Center in Pearl Harbor, Hawaii.
63The damage it left behind is "unbelievable," according to the Australian Red Cross.
64"Humanitarian needs will be enormous," the aid agency tweeted.
65"Many people have lost their homes. Shelter, food and water (are) urgent priorities. "
66A UNICEF Australia post, meanwhile, spoke of "countless homes torn apart, communities in ruin."
67But the airport in Port Vila has been damaged and it's not clear when it will be able to reopen to allow relief flights to land.
68The U.N. aid agency hopes to get its first team on the ground with vital aid on Sunday, Gudnitz said.
69Vanuatu's remote location won't help the international response.
70Port Vila is more than 1,100 miles northeast of Brisbane on Australia's east coast, and some 1,375 miles north of Auckland, the closest city in New Zealand.
71Until international teams can get in, it will be up to humanitarian agency staffers on the ground and the local communities themselves to do what they can among the wreckage.
72Many people will be spending another night in emergency shelters.
73At least they won't have to face the horrors of the height of the storm.
74At that point, Alice Clements was cowering under a bathroom sink.
75"But all what I could think about during that time is the people who might be literally clinging to coconut trees for their lives, and trying to hold on in those horrific winds," the UNICEF spokeswoman said.
76There's no power in the whole of Vanuatu, Clements said, making it impossible for people to recharge their cell phones to call in.
77The water supply is also cut off, adding to people's difficulties.
78Another UNICEF staffer in Port Vila, Andrew Parker, told CNN that he'd been through many cyclones, but this one "just went on and on forever."
79The winds and rain only started to subside about 1 or 2 p.m. on Saturday, he said, having raged since 5 p.m. on Friday.
80Port Vila alone ended up with about 9 inches (230 mm) of rain in a 36-hour period, which is 70% of its rainfall for the month.
81Only one cell phone tower was still standing in the whole of the archipelago, in Port Vila, according to Parker.
82Vanuatu has officially declared a state of emergency due to devastation caused by Pam, opening the door for other countries -- like Britain, which is already offering almost $3 million -- to help.
83"Port Vila looks like an absolute bomb has hit it," World Vision emergency specialist Chloe Morrison said.
84Trees blocked the path of Morrison's vehicle as she drove around the capital.
85"They've fallen across in piles so high in some places you can barely see over the top," she said.
86Pam is the South Pacific's second strongest cyclone since record-keeping began in 1970.
87And it's the strongest of any type since Super Typhoon Haiyan smashed the Philippines in 2013, obliterating parts of Leyte.
88That earlier storm shattered homes and trees into match sticks, and killed more than 6,000 people.
89Vanuatu, which lies east of Australia, is an archipelago comprising 83 small islands, about 65 of them inhabited and many remote and lacking infrastructure.
90People away from the capital live much like their ancestors did generations ago.
91Homes are built of weaker materials, including straw and corrugated steel, and may have had little stamina against Pam's raging winds.
92"This is often a tropical paradise island and a lot of these houses are what you would expect on an island. They are thatched roofs and thatched walls, and just not able to withstand a Category 5," Morrison said.
93"We've seen villages literally blown away."
94Pam "left no island untouched," Morrison added.
95"And the devastation that will be on those smaller, remote islands -- I can't even imagine it."
96"Once we start looking further afield from Port Vila, we are going to see more destruction because the houses there would be in a much different condition from what we have in Port Vila," Gudnitz said.
97"It's a disaster, there's no other way to describe it, really."
98The Adventist Development and Relief Agency, the humanitarian arm of the Seventh-day Adventist Church, said it was still waiting to hear from all its team in Vanuatu.
99Five have not been accounted for on Pentecost Island and five on Malakula Island, presumably unable to communicate because cellular networks are down, the organization said.
100Field staff have reported that even families whose homes had survived two previous cyclones lost them during Pam.
101"Families are trying to gather as much as they can," said Mark LeRoux, the organization's Vanuatu country director.
102"Even sturdy houses didn't make it -- walls and roofs came down."
103Even in a concrete house in Port Vila, emergency worker Morrison was terrified by the whirring force.
104She huddled in a back room with seven other people.
105"Seven hours hunkered down and it's still not safe to go outside," she said early Saturday.
106"The winds are still really howling."
107It sounded like there was an angry ocean at their door.
108As a piece of tin twisted off the roof and landed by a window, Morrison felt lucky to be shielded by solid walls.
109One positive amid the devastation is that Vanuatu has known that Pam was coming for more than a week, giving time for people to evacuate to shelters, though not every island even has a concrete structure, Morrison said.
110The biggest priority now for many will be access to clean water, she said.
111In some outlying communities this was difficult even before Pam hit.
112There is also the issue of food.
113Many people on Vanuatu's islands are subsistence farmers and the cyclone may have destroyed most or all of the crops they rely on to survive.
114McLennan, the journalist who lives in Port Vila, said crops with roots fared much better through the storm than those that grow on trees.
115World Vision's staff hopes preparations will have paid off.
116They had positioned clean water, food, blankets, tarpaulins, and shelter, hygiene and kitchen kits in key places before the cyclone arrived.
117Pam isn't done yet, heading south-southeast at a 32 mph (52 kph) clip, the Meteorological Service of New Zealand reported at 7 a.m. Sunday.
118It should start affecting New Zealand's North Island late Sunday, though the good news -- according to CNN's Cabrera -- is that it won't be the same "monster storm."
119The weather service said, "Heavy rain and strong wind warnings and watches are in force for the northern and eastern parts of the North Island from this evening through to Tuesday. "
120New Zealand's official weather agency forecasts up to 8 inches of rain and 100 mph wind gusts in parts of that island, though the overall impact won't be anywhere near as bad as in Vanuatu.
121In the meantime, a new cyclone -- Nathan -- is brewing over Queensland, Australia.
122It is expected to reach Vanuatu this week.

19CHRISTCHURCH, New Zealand (AP) - At least eight people were confirmed dead in Vanuatu after a massive cyclone tore through the tiny South Pacific archipelago, and the death toll is likely to rise much higher once communications are restored with outlying islands, aid workers said Sunday.
20Packing winds of 270 kilometers (168 miles) per hour, Cyclone Pam tore through Vanuatu early Saturday, leaving a trail of destruction and unconfirmed reports of dozens of deaths.
21Chloe Morrison, a World Vision emergency communications officer in Port Vila, said officials from Vanuatu's National Disaster Management Office confirmed to her agency that at least eight people died in and around the capital, Port Vila.
22Officials have yet to assess the damage in many of the hard-hit outer islands because communications and power remain cut, she said.
23Morrison said she had heard reports of entire villages being destroyed in more remote areas.
24"People are really upset and it's really hard, just because for the last couple of years, we haven't received a really big cyclone like this one," said Isso Nihmei, Vanuatu coordinator for the environmental and crisis response group 350.
25"Most people right now, they are really homeless."
26He came upon one of the storm's victims on Saturday, while surveying the damage along the coastline with other relief workers.
27The group spotted a man lying on the ground, not breathing, and rushed him to the hospital.
28By the time they arrived, however, he was dead, Nihmei said.
29Structural damage across Port Vila was extensive, Nihmei said, with the majority of homes severely damaged or destroyed.
30Some residents began cleaning up what was left of their wrecked houses and checking on family members.
31Relief workers, meanwhile, were trying to get temporary shelters to victims as fast as possible, Nihmei said.
32"We're still not having communications with the other provinces," Nihmei said.
33"We're just running around trying to get information around Port Vila; with the other islands it's really hard to get anything."
34A westward change of course put populated areas directly in the path of Pam.
35The U.N. Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs said there were unconfirmed reports of an additional 44 deaths in Vanuatu's northeastern islands after Pam moved off its expected track.
36Residents awoke to much calmer weather Sunday after many hunkered down in emergency shelters for a second straight night, Morrison said.
37Many people who have ventured out from 23 emergency shelters around Port Vila have found their homes damaged or blown away altogether.
38Teetering trees and downed power lines have made parts of the capital hazardous.
39Morrison said communications have been so problematic that her aid group hasn't yet been able to account for many of its own 76 staff members on the islands.
40For anybody who wasn't in a secure shelter during the cyclone "it would have been a very, very tough time for them," she said.
41Vanuatu has a population of 267,000 spread over 65 islands.
42About 47,000 people live in the capital.
43UNICEF estimated that 54,000 children were among those affected by the cyclone.
44U.N. Secretary General Ban Ki-moon said the impact and scope of the disaster caused by the cyclone wasn't yet clear, but he feared the damage and destruction could be widespread.
45"We hope the loss of life will be minimal," Ban said Saturday at the World Conference on Disaster Risk and Reduction in Japan.
46The U.N. said it was preparing to deploy emergency rapid response units.
47The president of Vanuatu, Baldwin Lonsdale, who was attending the conference, told participants, "I do not really know what impact the cyclone has had on Vanuatu."
48"I am speaking to you today with a heart that is so heavy," he said.
49"I stand to appeal on behalf of the government and the people to give a helping hand in this disaster."
50Morrison said the first priority was to ensure people had adequate food, drinking water and shelter.
51Beyond that, she said, there would need to be a long and concerted rebuilding effort in the months ahead.
52New Zealand pledged 1 million New Zealand dollars ($734,000) to help with relief efforts.
53On Sunday, Australia pledged 5 million Australian dollars ($3.8 million) in aid, and sent in a relief team with supplies including water and temporary shelters.
54The small island nation, located about a quarter of the way from Australia to Hawaii, has repeatedly warned it is already suffering devastating effects from climate change with the island's coastal areas being washed away, forcing resettlement to higher ground and smaller yields on traditional crops.
55Scientists say it's impossible to attribute single weather events like Cyclone Pam to climate change.
56The cyclone has already caused damage to other Pacific islands, including Kiribati and the Solomon Islands.
57Authorities in New Zealand are preparing for Cyclone Pam, which is forecast to pass north of the country on Sunday and Monday.
