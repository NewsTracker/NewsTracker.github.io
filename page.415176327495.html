4Like radio's acerbic Bickersons or television's feuding Honeymooners, Nuttall and Korody were terrorism's testy twosome - each verbally giving as good as they got as tension mounted and they drew closer to the planned July 1 bombing at the provincial legislature.
5In this unique trial, now entering its second month, no one is on the stand testifying in B.C. Supreme Court again this week.
6Instead, the jury on Monday was again watching and listening to a veritable epic-length movie of the alleged offences produced by an RCMP national security team over several months.
7They have seen an undercover Mountie pretending to be an Arab businessman buy the couple food, treat them as friends, drive them to buy parts for an explosive device, arrange a hotel room so they could build the bombs undisturbed, and encourage the jihadist attack in Victoria.
8Days of secret surveillance footage are being played of the elaborate sting that ended in the arrest of the pair, who have pleaded not guilty to four terrorism-related charges.
9Forget the right to silence, the couple is captured in their most intimate moments - retching in the toilet, arguing over who drank a missing can of pop, whispering tendernesses: Their jibes, boasts, doubts, hopes, dreams and, even, prayers.
10Throughout, the streaming conversation captured by the hidden cameras and microphones ranges from the banal - what will happen to their cat after martyrdom - to the chilling: rusty nails are used to ensure those wounded and maimed would later suffer from tetanus and infection.
11At the moment, in the prosecution's seeming documentary, it is June 28, 2013, and the pair is in a Delta motel room awaiting the arrival of the plot's linchpin - a Mr. Big-style character known as The Other Brother, who can provide explosives and guns.
12Like any good soap opera, suspense is building as Nuttall and Korody express misgivings about the murderous operation.
13Much of the time, the soundtrack is a playlist of nasheeds - the hypnotic Arabic chants or songs of blood and devotion that have been turned into a recruiting tool by the extremist group ISIS.
14An musical tradition dating from the 8th century, the nasheeds were resurrected as a form in the 1970s on cassette tapes circulated by the Muslim Brotherhood and others that featured sermons by radical imams.
15Osama bin Laden, for instance, belonged to a nasheed group.
16Along with the nasheeds, Nuttall and Korody also listen to Islamic lectures to improve their theological understanding.
17They want to make a good impression with The Other Brother, but Nuttall - who rehearses his rudimentary Arabic - is worried that with their continuing drug issues and wavering resolve, he may not want them.
18The Other Brother will not be impressed by "two junkies," he noted.
19"We're going to war. & Do you want to jihad or not?"
20Nuttall gleaned the plans for the homemade bombs from the al-Qaida Internet magazine, Inspire.
21His dream was to be part of a victorious, black-clad Islamic army.
22At one point, he mocked the security services and police for relying so heavily on forensic science and video security cameras to make arrests and win convictions.
23That's the reason they were being so careful, he explained to Korody - wearing gloves, hair coverings and cleaning everything connected with the pressure cookers - hypersensitive to leaving the slightest trace of DNA much less a latent fingerprint.
24Korody suggested wearing double gloves and he happily agreed.
25Gleeful at the expected frustration of future investigators, he added sarcastically, mimicking a police officer: "What, there's no video? There's no DNA? You mean our job's not done for us? Put an ad on the TV [asking people] to turn this man in and do our job for us."
26The trial was adjourned early Monday as Korody was feeling nauseous.
