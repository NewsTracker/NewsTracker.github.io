Israel's Netanyahu reportedly close to forming coalition

2With a little over 24 hours to go, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu is scrambling to get his governing coalition together.
3Netanyahu has had a tough time striking deals with potential partners to form a majority coalition in the 120-seat parliament.
4If he fails by midnight Wednesday then President Reuven Rivlin will task someone else with the job.
5With the clock ticking toward a midnight deadline, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu on Wednesday inched closer toward a deal to form a new government as he rushed to work out the details of an emerging agreement with his final coalition partner.
6A last-minute deal with the hawkish Jewish Home Party was necessary to prevent an almost-unimaginable scenario by which Netanyahu would be forced out of office.
7With Jewish Home, Netanyahu would secure a narrow 61-seat majority in the 120-seat parliament.
8If he fails by the end of the day, President Reuven Rivlin must appoint someone else to form a coalition.
9Israeli TV stations said Netanyahu's Likud and the Jewish Home were closing in on a deal that would give the Jewish Home control of the influential Justice Ministry.
10One person familiar with the talks said the sides were finalizing the details of the agreement, since Netanyahu is seeking to retain control over some of the ministry's responsibilities.
11The official, speaking on condition of anonymity because he was discussing secret negotiations, said a deal was expected before the midnight deadline.
12After Netanyahu's Likud Party won March 17 elections with 30 seats, it seemed he would have a relatively easy time forming a coalition.
13But talks stalled this week when Foreign Minister Avigdor Lieberman unexpectedly stepped down and announced his secular nationalist Yisrael Beitenu party was joining the opposition.
14That left Netanyahu dependent on Jewish Home leader Naftali Bennett, a former aide who has a rocky relationship with Netanyahu.
15Netanyahu has secured deals with three partners controlling 53 seats.
16They include Kulanu, a centrist party focused on economic issues, and two ultra-Orthodox religious parties.
17Fuming over the deals giving ultra-Orthodox parties ministerial power over religious services, Bennett was driving a hard bargain.
18Slated to serve as education minister, Bennett also demanded the job of justice minister for a senior party member, Ayelet Shaked, who is an outspoken critic of the country's judiciary.
19The Jewish Home party is linked to the West Bank settler movement.
20Despite its anger toward Netanyahu, it was unlikely to push for his ouster, a move that could open a potential path to government of the more dovish Zionist Union.
21But even if they reach a deal, Netanyahu would have a thin majority, leaving him vulnerable to the demands of any individual coalition lawmaker.
22A narrow coalition would have a difficult time passing economic reforms favored by Kulanu.
23It also would be averse to Palestinian peace moves and likely favor expanded settlement construction, putting it on a collision course with the international community.
24Likud officials concede such a government would not be effective or last long, raising speculation that Netanyahu will ultimately reach out to the Zionist Union and its leader, Isaac Herzog.
25He insists on serving as the opposition leader.
