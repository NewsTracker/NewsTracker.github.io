Orioles' return launches big sports week in Baltimore

1Ushers check fans' tickets as they enter Orioles Park at Camden Yards before a baseball game between the Baltimore Orioles and the Toronto Blue Jays, Monday, May 11, 2015, in Baltimore.
2Playing in front of their home fans for the first time in two weeks, the struggling Orioles hope a game at Camden Yards can serve as a pick-me-up for themselves and the beleaguered city.
3BALTIMORE (AP) - As the Baltimore Orioles prepared to play before their home fans for the first time in two weeks, the sights and sounds around Camden Yards on a lovely Monday afternoon were all about baseball.
4Vendors were hawking food, drinks and clothing.
5A few people were camped out near the home team parking lot, seeking autographs.
6About a half-dozen were looking to buy tickets at the window in the B&O Warehouse behind the center-field wall.
7There was one police car visible on the north edge of Camden Yards.
8It was a stark contrast to their last game in Baltimore.
9The city had been overrun by violence on April 27 after the funeral of Freddie Gray, a 25-year-old black man who died while in the custody of Baltimore police.
10Then in an unprecedented move, the Orioles hosted the Chicago White Sox on April 29 without allowing fans to enter.
11The Orioles had postponed two games against the White Sox before their no-fan game, then then played three "home" games against the Rays in Florida.
12The Orioles say they took steps to ramp up security before Monday's game against Toronto, but there were no protesters outside the gates before the contest.
13There were no helicopters soaring overhead.
14No angry people throwing bottles.
15No rows of police in riot gear.
16The focus seemed to be all on baseball, with an eye on horse racing.
17Monday also marked the beginning of Preakness Week, beginning the buildup to Saturday's Triple Crown horse race.
18Track officials say good weather could lead to record attendance numbers at Pimlico Race Course.
19The Orioles hoped that hosting a baseball game might have a positive effect on a city still very much in recovery mode - and get the week off to a positive start.
20"We understand it's just a game and there are other bigger things going on out there," Orioles reliever Zach Britton said.
21"If anything, the fact that we can come home and play again and have our fans here, maybe that will get some people's focus off what was going on out there. Just enjoy baseball again."
22The vendors outside the park were happy to be back at work.
23"I missed out on a lot of money," said Danny Little, who was selling hot dogs, sausages, sodas and chips.
24"And I got a 3-month-year-old daughter."
25Looking back to two weeks earlier, when the rioting started in earnest and caused the first postponement of the series against the White Sox, Little recalled, "We were watching the TVs when they were lighting fires and all that. We flipped the tables and all that waiting for them to come here."
26This day, however, was just right for a baseball game.
27"I think it'll be a nice atmosphere tonight with all that's been going on," said Steve Presgraves, wearing an Orioles jersey and standing near the ticket booth.
28"It's definitely good for the city, and I think it's going to be, just a good time, a nice crowd and everything."
