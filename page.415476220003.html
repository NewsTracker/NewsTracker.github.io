2Citizen Kane has been called the best film ever made.
3It was also at the center of an epic battle of egos.
4The main character was modeled after media titan William Randolph Hearst, who in real life tried ruthlessly to keep the movie from being released.
5Hearst Castle, the estate of newspaper publisher William Randolph Hearst, hosted its first-ever screening of Citizen Kane on Friday.
6Hearst hated the movie, and never allowed it to be shown there during his lifetime.
7Almost 75 years later, the family has called a truce, of sorts: This weekend, Citizen Kane was screened for the first time inside the millionaire's legendary home, the Hearst Castle.
8Back in 1941, the "boy wonder" Orson Welles was about to release his first feature film.
9RKO Pictures had given the 26-year-old director the cinematic keys to the castle: complete creative control to make whatever movie he wanted.
10The movie was Citizen Kane, the story of power-hungry and tragic Charles Foster Kane and his castle on the hill, Xanadu.
11Everyone knew that Charles Foster Kane was a stand-in for William Randolph Hearst, and Xanadu for Hearst's Castle in San Simeon, on the California coast.
12It was a place that defined the word decadent: 165 rooms, a quarter-million acres.
13Hearst had entire 15th-century ceilings imported from Europe.
14He packed the mansion with art and turned part of the property into the world's largest private zoo.
15Wild zebras still roam the grounds.
16Above is the Doge's Suite, a guest room in the Hearst Castle inspired by the Doge's Palace in Venice.
17Hearst was America's first media mogul, dominating newspapers, magazines, newsreels and movies.
18While he started his career on the left, by the 1930s Hearst had hired Benito Mussolini and Adolf Hitler as paid columnists, and he tried to destroy the New Deal.
19He became a villain to a new generation of lefties, including Orson Welles.
20The castle living room, pictured in 1958, shortly before it opened to tourists.
21When word got out that Welles had taken aim at Hearst, Hearst fired right back, first threatening an advertising blackout.
22Then, in an era long before TMZ, Hearst threatened worse than a blackout: bad publicity for the movie stars of RKO Pictures.
23"They said look, if you show this film, we are going to tell the life stories of lots of RKO people," says David Nasaw, author of The Chief: The Life of William Randolph Hearst.
24"And we're going to take the same liberties when we tell those stories as Welles has taken with Hearst."
25Yet after all of that, the studio still took the plunge and released Citizen Kane.
26It was a hit with critics, but bombed at the box office.
27The movie was most definitely not shown inside Hearst's lush, private theater.
28A few years ago, however, Citizen Kane was played at the Hearst visitor center, down the hill from the castle.
29Then this year, Hearst's great-grandson Stephen, a vice president for the Hearst Corp., gave the thumbs-up to a screening in the mansion, for the San Luis Obispo Film Festival - raising eyebrows and questions among people who knew about the famous feud.
30"One of them hit me straight on," Stephen Hearst recalls, "and said, 'Do you think your great-grandfather would be rolling in his grave?' And I let him know that, based on my current responsibilities, I also have control of the mausoleum, and if necessary, I can check."
31Hearst's descendants still get to use the castle, with its Roman pool and tennis courts.
32They also have the name - still at No.
336 on the Forbes list of America's most wealthy families.
34But the estate is now, of all things, a state park, and about 750,000 non-Hearsts come to ogle it every year.
35Not surprisingly, the mansion takes a lot of resources to maintain; the party's $1,000-a-pop tickets will help.
36While he agrees the movie is a classic, Hearst biographer David Nasaw says that Charles Foster Kane and William Randolph Hearst shouldn't stay linked in the public imagination.
37"One of the reasons why Citizen Kane is dreadful biography and dreadful history is that it presents Hearst as a failure, as a bitter, nasty old man," Nasaw says.
38"Hearst had a great life. I mean he made lots and lots of enemies. But along the way, he had a grand time!"

4LONDON (AP) - A former hostage held in Syria by Islamic State extremists for more than six months has described mock executions and other forms of psychological torture against him and other captives.
5Spanish journalist Javier Espinosa wrote in The Sunday Times that militant Mohammed Emwazi played a lead role in the mock executions.
6Emwazi, a Londoner nicknamed "Jihadi John," is believed to have played a central role in a series of beheading videos.
7Espinosa, who works for El Mundo and was on assignment for the Spanish daily when he was abducted in September 2013, characterized Emwazi as a bloodthirsty psychopath who enjoyed threatening him and other Western hostages.
8He said Emwazi "caressed" his neck with a long blade and described how he would behead Espinosa: "'Feel it? Cold, isn't it? Can you imagine the pain you'll feel when it cuts? Unimaginable pain,'" he quoted Emwazi as saying.
9Emwazi went on to describe precisely how the beheading would occur, the former hostage said, and explained where he would place Espinosa's head once it had been done.
10Espinosa described how the militants took pleasure in telling their more than 20 hostages each day that they would be beheaded.
11He said Emwazi used an antique sword for the mock executions and followed up by placing a Glock pistol against his head and pulling the trigger three times.
12Espinosa said he and the other hostages dubbed three English jihadis who were part of the Islamic Stage group "The Beatles" - even though the world-famous Liverpool band had four members.
13The nickname "Jihadi John" - which later caught on in the British press - is a reference to former Beatle John Lennon.
14Espinosa was released in March 2014 after being held captive for 194 days.
15Most of the hostages were eventually released, but seven have died, including at least six who were killed, and one is known to still be a captive.
16Marc Marginedas, a second Spanish journalist who was freed at the same as Espinosa, gave his account in the Periodico de Catalunya newspaper in Spain Sunday.
17He said he had explained to his captors that he wasn't a spy and had entered Syria twice before to report and had been treated well.
18"'You entered Syria twice and it worked out well for you, but now we'll kill you,'" was the reply from his captor.
