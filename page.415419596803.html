N.H. voters get first look at Jeb Bush, Scott Walker

Ferguson mayor, under fire, says he shouldn't be held accountable

1Jeb Bush and Scott Walker are going out on their first dates with GOP activists in New Hampshire, hoping to make a good impression this weekend with the political leaders who can help their presidential prospects.
2The two leading Republican hopefuls in the 2016 race arrive Friday.
3Bush, the former Florida governor, kicks off his first political visit to New Hampshire in 15 years with a business roundtable discussion at the Nashua Chamber of Commerce.
4Then he's off to a house party hosted by Fergus Cullen, a former New Hampshire GOP chairman who hasn't endorsed in the still-developing campaign.
5Walker, the Wisconsin governor making his first Granite State visit since 2012, plans to meet with a host of key leaders such as John Sununu Sr., Scott Brown and Manchester Mayor Ted Gatsas before speaking Saturday at a training event for the state GOP.
6Compared with 2012, when Mitt Romney seemed to have a lock on winning the New Hampshire GOP primary, the still-emerging 2016 race is wide open.
7Bush was at the top of a WMUR-Granite State poll taken in February with 17% support, with Walker right behind him with 12% support.
8Making a connection is key in a state where retail politicking is a requirement.
9Dante Scala, a political science professor at the University of New Hampshire, said it's the little things that count to New Hampshire voters.
10For Bush that means saying " 'I'm here to court voters. I'm here to answer questions. I'm not running a coronation,' " Scala said.
11"He has to take time to listen to voters. He can't just breeze in and out."
12In the WMUR-Granite State poll, nearly half (46%) of the state's voters say they don't know enough about Walker, which suggests he's got room to grow.
13Unlike Bush or Rand Paul, who has a pre-existing relationship with the New Hampshire voters who supported his father's 2012 presidential bid, Walker has a tougher job of introducing himself this weekend.
14"He has to live up to the hype in some fashion," Scala said about Walker.
15"There will be a fair number of conservative to very conservative voters in New Hampshire who are searching for the opponent who can knock off Jeb Bush."
16Bush and Walker aren't the only GOP hopefuls giving New Hampshire some love this weekend.
17Rick Perry, who arrived Thursday, will make stops in four towns on Friday, and Ted Cruz arrives Sunday for a two-day political trip.
1FERGUSON, Mo.
2-- The top elected official here says he had limited authority to tackle enormous problems and quell racial tensions.
3"I make $350 a month before taxes for being part-time mayor of Ferguson," Mayor James Knowles told USA TODAY.
4"You want to hold me accountable for not knowing that some employees were sending racist e-mails. I have no executive authority. I have no administrative authority. The charter doesn't allow me to hire, fire or even give direction to city employees."
5Ferguson's city manager, police chief and municipal judge have been forced out of their jobs, after a Justice Department probe published earlier this month found that Ferguson police and the city's municipal court had engaged in systemic patterns of misconduct.
6Knowles said he's dedicated to helping this city turn around as it continues to grapple with the fallout of the Aug. 9 death of Michael Brown, an unarmed black teenager, who was fatally shot by a white police officer.
7Brown's killing spurred months of occasionally violent protests.
8Knowles, who claimed early in the protests that Ferguson has no racial divide, has had a bumpy ride and remains under fierce scrutiny.
9He said his supporters have told him that there's a possible mayoral recall effort underway.
10In the days since the resignation of the top officials - as well as a few lower level Ferguson cops and bureaucrats - protesters and some community leaders have turned the attention to Knowles, saying that he must resign, and that the city should disband its police department and build it anew to help the city heal.
11He said he has been told by supporters that they have been approached to gather signatures to begin a mayoral recall.
12On Wednesday, two police officers were wounded outside the Ferguson Police Department at the end of a protest following the announcement that Police Chief Tom Jackson was stepping down.
13No suspects have been identified.
14"Dismantling the police department would be the ultimate thing to do - outside of the mayor leaving," said state Rep.
15Courtney Curtis, a Democratic lawmaker whose district includes Ferguson.
16"I don't think we have time. I don't think the patience is there. The damage has been done and the damage is being done each day they drag their feet."
17But Knowles questioned what good his resigning would do.
18"I realize everybody is out there singing for ceremonial heads to be rolling," said Knowles, who indicated many Ferguson residents have pleaded with him to stay in the job.
19"So you're going to put another council member in my place?"
20While activists and some lawmakers are pressing for quick and widespread reform in how the city operates its police and courts, the Justice Department is likely, if past behavior is any precedent, to push Ferguson to make substantive reforms.
21But it will also give this St. Louis suburb some space to enact them.
22Under Attorney General Eric Holder, who is soon expected to step down from his post, the Justice Department has launched more than 20 investigations of police departments - including in cities such as Cleveland, Miami and Portland -- for patterns of misconduct that have suggested police brutality, abuse of the mentally ill and racial bias.
23But in all those cases, the police departments have agreed to make changes that line up with the Justice Department recommendations.
24In a 102-page report it released on March 4, the Justice Department called for a litany of changes in how Ferguson goes about policing.
25Some of the reforms call for the city to embrace community policing techniques which would allow officers to build relationships in the neighborhoods they patrol, reorient officers' approach to using force, and provide recurring training to all officers that racial profiling and other forms of discriminatory policing won't be tolerated.
26At the heart of Ferguson's police problems was a department that was more focused on raising revenue through tickets and fines than actually looking out for the safety of residents, the Justice Department report concluded.
27Holder suggested on Thursday that he was pleased with some of the early steps taken by Ferguson officials, noting "we have begun to see really important signs of progress."
28Knowles said that Ferguson officials are expected to meet with Justice Department officials in the coming weeks to start setting a path forward.
29"Whatever we do has to be sustainable for the community, and not financially bankrupt us," Knowles said.
30"Obviously, if the Department of Justice wants to bankrupt us with huge burdens ... they could."
31Mark Byrne, a city council member, said Ferguson officials had started addressing some of the issues that the Justice Department raises in its new report soon after protesters began taking to the streets following Brown's killing last August.
32The court revamped how fines and fees are handled, including creating a special docket for those who have garnered financial penalties from the municipal court for failure to appear.
33The failure-to-appear fines were blamed for creating a vicious circle for poor people of color, who were disproportionately targeted by police officers for small infractions and could not afford to pay their fines.
34The city council also passed an ordinance that caps the amount of its budget that can come from municipal court revenue to 15%.
35In addition, the city council has taken steps to create a citizen review board, which will advise and review operations of the Ferguson Police Department.
36The board will be made up of citizens currently not involved in government.
37Ferguson has also established a scholarship program to help recruit minority candidates to attend the police academy and sought out other partnerships to help provide college scholarships and mentoring in the city's African-American community.
38"Anybody that is still saying that the city isn't doing everything it can to right this ship what exactly is it that we can do that we're not doing?"
39Byrne said.
40Ashley Yates, who has been among the more prominent activists to take part in the Ferguson movement, said it is also crucial that Ferguson disband the police department and rehire its force.
41Such a move, which was used in recent years in the nearby city of Jennings, Mo., when it was dealing with endemic issues of corruption, was not among the proposals offered by the Justice Department.
42City officials also are pushing back against the suggestion.
43"It seemed a no-brainer to me to fire a police chief who fumbled the (Mike Brown) case from Day One," Yates said.
44"It seemed like a no-brainer to me to get rid of a city manager who'd been hiding in the midst the crisis, and yet they didn't do it until recently."
45But Byrne said that the push to disband the police department is one that is largely advocated by outsiders.
46"I am hearing overwhelmingly - and from every single candidate that is running for city council - that the better approach is to keep the police department we have and reform," Byrne said.
47"At the end of the day if we can have the absolutely best trained police force in conflict resolution, in diversity training, in use of force, in use of tasers and all of those things Who can argue against that?"
48While Ferguson has lost some of its highest profile leaders, the city is managing to muddle through by spreading out the duties among other officials.
49Municipal court hearings were canceled this week and cases are going to be handled by a state court judge.
50Byrne said it may take some time for the city council to properly vet and hire a new city manager.
51The council may seek to hire a temporary manager through a contracting firm until a permanent hire can be made, he said.
52Under Ferguson's charter, the city manager is charged with hiring the police chief.
53There's plenty of interest in both jobs.
54"I've already received e-mails from candidates inquiring about jobs," he said.
55"I'm sure my colleagues have, too."
