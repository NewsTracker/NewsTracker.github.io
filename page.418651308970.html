At training camp, children told to behead dolls as Islamic State militants mold new generation

1SANLIURFA, Turkey - The children had all been shown videos of beheadings and told by their trainers with the Islamic State group that they would perform one someday.
2First, they had to practice technique.
3The more than 120 boys were each given a doll and a sword and told, cut off its head.
4A 14-year-old who was among the boys, all abducted from Iraq's Yazidi religious minority, said he couldn't cut it right.
5He chopped once, twice, three times.
6"Then they taught me how to hold the sword, and they told me how to hit. They told me it was the head of the infidels," the boy, renamed Yahya by his IS captors, told The Associated Press last week in northern Iraq, where he fled after escaping the IS training camp.
7When Islamic State extremists overran Yazidi towns in northern Iraq last year, they butchered older men and enslaved many of the women and girls.
8Dozens of young Yazidi boys like Yahya had a different fate: The IS sought to re-educate them.
9They forced them to convert to Islam from their ancient faith and tried to turn them into jihadi fighters.
10It is part of a concerted effort by the extremists to build a new generation of militants, according to AP interviews with residents who fled or still live under IS in Syria and Iraq.
11The group is recruiting teens and children using gifts, threats, and brainwashing.
12Boys have been turned into executioners and suicide bombers.
13An IS video issued last week showed a boy beheading a Syrian soldier under an adult militant's supervision.
14Last month, a video showed 25 children unflinchingly shooting 25 captured Syrian soldiers in the head.
15In schools and mosques, militants infuse children with extremist doctrine, often turning them against their own parents.
16Fighters in the street befriend children with toys.
17IS training camps churn out the Ashbal, Arabic for "lion cubs," child fighters for the "caliphate" that IS declared across its territory.
18The caliphate is a historic form of Islamic rule that the group claims to be reviving with its own radical interpretation, though the vast majority of Muslims reject its claims.
19"I am terribly worried about future generations," said Abu Hafs Naqshabandi, a Syrian sheikh who runs religion classes for refugees in the Turkish city of Sanliurfa to counter IS ideology.
20The indoctrination mainly targets Sunni Muslim children.
21In IS-held towns, militants show young people videos at street booths.
22They hold outdoor events for children, distributing soft drinks and candy - and propaganda.
23They tell adults, "We have given up on you, we care about the new generation," said an anti-IS activist who fled the Syrian city of Raqqa, the caliphate's de facto capital.
24He spoke on condition of anonymity to preserve the safety of relatives under IS rule.
25With the Yazidis, whom IS considers heretics ripe for slaughter, the group sought to take another community's youth, erase their past and replace it with radicalism.
26Yahya, his little brother, their mother and hundreds of Yazidis were captured when IS seized the Iraqi town of Sulagh in August.
27They were taken to Raqqa, where the brothers and other Yazidi boys aged 8 to 15 were put in the Farouq training camp.
28They were given Muslim Arabic names to replace their Kurdish names.
29Yahya asked that AP not use his real name for his and his family's safety.
30He spent nearly five months there, training 8 to 10 hours a day, including exercises, weapons drills and Quran studies.
31They told him Yazidis are "dirty" and should be killed, he said.
32They showed him how to shoot someone execution-style, from close range.
33The boys hit each other in some exercises.
34Yahya punched his 10-year-old brother, knocking out a tooth.
35The trainer "said if I didn't do it, he'd shoot me," he said.
36"They ... told us it would make us tougher. They beat us everywhere."
37In an IS video of Farouq camp, boys in camouflage do calisthenics and shout slogans.
38An IS fighter says the boys have studied jihad so "in the coming days God Almighty can put them in the front lines to battle the infidels."
39Videos from other camps show boys crawling under barbed wire and practicing shooting.
40One kid lies on the ground and fires a machine gun; he's so small the recoil bounces his entire body back a few inches.
41Boys undergoing endurance training stand unmoving as a trainer hits their heads with a pole.
42IS claims to have hundreds of such camps.
43The Syrian Observatory for Human Rights documented at least 1,100 Syrian children under 16 who joined IS this year.
44At least 52 were killed in fighting, including eight suicide bombers, it said.
45Yahya escaped in early March.
46Fighters left the camp to carry out an attack, and as remaining guards slept he and his brother slipped away, he said.
47He urged a friend to come too, but he refused, saying he was a Muslim now and liked Islam.
48Yahya's mother was in a house nearby with other abducted Yazidis - he had occasionally been allowed to visit her.
49So he and his brother went there.
50They travelled to the Syrian city of Minbaj and stayed with a Russian IS fighter, Yahya said.
51He contacted an uncle in Iraq, who negotiated to pay the Russian for the two boys and their mother.
52A deal struck, they met the uncle in Turkey then went to the Iraqi Kurdish city of Dohuk.
53Now in Dohuk, Yahya and his brother spend much of their time watching TV.
54They appear outgoing and social.
55But traces of their ordeal show.
56When his uncle handed Yahya a pistol, the boy deftly assembled and loaded it.
57And he will never forget the videos of beheadings IS trainers showed the boys.
