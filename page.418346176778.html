Greece faces 'Grexit' from euro: economist

1FRANKFURT, Germany -- Greece is at the last chance saloon, thirsty and out of credit.
2Next stop could be the badlands of euro exit.
3Without a deal on more bailout loans, the heavily indebted country faces looming financial hurdles in the coming days.
4If it stumbles, it could leave the shared currency in a chaotic mess.
5A resounding "no" vote in a referendum Sunday on the tough conditions attached to more loans leaves Athens at odds with its main creditors - the other eurozone governments, led by Germany.
6"Greece is in limbo and is sliding fast toward Grexit," said Berenberg Bank economist Holger Schmieding, using the shorthand for Greek exit.
7Here are questions and answers on what Greece faces next.
8Q: What happens first?
9A: The politicians will try to re-start bailout talks - not easy, after Greek voters rejected the last formal offer from the creditors.
10Meanwhile, the creditors' resistance to softening terms remains high.
11There are concerns that rewarding Greek obstinacy will mean it and other indebted countries that need financial assistance will also resist spending cuts and economic reforms in the future.
12Greece, which overspent for years until its troubles became clear in 2009, has already been granted 240 billion euros in loans from the other eurozone countries.
13But the spending restraint demanded as a condition for the loans hurt economic growth, and reforms to make Greece more business-friendly have been slower than hoped.
14Chancellor Angela Merkel from Germany meets French President Francois Hollande on Monday evening.
15That's followed by a meeting of eurozone finance ministers on Tuesday, and a full summit of the leaders of the 19 euro countries that evening.
16After months of talks, the basic issues are well known, so the rejected deal, with tweaks, could serve as a basis for some kind of agreement.
17Greece's previous bailout line of credit expired June 30 before a deal could be reached to tap the last payout.
18So a new one will have to be negotiated.
19That could take time.
20Germany's parliament, for instance, would have to vote to approve a new negotiating mandate just to begin.
21And time is short.
22Schmieding said any new offer to Greece "will be at least as tough as the offer Greece rejected."
23That offer included painful increases in value-added tax paid at the point of sale by consumers, and reductions in state pensions.
24James Nixon, chief European economist at Oxford Economics, says there's "a narrow trajectory from here that sees an emboldened Greek parliament accepting the need for reform in return for a debt write-down."
25Q: What happens while we wait for talks to get started again?
26A: The most pressing question is the country's banks, which closed a week ago after the European Central Bank refused to let them tap more emergency credit.
27Banks needed the credit to replace deposits as Greeks pulled their savings out, fearing it would be lost in a bank collapse or changed into a new currency that is worth less.
28ATM withdrawals are limited to 60 euros ($66) a day to keep banks from collapsing.
29Suppliers are demanding business pay cash up front, making normal commerce impossible.
30The banks have barely enough money left to meet limited withdrawals.
31The ECB is to review the situation Monday, but likely won't turn the tap back on unless it is convinced Greek banks are solvent and it's not pouring taxpayer money into a black hole.
32The banks' health depends on the government's finances, since the banks hold government bonds and much of their capital consists of future tax breaks - money that vanishes if the government goes bankrupt.
33So the ECB may wait on the politicians to see if a bailout deal for the government takes shape.
34The bank's preference is for the politicians to come up with a solution.
35The ECB faces tough choices.
36It doesn't want to pull the plug on Greece.
37But it doesn't to pour in more money only to lose it.
38Schmieding says for now the ECB "will play a holding game."
39A: Perhaps the biggest drop-dead date is July 20.
40That's the day Greece must pay 3.5 billion euros on a bond held by the European Central Bank.
41If it doesn't pay, the ECB could withdraw all the emergency credit, collapsing Greece's banking system.
42Many analysts think that would result in Greece leaving the euro.
43If the government defaults to the ECB itself, the thinking goes, it would be impossible to deny the government is bankrupt.
44And that could extend to the banks that are tied to it.
45In that case, the ECB could not only cap its credit, as it has done so far, but withdraw the existing 89 billion euros it has already extended to Greek banks.
46With the banks broke, the ECB's supervisory arm would likely order that they either be wound down or saved with new investment.
47Yet Greece doesn't have enough euros for a bank rescue.
48So, barring a deal with creditors to refloat the banks, it would have to print a new currency.
49Q: So they've got until July 20?
50A: Not necessarily.
51The government is running out of euros to pay its regular bills such as paychecks to government employees and pensions.
52If it starts handing them IOUs instead, that could be the first step toward a new currency.
53It would take months to actually print new money and get it into circulation.
54Meanwhile, every week the banks are closed the recession gets worse, tax revenues fall, and the amount of help Greece will need rises.
55If Grexit is to be avoided, the country needs some kind of a deal.
