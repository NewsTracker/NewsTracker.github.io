Kremlin critic Navalny released after 15 days in custody

Ukraine Conflict Tops Agenda Of EU Meeting In Riga

5MOSCOW (AP) - Leading Russian opposition figure Alexei Navalny was released Friday after spending 15 days in custody and vowed that he and his supporters will not be intimidated by the slaying of a top Kremlin critic.
6Navalny, the driving force behind the 2011-2012 mass protests in Moscow, said the killing of opposition leader Boris Nemtsov will not force him to scale down his campaign against President Vladimir Putin's government.
7"This act of terror hasn't achieved its goal, it will not frighten me or my comrades," Navalny said after his release.
8"We will not reduce our efforts, we will not step back."
9Navalny walked free after serving the sentence handed down by a Moscow court, which found him guilty of violating the law when he distributed leaflets in the subway campaigning for an opposition rally.
10The protest was turned into a mourning march to pay tributes to Nemtsov.
11Nemtsov, 55, a former deputy prime minister and one of Putin's most vehement critics, was killed just outside the Kremlin hours after a radio interview in which he denounced the president for his "mad, aggressive" policies in Ukraine.
12Before his death, Nemtsov was working on a report about Russian involvement in the war in eastern Ukraine.
13No suspects have been detained in the killing, despite an reward of 3 million rubles (nearly $50,000) offered for information related to the case.
14Kremlin critics say the spiteful nationalist propaganda on state television, which cast Nemtsov and other liberals as Western stooges, helped prepare the ground for his killing.
15Many believe that his shooting death in a tightly secured area near the Kremlin wouldn't have been possible without official involvement, and could be an attempt to scare other government foes.
16Putin dubbed Nemtsov's killing a "provocation."
17The nation's top investigative agency echoed that comment, saying it was looking into whether Nemtsov had been a "sacrificial victim" to destabilize Russia.
18It said it was also investigating whether Islamic extremism, the Ukraine conflict and Nemtsov's personal life were possible motives.
19A court rejected Navalny's plea for early release to attend Nemtsov's funeral Tuesday.
20Navalny was convicted in late December of fraud and given a -year suspended sentence and ordered to remain under house arrest until his appeals were exhausted.
21He has repeatedly violated his house arrest since then.
22Navalny's brother was imprisoned under the verdict, which many see as a vendetta by the Kremlin.
1The two days of informal talks started on March 6 amid heightened tension over Moscow's support for separatists in Ukraine and the implementation of the Minsk cease-fire agreement between Ukrainian government forces and the rebels.
2More than 6,000 people have been killed since fighting erupted in eastern Ukraine in April 2014, after Russia annexed Ukraine's southern Crimea Peninsula in March.
3Kyiv and the West accuse Russia of deploying troops and weaponry in Ukraine's east, which Moscow denies.
4Ahead of the Riga meeting, EU foreign affairs chief Federica Mogherini said the European Union is ready to step up sanctions against Russia and separatist leaders if necessary but that the implementation of the truce deal reached in Minsk on February 12 is "the way to go forward."
5Speaking about the fragile cease-fire between the warring sides, Mogherini said, "For sure the trend is a positive one, even if not perfect."
6She said the key issues were to monitor the cease-fire, especially the withdrawal of heavy weapons from the front line by both sides, and to strengthen the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe (OSCE) which monitors implementation on the ground.
7German Foreign Minister Frank-Walter Steinmeier said the Riga meeting would discuss how to help the OSCE monitoring implementation on the ground, and that more needs to be done to make the cease-fire accord work.
8"At the moment, we are trying to accelerate again the process."
9Steinmeier said.
10"That is the issue in the foreground and not the question if there will be new sanctions."
11In Warsaw, visiting British Foreign Secretary Philip Hammond urged the West to prepare "a whole range of potential sanctions options, so that we have maximum flexibility, maximum agility and maximum speed in reacting to any provocation."
12Hammond and Polish Foreign Minister Grzegorz Schetyna said excluding Russia from the SWIFT international electronic-payments system would be a measure of last resort if the crisis in Ukraine escalates.
13"When it comes to sanctions, SWIFT is the nuclear weapon," Schetyna said.
14Hammond said discussion on the option was taking place "primarily in the U.S."
15Also on March 6, the European Union removed four Ukrainians from a sanctions list due to lack of evidence.
16The EU list, announced in March 2014, had targeted 22 Ukrainian officials suspected of embezzling state funds and illegally transferring the funds outside Ukraine.
17The EU has frozen their assets held in the 28-nation bloc.
18Latvia, which currently chairs the EU Council, its Baltic neighbors Estonia and Lithuania, and other NATO members have voiced concern over what they see as Russia's increasingly aggressive attitude.
19The alliance has increased its military presence in the Baltic states, holding military drills and training exercises.
20Lithuania announced last month it would renew military conscription for the first time since 2008 after a rise in exercises by the Russian military and overflights along its border.
21On March 5, Russia announced that more than 2,000 servicemen were taking part in "large-scale" air-defense exercises in southern Russia, Crimea, and the breakaway Georgian regions of Abkhazia and South Ossetia.
22In Kyiv, Ukraine's military said government forces were withdrawing Uragan (Hurricane) multiple-launch rocket systems from the front line in accordance with the Minsk agreement.
23The sides in the conflict have given assurances they were complying with pullback requirements, but OSCE monitors complain that they are being obstructed from conducting exhaustive verifications.
24Exchanges of fire continue to be reported daily in eastern Ukraine.
25Ukrainian military spokesman Andriy Lysenko said four soldiers had been wounded in the past 24 hours.
26He also accused separatists of mustering weapons and manpower in several areas in preparation for potential offensives.
27In the government-controlled eastern city of Kharkiv, a senior special police officer who is loyal to Kyiv has been injured along with his wife in a car-bomb blast.
28Also on March 6, Britain said it planned to supply Ukraine's army with non-lethal military equipment to help it defend itself against what Defense Secretary Michael Fallon called "Russia's aggression."
29The equipment, worth $1.3 million, will be gifted to Ukraine in the coming weeks and includes first aid kits, night vision goggles, laptop computers, helmets, and GPS units.
