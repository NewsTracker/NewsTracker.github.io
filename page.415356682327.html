3We've bathed, read and rocked our way into different routines, to no avail.

5In my son's defense, I was (and, let's be honest, am still) the same way.
6The struggle is real.
7I don't like going to bed any more than he does.
8Bedtime around here is the time I feel my husband's absence the most.
9We generally tag team dishes and bath, so once the kids are asleep we can enjoy some precious alone time (we also have a 4-year-old daughter).
10With him gone, I keep holding out hope for a fairy godmother to come do the dishes and fold laundry while I handle bedtime.
11After several snow days last week, getting both of my babies to sleep was particularly stressful.
12My little guy would not stay in bed.
13The sound of those size 9 extra-wide feet running on the wood floor reverberates through the house.
14I'd already gone through our routine and I'd already been up an extra time to tuck him in.
15About the fifth time he got out of bed, I went up the stairs prepared to use my scary mommy voice.
16I paused about halfway up, took a deep breath, and reminded myself that getting angry at him would not make my night any easier.
17I walked into his room, where he was standing on top of his (bolted down) dresser.
18I put both hands on my head, and felt myself literally trying to pull my hair out.
19Another deep breath.
20I gently picked him up, placed him on the floor and knelt beside him.
21Trying to stifle my exasperation with the only ounce of patience I had left, I asked him, "What is going on?"
22His answer took me by surprise.
23He burst into tears.
24"I miss my daddy," he wailed.
25I pulled him close, and despite my best effort, felt my eyes well up also.
26"I know, bud. I miss him too," I whispered.
27Back into bed, I snuggled next to him, quietly reminding him just how much his daddy loves him.
28His little fingers intertwined with mine, I stayed long after he fell asleep.
29As I studied his beautiful face in the silence, I replayed that scene, over and over again.
30He's not even 3; could he possibly understand those emotions?
31Was he "gaming the system"?
32Did he use my loneliness against me?
33And did I, by giving in at that moment, guarantee more of the same?
34I reached out to Dorinda Silver Williams at Zero to Three, the National Center for Infants, Toddlers and Families.
35Dr. Williams is their director of Military Family Projects, and recently sent me a few picture books to help my children better understand their father's frequent underways, and soon, his nearly yearlong deployment.
36Parenting young children is hard.
37There's just no way around it.
38Our infants and toddlers are not yet equipped with the cognitive or emotional skills to manage the everyday stressors of daily life, let alone handle those more challenging events, such as a separation from a beloved parent.
39And so they turn to us, borrowing from our own coping skills, to make sense and meaning out of a situation that they can neither control, nor understand.
40If only our youngest children had the language and insight to fully articulate what they were experiencing.
41"Excuse me, Mommy. I'm a little confused over all of the changes going on around here. I know that you've already got a lot on your plate right now, but if you could just help me to understand the situation and manage my feelings, I'd really appreciate it."
42If only.
43Instead, our youngest children primarily communicate their feelings through their behaviors.
44And in times of stress, those behaviors - crying, biting, clinginess, withdrawal, eating or sleeping difficulties, or a return to earlier behaviors - can wreak havoc on the nerves of anyone, let alone a parent who is already feeling physically and emotionally fatigued.
45And yet, in the face of this exhausting exchange, something quite extraordinary took place.
46For your strength and calm, you were rewarded with a rare response from a 2-year-old, half-drunk on adrenaline and fatigue.
47He helped you connect the dots between his external and internal world.
48With his proclamation, "I miss Daddy," he was heard.
49And yet, we all know that's no easy feat.
50It's hard to be the calm center amidst the whirling dervish of our toddler's tantrum.
51It's hard to give our all to our baby, when we're practically running on fumes.
52By taking a reflective pause, just long enough to wonder about the powerful emotions beneath your son's attempt at an all-nighter, you were able to respond with empathy, rather than exasperation.
53What a gift to you both.
54Who needs a fairy godmother with a response like that?
55Now if she could just teach my toddlers to fold the laundry.
