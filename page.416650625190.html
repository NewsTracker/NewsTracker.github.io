Pictures of movie theatre carnage shown for 1st time at James Holmes shooting trial

1The bodies lay where they fell, sprawled on steps or wedged between rows of seats, surrounded by spent ammunition, scattered popcorn and shoes left behind in the panic to escape.
2Prosecutors showed jurors graphic crime scene photos Thursday in the Colorado theater shooting trial, the first time images of the bloodshed were made public since the July 20, 2012, attack.
3One juror briefly turned his head after looking at one of the photos displayed on a video screen.
4Most jurors studied the images intently but showed no emotion.
5Some spectators in the gallery wept.
6A woman broke down in sobs and left the courtroom.
7Twelve people were killed and 70 injured in attack at the Century 16 theater in the Denver suburb of Aurora.
8Ten victims died at the theater, while the other two were pronounced dead at hospitals.
9If James Holmes - who has admitted he was the shooter - felt any reaction to the photos, it wasn't visible.
10He watched from the defense table, where he is tethered to the floor by a harness and cable under his street clothes.
11It was a grim ending to the first short week of the trial, which won't resume until Monday to accommodate plans that one juror made before the case got underway.
12The photos added still more emotional weight to the prosecution's case.
13Since testimony began Tuesday, jurors have heard victims describe the burning pain of gunshot wounds and the agony of watching loved ones collapse before their eyes.
14Police officers - some so overwhelmed by grief they had to pause to pull themselves together - described rushing to hospitals with gasping victims in their patrol cars.
15Holmes pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity to multiple counts of murder and attempted murder.
16Defense attorneys say his mind is so distorted by schizophrenia that he didn't know right from wrong.
17If the jury finds he was insane, he would be committed indefinitely to the state mental hospital.
18Prosecutors have described Holmes as calculating and smart, and they say he believed killing others increased his self-worth.
19They are asking jurors to convict him of murder and sentence him to be executed.
20Police officers testified Thursday that Holmes seemed keenly interested in the attack's aftermath, peering out the window of a squad car as injured victims were treated nearby.
21He first wore a vacant expression and seemed calm and detached - but sweaty and smelly - after police handcuffed him in the parking lot behind the theater, the officers said.
22But when they placed him in the police car near a back door to the theater, "he would look around like he was taking it all in," Aurora police officer Jason Oviatt said.
23It was a tumultuous scene, with emergency responders treating the wounded and loading them into police cars to be rushed to hospitals because no ambulances had arrived.
24"He would sort of look around whenever a car went speeding past or when there was something else going on, somebody shouting outside the car," Oviatt said.
25Prosecutors called four law-enforcement officers as witnesses Thursday.
26For the first time since testimony began, the defense cross-examined some of them.
27Questioned by defense lawyer Daniel King, the officers said Holmes had disheveled, reddish-orange hair, that his pupils were extremely dilated and that he appeared disoriented and stared off into space when he was first arrested.
28King's questions mirrored an argument the defense made in opening statements - that Holmes' behavior was shaped by his mental illness.
29Holmes' mother, Arlene, attempted to pass a note to the defense table from her seat nearby in the gallery Thursday, but a deputy intercepted it.
30Arlene Holmes then left the courtroom with her husband, Bob, and defense attorney Tamara Brady.
31It wasn't clear what the note said or why they left.
