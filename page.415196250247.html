Lawyers: Georgia waffles on execution with cloudy drug

1Gissendaner was scheduled to be executed Monday, March 2, 2015, but citing concerns about the drug to be used in a lethal injection, corrections officials in Georgia postponed Gissendaner's execution for the second time in a week.
2ATLANTA (AP) - Georgia officials waffled on whether to proceed with an execution when the lethal injection drug was discovered to have a cloudy appearance, according to a court filing, and one expert said their decision to postpone it was the right move.
3The execution of Kelly Renee Gissendaner was delayed late Monday night.
4A day later, officials decided to temporarily halt executions until they could more carefully analyze the pentobarbital, which is supposed to be clear.
5"If it's a solution that's supposed to be clear and it's not clear, it should never be injected," said Michael Jay, a professor at the University of North Carolina's Eshelman School of Pharmacy.
6"So they did the right thing by not injecting it."
7Gissendaner, who was convicted of murder in the February 1997 slaying of her husband, had originally been set for execution last week on Feb. 25, but it was postponed because of a threat of bad weather.
8Attorneys for Gissendaner said in a filing with the U.S. Supreme Court that a lawyer for the state called them around 10:25 p.m. Monday to say the execution would be postponed several days because the drug appeared cloudy.
9The state's lawyer called back about five minutes later to say the prison wasn't sure which drugs they had checked and that they were considering going forward, the filing says.
10The lawyer called a third time, saying they weren't going to proceed, according to the court filing.
11About 11 p.m., the state told reporters an independent lab checked the drug's potency and it was acceptable, but it later appeared cloudy and the execution was postponed "out of an abundance of caution."
12The back and forth was detailed in Gissendaner's emergency motion for a stay.
13Corrections spokeswoman Gwendolyn Hogan said "there was never any confusion within the department about the drugs which were to be used."
14Georgia Attorney General Sam Olens said in a written statement it is "essential that executions are carried out in a constitutional manner" and that he approved of the decision to temporarily suspend executions.
15The cloudiness could be contamination by bacteria or some impurity, said Jay, the pharmacy professor.
16If the particles were big enough, they could clog blood vessels when injected or could lodge in the lungs, Jay said.
17It could also make the drug less potent, causing sleepiness but not killing the inmate, he said.
18Compounded drugs have a shelf life of 30 days, Jay said.
19Georgia corrections officials received the drug planned for use Monday on Feb. 24 and it was set to expire March 26, Hogan, the department spokeswoman, said Wednesday.
20Georgia and other death penalty states have bought execution drugs from compounding pharmacies in recent years after pharmaceutical companies stopped selling to U.S. prisons.
21And many of them have adopted laws to hide the identity of their drug providers, saying it's necessary to protect their suppliers and maintain a source of the drugs.
22Critics say executions should be as transparent as possible.
23The cloudy drug "raises significant concerns about Georgia's ability to carry out executions in compliance with the Constitution, but we don't know what really happened," said Megan McCracken of the University of California, Berkeley, School of Law's Death Penalty Clinic.
24"Because of the secrecy surrounding Georgia's procedures, it is impossible to say if this is indicative of a larger problem," she said.
25Richard Dieter, executive director of the Death Penalty Information Center, which tracks data on executions, said he's never heard of an execution being halted at the last minute because of a problem with the drug itself.
26Missouri and Texas also use compounded pentobarbital in a one-drug formula for executions, and South Dakota has also used this method, McCracken said.
27Other states have been using a three-drug combination starting with the sedative midazolam.
28But executions in those states are mostly on hold pending a U.S. Supreme Court ruling in an Oklahoma case sparked by a botched execution there.
29Prosecutors said Gissendaner repeatedly pushed her on-again, off-again lover Gregory Owen to kill her husband, Douglas Gissendaner.
30Owen ambushed her husband, forced him to drive to a remote area and stabbed him multiple times.
31Owen and Gissendaner then met up and set fire to the dead man's car.
32Owen, who eventually confessed and testified against his former girlfriend, is serving a life prison sentence and is eligible for parole in eight years.
33Douglas Gissendaner's parents and sisters have made it clear they want to see Gissendaner executed.
