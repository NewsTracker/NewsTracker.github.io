Texas nurse sues Dallas hospital over Ebola infection

Five Qld greyhound trainers banned

1DALLAS (Reuters) - The first person infected with Ebola in the United States, nurse Nina Pham, sued a Texas hospital on Monday, saying it did not do enough to prevent her from contracting the deadly virus and invaded her privacy after she was diagnosed with it.
2In the suit against Texas Health Resources (THR) in Dallas County Court, Pham claims the hospital did not initially provide nurses with proper protective equipment or properly train staff on how to treat the disease.
3The suit accuses the hospital of negligence and deception.
4It does not specify an amount in damages.
5Pham, 26, was one of two nurses at the Dallas hospital who contracted the disease when treating Thomas Duncan, who was admitted in late September and died less than two weeks later.
6Both nurses recovered.
7Duncan contracted the disease in Liberia.
8Pham became a national symbol of hope in fighting Ebola after she made a recovery and was greeted in the Oval Office by President Barack Obama.
9The suit claims that the THR's Texas Health Presbyterian Hospital did not initially provide adequate protective gear for medical staff or instructions on how to treat someone with Ebola.
10It also describes a hospital in chaos, where medical waste that could have carried the virus was left to pile up.
11"The nurses were just using their best guesses and their instincts to protect themselves," the suit said.
12The hospital did not address the details of Pham's accusations and said in a statement: "As distressing as the lawsuit is to us, we remain optimistic that we can resolve this matter with Nina."
13Pham claims the hospital used her as a "public relations pawn" to improve its plummeting image.
14While it issued news releases saying her condition had improved to "good," it was having end of life conversations with her, Pham alleges.
15Pham said the hospital did not respect her right to privacy.
16In one instance, she was videotaped speaking to a doctor and the video was released to the media.
17Pham said both the taping and release happened without her permission.
18"I was hoping that THR would be more open and honest about everything that happened at the hospital, and the things they didn't do that led to me getting infected with Ebola. But that didn't happen and I felt I was left with no choice but to turn to the courts for help," Pham said in a statement.
1Five Queensland greyhound trainers have been banned for life over the live baiting scandal.
2The five were previously issued with show cause notices as to why they should not be warned off.
3Reg Kay, Debra Arnold, James Harding, Tom Noble and Tony McCabe received the life bans on Monday night after the Queensland All Codes Racing Industry Board considered the matters.
4Greg Stella made a verbal submission to the show cause notice and his case has been referred back to stewards while Michael Chapman's case has been adjourned to consider further evidence.
5Queensland Racing boss Kevin Dixon said his board considered the five trainers unworthy of being involved in the industry.
6"As a board we determined the actions of these individuals proved they should not be considered fit and proper persons to continue to be involved in the greyhound industry," Mr Dixon said in a statement.
7"The conduct we saw from these people in the evidence provided to us is not only against the rules of greyhound racing, it is deplorable by its very nature."
8Earlier on Monday, the Queensland government announced it will conduct an independent review of the state's greyhound industry.
9Racing Minister Bill Byrne says the $3 million review, to be fully funded by the racing industry, would be headed by barrister Alan McSporran SC.
10He said the review, to take about three months, would determine how misconduct that led to the suspension of trainers allegedly involved in live-baiting occurred without earlier detection.
