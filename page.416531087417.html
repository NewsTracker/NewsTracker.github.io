More than 2,200 confirmed dead in Nepal earthquake

1A powerful aftershock shook Nepal on Sunday, making buildings sway and sending panicked Kathmandu residents running into the streets a day after a massive earthquake left more than 2,200 people dead.
2The cawing of crows mixed with terrified screams as the magnitude 6.7 aftershock pummeled the capital city early Sunday afternoon.
3It came as planeloads of supplies, doctors and relief workers from neighboring countries began arriving in this poor Himalayan nation.
4"The aftershocks keep coming ... so people don't know what to expect," said Sanjay Karki, Nepal country head for global aid agency Mercy Corps.
5"All the open spaces in Kathmandu are packed with people who are camping outdoors. When the aftershocks come you cannot imagine the fear. You can hear women and children crying."
6Saturday's magnitude 7.8 earthquake spread horror from Kathmandu to small villages and to the slopes of Mount Everest, triggering an avalanche that buried part of the base camp packed with foreign climbers preparing to make their summit attempts.
7At least 17 people died there and 61 were injured.
8The earthquake centered outside Kathmandu, the capital, was the worst to hit the South Asian nation in over 80 years.
9It destroyed swaths of the oldest neighborhoods of Kathmandu, and was strong enough to be felt all across parts of India, Bangladesh, China's region of Tibet and Pakistan.
10By Sunday afternoon, authorities said at least 2,169 people had died in Nepal alone, with 61 more deaths in India and a few in other neighboring countries.
11At least 721 of them died in Kathmandu alone, and the number of injured nationwide was upward of 5,000.
12With search and rescue efforts far from over, it was unclear how much the death toll would rise.
13But outside of the oldest neighborhoods, many in Kathmandu were surprised by how few modern structures - the city is largely a collection of small, poorly constructed brick apartment buildings - collapsed in the quake.
14While aid workers cautioned that many buildings could have sustained serious structural damage, it was also clear that the death toll would have been far higher had more buildings caved in.
15On a flight into Kathmandu on Sunday morning, an AP correspondent was unable to spot any collapsed buildings.
16Aid workers also warned that the situation could be far worse near the epicenter.
17The U.S. Geological Survey said the quake was centered near Lamjung, about 80 kilometers (50 miles) northwest of Kathmandu, in the Gorkha district.
18Roads to that area were blocked by landslides, hindering rescue teams, said chief district official Prakash Subedi.
19Teams were trekking through mountain trails to reach remote villages, and helicopters would also be deployed, he said by telephone.
20The aid group World Vision said in a statement that remote mountain communities, including in Gorkha, were totally unprepared for the level of destruction caused by the earthquake.
21Villages near the epicenter "are literally perched on the sides of large mountain faces and are made from simple stone and rock construction. Many of these villages are only accessible by 4WD and then foot, with some villages hours and even entire days' walks away from main roads at the best of times," the group's local staff member, Matt Darvas, said in the statement.
22"It will likely be helicopter access only for these remote villages," he said.
23Nepal's worst recorded earthquake in 1934 measured 8.0 and all but destroyed the cities of Kathmandu, Bhaktapur and Patan.
24With people fearing more quakes, tens of thousands of Nepalese spent Saturday night outside under chilly skies, or in cars and public buses.
25They were jolted awake by strong aftershocks early Sunday.
26"There were at least three big quakes at night and early morning. How can we feel safe? This is never-ending and everyone is scared and worried," said Kathmandu resident Sundar Sah.
27"I hardly got much sleep. I was waking up every few hours and glad that I was alive."
28As day broke, rescuers aided by international teams set out to dig through rubble of buildings - concrete slabs, bricks, iron beams, wood - to look for survivors.
29In the Kalanki neighborhood of Kathmandu, police rescuers finally extricated a man lying under a dead person, both of them buried beneath a pile of concrete slabs and iron beams.
30Before his rescue, his family members stood nearby, crying and praying.
31Police said the man's legs and hips were totally crushed.
32Hundreds of people in Kalanki gathered around the collapsed Lumbini Guest House, once a three-story budget hotel and restaurant frequented by Nepalese.
33They watched with fear and anticipation as a single backhoe dug into the rubble.
34Police officer RP Dhamala, who was coordinating the rescue efforts, said they had already pulled out 12 people alive and six dead.
35He said rescuers were still searching for about 20 people believed to be trapped, but had heard no cries, taps or noises for a while.
36Most areas were without power and water.
37The United Nations said hospitals in the Kathmandu Valley were overcrowded, and running out of emergency supplies and space to store corpses.
38Plumes of smoke, meanwhile, rose above the capital as friends, relatives and others gathered by the river to quickly cremate loved ones' remains.
39Most shops in Kathmandu were shut; only fruit vendors and pharmacies seemed to be doing business.
40Karki, of Mercy Corps, said there were long lines outside pharmacies because people fear they will run out of medicine.
41"This is all we will have for awhile. We don't expect any more shipments for at least a week. More people are coming now. They cannot cook so they need to buy something they can eat raw. We try to help everyone. But we are not raising prices. That would be illegal, immoral profit. That would be wrong," Jaiswal said.
42The quake will likely put a huge strain on the resources of this impoverished country best known for Everest, the highest mountain in the world.
43The economy of Nepal, a nation of 27.8 million people, relies heavily on tourism, principally trekking and Himalayan mountain climbing.
44With Kathmandu airport reopened, the first aid flights began delivering aid supplies.
45The first to respond were Nepal's neighbors - India, China and Pakistan, all of which have been jockeying for influence over the landlocked nation.
46Still, Nepal remains closest to India with which it shares deep political, cultural and religious ties.
47Indian air force planes landed Sunday with 43 tons of relief material, including tents and food, and nearly 200 rescuers, India's External Affairs Ministry spokesman Vikas Swarup said.
48The planes were returning to New Delhi with Indian nationals stranded in Kathmandu.
49More aid flights were planned for Sunday.
50India suffered its own losses from the quake, with at least 61 people killed there and dozens injured.
51Sunday's aftershock was also widely felt in the country, and local news reports said metro trains in New Delhi and Kolkata were briefly shut down when the shaking started.
52A 62-member Chinese search and rescue team also arrived Sunday.
53Other countries sending support Sunday included the United Arab Emirates, Germany and France.
54Pakistan prepared to send four C-130 aircraft, carrying a 30-bed temporary hospital comprising army doctors, surgeons and specialists.
55An urban search and rescue team was also sent with ground-penetrating radars, concrete cutters and sniffing dogs.
56Pakistan was also sending 2,000 ready-to-eat meal packs, water bottles, medicines, 200 tents, 600 blankets and other necessary items.
57When the earth first shook, residents fled homes and buildings in panic as walls tumbled, trees swayed, power lines came crashing down and large cracks opened up on streets.
58After the chaos of Saturday - when little organized rescue and relief was seen - there was more order on Sunday as rescue teams fanned out across the city.
59Workers were sending out tents and relief goods in trucks and helicopters, said disaster management official Rameshwar Dangal.
60He said government and private schools have been turned into shelters.
61Mukesh Kafle, the head of the Nepal Electricity Authority, said power has been restored fully to main government offices, the airport and hospitals.
62Among the destroyed buildings in Kathmandu was the nine-story Dharahara Tower, a Kathmandu landmark built by Nepal's royal rulers as a watchtower in the 1800s and a UNESCO-recognized historical monument.
63It was reduced to rubble and there were reports of people trapped underneath.
64The Kathmandu Valley is listed as a World Heritage site.
65The Buddhist stupas, public squares and Hindu temples are some of the most well-known sites in Kathmandu, and now some of the most deeply mourned.
66The head of the U.N. cultural agency, Irina Bokova, said in a statement that UNESCO was ready to help Nepal rebuild from "extensive damage, including to historic monuments and buildings of the Kathmandu Valley."
