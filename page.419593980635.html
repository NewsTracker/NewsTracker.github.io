Trump has Univision anchor taken out of Iowa news conference amid immigration confrontation

1DUBUQUE, Iowa -- Republican presidential candidate Donald Trump engaged in a prolonged confrontation with the anchor of the nation's leading Spanish-language network during a news conference Tuesday, first having the well-known news personality removed before allowing him back in.
2Jorge Ramos, the Miami-based anchor for Univision, stood up and began to ask Trump about his immigration proposal, which includes ending automatic citizenship for infants born in the United States to parents in the country illegally.
3As Ramos began to speak, Trump interrupted him, saying he hadn't called on Ramos before repeatedly telling him to "sit down" and then saying, "Go back to Univision."
4As one of Trump's security detail approached Ramos, the Mexican-American anchor continued to speak, saying, "You cannot deport 11 million people."
5Ramos was referring to Trump's proposal to deport all people in the country illegally before allowing some of them to return.
6As he was taken from the room, Ramos said, "You cannot build a 1,900-mile wall," another proposal in Trump's plan.
7Moments later, Trump justified Ramos' removal, saying: "He just stands up and starts screaming. Maybe he's at fault also."
8The billionaire businessman's immigration proposal has sparked intense debate within the 2016 Republican field.
9Several candidates, including former Gov. Jeb Bush, have called it "unrealistic," and Wisconsin Gov. Scott Walker bobbled his answer on whether he supports ending birthright citizenship.
10Citizenship for infants born in the United States is guaranteed by the 14th Amendment of the U.S. Constitution, and changing that would require amending the Constitution -- a process that requires two-thirds majorities in both chambers of Congress and ratification by three-quarters of the states.
11A Washington think-tank that favours immigration restrictions, the Center for Immigration Studies, estimates that about 40,000 babies annually are born to parents who enter the United States specifically to have a child, and that most of those parents enter the country legally.
12A Pew Research Center study in 2010 estimated 340,000 babies born in 2008 were the offspring of people in the country illegally.
13Ramos was later allowed back into the news conference.
14Trump greeted him politely, though they quickly resumed their argument, interrupting each other during an extended back-and-forth.
15"Your immigration plan, it is full of empty promises," Ramos began.
16"You cannot deny citizenship to children born in this country."
17"Why do you say that?"
18Trump replied.
19"Some of the great legal scholars agree that's not true."
20During the five-minute exchange, Ramos claimed that 40 per cent of people in the country illegally enter through airports, not over the Mexican border.
21"I don't believe that. I don't believe it," Trump responded.
22A 2006 report by the Pew Hispanic Center found that as much as 45 per cent of the people in the U.S. illegally entered with legal visas but overstayed them.
23Trump said he did not believe that a majority of immigrants in the U.S. illegally were criminals, or in the country to commit crimes.
24"Most of them are good people," he said.
25But he described recent cases where people had been killed by assailants later determined to be in the country illegally.
26Finally, Trump reminded Ramos that he was suing Univision, which dropped Trump's Miss Universe pageant after he described Mexican immigrants in the U.S. illegally as "criminals" and "rapists."
27"Do you know how many Latinos work for me? Do you know how many Hispanics work for me?"
28Trump said.
29"Thousands. They love me."
30Isaac Lee, chief executive officer of Univision, responded to the confrontation with a written comment: "We'd love for Mr. Trump to sit down for an in-depth interview with Jorge to talk about the specifics of his proposals."
31Trump also said he would offer a foreign policy proposal within the next month.
32But he hinted at some of his thoughts during a free-wheeling rally in the Dubuque convention auditorium that followed the news conference.
33"We protect Germany for no money. We protect South Korea for no money," Trump said during the rally -- which, despite the crowd size, was more subdued than one he hosted last month in Oskaloosa, Iowa.
