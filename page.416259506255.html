7MONTICELLO, Iowa (AP) - Hillary Rodham Clinton is searching for a second chance in the state that delivered the first blow to her failed presidential campaign eight years ago.
8On a two-day swing through Iowa, the opening act of her 2016 campaign, Clinton is trying to show Democratic voters that she's taking nothing for granted this time around.
9She's doing so with a series of smaller, more personal interactions.
10On Wednesday, Clinton visited the Tremont Grille in Marshalltown, ordering a skim latte and chatting with a table of local officials before afternoon events that were expected to include a private meeting with Democratic legislators at the statehouse.
11Speaking to students and teachers in Monticello on Tuesday, she sought to show a familiarity with Iowa issues, pointing to former Iowa Sen. Tom Harkin's work to help people with disabilities and noting that the average college student in the state carries nearly $30,000 in debt after graduation.
12The approach is starkly different than her previous effort in the state.
13In 2007, Clinton staged her major education, foreign policy, health care and energy rollouts in Iowa.
14She campaigned to big crowds across Iowa alongside members of the state Democratic establishment.
15She finished third.
16This year, she made her Iowa debut at a coffee shop in the Mississippi River town of LeClaire, then on to a community college event in Monticello.
17Declaring herself a "champion" for struggling families, Clinton laid out four pillars for her campaign for the Democratic nomination, listing the need to build "the economy of tomorrow, not yesterday," strengthen families, fix dysfunctional government, and protect the country from threats.
18And though she's running in what's expected to be the most expensive election in history, Clinton embraced the idea of a constitutional amendment to get "unaccountable money" out of the campaign finance system.
19Clinton didn't get into any specifics on Tuesday about how she would achieve her goals, promising that she would do so in coming weeks.
20Instead, it was a day for political messaging, as Clinton presented a progressive rationale for her candidacy to quell skepticism from liberals in her party about her commitment to tackling income inequality.
21Not all were sold, including some past allies.
22As Clinton spoke in Iowa, New York City Mayor Bill de Blasio, who ran her campaign for Senate in New York, refused to endorse her, saying he wanted to hear more about her policy positions.
23"The last time she was a candidate for president eight long years ago, the Great Recession had just begun," he told reporters in the Bronx.
24"This is a different country we are living in right now. We need a vision that relates to this time, not eight years ago."
25Despite the support she expressed for overhauling the campaign finance system, her team has no plans to ask Democratic organizations, expected to raise hundreds of millions of dollars in support of her candidacy, to back down.
