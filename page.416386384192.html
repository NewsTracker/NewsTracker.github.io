2runner Lelisa Desisa finished first in the Boston Marathon on Monday, reclaiming the top spot in a race he last won two years ago when it was struck by a deadly bombing attack.
3Caroline Rotich of Kenya led the women's field, affirming African athletes' dominance at the race.
4Desisa, who famously gave his winner's medal to the city following the 2013 bombing, pulled ahead to sprint down Boylston Street alone, finishing with a time of 2 hours, 9 minutes and 17 seconds.
5"Strong Boston!"
6Desisa shouted after he crossed the finish line, in a version of the "Boston Strong" motto that became the city's rallying cry after the April 15, 2013, attack that killed three and injured 264.
7He has no plans to donate this year's medal.
8"This medal is, I think it is for me," Desisa told reporters.
9Kenya's Rotich had a more dramatic finish, besting Ethiopia's Mare DiBaba in a sprint down Boylston Street, turning in a time of 2 hours, 24 minutes and 55 seconds.
10Security was high near the start line in Hopkinton, along the 26.2 mile (42.16 km) course and around the finish line in Boston, in recognition of the bombing, one of the most visible attacks on U.S. soil since Sept.

12Desisa unseated reigning champion Meb Keflezighi of California, whose 2014 victory marked the first time that a U.S. man had won the race in three decades.
13Keflezighi, who finished eighth, crossed the line hand-in-hand with another U.S. runner, Hilary Dionne, who finished 15th in the women's field.
14"I've never met her. I had to sprint hard to catch up with her to do that," Keflezighi said afterward.
15"It was fun to do that."
16The top U.S. male finisher was Dathan Ritzenhein, of Michigan, who finished seventh.
17The elite men's and women's fields kept large packs for the first half of the race, with runners mindful of the early breakaway that set the stage for Keflezighi's 2014 victory.
18Desisa, 25, ran shorter events before switching his focus to marathons in 2013, when he surprised the field at Boston with his fast performance.
19He started the race in 2014 but walked off the course with an injury.
20Rotich, 30, was born in Kenya but attended high school in Japan.
21She has posted top-ten performances at top marathons in Tokyo, New York and Chicago.
22The top U.S. women's finisher, Desiree Linden, led the pack for much of the race before Rotich passed her in the final miles, leaving Linden to finish fourth.
23Linden said the long lead was a strategic move intended to winnow the field.
24"I know a lot of people will question it, 'Why did you run in the front and put yourself in the wind?'" Linden told reporters.
25"But I think that's how you have to run here. You have to be gritty and aggressive."
