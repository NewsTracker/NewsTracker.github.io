3MOSCOW (AP) - A year since a Malaysia Airlines Boeing 777 was blown out of the sky over war-ravaged eastern Ukraine - killing 298 people - there has been little official word of progress in determining what brought down Flight MH17.
4One of the probes under way reportedly points toward a ground-to-air missile fired from a village held by Russia-backed separatist rebels, but the report isn't to be public for months.
5Another investigation trudges forward with painful slowness, and contradictory theories emerge from Moscow.
6A fog of vehement allegations, macabre claims and self-serving rhetoric shrouds the tragedy.
7Within hours of the July 17, 2014 crash, a crucial fact appeared clear - the wreckage was strewn over such a wide area that the plane must have broken into bits long before it fell.
8Most evidence points to the plane, which departed from Amsterdam en route to Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia, being taken down by a missile over an area controlled by pro-Russia rebels.
9But whether the missile was fired from a warplane or from the ground remains unclear.
10As is the question of who fired: the Ukrainian army, the separatist rebels or Russian forces allegedly backing them with troops and arms.
11An international investigation may be able to answer the first question, but not until at least October.
12Another probe addressing the second is likely to take until at least next year.
13The investigation led by the Dutch Safety Board aims only to determine the crash cause, not to ascribe blame.
14That's likely to produce a report loaded with esoteric technical detail.
15A preliminary report from the board in September was able to say only that the plane was destroyed by "high-energy objects" that pierced it from outside.
16A draft version of the final report was circulated this month to representatives of Malaysia, Ukraine, the U.S., Russia, Britain, Australia and the Netherlands for their comments and suggested revisions.
17Oleg Storchevoi, a deputy chief of the Russian aviation agency, said the agency has complaints regarding both the technical data and the arguments in the report.
18He did not give details.
19But a U.S. official told The Associated Press that the draft says the plane was destroyed by a Russian-made Buk surface-to-air missile fired from the village of Snizhne, which was under rebel control.
20The official, who wasn't authorized to comment publicly, spoke on condition of anonymity.
21The probe is being led by The Netherlands because 196 of the victims were Dutch, and Ukraine agreed to give Holland formal responsibility for the investigation.
22A probe by the Dutch national prosecutor's office aims to establishing who was responsible.
23This investigation includes authorities from Ukraine, Malaysia and other countries whose nationals were among the victims, but Russia is not a participant.
24It is unclear whom the investigators are questioning, or the extent of the evidence they've collected.
25The prosecutor's office released a video calling on witnesses to come forward and indicated the probe is focusing on rebel or Russian involvement.
26The video asks for information about a Buk-11 missile system that was spotted moving through the rebel territory before and after the crash, then possibly heading for the Russian border.
27The investigative organization Bellingcat also has devoted attention to this missile-launcher, claiming it can be traced to one that was stored in the rebels' main stronghold of Donetsk.
28Rebel officials at the time denied having any such missile systems, although Russian news agencies had reported rebel claims of seizing some from Ukrainian forces a few weeks earlier.
29Russia is opposing a proposed UN Security Council resolution to establish an international criminal tribunal for the crash; Moscow has veto power in the security council.
