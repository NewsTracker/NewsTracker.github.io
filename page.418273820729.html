Binges and bailouts: How Greece got to the brink

1FRANKFURT, Germany -- After two bailouts totaling 240 billion euros ($266 billion) and six years of depression, spending cuts and lost jobs, Greece teeters on the edge of collapse.
2How did it come to this?
3Why couldn't all that money and all that sacrifice turn around a country that makes up less than two per cent of the 19-country eurozone economy?
4On Sunday the country will hold a referendum on whether or not to accept the tough creditor conditions attached to loans needed to avoid default and a banking collapse.
5A "no" may lead to a chaotic departure from the shared euro currency.
6Even "yes" is no guarantee the creditors will agree to lend.
7There is no way around it: Greece engaged in a three-decades-long credit binge starting in the early 1980s, spending the money on plush government jobs for supporters of the country's two major political parties - the center-left PASOK and center-right New Democracy.
8Taking turns in office, they paid their followers well - driving up private sector wages and making Greece a costly place to do business.
9They also looked the other way on widespread tax evasion.
10Independent professionals - including doctors and lawyers - often reported less income than factory workers.
11Despite its rickety finances, Greece shaped up a little for a few years and qualified to join the single currency in 2001.
12It was a time of euphoria and confidence in the euro, seen as a rival to the dollar as a global currency.
13New members were desired to boost the EU's biggest hope for European integration.
14Greece was waved in, and promptly went back to its old ways.
15The arrival of the euro only fueled the debt binge.
16German and French banks found they could now buy Greek government bonds in euros, not drachmas that might devalue.
17Greece borrowed at what in retrospect were ridiculously low interest rates, just a bit more than those charged to rock-solid Germany.
18They were in the euro, the thinking went, what could go wrong?
19In October 2009, after the global financial crisis had made investors more wary of risk, Greece revealed its deficit was far higher than advertised, and its finances were out of control.
20Its borrowing costs shot up.
21It couldn't pay.
22When the party ended, Greece got a 110 billion euro bailout in 2010 from the other eurozone countries and the International Monetary Fund.
23The creditors attached tough conditions to cut spending and deficits, and to tackle the rampant bureaucracy and corruption.
24Yet the cuts quickly undermined growth, which fell below the optimistic estimates of the creditors who miscalculated how much they would weigh on the economy.
25There was a contradiction that couldn't be resolved: cuts in public spending were needed to reduce Greece's cost base because it couldn't devalue.
26Yet the lower public spending pushed down GDP and simply made the debt burden bigger compared to the size of the economy.
27With its own currency, Greece could have defaulted on its debts and devalued, quickly erasing its international cost problem, and moved on within a few years.
28Cutting wages and prices is tougher, and takes longer.
29In that sense, the euro has prolonged the agony.
30By 2012, it was clear the first bailout wasn't doing the trick.
31A second rescue included sticking Greece's private creditors with losses on their bonds, along with yet more loans.
32Yet creditors continued to underestimate how much damage austerity would do to growth.
33The debt level, measured as a fraction of the economy, continued to increase.
34Even at the time of the deal, most economists and policy makers expected that further debt reduction or easier terms were inevitable.
35But the reshuffle of Greece's debt meant that instead of owing bond investors, Greece's creditors were now European taxpayers, the IMF and the ECB.
36With taxpayers on the hook, resistance to further debt relief stiffened in place like Germany, the Netherlands, Austria and Finland.
37Still, for a while it looked like the second bailout might do the trick.
38The Greek government under Prime Minister Antonis Samaras narrowed deficits.
39Some reforms were implemented, while others were passed but didn't really take effect.
40The economy bottomed out, after an agonizing fall of around 25 per cent, and appeared ready to start growing.
41Greece did get some easier terms on interest and time to pay.
42In September 2014, Greece had only weeks left before it successfully completed the second bailout and transitioned to milder forms of assistance.
43Approaching the finish line, Samaras stumbled.
44He announced that Greece would expel its hated creditor monitors and return to bond markets without outside help or advice.
45The idea fell flat.
46Interest yields rose sharply on its remaining debt.
47Instead of getting the last of the bailout money, in December the floundering Samaras had to call an early election held in January.
48Voters, who were fed up with years of agony, chose Syriza, a hard-left party that won support with its demands for debt reduction and no more austerity.
49Creditors balked.
50Uncertainty quickly tanked the fledgling recovery.
51The bailout was first extended and then allowed to expire Tuesday as Syriza and creditors talked past one another.
52The last bailout money, some 7.2 billion euros, had slipped out of Greece's grasp.
53And with it, perhaps, its chance of remaining in the eurozone.
