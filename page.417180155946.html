Russia could block Google, Twitter and Facebook

1Frankfurt: Russia's media watchdog has written to Google, Twitter and Facebook warning them against violating Russian internet laws and a spokesman said on Thursday they risk being blocked if they do not comply with the rules.
2Roskomnadzor said it had sent letters this week to the three US-based internet firms asking them to comply with internet laws which critics of President Vladimir Putin have decried as censorship.
3"In our letters we regularly remind [companies] of the consequences of violating the legislation," Roskomnadzor spokesman Vadim Ampelonsky said.
4Because of the encryption technology used by the three firms, Russia had no way of blocking specific pages or posts it deemed in violation of law, and would have to block access to their entire services , he added.
5To comply with the law, the three firms must hand over data on Russian bloggers with more than 3000 readers per day, and take down pages that Roskomnadzor sees as containing calls for "unsanctioned protests and unrest", Mr Ampelonsky said.
6Mr Putin, a former KGB spy, once described the internet as a project of the CIA, highlighting deep distrust between Moscow and Washington, whose ties are now badly strained.
7He promised late last year not to put the internet under full government control, but Kremlin critics see the country's internet laws as part of a crackdown on freedom of speech since Mr Putin returned to the Kremlin for a third term in 2012.
8A law passed last year gives Russian prosecutors the right to block without a court decision websites with information about protests that have not been sanctioned by authorities.
9Under other legislation, bloggers with large followings must go through an official registration procedure and have their identities confirmed by a government agency.
10Facebook says it responds to government data requests about its users that comply with company policies and local laws and meet international standards of legal process.
11Facebook's Transparency Report shows it rejected both of two Russian government requests for information on its users last year.
12This compares with more than 14,000 requests made by US courts, police and government agencies in the second half of 2014, which Facebook produced some data in 80 per cent of cases.
13Twitter had a similar response rate in the United States but rejected 108 Russian government requests in the second half of the year, according to the company's Transparency Report.
14In its semi-annual Transparency Report, Google said it provided some information on users in response to 5 per cent of 134 Russian government requests made in the second half of 2014 again far less than in the United States.
15The company says it complies with requests that follow accepted legal procedures and Google policies.
16"We realise they are registered under US jurisdiction. But I think in this case they should demonstrate equal respect to national legislation," Mr Ampelonsky said.
17If the companies do not pay more attention to Russian government requests for data, he added, "we will need to apply sanctions."
