US stocks slip amid global sell-off after Greek 'no' vote

1NEW YORK (AP) - Stocks in the U.S. fell broadly following drops in overseas markets as Greeks voted to reject creditor conditions for more loans, but the losses weren't as steep as many had feared.
2With time running out for Greece to strike a new deal and its banks desperately short of cash, a wave of selling that started in Asia early Monday spread to Europe, then the U.S. By the end of the day, nine of the 10 industry groups in the Standard and Poor's 500 index were down.
3But the index itself had fallen a modest 0.4 percent.
4Still, many investors were clearly nervous, putting money into assets considered safe bets in turbulent times such as U.S. government bonds.
5A rout in the stocks of oil drillers and other energy companies fed the selling as the price oil plunged nearly 8 percent.
6In a Sunday referendum on creditor demands for spending cuts and tax hikes in exchange for more bailout money, 61 percent of Greeks voted "no," a much higher proportion than anticipated.
7Some analysts attributed the lack of sharper sell-off in stock markets to the resignation of the Greek finance minister, which might help bailout talks resume.
8The Dow Jones industrial average fell 46.53 points, or 0.3 percent, to 17,683.58.
9The S&P 500 gave up 8.02 points, or 0.4 percent, to 2,068.76.
10The Nasdaq composite fell 17.27 points, or 0.3 percent, to 4,991.94.
11In Europe, Germany's DAX fell 1.5 percent while the CAC-40 in France fell 2 percent.
12The FTSE 100 index of leading British shares was 0.8 percent lower.
13Many in the markets fear that the Greek vote has pushed the country one step closer to leaving the euro.
14Greek banks are running out of cash even after the government last week placed limits on how much depositors can withdraw.
15The European Central Bank has been providing emergency credit to the banks, but on Monday said it could not increase the amount offered because the banks' collateral was weaker now.
16Meeting in Paris with her French counterpart, German Chancellor Angela Merkel stressed the importance of Greece taking "responsibility" for reforming its economy.
17Both she and French President Francois Hollande said the door was open to more negotiations.
18Eurozone leaders meet Tuesday to discuss the crisis.
19Several reports from global banks and investment firms on Monday predicted the country will have no choice but to exit the euro and issue its own currency to relieve the cash crunch.
20A so-called "Grexit" from the euro is considered one of the biggest risks facing the global economy.
21"The prospects of Greece remaining in the eurozone have suffered a setback," said Bill O'Neill, head of the U.K. Investment Office at UBS Wealth Management.
22"A deal to keep Greece in the eurozone remains possible, but the odds against a successful conclusion have now lengthened."
23Russ Koesterich, chief strategist at giant money manager BlackRock, wrote in note to clients that he doesn't think the Greece crisis poses a "longer-term" threat to the global economy or financial markets.
24Oil fell $4.40 to $52.53 a barrel on worries that a slowdown in Europe from the Greek crisis will cut demand for oil.
25Prices also reflected worries that Iranian crude held back by sanctions will soon hit the market as talks with the U.S. over Iran's nuclear program continue to progress.
26Some hopes for progress in the talks with Greece grew Monday after Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis quit.
27Over months of negotiations, Varoufakis' relations with his peers in the eurozone had deteriorated significantly.
28"The fact that Varoufakis has resigned hints that the Greek government may at least be offering an olive branch given his reputation for using aggressive terms such as 'water-boarding' to describe the creditors' actions," said Jane Foley, a senior currency analyst at Rabobank International.
