Young Western women who joined Islamic State and married jihadi fighters 'now widows'

1At least 15 young Western women who joined Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant (Isil) and married jihadi fighters are now widows after the militant group suffered losses in clashes in Syria and Iraq, according to researchers who closely monitor Islamist radicals online.
2Ms Smith said most of the women believed to be widows lost their husbands in the last six months.
3Isil, which swept through northern Iraq last June, has recently suffered some battlefield setbacks including at the Syrian city of Kobane where it was defeated by Kurdish forces backed by US-led airstrikes.
4The morale of foreign women is a concern for Isil because it is trying to attract females from abroad to marry Islamist fighters and populate the territory it holds.
5The latest edition of the Isil online magazine Dabiq urged "every (foreign) sister who has been afflicted with the loss of her husband on the battlefield" not to become disheartened.
6"Be firm, my dear sister, be patient, and await your reward. "
7Be wary, be wary of thinking of going back (home)," it said in an article, which told foreign women that migrating to the Islamic State is a binding religious obligation.
8Given the anonymity of the Internet and the status of Isil women who are hidden from public view, identifying Western females in the group's ranks is difficult.
9Ms Smith's database counts foreign women who identify themselves as living in territory controlled by Isil and are active on social media.
10They will often indicate their nationality in Twitter bios or pseudonyms.
11In Isil, women whose husbands have been killed soon move out of their marital homes and join a "shuhada" house for the widows of martyrs, according to their postings.
12The widows are encouraged to marry again soon after a traditional Islamic mourning period of four months and 10 days, according to Aqsa Mahmood, a Scottish woman of Pakistani origin whose family has acknowledged her identity.
13Mahmood has a Tumblr blog that details her life in Syria with Isil.
14The women often write about life in IS territory in detailed postings and tweet photographs of street scenes in Syria or Iraq.
15Their postings range from expressions of grief to calls to assassinate Western leaders to occasional expressions of discontent over their treatment.
16Their posts do not appear to be centrally controlled by Isil, researchers say.
17Rita Katz, founder of the US-based SITE Intelligence Group which monitors jihadi websites, said there were likely many more than 15 widows since the foreign women tend to marry Isil fighters.
18"Not all of them are online tweeting about it," she said.
19Ms Smith says that all of the 15 widows she has identified are between 16 and 26 years old.
20Based mostly on references in their postings, she believes that seven of them are British, with others from France, Belgium, Canada, the United States, Australia and South Africa.
21A woman known online as Umm Jihad said her Australian husband Suhan Rahman was killed in March.
22He "died the best of deaths so it's nothing to be upset about, it's just that we're still here, that's the hardest part," she later tweeted.
23BuzzFeed News reported that Umm Jihad is a 20-year-old woman of Yemeni descent from Hoover, Alabama, which would make her the first known US woman to be widowed.
24BuzzFeed interviewed the woman's father who acknowledged she had joined Isil.
