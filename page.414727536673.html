1Between 250 and 300 local police officers have been hunkered down in the station in the town of Santa Maria Coyotepec for the last 13 days to demand raises and better working conditions, the Oaxaca state government said in a statement.
2They shot at federal police who tried to remove them on Friday, the government alleged.
3Five federal agents were wounded in the legs by bullet shrapnel, but their lives were said not to be in danger.
4Some of the local officers contended it was not them but rather federal agents who opened fire in the pre-dawn confrontation.
5"The federal police tried to get in through the main door, but my companions reacted and the clash began," said a policeman inside the compound who gave his name as only Luis for fear of possible reprisals.
6Jeyco Perez, identified as one of the leaders of the revolt, told Milenio TV that they were only using shields to defend themselves and had not fired weapons.
7An Associated Press reporter at the scene saw the local officers carrying batons and riot shields, but no weapons were readily visible.
8The entry to the station was barricaded with a truck and metal fencing.
9The locals captured at least three federal officers but later released them.
10One, Mauricio Villela, said he was not harmed during his seven hours of captivity.
11He denied that it was federal police who opened fire, saying, "We did not shoot."
12Hundreds of police remained in the area of the station, which the state government said holds more than 3,000 firearms and nearly 500,000 rounds of ammunition.
13The Oaxaca state security commissioner, Victor Altamirano, told Milenio that the operation seeks to keep those munitions from being misused by the local police.
14Just before midday about 50 people who were apparently civilian residents of Santa Maria Coyotepec gathered at the station holding signs in support the protest.
15The town is about 3 miles (5km) south of the state capital, also named Oaxaca.
1The radical Sunni Islamists stormed the mosque in Peshawar - close to the Afghan border - during Friday prayers this afternoon and fired automatic weapons at random, local police said.
2Amid the ensuing chaos three suicide bomb blasts were heard inside the mosque, which the Taliban are understood to have targeted because they think Shi'ites are not true Muslims.
3The Pakistani Taliban, who are fighting to set up a hardline Sunni theocracy in the country, claimed responsibility for the attack, saying it was revenge for Pakistan's crackdown on militants following a massacre at a school in the city that killed 145 people, including 132 children.
4Police said armed men broke into the mosque, where people were attending Friday prayers, and opened fire, following which three explosions were heard inside the building.
5'Either Pakistan will become your graveyard, or God's law, sharia, will be implemented,' Taliban commander Khaleefa Omar Mansoor said in a video in which he was flanked by three young militants clutching AK-47 assault rifles.
6'This is the first in a series of revenge attacks... Wait for the rest,' Mansoor, who had earlier claimed responsibility for the December 16 school attack in which more than 150 people were killed, said in the video sent by email to reporters.
7The style of the mosque attack was similar to that of the school attack, when gunmen arrived in a car, set it on fire, and broke into the building using a back entrance.
8Peshawar's Hayatabad Medical Complex said at least 19 people had been killed in the latest attack.
9The head of the provincial police, Nasir Durrani, said four or five attackers entered the mosque from a building next door that was under construction, then jumped over an outer wall into the mosque courtyard.
10One person then blew himself up, creating a diversion so the other attackers could enter the mosque, Durrani said.
11He said at least three attackers died in the violence - two by blowing themselves up, and a third was killed by worshippers at the mosque.
12'People here showed great courage.
13They grabbed one of the attackers from his neck, and he couldn't detonate (his explosives), and he was shot and killed,' Durrani added.
14Authorities were still examining the scene, and Durrani did not say what happened to the remaining attacker or attackers.
15Some people who fled the mosque reported that at least some of the attackers were wearing security uniforms.
16'The prayer was about to end when a big bang happened, followed by dust and smoke.
17I have seen amid the smoke that one guy dressed in police uniform was firing shots and then there was another blast,' said one of the wounded, 22-year-old Syed Javed Hasan, speaking from his hospital bed.
18A witness, Shahid Hussain, said that Shi'ite worshippers had just finished prayers when five or six men wearing military uniforms broke into the mosque and started shooting.
19'We had no idea what was going on.
20One of the attackers then blew himself up and then there was huge smoke and dust all around,' he said.
21The attack came as Pakistan tries to adopt new measures to tackle militants following the school massacre, in which 134 children were among the dead.
22The government has pledged to crack down on all militant groups, and has reintroduced the death penalty, set up military courts to speed convictions and widened its offensive in northwestern areas on the Afghan border where militants find refuge.
23Yet Pakistan's religious minorities, among them Ahmadis, Christians and Hindus, say the government is doing little to alleviate their daily struggle against discrimination and violence.
24Last month, dozens of people were killed in a similar attack on a Shi'ite mosque in the southern town of Shikarpur.
