State Official: Hillary Clinton Email Practices 'Not Acceptable'

1State department official says use of private email server as Clinton did 'not acceptable.'
2Hillary Clinton's use of private email was not appropriate, a State Department official told a Senate committee on Tuesday.
3WASHINGTON (AP) - A State Department assistant secretary said Wednesday it's "not acceptable" for any agency employee to conduct government business on a private email server as former Secretary of State Hillary Rodham Clinton did.
4Joyce Barr, the agency's chief freedom of information officer, made the comment under questioning from Republican senators who used a Senate Judiciary hearing on open records laws to attack Clinton over her email practices.
5Sen. John Cornyn of Texas said that Clinton's approach amounted to a "premeditated and deliberate" attempt to avoid open records requirements.
6Sen. Thom Tillis of North Carolina said that anyone who took such an approach should be fired, and asked Barr whether it would be considered acceptable.
7Barr said that she had not been aware of Clinton's decision to conduct all her State Department email on a private server but that the agency has now made it clear to employees that such an approach would not be acceptable.
8"I think that the actions that we've taken in the course of recovering these emails have made it very clear what people's responsibilities are with regard to record-keeping," she said.
9"We continue to do training, we've sent department notices, telegrams, we've talked to directors and I think the message is loud and clear that that is not acceptable."
10Clinton, who is running for president, has defended using a personal email account while serving as secretary of state as a matter of personal convenience.
11She says she has turned over to the State Department all work-related emails, though House Republicans investigating the 2002 attacks on the U.S. mission in Benghazi, Libya, are demanding more.
12They insist the server itself should be examined by a third party.
13Barr acknowledged problems with the State Department's overall record on responding to open records requests, calling an existing backlog of 18,000 requests "unacceptable."
14But she insisted improvements were being made even as the number of requests keeps growing and the agency is understaffed.
