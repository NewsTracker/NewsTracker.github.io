Brick business booms as Nepal readies to rebuild after quake

1In this Wednesday, May 13, 2015 photo, Indian and Nepalese laborers work at a brick factory in Bhaktapur, Nepal.
2Nepal, facing billions in reconstruction costs, has appealed for foreign governments and agencies to help with aid and funds.
3Almost 745,600 buildings and homes have been damaged or destroyed, including at least 87,700 in the capital, according to Nepal's emergency authority.
4Engineers say only 40 percent of Kathmandu's damaged buildings as habitable.
5The worn-out workers of Jaya Mayaswori Brick Factory are packing clay and hauling loads with a new sense of purpose - helping to rebuild Nepal after a series of massive earthquakes and aftershocks ravaged the Himalayan nation, leaving hundreds of thousands homeless.
6There is urgency to the back-breaking work.
7Nepal was crippled by last month's magnitude-7.8 earthquake, which killed more than 8,000, while a string of strong aftershocks has further terrified the survivors.
8The capital of Kathmandu is in shambles.
9Entire villages are flattened into rubble and dust.
10And many businesses have shut down, leaving survivors scrambling for income.
11The brick factories clustered in Bhaktapur, a town on the outskirts of Kathmandu, are some of the few businesses still running.
12For the owners and workers who have not fled back to their villages, that means there is still a chance of earning daily wages and profits.
13"I am making my contribution in the rebuilding our country by making these bricks," said 17-year-old Barsa Pun Magar, who joined the factory six months ago.
14"There has been so much destruction," she said.
15"Every brick is going to help bring the country and people back on their feet."
16Though many of the factories have also been damaged, with kiln towers toppled and workers' housing destroyed, those who remain are laboring overtime before the anticipated rush for construction materials.
17In a few weeks, monsoon will arrive and create impassable mud swamps, trigger landslides and pummel the tarpaulin tents providing shelter to many across the country.
18"There is definitely going to be a big demand for bricks in the next few months as people start to rebuild," factory owner Shree Bhakta Sukhupayo said.
19He expects the price of bricks to go up, from a pre-quake level of about 17 U.S. cents to as much as 25 cents.
20But he insists he will not profit from the tragedy by raising his prices.
21"We have faced a big loss in the earthquake, we are also victims," he said.
22"We hope the government will bring programs that could help businesses like ours."
23Nepal faces billions in reconstruction costs, and has appealed for foreign governments and agencies for help.
24Brick-making is grueling work, involving packing clay into rectangular lumps, firing them in kilns and hauling them to enormous stacks awaiting transport.
25The hours are also long - sometimes 12 hours a day.
26But the workers, many with family in shattered villages, are also racing against time and weather.
27With Nepal's needs so vast, and its future prospects uncertain, many are doing overtime because it's the only job they can get.
28"I have to work to feed my family," said Kalu Ram Bika, 37, who sends some of the money he earns to his parents in their village in southern Nepal.
29"We work. We get fed. It does not matter how bad the situation is. We have to work."
