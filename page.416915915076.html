Al-Jazeera reporter sues channel over Egypt detention

1An Al-Jazeera journalist on retrial in Egypt has sued his Qatari employer for $100 million ( 64 million), his lawyers said on Monday, claiming the satellite network was negligent and supported blacklisted Islamists.
2Mr Fahmy and his Egyptian producer Baher Mohamed were freed on bail ahead of their retrial.
3• Who are the al-Jazeera three – and why were they imprisoned?
4On Monday, Mr Fahmy's Canadian lawyer Joanna Gislason said at a Cairo news conference that her client had now filed a case of "negligence" against the channel in a Canadian court.
5Mr Fahmy has demanded "100 million dollars for his detention and conviction" from the network, she told reporters.
6The three journalists were arrested in December 2013 during a crackdown on supporters of Morsi, who was ousted by then army chief and now President Abdel Fattah al-Sisi after mass protests against his sole year of divisive rule.
7Their first trial came against the backdrop of strained ties between Egypt and Qatar, which supported Mr Morsi.
8Egypt's authorities charged the three with spreading "false news" during their coverage of demonstrations, and also said they were working without valid accreditation.
9In a statement read out at the news conference, Mr Fahmy accused the network's now closed Egyptian channel, Al-Jazeera Mubasher Misr, of aiding the Muslim Brotherhood.
10"Al-Jazeera Mubasher was a sponsor for the Muslim Brotherhood and its equipment was used by Muslim Brotherhood activists across the country," said Mr Fahmy, who has given up his Egyptian citizenship to facilitate his deportation to Canada.
11Al-Jazeera Mubasher was "not just biased, but was a sponsor for the Muslim Brotherhood", he said.
12"Al-Jazeera is one of the foreign arms of Qatar."
13Mr Fahmy's Egyptian lawyer Mohamed Hamouda said his client had chosen Canada to file the lawsuit because "he is seen as a traitor in Egypt" since surrendering his citizenship.
14"He is negatively perceived. That's why he filed the case in Canadian courts," Mr Hamouda said.
