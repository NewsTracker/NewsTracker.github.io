Backlogged orders as shoppers get close-up look at Apple Watch

1SAN FRANCISCO/PARIS (Reuters) - Customers preordering Apple Inc's smartwatch on Friday will have to wait at least a month for delivery, a sign of strong early demand for company chief Tim Cook's first new major product.
2People flocked to Apple's stores around the world to get a close-up look at the Apple Watch, the tech company's foray into the personal luxury goods market, with Apple predicting demand would exceed supply at product launch.
3Cook, interviewed on cable television channel CNBC, said initial orders were "great" for the device, available for preorder online and to try out in stores by appointment, but not to take home.
4"We view this as an indication of solid demand paired with very limited supply," Piper Jaffray analyst Gene Munster wrote in a note to clients.
5"We continue to expect modest sales in the June quarter as demand ramps over time."
6A key factor in the watch's success will be demand once an initial wave of interest from Apple enthusiasts subsides.
7The watch goes on sale officially on April 24, online and through appointments in shops, including trendy fashion boutiques in Paris, London and Tokyo, part of Apple's strategy of positioning the wearable computer as a must-have accessory.
8But soon after online preorders opened on Friday, Apple's website listed shipping times in June for some models of the watch and four to six weeks for others.
9There was immediately brisk bidding on eBay for confirmed orders for watches, with hundreds of sellers looking to make a few hundred or even thousand dollars by passing on their watches, once received.
10Testing Apple's mastery of consumer trends, the watch is an untried concept for the Cupertino, California-based company.
11It straddles a technology market accustomed to rapid obsolescence and luxury goods whose appeal lies in their enduring value.
12The Apple Watch sport starts at $349 while the standard version comes in at $549 in the United States.
13High-end "Edition" watches with 18-karat gold alloys are priced from $10,000 and go as high as $17,000.
14At a San Francisco Apple store, dozens of customers crowded around newly installed wooden cabinets, snapping pictures of the gadgets on display under glass.
15Apple employees, admittedly still unfamiliar with the watches' finer points, guided customers through features like text messaging, maps and fitness tracking.
16At Apple's flagship store in New York, Jack Weber, who was visiting from Charlottesville, Virginia, said he would give his wife a top-of-the-line "Edition" as a 50th-anniversary gift.
17"What more perfect wedding present could there be than this watch?" he said.
18Long wait times will likely stimulate more demand for the watch - which allows users to check email, listen to music and make phone calls when paired with an iPhone - with little risk of losing impatient customers, said JMP analyst Alex Gauna.
19"You would want to catch up by the holiday season," Gauna said.
20"But based on what's out there in Android land, I don't think there's an extreme risk in near term of losing customers who must have a smartwatch and will go to some alternative."
21Android is Google's mobile operating system used on many smartwatches.
22Reviewers this week praised the watch as "beautiful" and "stylish" but gave it poor marks for relatively low battery life and slow-loading apps.
23Sales estimates for 2015 vary widely.
24Piper Jaffray predicts 8 million units and Global Securities Research forecasts 40 million.
25By comparison, Apple sold nearly 200 million iPhones last year.
26Apple's watch is widely expected to outsell those by Samsung, Sony Corp and Fitbit.
27It will likely account for 55 percent of global smartwatch shipments this year, according to Societe Generale.
28"Apple will outsell its wearable rivals by a very wide margin but it will do this on the power of its brand and its design alone," independent technology analyst Richard Windsor said.
29Apple shares closed 0.43 percent higher at $127.10 on Nasdaq.
