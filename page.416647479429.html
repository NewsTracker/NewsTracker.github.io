Moments of joy in Nepal as 2 are rescued 5 days after quake

1KATHMANDU, Nepal (AP) - The 15-year-old boy had been buried alive for five days, listening to bulldozers clearing mountains of debris, fearful the incessant aftershocks might collapse the darkened crevice he was trapped in.
2And then, "all of the sudden I saw light," Pempa Tamang said, recounting the moment Thursday he was pulled from a hole at the bottom of what was once a seven-story building in Kathmandu.
3Tamang did not know whether he was alive or dead.
4"I thought I was hallucinating," he said.
5The improbable rescue was an uplifting moment in Nepal, which has been overwhelmed by death and destruction since the 7.8-magnitude earthquake hit Saturday.
6By late Thursday, the government said the toll from the tremor, the most powerful recorded here since 1934, had risen to 6,130 dead and 13,827 injured.
7After night fell, police reported another dramatic rescue: A woman in her 20s, Krishna Devi Khadka, was pulled from a building in the same neighborhood as Tamang near Kathmandu's main bus terminal, according to an officer who spoke on condition of anonymity because he wasn't authorized to talk to the media.
8"Life has become a struggle to survive. It gives us hope," Hans Raj Joshi said after watching Tamang's rescue.
9"We thought they were only bringing out the dead. It's hard to believe people are still alive."
10When Tamang was finally extricated, rescue workers inserted an IV in his arm, propped him onto a yellow plastic stretcher - the same kind that has helped convey countless dead - and carried him through the ruins on their shoulders as if he was a newly crowned king.
11Lines of police stood on both sides, keeping back mobs of bystanders and journalists.
12A dazed Tamang, wearing a dark shirt with the New York Yankees logo and the words "New York Authentic," blinked at the bright sky.
13When the procession turned a corner and entered the main road outside, there came a sound Kathmandu hadn't heard in days: the jubilant cheers of thousands of ecstatic onlookers.
14Nepal, however, is far from normal.
15More than 70 aftershocks have been recorded in the Himalayan region by Indian scientists in the past five days, according to J.L. Gautam, the director of seismology at the Indian Meteorological Department in New Delhi.
16Shortages of food and water, and worry over the fate of relatives, have caused many to flee the capital.
17Thousands have boarded buses provided by the government to their rural hometowns.
18"I have to get home. It has already been so many days," said Shanti Kumari, with her 7-year-old daughter, who was desperate to see family in her home village in eastern Nepal.
19Although small shops have begun reopening, and the once ubiquitous tent cities have begun thinning out, an air of desperation remains.
20"We're still feeling aftershocks. It still doesn't feel safe," said Prabhu Dutta, a 27-year-old banker from Kathmandu.
21Some residents have begun returning to work, including at Dutta's bank, but he said it was impossible to concentrate.
22"We roam around the office. We only have one topic of conversation: the earthquake."
23Tamang recounted his story to a group of reporters inside a tent at an Israeli field hospital.
24He looked weak and tired.
25His dark hair was disheveled.
26But otherwise he seemed fine.
27When Saturday's quake began at 11:56 a.m., Tamang said he was having lunch with a friend in the hotel where he worked.
28As he ran down toward the way out, the stairs shook.
29He saw walls cracking, ceilings caving in.
30He was in the basement when "suddenly the building fell down. I thought I was about to die," he said.
31Tamang fainted, and when he regained consciousness, he could see little but darkness.
32He was buried face down in a tiny crevice deep in the rubble, and terrified.
33He could barely move.
34Tamang survived on cans of ghee, or clarified butter.
35He rested his head on chunks of concrete and broken piece of corrugated aluminum roof.
36Finally, on Thursday, one Nepalese team began combing the rubble in Tamang's neighborhood, a place they had found another survivor Monday.
37They cried out and knocked on broken concrete slabs, and then listened closely for any response.
38Mostly there was silence.
39But when an officer named L.
40Bahadu Basnet, shouted "Is anyone there?" he was shocked to get a reply.
41"Who is there? Brother, I am here!"
42Tamang shouted back weakly.
43The team used a car jack to help ensure a slab above the rectangular entrance did not cave in.
44Basnet took off his helmet, put on a headlamp, and crawled on his arms 10 feet (3 meters) inside, pushed in by his colleagues.
45He could see Tamang wedged lying down in a crevice behind a motorcycle, and was shocked how responsive he was.
46"He thanked me when I first approached him," Basnet said.
47"He told me his name, his address, and I gave him some water. I assured him we were near."
48It took a few hours to delicately clear the way for Tamang to be lifted out.
49Members of the U.S. Agency for International Development's Disaster Assistance Response Team brought in equipment to help and lowered a pole-mounted rotatable camera into the hole, said one of the team's members, Andrew Olvera.
50Police said Tamang asked for one thing while he waited: juice.
51Looking at a pair of huge, ripped concrete floors hanging precariously like curtains on the side of the destroyed building, just above the cratered rescue site, Olvera said the operation was dangerous.
52But, "it's risk versus gain. To save a human life, we'll risk almost anything."
53At the Israeli field hospital, doctors X-rayed Tamang and injected him with glucose.
54Lt. Libby Weiss of the Israeli Defense Forces said he was dehydrated but lucid and "in remarkably good shape," with no other injuries except scratches.
55"It's a miracle," Weiss said.
56"I think it's an amazing thing to see in the midst of all this calamity."
57Naryan Pandey was one of the spectators standing on the main street outside when Tamang was carried out.
58"I'm surprised he's still alive. We've seen dead bodies coming out of the rubble for five days," Pandey said.
59"But it doesn't change what we are going through. I've barely eaten. We don't have enough water. I'm hungry."
60And then he added, eyeing the rubble beyond: "My friend is still in there. He was a cook. He's still there."
