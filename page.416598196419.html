Baltimore protesters defy curfew, lob bottles; police fire pepper spray

1Woman, said to be Baltimore protester's mother, angrily drags him away.
2Defiant protesters threw bottles and police launched pepper spray on Baltimore's west side as authorities tried to clear the crowd in this riot-racked city after the 10 p.m. curfew passed.
3As a large crowd of reporters watched late Tuesday, angry demonstrators started small fires and knocked over some trash cans before they fled from the street near a burned-down CVS drugstore that has become the focus of this week's protests and violence.
4Police said on Twitter that "a group of criminals" had started a fire outside a library near the CVS.
5Overhead, a helicopter hovered, broadcasting an order over its loudspeaker: "You must go home. You will be subject to arrest."
6Some demonstrators had left on their own before curfew fell.
7Rep.
8Elijah E. Cummings (D-Md.)
9tried to clear the remaining crowd.
10"Please go home to your families.... I'm not asking you. I'm begging you," he said.
11Police moved slowly, trying to push protesters back without making direct contact.
12The crowd thinned.
13Tony Sellers, 40, said he lived two blocks from the CVS.
14"We can't go out of our own homes? Why? We ain't doing nothing wrong," he said.
15Darien Ford, 25, said the protests were a chance for the people to speak to power.
16"It looks like this is the only way to get some attention here in Baltimore," he said as curfew approached.
17On the city's south side, the police department tweeted about 15 minutes before curfew that at least one officer had been hurt in a rock-throwing incident.
18Earlier in the evening, a disembodied voice came over the police loudspeaker.
19The crowd thought the speaker, who didn't identify herself, was Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake, though that could not be immediately confirmed.
20The woman said, "Thank you for the peaceful protests.& Let's take our babies home.
21"We are going to show them, what they think is going to happen is not going to happen, because we care about Baltimore and want to build a better Baltimore," the woman said.
22"Let's show them what kind of Baltimore we have."
23As soon she stopped speaking, a group of men mostly dressed in red, with arms locked, marched like a human fence toward the demonstrators and swept them away from the police in riot gear.
24After the line moved away, a smaller crowd of more than 100 people, apparently determined to risk breaking curfew and possible arrest, remained near the CVS.
25Some young people lingered, saying they were determined to stay.
26"I'm a grown man," said one who declined to give his name.
27"I can do what I want."
28But the crowd began to thin as 10 p.m. approached.
29Several protesters asked what time it was, saying they wanted to leave before curfew.
30A community leader used an amplifier to urge the crowd to leave.
31"Please go home. Do not give anyone excuses to do something that's not right.... it's not about selling out," she said.
32Another man pleaded with the news media to leave, saying if they did the crowd would go too.
33But another man shouted, "We don't need no curfew. We are grown-ass people."
34A man on a bicycle sat in front of the police line, as if waiting to see what would happen.
35About 2,000 National Guard troops and more than 1,000 police officers were deployed to the streets of Baltimore, according to Maryland Gov. Larry Hogan.
36The city has been under a state of emergency after stone-throwing and arson erupted Monday hours after the funeral of a black man who suffered a mortal injury while in police custody.
37"Maintaining law and order, protecting innocent lives and property is our No. 1 priority," Hogan, who has temporarily moved his office from the Capitol in Annapolis to Baltimore, said at a televised news conference.
38"We've got a long night ahead of us."
39As darkness fell, police Capt.
40Eric Kowalczyk told reporters that in one group of demonstrators that had gathered on the streets, "There has been an increase in the level of anger and frustration in the crowd, and that is starting to grow. & We hope for peace."
41A couple hundred protesters gathered around City Hall after dark, about an hour before the 10 p.m. curfew, chanting "Justice for Freddy," singing "Lean on Me," and various Bob Marley tunes.
42"Police brutality is something that people of African descent have suffered ever since the slave ships brought them over," one speaker said under a bright moon and the glow of television Kleig lights.
43At least nine satellite trucks for reporters from around the world were parked around them to produce live shots.
44Police and National Guardsmen with military trucks stood guard outside a perimeter fence, holding guns and long billy clubs.
45In a late afternoon news conference, Rawlings-Blake said the city had been relatively calm Tuesday, and she thanked residents and community leaders who helped clean up the debris from Monday night's riots.
46"Today I think we saw a lot more of what Baltimore is about," she said.
47"We saw people coming together to reclaim our city."
48Baltimore Police Commissioner Anthony Batts said protests during the day had been peaceful.
49Officers arrested a few looters on the east side of the city Tuesday morning and one or two demonstrators who were part of a large march that moved down Pennsylvania Avenue on Tuesday afternoon.
50"This is where we live. This is where we worship," Batts said.
51"This is where our kids go to school, so don't destroy it."
52Baltimore's schools were to reopen Wednesday, and officials vowed to punish any students who participated in the riots.
53"I am deeply angered that the inexcusable actions of those few now threaten to color perceptions about the many," Baltimore City Public Schools Chief Executive Gregory E. Thornton said in a letter to parents Tuesday.
54"That is why I assert in the strongest possible terms that the students who engaged in violence on Monday will be held accountable. We are working to identify those students, who will experience consequences in full accordance with the law and City Schools' code of conduct."
55Thornton called the decision to cancel classes Tuesday "a necessary decision because of ongoing cleanup in some neighborhoods, lack of transportation through the Mondawmin transit hub [where rioting first broke out Monday], and most important, the need for district staff to plan and make arrangements to ensure the safety of students and staff at school for the remainder of the week."
56Earlier in the day, officials tallied the toll since unrest began Monday afternoon: 235 arrests, including 34 juveniles; 15 structure fires; 144 vehicles destroyed; and more than 20 police officers injured.
57At least one civilian was reported in critical condition, but no other details were given.
58At the Tuesday news conference, Batts said nearly all of the officers, some who suffered hand injures when deflecting rocks and bottles, had been treated and released.
59One was hospitalized overnight with a serious head injury but is expected to recover.
60In earlier remarks, Kowalczyk said police would enforce a curfew, set to begin at 10 p.m. and run until 5 a.m., but would use common sense.
61Those seeking medical care and returning from work were exempt from the curfew, he said.
62He defended the police response.
63"When we deployed our officers yesterday, we were deploying for a high school event," Kowalczyk said.
64"I don't think there's anyone that would expect us to deploy with automatic weapons and armored vehicles for 13- 14- and 15-year-olds."
65He added: "What we saw last night was a group of people take advantage of a situation, a very unfortunate situation, and use that to tear down their own neighborhoods."
66Hogan, the governor, said after touring the stricken areas earlier in the day, "This violence isn't accomplishing anything. It's counterproductive."
67He pledged that violence would be dealt with forcefully and that the city would not have to endure a repeat of Monday night.
68"This is not the Baltimore we love," the governor said.
69As residents prepared for the start of the weeklong curfew, much of the city remained closed Tuesday.
70Schools and many businesses were shuttered, and the Baltimore Orioles postponed a second straight game against the Chicago White Sox.
71The Orioles and White Sox will play their regularly scheduled game Wednesday, but it was moved from the evening to the afternoon, and no fans will be admitted, Major League Baseball announced.
72Camouflage-clad National Guard troops, armed with assault rifles, surrounded major public spaces such as City Hall and the Inner Harbor with a show of force that included heavy-duty military vehicles.
73The governor said thousands of officers and troops were on the streets, with more expected.
74He thanked fellow Republican Gov. Chris Christie of New Jersey for sending 150 state troopers, among the dozens from surrounding cities and states.
75Baltimore residents struggled to shake off the nightmarish violence that began hours after Freddie Gray was buried.
76Gray died April 19 of a severed spine, a week after he was taken into custody by Baltimore police.
77Officials are investigating the events, which drew early small and peaceful protests that escalated over the weekend and turned Baltimore into a battle zone Monday.
78At a Washington news conference with Japan's Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, President Obama said the issues of relations between African Americans and police were larger than the looters, whom he condemned.
79"There's no excuse for the kind of violence that we saw yesterday," Obama said.
80"It is counterproductive. When individuals get crowbars and start prying open doors to loot, they're not protesting, they're not making a statement - they're stealing. When they burn down a building, they're committing arson. And they're destroying and undermining businesses and opportunities in their own communities that rob jobs and opportunity from people in that area."
81But the president also defended the right to protest and called for a broader discussion of how the nation deals with racism and police.
82"We can't just leave this to the police. I think there are police departments that have to do some soul-searching. I think there are some communities that have to do some soul-searching. But I think we, as a country, have to do some soul-searching. This is not new. It's been going on for decades."
83Meanwhile, some parts of Baltimore tried to return to a semblance of normalcy.
84Holding brooms and shovels from their own homes, Baltimore residents showed up in droves to clean up the riot debris: shattered windows, rocks, ashes.
85On a sunny Tuesday morning, the mood was much more cordial toward police, who were repeatedly offered bottled water as they stood guard over damaged retail shops.
86But there was a pervasive feeling that the goodwill could sour at any time.
87"The anger you saw is about decades of pain and abuse in our community," said Megan Kenny, 38, an education provider in the city.
88"The movement isn't going to end. I mean, how do you end racism?"
89Kenny and her boyfriend, Paul Mericle, 31, who works for Baltimore public schools, took the opportunity of an unexpected day off to join residents along North Avenue to clean up debris.
90"People have been up cleaning since before dawn," Mericle said in the shadow of EZ Mart Tobacco and Convenience, which had been ransacked with shelves emptied.
91Across the street, a big rig with a green trailer sat with piles of garbage bags as people with dust trays and snow shovels walked by.
92Farther down the street, though, was a stark reminder of the tension.
93The CVS on North and Pennsylvania avenues sat smoldering as lines of county police stood with defensive shields.
94Opposite them was a crowd - one man with a bullhorn - talking about the death of Gray.
95As more residents began massing on the east side of Pennsylvania, police began handing out more shields out of a small trailer to the police.
96"The violence isn't over," said a Baltimore police officer who was not authorized to speak publicly on the rioting.
97"We have a long way to go with the community here. We have a lot of wounds to heal."
98Rawlings-Blake spoke of healing as she toured the damage.
99She said public transportation would be up and running and that she was working to make sure that "most government services can operate normally."
100Speaking at the West Baltimore CVS, Rawlings-Blake said: "What happened last night means that more people are struggling.... We worked very hard to get CVS to come here."
101Hogan said state insurance officials would work on helping residents.
102As the rioting ended, questions have continued about whether the city and state moved quickly enough to stop the violence.
103The governor was careful not to assign any blame to city officials, whom he praised.
104Hogan said the state had prepared to mobilize the National Guard and issue an emergency declaration on Monday afternoon as television broadcast the first images of the confrontation between teenagers and police.
105The formal declarations came about 6 p.m., seconds after they were requested by the city, he said.
106Asked if the mayor should have called for help sooner, Hogan replied that he didn't want to question what Baltimore officials were doing: "They're all under tremendous stress. We're all on one team."
107During comments as she toured the damaged areas of her city, Rawlings-Blake pushed back against her critics.
108"There are always going to be armchair quarterbacks that have never sat in my seat," she told reporters.
109"This isn't the first emergency that I've had to deal with, and I know you have to put in the work and manage the crisis on the ground."
110Batts, the police commissioner, said late Monday that the city simply didn't have enough officers to maintain control of all the neighborhoods, as looting and fires spread from one end of the city to the other.
111He said the extra manpower arriving late Monday and Tuesday would help the police regain control of neighborhoods and enforce a weeklong curfew.
112Batts said he was dismayed by scenes of Baltimore's teenagers looting and burning.
113He praised one woman who was filmed smacking her teenage son on the head and pulling off his hood.
114"I wish we had more parents that took charge of their kids out there tonight."
