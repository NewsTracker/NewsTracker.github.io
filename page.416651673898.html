Nepal quake toll passes 6,200; smell preventing survivors from returning home

1Photo: Officials have ordered immediate cremations to cope with the number of bodies being found.
2The death toll from Nepal's devastating earthquake has risen past 6,200 as fresh aftershocks and the smell of rotting bodies make it hard for nervous survivors to return to their homes.
3Disposal of the hundreds of bodies still being found six days after the magnitude-7.8 quake devastated the Himalayan nation of 28 million people was becoming a problem for officials, who have ordered immediate cremations.
4"Morgues are full beyond capacity and we have been given instruction to incinerate bodies immediately after they are pulled out," said Raman Lal, an Indian paramilitary force official working in coordination with Nepali forces.
5Aid is slowly beginning to reach remote towns and villages nestled in the mountains and foothills, but the overpowering smell of bodies has made it hard for residents to return to their homes.
6Many Nepalis have been sleeping in the open since Saturday's quake.
7According to the United Nations, 600,000 houses have been destroyed or damaged.
8More than 60 Australians have been evacuated from Kathmandu in RAAF C-17 Globemasters, which also delivered 15 tonnes of aid.
9Finance minister Ram Sharan Mahat said Nepal would need at least $2 billion to rebuild homes, hospitals, government offices and historic buildings and appealed for help from international donors.
10"This is just an initial estimate and it will take time to assess the extent of damage and calculate the cost of rebuilding," Mr Mahat told Reuters.
11The United Nations has said 8 million people had been affected, with at least 2 million in need of tents, water, food and medicines over the next three months.
12A home ministry official said the death toll had risen to 6,204, with 13,924 injured.
13Information minister Minendra Rijal said the government would provide $1,000 in immediate assistance to the families of those killed, as well as $400 for cremation or burial.
14Prime minister Sushil Koirala told Reuters earlier this week the death toll from the quake could reach 10,000.
15That would surpass the 8,500 who died in a 1934 earthquake, the last disaster on this scale to hit the Himalayan nation sandwiched between India and China.
16Home ministry official Laxmi Prasad Dhakal said that, even though the 1934 quake was more powerful, there were fewer people living in the Kathmandu valley then.
17"The scale of reconstruction will be unprecedented," Mr Dhakal said.
18"We have still not even been able to assess the damage in remote areas, which have been completely devastated."
19Officials have said the chances of finding any more survivors were fading, even though a boy and a woman had on Thursday been pulled from the rubble where they had lain trapped for five days.
20The ABC's Siobhan Heanue was in Patan's Durbar Square, a historic temple complex on the outskirts of Kathmandu, when the massive 7.9 magnitude earthquake hit Nepal.
21As rescuers slowly started reaching outlying areas, witnesses reported seeing 70 to 80 per cent of buildings severely damaged in Chautara, north-east of Kathmandu towards the border with China's Tibet.
22Anger over the pace of the rescue has flared in some areas, with Nepalis accusing the government of being too slow to distribute international aid that has flooded into the country.
23It has yet to reach many in need, particularly in areas hard to reach given the quake damage, poor weather and aftershocks.
24Tensions between foreigners and Nepalis desperate to be evacuated have also surfaced.
25In Ashrang village in Gorkha - one of the worst-hit districts about four hours by road west of Kathmandu - hundreds of villagers were living outdoors with little food and water even as boxes of biscuits, juice and sacks of rice and wheat were stored in a nearby government office.
26Nepal is also appealing to foreign governments for more helicopters to help the 20 at work in rescue operations.
27China was expected to send more, Mr Dhakal said.
28In the Himalayas, climbing is set to reopen on Mount Everest next week after damage caused by avalanches triggered by the quake is repaired, although many have abandoned their ascents.
