Manhunt Is Underway After Police Officers Are Shot in Ferguson

1- The police conducted a manhunt in this tense and battered city on Thursday in search of whoever shot two police officers as they worked at a protest after the resignation of the chief.
2The shooting just after midnight on Thursday morning, described as "really an ambush" by Chief Jon Belmar of the St. Louis County Police Department, was denounced by all sides in the continuing conflict over law enforcement here after the death, at the hands of the police, of an unarmed black teenager last summer, and the police crackdown on the ensuing protests.
3By Thursday night, relative calm had returned to the streets as clergy members and activists gathered for a candlelight prayer vigil that paired condemnations of the officers' shootings with support for nonviolent protests.
4About 100 protesters later marched outside the Ferguson Police Department, but by midnight, with a light rain falling, most of the demonstrators had cleared the way, and the streets were largely quiet.
5The police said no one had been arrested - neither protesters, nor suspects.
6Earlier, police SWAT units surrounded a house a few blocks from the scene of the shooting, and officers climbed onto the roof and broke through a vent to gain access.
7The police took in three people from the house for questioning and released them hours later.
8The three, Iresha Turner, who lives at the home, and her friends Martez Little and Lamont Underwood, said they had attended the protest but had nothing to do with the shootings.
9Ms. Turner and Mr. Underwood said they fled from the protest to Ms. Turner's house when the shots were fired, and Mr. Little said he came to Mr. Turner's home later and was also detained.
10Ms. Turner said her 6-year-old son had been traumatized by the search and the implication that his mother might have something to do with the crime.
11"I have to live here," said Ms. Turner, who identified herself as a single mother.
12"I have no help. I'm a good woman."
13Mr. Underwood speculated that someone might have seen him and Ms. Turner speeding away from the protest scene and reported it to the police.
14On Thursday, the St. Louis County Police Department and the Missouri State Highway Patrol assumed responsibility for security in Ferguson, just as they did after the unrest following the shooting of the teenager, Michael Brown Jr., by a white police officer, Darren Wilson.
15A grand jury did not indict the officer, and the Justice Department also declined to bring charges.
16The two police officers were shot shortly after midnight during a protest in front of the police station after the chief, Thomas Jackson, announced his resignation.
17His departure was the most recent in a shake-up of the city's most senior administrators after a recent Justice Department report that described a city that used its legal system to generate revenue, in the process violating constitutional rights and disproportionately targeting blacks.
18Attorney General Eric H. Holder Jr. denounced the shootings as "heinous and cowardly attacks."
19"This was not someone trying to bring healing to Ferguson," Mr. Holder said at a news conference in Washington.
20Community organizers extended sympathy to the officers who were shot, while trying to keep the focus on their own longstanding complaints.
21Gov. Jay Nixon visited, his motorcade rolling past the site of the shootings that he called, in a statement, "cowardly and reprehensible."
22The shootings came at a vulnerable time, just as the city was making "good-faith steps," as Mr. Holder put it, to restore faith in its criminal justice system.
23In a statement, the City of Ferguson said it was "diligently working to make systematic changes necessary to instill confidence," but added, "We cannot continue to move forward under threats of violence and destruction."
24With the issue of police shootings bringing political polarization, Mr. Holder, who has been at the forefront of the effort to bring change to Ferguson's policing, came in for some criticism.
25"There's an atmosphere of unbalance here," Rudolph W. Giuliani, a former mayor of New York, said on Fox News, blaming the Justice Department for emphasizing the faults of the Ferguson Police Department without saying that the officer who fatally shot Mr. Brown last August "did exactly what he should do."
26The late-night protest on Wednesday started as a celebration of Chief Jackson's resignation, but also as a call for more action.
27"Not just Jackson, we want Knowles, Ferguson has got to go!" the demonstrators yelled in reference to James Knowles III, the mayor.
28When the shots echoed through the crisp air, striking the two officers, demonstrators and police officers hit the ground.
29Many people ran for cover, and police officers clad in riot gear dragged their wounded comrades to safety.
30"We're lucky by God's grace we didn't lose two officers last night," Chief Belmar said at a midmorning news conference.
31It was clear that the police were the targets, he said.
32Based on the sound of the shots and the officers' wounds, he said, the weapon was a handgun, not a rifle.
33The officers who were shot were standing side by side, part of a cordon from multiple police departments, keeping protesters away from the police station.
34There had been as many as 69 officers in the evening, dwindling to about 50 at the time of the shooting, Chief Belmar said.
35One of the wounded officers was from the Webster Groves Police Department.
36He is 32 and a seven-year veteran of the force.
37The other was from the St. Louis County Police and is 41 with 14 years' experience, the county police said.
38Both were treated at Barnes-Jewish Hospital in St. Louis.
39The younger officer was shot in the cheekbone, just below his right eye, and the bullet lodged behind his right ear, Chief Belmar said.
40A bullet struck the other officer in the right shoulder and exited his back on the right side.
41No officers returned fire.
42The officers were released from a hospital Thursday morning.
43Chief Belmar said that people had a right to protest peacefully, but also that "there is an unfortunate association with that gathering" and the shooting.
44Witnesses among the demonstrators denied any link to the shootings, saying that they believed the shots originated from the top of a hill about 220 yards directly opposite the station.
45Chief Belmar did not specify a location but estimated the distance at 125 yards.
46"There's just no way anybody I know did that," said Bob Hudgins, a protester who is running for City Council.
47"Nobody's happy about this today."
48The Brown family, in a statement from its lawyer, denounced "the actions of stand-alone agitators" who tried to derail the protests.
49"We reject any kind of violence directed toward members of law enforcement," the statement said.
50"It cannot and will not be tolerated."
51Chief Belmar said the shooting realized his worst fears over the months of unrest since Mr. Brown's killing.
52He drew a parallel to the fatal shooting in December of two New York City police officers, Rafael Ramos and Wenjian Liu, by a man who said he was enraged by killings by the police, including Mr. Brown's.
53"We were very close to having happen what happened to N.Y.P.D.," Chief Belmar said.
