Palme d'Or up for grabs ahead of Cannes Film Festival finale

1Michael Caine and Harvey Keitel pose for photographers during a photo call for the film Youth, at the 68th international film festival, Cannes, southern France, Wednesday, May 20, 2015.
2Cate Blanchett and director Todd Haynes pose for photographers upon arrival for the screening of the film Carol at the 68th international film festival, Cannes, southern France, Sunday, May 17, 2015.
3The 68th Cannes Film Festival concludes Sunday with the awarding of its top honor, the prestigious Palme d'Or, as well as a handful of other distinctions.
4Few trophies in movies are more sought after than the Palme d'Or, but, unlike the Academy Awards, which whittles its field down to a few favorites over the course of months, Cannes winners are chosen clandestinely by a jury.
5Throughout the festival, the jury, led by Joel and Ethan Coen, has quietly slipped in and out of theaters.
6Their deliberations are private, but that doesn't stop the world's media from endlessly debating the possible preferences of the Coens and fellow jurors Guillermo del Toro, Jake Gyllenhaal, Sienna Miller, Sophie Marceau, Xavier Dolan, Rokia Traore and Rossy de Palma.
7The Cannes competition concluded Saturday with the stylish bloodbath of director Justin Kurzel's "Macbeth," starring Michael Fassbender and Marion Cotillard.
8All of the 19 films in competition could feasibly walk away with the Palme d'Or, but a handful of perceived favorites have emerged.
9One thing is for certain: For the first time in several years, the Palme d'Or winner won't be a three-hour art-house epic.
10In 2013, Steven Spielberg's jury chose the lesbian coming-of-age drama "Blue is the Warmest Color"; last year, Jane Campion's jury elected the glacial Turkish drama "Winter Sleep."
11First-time filmmakers rarely make it into the competition, let alone win the Palme d'Or.
12The Camera d'Or, the Cannes award for best first feature, could ultimately be what director Laszlo Nemes' "Son of Saul" takes home, but no other film here found the universal acclaim that this Hungarian Holocaust drama did.
13Hauntingly gripping, it tracks a member of the Sonderkommando at Auschwitz who believes his son is among the gas chamber dead.
14Italian director Paolo Sorrentino's latest was considered a slight step below his previous, Oscar-winning film "The Great Beauty."
15But his comic, melancholy tale of a retired composer (Michael Caine) reflecting on life and art at a Swiss spa was one of the liveliest entries of the festival.
16"Youth" is highlighted by the performances of two old vets: Caine and Harvey Keitel.
17Director Hou Hsiao-Hsien's latest was unquestionably the most beautiful film in Cannes, but was it the best?
18Many thought so, lavishing praise on the Taiwan director's lush, painterly imagery and his quiet inversion of the martial arts genre.
19The 68-year-old Hou is widely regarded as one of today's great filmmakers, but Western audiences' awareness of him has lagged behind that of the critics.
20A Palme d'Or would be the crowning of an acknowledged master.
21Todd Haynes' adaptation of Patricia Highsmith's 1952 novel landed with obvious resonance.
22This year's Cannes has been dominated by debate about gender equality in film, and Haynes' film, which stars Cate Blanchett and Rooney Mara as illicit lovers in '50s America, is precisely the kind of story that has struggled to find big-screen representation.
23Plus, the tender precision of its romance had Cannes critics swooning.
