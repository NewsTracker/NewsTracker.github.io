Islamic State's path of destruction through Syria and Iraq

1The Islamic State group's demolition of the Saint Elian Monastery in the central Syrian province of Homs is the latest in a long campaign that has destroyed or extensively damaged some of the Middle East's most spectacular archaeological and cultural sites.
2Some of the world's most precious cultural treasures, including ancient sites in the cradle of civilisation, are in areas controlled by the group and at the mercy of extremists bent on wiping out all non-Islamic culture and history.
3In addition to pre-Islamic sites, the militants have also targeted churches, mosques and museums.
4In May, the extremists captured the central Syrian town of Palmyra raising fears they would demolish the 2,000-year-old Roman-era city at the edge of the town – a Unesco world heritage site and one of the Middle East's most iconic archaeological sites.
5The 1,500-year-old monastery had already been damaged by Syrian government shelling in recent weeks, according to an official with an organisation representing Assyrian Christians.
6On Friday, Isil posted photographs on social media sites showing bulldozers destroying the monastery.
7Islamic State fighters fully captured the central Syrian town, home to one of the Middle East's most spectacular archaeological sites, in late May. In June, the head of the Syrian government's Antiquities and Museums Department, Maamoun Abdulkarim, said Isil militants had destroyed a lion statue dating back to the 2nd century.
8The statue, discovered in 1975, had stood at the gate of the town's museum, and had been placed inside a metal box to protect it from damage.
9In July, Isil released a statement saying that six busts from Palmyra had been confiscated from a smuggler.
10Photographs released by the group showed Isil militants destroying the busts with large hammers and the smuggler being whipped.
11On Tuesday, Isil militants publicly beheaded Khaled al-Asaad, an 81-year-old Palmyra resident and antiquities scholar whose lifelong work had earned him the nickname "Mr Palmyra" in the archaeological community.
12This week, Unesco chief Irina Bokova told The Associated Press that satellite images of Palmyra revealed a network of holes dug in the area for "illicit excavations and then eventually trafficking and looting".
13In the 9th century B.C., Nimrud, also known as Kalhu, became the second capital of Assyria, an ancient kingdom that came to rule much of present-day Iraq and the Levant and became a great regional power.
14The city, which was destroyed in 612 B.C., is located on the Tigris River just south of Iraq's second largest city, Mosul, which was captured by Isil in June 2014.
15The late 1980s discovery of treasures in Nimrud's royal tombs was one of the 20th century's most significant archaeological finds.
16The government said militants destroyed the site in March using heavy military vehicles.
17One day after the destruction of Nimrud, Isil militants bulldozed the 2,300-year-old ruins of Hatra, a well-preserved complex of temples south of Mosul and a Unesco World Heritage site.
18The move was described by UN Secretary General Ban Ki-moon as a "war crime."
19On Feb 26, a video emerged on militant websites showing Islamic State militants with sledgehammers destroying ancient artefacts at the museum in Mosul which they referred to as idols.
20They also destroyed the Nirgal Gate, one of several gates to Ninevah, the one-time capital of the Assyrian Empire.
21In January, Islamic State militants ransacked the Central Library of Mosul, smashing the locks and taking around 2,000 books – leaving only Islamic texts.
22Days later, militants broke into the University of Mosul's library.
23They made a bonfire out of hundreds of books on science and culture, destroying them in front of students.
24Last year, militants destroyed the centuries-old Mosque of the Prophet Younis – believed to be the burial place of the Prophet Jonah – and the Mosque of the Prophet Jirjis, two revered ancient shrines in Mosul.
25They also threatened to destroy Mosul's 850-year old Crooked Minaret, but residents surrounded the structure to protect it.
26The 2,300-year-old city overlooking the Euphrates River is a remarkably well-preserved cultural crossroads, a city first founded by Alexander the Great's successors and later ruled by the Romans and various Persian empires.
27It boasts pagan temples, churches and one of the earliest known Jewish synagogues.
28Satellite imagery taken last year show the site pockmarked with holes from pillaging and illegal digs.
29It also showed hundreds of people conducting illegal excavations.

31It is believed to have been inhabited since the 5th millennium B.C. and was discovered in the early 1930s.
32It has also been severely looted by Isil.
