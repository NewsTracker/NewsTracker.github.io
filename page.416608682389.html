'We are hungry:' Aid reaches epicenter, Nepal toll tops 5000

1A female villager named Ramaya clasps her hands as she pleads for food after an aid relief helicopter lands at the remote mountain village of Gumda, near the epicenter of Saturday's massive earthquake in the Gorkha District of Nepal, Wednesday, April 29, 2015.
2GUMDA, Nepal (AP) - Hands pressed together in supplication, the Nepalese women pleaded for food, shelter and anything else the helicopter might have brought on an in-and-out run Wednesday to this smashed mountain village near the epicenter of last weekend's mammoth earthquake that killed more than 5,000 people.
3Unlike in Nepal's capital, where most buildings were spared complete collapse, the tiny hamlets clinging to the remote mountainsides of Gorkha District have been ravaged.
4Entire clusters of homes were reduced to piles of stone and splintered wood.
5Orange plastic tarps used for shelter now dot the cliff sides and terraced rice paddies carved into the land.
6"We are hungry," cried a woman who gave her name only as Deumaya, gesturing toward her stomach and opening her mouth to emphasize her desperation.
7Another woman, Ramayana, her eyes hollow and haunted, repeated the plea: "Hungry! We are hungry!"
8But food is not the only necessity in short supply out here beyond the reaches of paved roads, electricity poles and other benefits of the modern world.
9These days, even water is scarce.
10Communication is a challenge.
11And modern medical care is a luxury many have never received.
12Gumda is one of a handful of villages identified as the worst hit by Saturday's 7.8-magnitude earthquake, from which it will almost certainly take years to recover.
13As in many villages, though, the death toll in Gumda was far lower than feared, since many villagers were working outdoors when the quake struck at midday.
14Of Gumda's 1,300 people, five were killed in the quake and 20 more were injured.
15As the helicopter landed Wednesday with 40-kilogram (90-pound) sacks of rice, wind and rain whipped across the crest of the mountain.
16Seeing the conditions, the U.N. World Food Program's Geoff Pinnock shouted over the roar of the propellers, "the next shipment has to be plastic sheets. These people need shelter more than they need food."
17About 200 villagers huddled under a few umbrellas and plastic tarps as they waited to receive the aid, some with runny noses and chattering teeth.
18With the erratic Himalayan weather, aid workers are worried about keeping people warm, fed and safe.
19"More helicopters, more personnel and certainly more relief supplies including medical teams, shelter, tents, water and sanitation and food are obviously needed," said Pinnock, who was coordinating the aid relief flights.
20With 8 million Nepalese affected by the earthquake, including 1.4 million needing immediate food assistance, Pinnock said the relief effort would stretch on for months.
21Nepalese police said Wednesday the death toll from the quake had reached 5,027.
22Another 18 were killed on the slopes of Mount Everest, while 61 died in neighboring India, and China's official Xinhua News Agency reported 25 dead in Tibet.
23The disaster also injured more than 10,000, police said, and rendered thousands more homeless.
24The U.N. says the disaster has affected 8.1 million people - more than a fourth of Nepal's population of 27.8 million - and that 1.4 million needed food assistance.
25Planes carrying food and other supplies have been steadily arriving at Kathmandu's small airport, but the aid distribution process remains fairly chaotic, with Nepalese officials having difficulty directing the flow of emergency supplies.
26About 200 people blocked traffic in the capital Wednesday to protest the slow pace of aid delivery.
27The protesters faced off with police and there were minor scuffles but no arrests were made.
28Police arrested dozens of people on suspicion of looting abandoned homes as well as causing panic by spreading rumors of another big quake.
29Police official Bigyan Raj Sharma said 27 people were detained for stealing.
30But in a sign that life was inching back to normal, banks in Kathmandu opened for a few hours Wednesday and stuffed their ATMs with cash, giving people access to money.
