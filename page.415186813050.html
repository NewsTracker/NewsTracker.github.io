Kenya burns 15 tonnes of ivory to deter elephant poachers

1A Kenya Wildlife Services officer stands near a burning pile of 15 tonnes of elephant ivory seized in Kenya.
2Nairobi:Kenya has burned 15 tonnes of elephant tusks and rhino horns as part of efforts to curb poaching of the mammals in the East African country.
3President Uhuru Kenyatta set ablaze the three-metre high pile on Tuesday at Nairobi National Park, where one of his predecessors took a similar step to protect the nation's wildlife.
4The government plans to burn the rest of its ivory stockpile later this year, Mr Kenyatta said.
5"This shows we are committed to save our elephants and we want the world to know that ivory belongs to the elephants," Paul Udoto, spokesman for the Kenya Wildlife Service, said at the ceremony.
6"No one should pretend to look more beautiful by putting on ivory".
7A Kenya Wildlife Service officer next to the pile of contraband ivory.
8African elephants are considered endangered, with about 470,000 left in the wild in 37 countries, according to the African Wildlife Foundation.
9Poaching of the animals, which is reducing their population faster than they can reproduce, is worth as much as $240million a year, according to figures from the United Nations Congress on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora.
10Poachers killed 164 elephants in Kenya last year, compared with 302 in 2013 and 384 in 2012, while 53 rhinos were killed in 2014 compared with 59 and 30 in the previous two years, according to the Kenya Wildlife Service.
11The Kenyan government partly attributes the reduction in poaching to tougher laws enacted last year, said Paul Muya, deputy spokesman for the service.
12Kenya's President Uhuru Kenyatta, centre, looks on as 15 tonnes of ivory confiscated from smugglers and poachers is burnt in Nairobi National Park.
13"Burning of contraband ivory, coupled with China stopping importing ivory for one year starting last week, will help greatly in the fight against poaching," Mr Muya said.
14In 1989, former Kenyan President Daniel Arap Moi burned 12 tonnes of ivory worth more than $1.2 million at Nairobi National Park.
15Kenya's elephants were on the brink of extinction at the time, with the population having dropped to 16,000 from 167,000 in 1973.
16The event halted the decline in their number, which currently stands at about 38,000.
17"The ivory burned by Moi was mainly from internal poaching, but today we have tusks from elephants poached internally and those confiscated while in transit through Kenya," Mr Muya said.
18In 2009, former President Mwai Kibaki burned 5 tonnes of tusks that were traced and returned from Singapore, according to Mr Muya.
19Kenya may have as much as 100 tonnes of contraband ivory in its stockpiles, according to Paula Kahumba, chief executive officer of WildlifeDirect, a Nairobi-based non-governmental conservation organisation.
