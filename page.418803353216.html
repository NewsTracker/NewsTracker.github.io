Kurds fight the IS group while being bombed by Turkey

1BAGHDAD (AP) - Turkish jets struck camps belonging to Kurdish militants in northern Iraq Friday and Saturday in what were the first strikes since a peace deal was announced in 2013.
2The strikes in Iraq targeted the Kurdistan Workers' Party, or PKK, whose affiliates have been effective in battling the Islamic State group.
3The Kurds of Syria and Iraq have become a major part of the war against the Islamic State group, with Kurdish populations in both countries threatened by the militants' advance.
4Syrian, Iraqi and Turkish Kurds took part in cross-border operations to help rescue tens of thousands of displaced people from the minority Yazidi group from Iraq's Sinjar Mountain in August last year and they continue to fight in cooperation with one another against the Islamic State group in areas along the Iraq-Syria border.
5They have been somewhat effective in limiting the expansion of the Islamic State militants across northern Iraq but there are concerns that Turkish airstrikes on the PKK could jeopardize Kurdish positions.
6WHO ARE THE KURDS?
7The Kurds are an ethnic group with their own language and customs whose nomadic past led to their modern-day dispersal across several countries, mostly Turkey, Iraq, Syria, Iran and Armenia.
8Sunni Muslims make up the vast majority, but there is a sizeable Shiite population, particularly in Iran.
9After the collapse of the Ottoman and Qajar empires and the subsequent creation of these modern states, Iraq, Iran and Turkey each agreed to oppose the creation of an independent Kurdistan, making them the largest stateless minority group in the world.
10With nearly 25 million people living in five countries, they continue to push for self-rule.
11WHAT IS THEIR ROLE IN TURKEY?
12Turkey is home to an estimated 15 million Kurds, about one-fifth of the country's population of 76 million.
13Most are Sunni Muslim.
14The PKK has fought a three-decade war, initially for independence and later for autonomy and greater rights for Kurds.
15The conflict with the PKK has killed tens of thousands of people since 1984.
16Turkey and its U.S. and European allies consider the PKK - which has Marxist origins - a terrorist organization for killing civilians in urban bombings.
17In 2012, Turkey launched secret talks with the PKK's imprisoned leader, Abdullah Ocalan, to end the conflict.
18The talks were made public in 2013 and the PKK declared a cease-fire a few months later.
19Kurds accused Turkey of not doing enough to help Syrian Kurds during the battle against Islamic State militants over the Syrian Kurdish border town of Kobani, prompting violent clashes and straining the fragile peace process.
20Tensions flared again after an Islamic State suicide bombing in the southeastern Turkish city of Suruc on Monday killed 32 people.
21Kurdish groups held the Turkish government responsible, saying it had not been aggressive in battling the Islamic State group.
22Turkey's pro-Kurdish party, the People's Democratic Party, said the strikes on the PKK in Syria and Iraq amounted to an end of the two-year-old truce.
23It called on the government to end the bombing campaign and resume a dialogue with the Kurds.
24Turkey views Kurds in Iraq as an ally but is suspicious of Syrian Kurds who are affiliated with the PKK.
25Ankara is worried that Kurdish gains in Iraq and in Syria will encourage the aspirations of its own Kurdish population.
26WHERE DO THEY STAND IN IRAQ?
27Five million Kurds have their own government in Iraq's semi-autonomous north and have significant representation in the central government with several key posts including the presidency, which is allocated to Kurds.
28They currently represent about 20 percent of Iraq's population, making them the largest ethnic minority.
