Volcano erupts on remote Japanese island, residents flee by boat

1A video grab from the Japan Meteorological Agency's live camera image shows an eruption of Mount Shindake on Kuchinoerabujima island, Kagoshima Prefecture, southwestern Japan, May 29, 2015.
2Japan Meteorological Agency's Senior Coordinator for volcanic affairs Sadayuki Kitagawa holds an emergency news conference following the eruption of a volcano in southern Japan, as a screen shows a map of the volcano on Kuchinoerabujima, at the agency in Tokyo May 29, 2015.
3TOKYO A volcano erupted on a remote Japanese island on Friday, blasting black smoke thousands of meters into the sky and forcing residents to flee by boat and an airline to re-route flights.
4A pyroclastic flow of super-heated gas and rock rolled down the side of Mount Shindake, on the southern island of Kuchinoerabujima, and into the ocean.
5A 72-year-old man suffered minor burns to his face but there were no reports of other injuries among the island's 137 residents.
6"It sounded like dynamite had exploded, and the house shook," one resident told TV Asahi.
7Others described a smell of sulfur and clouds of smoke that blacked out the sky.
8Ash blanketed lower slopes and fell like snow on Yakushima, 12 km (7 miles) to the east.

12"I'm really worried about things back home."
13Smoke shot more than 9,000 meters (29,000 feet) into the sky and officials warned of more big eruptions and urged "extreme caution".
14All Nippon Airways said it would divert some flights as a precaution but it did not cancel any.
15Japan Airlines said it did not have any plans to change flights.
16Kuchinoerabujima is about 130 km (70 miles) south of Japan's southernmost main island of Kyushu, and 1,000 km (620 miles) southwest of Tokyo.
17It was not clear if the eruption would affect the restart of Kyushu Electric Power's Sendai nuclear plant, which on Wednesday cleared the last of the nuclear regulator's safety hurdles, introduced after the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear plant disaster triggered by a March 11, 2011, earthquake and tsunami.
18The company said the volcano posed no risk to the Sendai plant on Kyushu, and volcanologists agreed.
19The island has seen several eruptions, including one in 1933 that killed eight people, but Mount Shindake was dormant for 34 years until last year.
20Japan is one of the world's most seismically active countries and there has been an upsurge in volcanic activity in recent weeks, which volcanologists said may be linked to the big 2011 earthquake that set off a tsunami that killed nearly 20,000 people.
21In 2014, 63 people were killed when Mount Ontake in central Japan erupted while packed with hikers.
