Israelis go to the polls

1The outcome of the race, in which opinion polls suggest Netanyahu will win fewer seats than the centre-left Zionist Union, is likely to help determine the prospects for new Middle East peace talks and Israel's troubled relations with its US ally.
2Around six million voters are eligible to take part in the election for Israel's 120 members of parliament.
3Polls close at 2000 GMT (0700 AEDT) with exit polls by Israel's main media outlets set to give the first indications of the likely outcome minutes afterwards.
4Netanyahu brought the election on himself, calling the snap vote after firing centrist ministers from his fractious coalition just two years into its term.
5It will be Israel's third election since 2009 and the biggest challenge yet for the 65-year-old incumbent, who is seeking a third consecutive term.
6Final opinion polls published on Friday gave the Zionist Union of Labour leader Isaac Herzog a three to four seat lead over Netanyahu's Likud party.
7But the surveys also suggested Netanyahu will have an advantage when it comes to piecing together a coalition with smaller allies from the right.
8As he cast his own ballot on Tuesday, Netanyahu ruled out any alliance with Herzog.
9"There will not be a unity government with Labour. I will form a national (rightwing) government," he said.
10Netanyahu has warned a vote for the Zionist Union could endanger Israel's security and lead to the division of Jerusalem and the establishment of a Palestinian state in the annexed eastern sector.
11On Monday he was asked by the rightwing NRG website if it was true that there would be no Palestinian state established if he was reelected.
12"Indeed," said Netanyahu, who in 2009 had endorsed the idea of two states living side by side.
