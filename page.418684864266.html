RBA keeps close eye on China and Greece

1The Reserve Bank will keep a close watch on China's stock markets, the Greek debt crisis and soft local business investment to assess whether further rate cuts are needed.
2The RBA cut the cash rate to a historic low of two per cent in May and has left rates on hold at its June and July meetings.
3In the minutes of the July meeting, released on Tuesday, the central bank said international and local economic news would play a part in future rate decisions.
4"Information to be received over the period ahead on economic and financial conditions would continue to inform the board's assessment of the outlook," the RBA said.
5That assessment would inform "whether the current stance of policy remained appropriate to foster sustainable growth and inflation consistent with the target," it said.
6The Reserve Bank said economic growth in Australia's major trading partners was around average, but key considerations remained turbulence on China's stock markets and Greece's ongoing debt saga.
7"Recent volatility in Chinese equity markets and potential spillovers from developments in Greece would require close monitoring," the RBA said.
8But the direct impact of any further Greek turmoil was likely to be "relatively limited", it said.
9The minutes come after a breakthrough in Greece's debt negotiations with its international creditors on a third bailout deal, and big falls on Chinese equities markets earlier in July despite measures by Chinese authorities to support the market.
10The RBA said the local economic picture remained largely unchanged from previous months, with low interest rates supporting housing investment and consumption growth, while mining and non-mining investment remained weak.
11"Nevertheless non-mining business profits had increased over the past year and surveys suggested that business conditions had generally improved," the RBA said.
12The national housing market was little changed, with Sydney experiencing "notable strength", and the effect of greater regulator scrutiny of investor remains unclear, it said.
13The Australian dollar's drop against the US dollar to levels not seen since 2009 was not boosting the economy by as much as the bank expected.
14"Further depreciation seemed both likely and necessary," the RBA said.
15The bank noted signs of continued improvement in the jobs market, and said inflation remains well contained.
