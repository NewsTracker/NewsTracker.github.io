1In an update to its community standards page, the world's largest online social network gave users more guidance on why, for example, it might take down a post that featured sexual violence and exploitation, hate speech, criminal activity or bullying.
2It also explained why it not only bans terrorist and organized crime groups, but it also removes content supporting them.
3The Menlo Park, California-based company said it isn't changing how it regulates the content of posts, and that while some of the guidance for users is new, "it is consistent with how we've applied our standards in the past."
4In a blog post Monday, Facebook said it is a challenge to maintain one set of standards that meet the needs of its entire community.
5More than 80 percent of Facebook users are outside the U.S. and Canada.
6"People from different backgrounds may have different ideas about what's appropriate to share - a video posted as a joke by one person might be upsetting to someone else, but it may not violate our standard," wrote Monika Bickert, head of global policy management, and Chris Sonderby, deputy general counsel, in the post.
7Facebook users who believe that a particular page or content violates the site's standards can click a "report" link to notify Facebook.
8The company then considers whether to take it down.
9Some content is only removed in some countries.
10Facebook restricts content in countries where it violates local laws, even if that content does not violate its community standards.
11Separately, Facebook also released its latest report on requests it gets from governments worldwide, covering the second half of 2014.
12The report shows that requests from governments for data and to restrict information are both increasing.
