How to fix FIFA: Experts say agency needs outside reformer

1RIO DE JANEIRO (AP) - Sepp Blatter thinks FIFA can reform itself.
2Anti-corruption experts say an institution in that much trouble won't be able to clean itself up without an outsider.
3The arrest of seven top soccer officials in Zurich and Blatter's resignation provide an opening for transforming what Transparency International's managing director Cobus de Swardt termed FIFA's "sordid empire of corruption."
4But any change clashes with the reality of politics at FIFA: Its 209 members from Vanuatu to Venezuela, and the powerful executive committee are unlikely to act against their own entrenched power and privileges.
5Mark Pieth is a Swiss law professor and FIFA's former top anti-corruption adviser.
6His work for FIFA ended last year with all of his key reform proposals being rejected.
7Pieth wants Blatter to go now - not in seven to 10 months after a new president is elected.
8He is calling for an interim caretaker from outside to stabilize FIFA and restore its credibility.
9Only then should there be an election, which may not happen for up to two years.
10"Blatter cannot cling to his job now for months as a lame duck," Pieth told The Associated Press.
11"There is no use in wasting time and getting into political games. You don't step down in bits and pieces."
12Pieth suggested "someone on the outside, but someone who knows the inside" as the caretaker.
13He mentioned former German football association president Theo Zwanziger or Sunil Gulati, the head of the U.S. Soccer Federation.
14He also said Gulati might be a strong candidate for the long-term presidency.
15"It has to be somebody out of the midst football, but someone who is not tainted by the former system," Pieth said.
16"It shouldn't be one of these old hands because they will be immediately in great trouble again and find themselves discredited."
17This won't please the presumed frontrunners - Michel Platini, head of the European federation UEFA, or Issa Hayatou, president of the African federation.
18A selection of ideas for changing FIFA.
19Wrage was part of an Independent Governance Committee at FIFA before resigning in 2013.
20"Blatter wanted to appear to be a reformer," she said in an email.
21"I don't believe he was serious about reform then, and it's a bit absurd to hear him talking about his commitment to 'push through' reform measures in the next few months that he rejected a few years ago."
22Wrage also supports a short-term caretaker from outside.
23She calls for immediate restrictions on "the giving or receipt of gifts and lavish hospitality."
24And she wants leaders to have a "one-time chance" to disclose conflicts of interest.
25"Thereafter, there should be zero tolerance for conflicts that come to light," she said.
26Wrage is pushing for at least two executive committee members "from outside the soccer world."
27"This is a tightly knit club and its members have benefited enormously from the status quo," she said.
28"It will take seismic change to restore FIFA's reputation and there is no certainty we'll see that. There's a very real risk that we'll have another president a lot like Blatter who owes his position to many of the same people."
29Transparency has issued seven recommendations.
30One key is that FIFA disclose officials' pay, expenses and detail how money is spent.
31National federations also should publish how much money they get from FIFA, and how it is spent.
32Transparency also calls for term limits for executive committee members and asks sponsors to "take collective action to pressure FIFA ... to meet the highest standards of compliance and ethics."
33It also asks for "tougher and more transparent compliance standards in TV-rights companies."
34Scala is the independent Chairman of FIFA's Audit and Compliance Committee.
35Scala spoke the day Blatter resigned and listed priorities for change.
36He said "nothing will be off the table" in reforming FIFA.
37He also focused on the structure of the executive committee.
38He said members should undergo "integrity checks," a move that has already been rejected by FIFA's member confederations.
39He said many question FIFA's transparency.
40He said FIFA should publicize the "compensation of the president and executive committee members."
41Peter Alegi, a historian at Michigan State University who has studied sports governance, said a quick exit by Blatter increases the chances for change.
42"If the noose tightens more on Blatter, if it reaches a lot of top FIFA executives, then I think there is a real possibility that at least the old guard will be thrown out, if not imprisoned," Alegi said.
43He also called for more diversity inside FIFA - women, former athletes, fans and even academics.
44"Everyone who is now in FIFA comes from the same kind of class," he said.
45"Obviously there'll all men, and with very few exceptions."
46"It's likely there will a kind of moderate reformer who comes forward," he said.
47There have been suggestions to turn FIFA into a public company.
48Others argue the regional confederations are the problem, especially those in countries with weak judicial systems.
49Many of the regional confederations lack transparency and work like mini-FIFAs.
50Roger Pielke Jr. of the University of Colorado published a study several years ago entitled: "How Can FIFA be Held Accountable?"
51"FIFA demonstrated time and again that it has essentially no hierarchical, supervisory, peer or public reputational accountability, and minimal fiscal accountability," Pielke wrote.
