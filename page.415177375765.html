1In Washington, U.S. officials said they would welcome Snowden's return to the United States but he would have to face criminal charges which have been filed against him.
2Snowden's lawyer, Anatoly Kucherena, who has links to the Kremlin, was speaking at a news conference to present a book he has written about his client.
3Moscow granted Snowden asylum in 2013, straining already tense ties with Washington.
4"I won't keep it secret that he... wants to return back home. And we are doing everything possible now to solve this issue. There is a group of U.S. lawyers, there is also a group of German lawyers and I'm dealing with it on the Russian side."
5The United States wants Snowden to stand trial for leaking extensive secrets of electronic surveillance programmes by the National Security Agency (NSA).
6Russia has repeatedly refused to extradite him.
7Snowden has said in the past he would like to return home if he was assured he would be given a fair trial.
8A deeply divisive figure, he is praised by some as a civil rights campaigner and whistleblower and condemned by others as a traitor who compromised U.S. security.
9Kucherena said in August Snowden had been given a three-year Russian residence permit.
10U.S. Justice Department spokesman Marc Raimondi told Reuters: "It remains our position that Mr. Snowden should return to the United States and face the charges filed against him. If he does, he will be accorded full due process and protections."
11The U.S. position is that "Snowden is not a whistleblower. He is accused of leaking classified information and there is no question his actions have inflicted serious harms on our national security," Raimondi said.

2A Mundelein teenager who admitted to killing her younger sister in a rage because she felt unappreciated will remain in a juvenile detention facility until she turns 21, with a possibility of being released one year early by a parole board.
3The girl, who was 14 when she was accused more than a year ago of killing her 11-year-old sister, Dora Betancourt, pleaded guilty to first-degree murder in January.
4Now 15, the girl read a statement in Lake County juvenile court Tuesday saying she was grateful for the opportunity for the intensive counseling she is to receive in detention.
5She said she misses her sister and hopes her family can forgive her.
6By pleading guilty Tuesday to the murder of her 11-year-old sister, a Mundelein teenager will be allowed to remain in the juvenile system with "intensive mental health services" - a deal that would not have been an option under Illinois law had she been a few months older.
7"I am truly sorry for the pain I've caused them," she said of her mother and former stepfather.

11The teen, described as a "mini-mom" to her younger sister, recalled the things she and her sister used to do together, like watching movies, making bracelets and playing sports.
12"Now I can only remember those times," she said.
13"I can never get them back."
14During the hearing, lawyers discussed the findings of two doctors who had evaluated the teen's mental health and agreed that she needed help to control her anger.
15Officials revealed that the teen had formerly been in counseling after waving a baseball bat at her mother at age 11.
16She was forced to take on many adult responsibilities because her mother, a single parent, worked two jobs to support her daughters and save for private school, said the teen's lawyer, Michael Conway.
17Prosecutor Claudia Kasten said the teen admitted to stabbing Dora multiple times Jan. 22, 2014, because the younger sister "did not appreciate her."
18Kasten read aloud the teen's two-page statement that she gave police, in which she said, "My mind was in another place and the devil was in me, and that is why my sister & is dead."
19On Tuesday, Kasten said: "We know that the loss is great. This family is broken and shattered."
20Prosecutors and defense attorneys agreed to a deal that will have the teen remain in a juvenile facility until her 21st birthday.
21After five years, her lawyers will be allowed to request her parole.
22Reading from her prepared statement, the teen said she plans to finish high school and pursue a career in nursing.
23"I am also grateful because of the second chance I am getting," she said.
24"I'll get out at 21 and start my life over. Not everyone gets that chance."
25David Betancourt, Dora's father and the teen's former stepfather, gave a brief statement in court, saying: "It's been tough on all of us, losing Dora, losing (her sister). She wants to get help."

27The court will monitor the teen's progress and access to mental health services every six months, said Judge Valerie Boettle Ceckowski.
28Mental health experts for the defense and prosecution agreed that, if she receives such services, she should pose no threat to the community on her release.
29After Tuesday's hearing, Lake County State's Attorney Michael Nerheim said he was initially "leaning" toward charging the teen as an adult.
30He changed his mind based on the girl's age, the medical reports and family history, he said.
31"It was a very difficult case," Nerheim said.
32"There's no easy answer, no perfect decision. We try to balance the interests of justice & and be sure she gets all the services she needs."
33The outcome would have been far different if the teen was 15 at the time of the murder.
34Under a controversial Illinois law, juveniles 15 and older are automatically handled in adult court for several felony offenses, first-degree murder chief among them.
35Many juvenile justice experts, advocates and lawmakers have called for an end to the law, saying it has failed to enhance public safety.
36Experts have said the law does not take into consideration a growing body of scientific research indicating the brains of teens are less developed than those of adults.
37Supporters of a change in the law say a judge should have discretion to decide if older teen defendants should be tried in juvenile or adult court, based on factors like background and potential for rehabilitation.
38The Mundelein girl initially told police an intruder killed her sister.
39She later confessed when her story didn't add up, prosecutors have said.
40"What happened a little over a year ago & is not who she is," said her lawyer, Conway.
41"It was an aberration."
