What Crisis? Stronger Banks, Economies Ease Fear Over Greece

1In 2012, financial markets were rattled by the possibility that Greeks would elect a left-wing government, default on their debts and drop the euro currency.
2The fears pushed the Standard & Poor's 500 stock index down nearly 10 percent that spring.
3Here's how things look now: The left-wing party, Syriza, holds power in Athens.
4Greece actually did miss a loan payment to the International Monetary Fund late Tuesday.
5And the nation's future in the eurozone hinges precariously on a referendum Sunday.
6Yet investors seem to be taking events in stride.
7Stocks around the world mostly rose Wednesday.
8Part of the calm reflects hope that Greece ultimately will reach a deal with its creditors and that Greeks will vote Sunday to accept their demands and keep the euro.
9But investors also have been reassured by the defenses the Europeans have built the past three years to limit potential damage.
10Improving economic conditions in Europe and America help too.
11"If things go off the rails and Greece is pushed out of the eurozone, the fallout should be temporary and modest," says Mark Zandi, chief economist at Moody's Analytics.
12"I don't think it's going to be a major financial event ... I don't think it would have a meaningful impact on European growth."
13Some analysts still warn that the fallout from a so-called Grexit is unpredictable.
14Seven years ago, few thought troubles in the U.S. sub-prime mortgage market would trigger a worldwide financial panic and the worst economic downturn since the 1930s.
15Panic, however, can spread in surprising ways.
16But Europe and the rest of the world appear to be in a much better position to avoid a doomsday scenario.
17Three years ago, it wasn't just Greece that worried investors.
18Portugal, Ireland, Italy and Spain, countries with a lot of government debt and shrinking economies, also scared them.
19Rescuing Greece was one thing.
20But if all five countries defaulted on their debts, investors thought, Europe's currency system would likely unravel since the combined group was too big to bail out.
21And if Greece left the eurozone, they fretted, other countries might follow, threatening European economic and political unity.
22Investors demanded onerous interest rates to hold the debt of the most vulnerable European countries.
23Rates spiked to levels that squeezed government finances and damaged fragile economies.
24In the early summer of 2012, the cost to borrow for 10 years topped 11 percent for Portugal, 7 percent for Spain and 6 percent for Italy.
25Then, in July 2012, European Central Bank President Mario Draghi pledged to "do whatever it takes" to preserve the euro by pumping money into the financial system.
26His words reassured investors and rates started to come down.
27Now, even with the recent turbulence, 10-year government borrowing rates for Portugal, Spain and Italy remain below 3 percent.
