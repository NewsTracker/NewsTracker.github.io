Kerry says United States, Egypt return to 'stronger base' in ties

1CAIRO The United States and Egypt are returning to a "stronger base" in bilateral ties despite tensions and human rights concerns, U.S. Secretary of State John Kerry said on Sunday after talks with his Egyptian counterpart.
2"Egypt remains vital ... to engagement and stability in the region as a whole," said Kerry, who held the first bilateral strategic dialogue since 2009.
3"There are obviously circumstances where we have found reason to have grave concern and we have expressed it very publicly," he said at a news conference with Foreign Minister Sameh Shukri.
4"But we have multiple issues that we need to work on simultaneously."
5U.S.-Egyptian relations cooled after Islamist president Mohamed Mursi was ousted in 2013 by the military amid mass protests against his rule.
6Cairo remains one of Washington's closest allies in the Middle East, an increasingly crucial role in a region beset by turmoil in Syria, Iraq, Yemen and Libya.
7Kerry also lobbied for last month's nuclear deal between world powers and Iran, which has been met with scepticism by Washington's Arab allies.
8"There can be absolutely no question that the Vienna plan, if implemented, will make Egypt and all the countries of this region safer than they otherwise would be," he said.
9Kerry said Iran's role as the "number one state sponsor of terror in the world" made the deal even more important.
10"If Iran is destabilizing, it is far, far better to have an Iran that doesn't have a nuclear weapon than one that does," he said.
11Shukri said Cairo had no major disagreements with Washington, only "differences in points of view over some issues, which is natural".
12Washington supports former general Abdel Fattah al-Sisi, who led Mursi's overthrow and was elected to succeed him, but has cautiously criticized Egypt's human rights record.
13Following Mursi's ouster, Cairo cracked down on the now outlawed Muslim Brotherhood.
14Security forces killed hundreds of Brotherhood supporters and jailed thousands more, and later pursued liberal activists.
15Egypt says the Brotherhood is a terrorist group.
16The Interior Ministry denies allegations of widespread human rights abuses.
17Kerry later met Sisi and a senior State Department official said he stressed "the importance of press freedom and the protection of peaceful dissent, stressing that free participation in the political process is essential to help stem the growth of violent extremism".
18In one such rights case, a Cairo court on Sunday postponed its verdict in the retrial of Al Jazeera television journalists accused of aiding a terrorist organization, a reference to the Brotherhood.
19Shukri said no journalists in Egypt were in jail over their reporting.
20The Committee to Protect Journalists said a prison census it conducted on June 1 found at least 18 Egyptian journalists were in jail for their reporting.
21Cairo and Washington have been exploring ways to expand their security relationship.
22In late March, U.S. President Barack Obama lifted a hold on supplying arms to Cairo, authorizing deliveries valued at over $1.3 billion.
23The United States delivered eight F-16 fighter jets last week to Egypt, which is battling insurgents in the Sinai Peninsula who have killed hundreds of soldiers and police since Mursi's removal.
24The most active group there is an affiliate of Islamic State.
25Also on Sunday, demonstrators in central Cairo burned American and Israeli flags in protest against the death of a Palestinian toddler who died in a suspected Jewish extremist arson attack on his home in the occupied West Bank on Friday.
26Kerry will travel on to Qatar to discuss the fight against Islamic State with other Arab leaders and seek to reassure them about the Iran nuclear deal.
