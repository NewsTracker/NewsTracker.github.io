Australia a puzzling hotbed of Islamic State recruiting

1A man who tweeted a photo of his young son clutching a severed head.
2A teenager who is believed to have turned suicide bomber, and others suspected of attempting to travel to Syria to join the Islamic State movement.
3All of them, Australian.
4The London-based International Center for the Study of Radicalization and Political Violence reports that between 100 and 250 Australians have joined Sunni militants in Iraq and Syria.
5Given Australia's vast distance from the region and its population of just 24 million, it is a remarkable number.
6The center estimates that about 100 fighters came from the United States, which has more than 13 times as many people as Australia.
7Experts disagree about why Islamic State has been so effective recruiting in Australia, which is widely regarded as a multicultural success story, with an economy in an enviable 24th year of continuous growth.
8Possible explanations include that some Australian Muslims are poorly integrated with the rest of the country, and that Islamic State recruiters have given Australia particular attention.
9In addition, the Australian government failed to keep tabs on some citizens who had been radicalized, and moderate Muslims have been put off by some of Prime Minister Tony Abbott's comments about their community.
10Greg Barton, a global terrorism expert at Monash University in Melbourne, said Australia and some other countries underestimated Islamic State's "pull factor."
11"We're all coming to terms with the fact that this is a formidable targeter and predatory recruiter that goes after individuals one by one with a very masterful use of technology, and our sense of confidence that because we've got society working well makes us secure misses the point," Barton said.
12Muslims make up about 2.2 percent of the population in Australia, compared to just 1 percent in the United States.
13And while many U.S. Muslims are from families who migrated in pursuit of the American economic dream, a larger proportion of Australian Muslims are from families who fled Lebanon's civil war in the 1970s and '80s.
14Australian Muslims of Lebanese origin are largely based in Sydney, the country's biggest city.
15They have been less successful in integrating into Australian society than many other groups, and the first Australian-born generation of these migrant families has been overrepresented in terrorism offenses and general street crime.
16Mohammad Ali Baryalei, an ethnic Lebanese who reportedly became a high-ranking member of Islamic State's operational command, was formerly a Sydney nightclub bouncer and bit-part television actor.
17Australian security agencies suspect he single-handedly recruited dozens of Australians and helped them enter Syria.
18Once a Sydney street preacher with the Muslim group Street Dawah, Baryalei was reportedly killed in battle in Syria last fall at age 33.
19The Australian government has yet to confirm his death.
20Baryalei is accused in court documents of inciting from afar Islamic State sympathizers in Sydney to brutally slay a randomly selected victim.
21Security services recorded a telephone conversation between him and Omarjan Azari, who is awaiting trial on charges that include preparing to commit a terrorist act.
22"What you guys need to do is pick any random unbeliever," Baryalei allegedly told Azari, according to court testimony.
23"Backpacker, tourist, American, French or British, even better."
24Sydney-born Khaled Sharrouf, also ethnic Lebanese, horrified millions last year by posting on his Twitter account a photo of his 7-year-old son clutching the severed head of a Syrian soldier.
25U.S. Secretary of State John Kerry described the image as "one of the most disturbing, stomach-turning, grotesque photographs ever displayed."
26Sharrouf's appearance on the Syrian battlefield highlighted a flaw in Australia's defenses against Islamic State: lax border security.
27Sharrouf had served a prison sentence in Australia for planning a foiled terrorist attack and had been banned from leaving the country, but used his brother's passport to leave in 2013.
28The Australian government acknowledged there was a problem with a system of airport security that was more focused on who was coming in than on who was leaving.
29The government announced in August that biometric screening will be rolled out at all Australian international airports as part of 630 million Australian dollars ($500 million) in new spending on intelligence, law enforcement and border protection.
30Counterterrorism police units have been attached to major airports to screen passengers.
31The unit at Sydney Airport was instrumental in recently intercepting two Sydney-born brothers, aged 16 and 17, who were about to fly to Turkey without their parents' knowledge.
32Authorities suspect the brothers were headed to Syria.
33Australia's net still has holes.
34Jake Bilardi, an 18-year-old who converted to Islam a few years ago, had avoided Australia's counterterror radar when he left his Melbourne home for Syria in August.
35After Bilardi's family reported him missing, police found chemicals that could be used to make a bomb at his home.
36Images of Bilardi armed with a rifle in front of Islamic flags appeared on social media sites later that year.
37A picture of a young man resembling Bilardi behind the wheel of a van was posted this month with claims from Islamic State that foreign fighters from Australia and other countries took part in a near-simultaneous attack in Iraq that involved at least 13 suicide car bombs and killed two police officers.
38The Australian government has yet to confirm Bilardi's death.
39Foreign Minister Julie Bishop has been granted enhanced powers to prevent Australians joining IS and, in some cases, from returning to Australia.
40She has canceled about 100 passports, including Bilardi's, though he left before his passport was revoked.
41Keeping would-be militants from leaving Australia, however, increases the risk that they will wreak havoc at home.
42Numan Haider, an 18-year-old Muslim Australian of Afghan origin, stabbed two Melbourne police officers and was shot dead in September, a week after his passport had been canceled.
43He had caught authorities' attention months earlier over what police considered to be troubling behavior, including waving what appeared to be an Islamic State flag at a shopping mall.
44Australian authorities were clearly taken by surprise by the growing domestic menace posed by Islamic State followers.
45Less than a year ago, officials reduced security at Parliament House to cut costs.
46Since then, security at the seat of national government has been increased to unprecedented levels.
47In September, the government raised Australia's terrorist threat level to the second-highest level on a four-tier scale.
48Police attempting to disrupt terrorist plots have raided scores of homes.
49Several suspects have been charged and others have been detained without charge under new counterterrorism laws.
50The nation's main domestic spy agency is juggling more than 400 high-priority counterterrorism investigations - more than double the number a year ago.
51But the intensified vigilance was no hindrance to Man Monis, a 50-year-old Iranian-born, self-styled cleric with a long criminal history.
52In December, Monis took 18 people hostage at a downtown Sydney cafe, forced them to hold up a flag bearing the Islamic declaration of faith against a cafe window and demanded he be delivered a flag of the Islamic State group.
53Monis and two hostages were killed at the end of a 16-hour siege.
54A government review found that Monis had fallen off a terrorist watch list despite repeated warnings to security services from members of the public concerned by his online rants.
55As a Shiite Muslim, he was thought an unlikely recruit to Islamic State, a Sunni Muslim movement.
56As traumatic as the hostage crisis was, it could not be compared to the enormity of the Sept.
5711 terror attacks on the United States.
58Hass Dellal, executive director of the Australian Multicultural Foundation, which promotes awareness of cultural diversity within Australia, said that history might make Americans more resistant to Islamic State recruiting.
59Dellal also said public discussion of issues around radicalization and extremism is more balanced in the United States than in Australia, which effectively banned Middle Eastern Muslims from immigrating until the 1970s.
60Some Muslims have been critical of comments by Prime Minister Abbott, accusing him of driving a wedge between them and the rest of Australia.
61"I've often heard Western leaders describe Islam as a religion of peace. I wish more Muslim leaders would say that more often, and mean it," Abbott in a speech in February that angered many Muslims with its suggestion of duplicity.
62Barton, the Monash University expert, said Australia may prove to be not so different from the United States, if Islamic State expands its influence in America.
