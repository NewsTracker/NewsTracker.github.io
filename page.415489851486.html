Pakistani Christians riot after two churches bombed during prayer services

Vanuatu president, heading home from Japan disaster conference, rues devastation from cyclone

1Fourteen people were killed and more than 70 injured when two Taliban suicide bombers attacked churches in Pakistan's Lahore on Sunday, sparking mob violence in which two other suspected militants died.
2The bombings occurred during prayers at two churches, one Catholic and one Protestant, located about 500 metres apart in the city's Youhanabad neighbourhood, which is home to more than 100,000 Christians, officials said.
3Up to 4000 then took to the streets of the eastern city, many armed with clubs, smashing vehicles and attacking a city bus station in a rare show of anger by the beleaguered minority.
4Pakistani Christians in Karachi chant slogans during a demonstration to condemn the suicide bombing attack on two churches in Lahore.
5Dr Mohammad Saeed Sohbin, medical superintendent of the General Hospital, told AFP: "We have received 14 dead bodies and 70 injured," adding that the figure did not include the suspected militants who were lynched or the bombers.
6Zahid Pervez, the top health official in Lahore, confirmed the death toll and told reporters 78 people were wounded in the two attacks, which left blood and shoes scattered across the blast sites.
7Witness Jacob David, 55, said people jumped out of a church window to save their lives.
8"These were huge blasts, one after another. Everyone started running here and there. Some people jumped outside after breaking the window," he said.
9Police spokeswoman Nabila Ghazanfar said two policemen guarding the churches were among those killed in the attacks, while two people were beaten to death by protesters who took to the streets after the blasts.
10"Policemen on duty at both the entrances tried to stop them, but the bombers blew themselves up," she said.
11"The angry mob protesting after the blast beat to death two people whom they suspected of being associates of the attackers."
12An AFP photographer saw the bodies of the two suspected militants on fire after the beatings.
13It was not clear whether they were still alive at the time.
14The Pakistani Taliban's Jamaat-ul-Ahrar faction described the blasts as suicide attacks and vowed to continue a campaign for the enforcement of Sharia Islamic law, in a statement emailed to the media.
15Christians, who make up about 2 per cent of Pakistan's population of 180 million, have been targeted by attacks and riots in recent years, often over allegations of profanity regarding the Koran or the prophet Muhammad.
16Sunday's attacks were the worst on the community since a devastating double suicide-bombing in the north-western city of Peshawar in September 2013 killed 82 people.
17That came months after more than 3000 Muslim protesters torched about 100 houses as they rampaged through Joseph Colony, another Christian neighbourhood of Lahore, following blasphemy allegations against a Christian man.
18Pope Francis on Sunday condemned the attacks and accused the world of "seeking to hide" the persecution of Christians.
19"It's with pain, much pain, that I was told of the terrorist attacks against two Christian churches in Lahore in Pakistan, which have caused numerous deaths and injuries," the Pope said after his weekly Angelus prayer in Rome.
20The thousands of Christian protesters who clashed with police on Sunday attacked their cars with stones and sticks, as women wept and beat their heads and chests.
21The protesters, some wearing crosses round their necks, later turned on the city's bus rapid transit system - a signature project of the ruling PML-N party of Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif.
22Mr Sharif in a statement condemned the church bombings and "directed provincial governments to ensure the security of [the] public and their properties".
23Zaeem Qadri, a spokesman for the provincial government, said efforts were being made to stop the rioting but "emotions are very high because their churches have been attacked".
24Christians also took to the streets in other cities, including Pakistan's largest, Karachi, where about 200 protesters blocked a main road and burnt tyres.
25There were also demonstrations in Peshawar, Multan and Quetta.
26European Union foreign policy chief Federica Mogherini urged action on the attacks and groups "preaching hate", saying the bloc stood with Pakistan in its anti-terrorism fight and with religious minorities.
27"Freedom of religion or belief is a central tenet of democratic societies," she said in a statement.
28Sunday's attack was the first by the Taliban since three of their major factions said on Thursday they had reunited.
29Pakistan's military has stepped up its fight against militants since Taliban gunmen massacred more than 150 people, mostly children, at a Peshawar school in December.
1The Pacific island nation of Vanuatu has lost years of development progress and must "start over" following a powerful cyclone that destroyed or damaged 90 percent of the buildings on the main island of Port Vila, the country's president said Monday.
2SENDAI, Japan - Baldwin Lonsdale, president of Vanuatu, the Pacific island nation devastated by a powerful tropical storm last week, was attending a disaster conference in the Japanese city of Sendai when the cyclone struck.
3So far, he and his delegation have received information only from Vanuatu's main city of Port Vila.
4Following are excerpts from an interview with The Associated Press.
5Q: What is the situation in Vanuatu now?
6A: "Cyclone Pam has devastated Port Vila. More than 90 percent of the buildings and houses in Port Vila have been destroyed or damaged. The state of emergency that has been issued is only for Port Vila. Once we receive an update on the extent of the damage in the provinces then another state of emergency will be issued for the outer islands."
7Q: Is there an update on casualties and damage?
8A: "More than 1,000 people have been evacuated to evacuation centers and will be returning to their homes sometime later today, if their homes still stand. That's in Port Vila alone. Confirmed dead in Port Vila is six and more than 30 injuries. I do believe the number of casualties will not be high.
9Q: Is there anything more that could have been done to prepare for the storm?
10A: "This is a very devastating cyclone in Vanuatu. I term it as a monster, a monster. It's a setback for the government and for the people of Vanuatu. After all the development that has taken place, all this development has been wiped out. So it means we will have to start anew again."
11Q: What are the urgent needs for Vanuatu?
12A: "The first priority is the humanitarian needs. People have lost many of their properties. Clothing, eating utensils, bathing . most of the necessary items of the households, all this has been destroyed and damaged. I really request for humanitarian needs and assistance at this stage. Tarpaulins, water containers, medical needs, gathering tools, construction tools all these are very important right now."
13Q: Vanuatu is vulnerable to many disaster risks, including earthquakes, volcanoes, extreme weather and sea level rises due to climate change.
14Do you see the impact of climate change yourself?
15A: "Climate change is contributing to the disasters in Vanuatu. We see the level of sea rise. Change in weather patterns. This year we have heavy rain more than every year."
16Q: How are you feeling as you prepare to head back home?
17A: "Very emotional. My heart is for the people. Everyone has that same feeling. We don't know what has happened to our families. Because there is a breakdown in communications we cannot reach our families. We do not know if our families are safe or not. As the leader of the nation, my whole heart is for the people, the nation."
