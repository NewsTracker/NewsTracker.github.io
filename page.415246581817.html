6NPR's David Welna reports.
7DAVID WELNA, BYLINE: On Wednesday, the commander of U.S. forces in Afghanistan, General John Campbell, assured the House Armed Services Committee the Afghan forces are up to the task.
8GENERAL JOHN CAMPBELL: They're ready, and it's time.
9In their second fighting season in the lead, the ANSF were challenged and tested, but they held their own against a very determined enemy.
10WELNA: But a new report from the Special Inspector General for Afghanistan Reconstruction suggests Campbell's appraisal may be too upbeat.
11The report showed Afghanistan's army is shrinking.
12Oklahoma Republican Jim Bridenstine asked General Campbell about that attrition.
13REPRESENTATIVE JIM BRIDENSTINE: In 2014, the Afghan security forces lost over 20,000 personnel to desertions and deaths.
14Does that concern you?
15CAMPBELL: It hasn't had a severe impact on their readiness.
16Any desertion, any casualty, of course that would concern me.
17It concerns our leadership, it concerns the president.
18But I think it is about having processes in place to bring those people on board to keep them in.
19WELNA: That could prove a challenge.
20Over the past year, the size of Afghanistan's army shrank nearly 9 percent to under 170,000 troops.
21That's the smallest it's been since 2011.
22General Campbell had initially blocked releasing those numbers, even though they had been a regular part of the Inspector General's quarterly reports for the past six years.
23CAMPBELL: I said anything that is readiness data for the Afghans needs to be classified.
24We just can't put that out, for the Afghans' good, and also because we're wholly dependent upon the Afghans now for our own force protection.
25WELNA: When the quarterly report came out at the end of January without the Afghan troop numbers, some lawmakers wanted to know why they weren't there.
26Senator Richard Blumenthal is a Democrat from Connecticut.
27SENATOR RICHARD BLUMENTHAL: Classification of this information seems virtually incomprehensible and perhaps unacceptable.
28WELNA: So General Campbell did an about-face - he classified most of the information about Afghanistan's fighting forces that he'd previously made secret.
29Now that those numbers are public, the problems with those forces are more apparent.
30Illinois Democrat and Iraq war veteran Tammy Duckworth told Campbell she was concerned.
31REPRESENTATIVE TAMMY DUCKWORTH: The numbers of Afghan military and police forces fluctuate significantly from time - from quarter to quarter by as much as 20,000 or 40,000 personnel, and I'm worried.
32WELNA: Campbell acknowledged those numbers have been muddled.
33CAMPBELL: We have to do a much better job at my headquarters to make sure that we have processes in place, that we can provide the right data at the right time.
34But we have to have a better procedure to do that.
35And we're working on that.
36WELNA: Overall, Afghanistan's fighting force is about 17 percent smaller than what the Pentagon hoped it would be at this point, which may be why U.S. officials are saying the plan to reduce American forces there to 5,500 by the end of the year may be reconsidered.
37David Welna, NPR News.
