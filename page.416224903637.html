5 Things to know about Clinton's decision to run again

1While the Democratic political apparatus had been pushing Clinton to run since the day she left the State Department, she waited until last fall to fully engage in the process.
2Once she did, Clinton met with dozens of policy and political experts, pored through countless memos and delved deeply into what went wrong with her failed 2008 campaign.
3As Clinton was returning to private life after her four years in the Obama administration, an outside group called Ready for Hillary was pushing her to run for the White House.
4At first, the group was a source of frustration for her advisers, who dismissed it as a JV operation that was putting too much pressure on Clinton far too quickly.
5But soon, the Clinton team's view of Ready for Hillary would shift from annoyance to acceptance.
6The energy the organization was harnessing reminded them of the kind of grassroots movements they jealously watched mobilize around Barack Obama in 2008.
7There was plenty that gave Clinton pause as she started weighing another campaign.
8She grimaced at the thought of giving up her privacy again.
9She worried about putting daughter Chelsea, her husband Marc and their baby in the spotlight.
10She felt bad that her candidacy would crowd out Vice President Joe Biden, a longtime friend who was also considering a White House run.
11Clinton was also mindful of chatter that she could be challenged from her party's liberal left flank.
12She set up a meeting with Sen. Elizabeth Warren, whose anti-Wall Street positions were galvanizing progressives and stoking talk that she could launch her own 2016 campaign.
13The meeting was as much an opportunity for Clinton to gauge the mood of progressives as it was a chance to size up the seriousness of Warren's repeated denials of a White House run.
14Clinton campaigned aggressively for Democrats in the midterm elections, holding 45 events for 26 candidates.
15More than half of those candidates lost.
16Clinton had expected the results to be bad, but not this bad.
17Democrats had run scared, she concluded.
18The party had done little to defend its accomplishments on the economy and health care, and done even less to argue what Democrats wanted to do in the future.
19Clinton's takeaways about Democrats' failures would guide the final months of her decision-making about running for president.
20Though the fall, Clinton delved into meetings with political and policy experts.
21As she navigated what a successful campaign would look like, she consulted several operatives who worked for Obama.
22Among them: former Obama campaign manager David Plouffe, ground game guru Jennifer O'Malley Dillon, and digital director Teddy Goff.
23Clinton also convened long economic discussions that centered on income inequality and the widening achievement gap.
24Foreign policy experts gave her their diagnosis of pressing problems in the Middle East and with Russia.
25Clinton took hand-written notes and stockpiled multi-page memos.
