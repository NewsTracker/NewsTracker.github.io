Turkish jets bomb Islamic State militants, marking pivotal shift and end of tacit truce

1ISTANBUL - Last month, the first edition of the Islamic State group's Turkish-language magazine contained not a word of criticism of the Turkish government.
2This week, the second edition calls Istanbul occupied territory and blasts President Recep Tayyip Erdogan as a tyrant.
3The difference?
4Turkey has started to crack down on the group under Western pressure and Islamic State now sees Turkey as the enemy, raising the stakes in the struggle against the extremist network.
5And Turkey's decisive response on Friday - airstrikes on Islamic State targets and 290 arrests nationwide - show how seriously the nation is now taking a threat it had long downplayed.
6The abrupt shift in Islamic State's Turkish propaganda magazine shows just how quickly a tacit truce has come apart.
7But the underlying changes have not happened overnight.
8Islamic State - also known by acronyms ISIS and ISIL - has spent years building its network inside Turkey, even as Turkish security services monitored the group to glean valuable intelligence.
9"There is significant evidence that ISIS has built a network and an infrastructure in Turkey to support its operations in both Syria and Iraq," said Andreas Krieg, an analyst at King's College London.
10"Turkey has never thought that these jihadists would ever become a problem for Turkey itself. Quite on the contrary, they were under the impression that jihadists who wanted to go to Syria are embarking on a local - not a global - jihad."
11While Turkey was early to brand IS a terrorist group, the two largely refrained from attacking each other.
12Turkey took a soft line on Islamic State because it was fighting the Syrian regime of President Bashar Assad - Turkey's arch-enemy - and Ankara has actively supported other Islamist groups trying to bring down Assad.
13That gave Islamic State cover to continue building its network in the country.
14Now, Turkey faces questions about whether its policy of restraint has backfired - finding itself facing an extensive, well-funded and organized extremist group threatening it from within.
15This week's suicide bombing that killed 32 people in the border city of Suruc forced Turkey's hand, causing it to beef up anti-IS efforts with Washington.
16Then came a big escalation.
17On Thursday, as Turkey's government tried to counter criticism over the suicide bombing, Islamic State struck again, this time in an ambush along the border that left a Turkish soldier dead.
18Turkish police then carried out simultaneous raids in Istanbul and 12 provinces on Friday, detaining 290 people before lunch, compared with about 500 in recent months.
19Officials say the mass arrests reflect careful monitoring of IS's followers over a long time, but the scale of detentions also underscores how broad Turkey has allowed the Islamic State network to become.
20The IS web in Turkey even extended into the jailhouse, according to court documents for a Spanish woman detained in Turkey and accused of recruiting for Islamic State.
21Islamic State hired a lawyer linked to the group to represent the woman.
22Samira Yerou was granted repeated phone calls that allowed her to be "duly informed, by at least two members of ISIL, of the passage of female recruits joining the group from Turkey to Syria, changes in hiding places, etc., even from within the detention center," according to the court documents.
23In one chilling episode, Yerou, who was jailed along with her 3-year-old son, had a freewheeling phone call with a Saudi emir for Islamic State, telling the child what to say.
24"Tell him, 'I will behead the police,'" she ordered.
25The boy repeated her words and both she and the Saudi emir laughed after the child spoke.
26The documents do not indicate how authorities obtained the transcript, but make clear that communications and information were freely available in the jail - and that recruitment continued apace behind bars.
27Authorities and analysts say Turkish citizens are a relatively small proportion of IS fighters and supporters, but they are crucial for shepherding supplies and recruits from the West and North Africa safely across the border to Syria or Iraq.
28According to Omer Ozdemir, a researcher at Sakarya University, most of the IS supply network in Turkey is contracted out to large tribes that have worked as smugglers on both sides of Turkey's borders for generations.
29He said the government could work to put pressure on the tribes to end the relationships and significantly squeeze IS.
30While Turkey appears to have a close eye on IS operations in Turkey, this week's suicide bombing shows that Turkish authorities can't afford to miss one IS operative.
