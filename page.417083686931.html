Greece fears over bailout grow; Spain sees deal within days

5Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras gives a speech at the annual conference of the Hellenic Federation of Enterprises in Athens, on Monday, May 18, 2015.
6Tsipras said his radical left-led government has done all it can to seek an agreement with bailout creditors that will ease the country out of its current acute liquidity crunch - which he blamed on "tough negotiating tactics" by the country's creditors.
7Earlier Monday, the government signaled it needs a deal with lenders by the end of the month to avoid defaulting on its debt, as renewed concern rattled the country's finance markets.
8Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis said Monday that he expects an agreement with bailout creditors within the next week, which would save the cash-strapped country from fast-approaching bankruptcy.
9"I think we are very close," Varoufakis said.
10"Let's say (it's a matter) of about a week."
11For almost four months, Greece's radical left-led government has been haggling with its creditors from the 19-country eurozone and International Monetary Fund over economic reforms it must make to secure a 7.2 billion euro ($8 billion) cash injection.
12That is the final payment due from the country's 240 billion-euro bailout program, launched five years ago after Greece's public finances spiraled out of control and it was locked out of international bond markets.
13In a late-night interview with private Star TV, Varoufakis said creditors also appear to believe that the time is ripe for a deal.
14But he insisted that he would reject any compromise that the radical left-led government in Athens considers to be "non-viable."
15Earlier, Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras said the country has tabled detailed proposals for a viable deal.
16But he warned that Greece is in a state of "financial strangulation."
17Over the past few weeks, Greece has survived by scraping together cash from reserve accounts to pay debts as well as day-to-day commitments like wages and pensions.
18However, Athens admits it is running out of options as further debt repayments come due next month.
19If no deal is agreed, Tsipras' government may be faced with a choice of what to pay, imposing capital controls or even leaving the euro.
20"I assure you that if we face a dilemma between paying a creditor who refuses to sign an agreement with us and a pensioner, we will pay the pensioner," Varoufakis said.
21"I hope we will be able to pay both."
22Tsipras also faulted Greece's creditors for the liquidity crunch.
23"The lack of liquidity is neither the choice nor the responsibility of the Greek government," he said.
24"It is a tough negotiating tactic of our partners, and I do not know whether everybody in Europe feels proud of it."
25Government spokesman Gabriel Sakellaridis said that to honor its debt obligations this summer, Greece needs more financial assistance.
26As a result, Sakellaridis expects a deal with creditors "by the end of May."
27Markets appear to be uncertain about how the latest episode in the Greek debt crisis will pan out.
28Though uncertainty over Greece caused the interest rate on the government's two-year bonds - a gauge of default risk - surge about 3 percentage points on Monday to above 24 percent, the Athens Stock Exchange closed 1.6 percent higher.
29In the talks, Athens is proposing reform measures that might protect Greeks hammered by a six-year recession, but lenders say the proposals are too vague.
30Varoufakis said the main sticking points include demanded pension and labor reforms, as well as the height of the primary budget surplus - which excludes the cost of servicing Greece's huge debt - in coming years.
31Government officials have suggested that Athens could seek popular approval for the final deal, through elections or a referendum.
32But Varoufakis said he didn't expect either, and the agreement would be ratified in parliament.
33He said it would be "foolish" to hold elections just months after the government came to power, adding that any referendum would effectively be a vote on whether to keep or lose the euro currency.
34"And it would be unfair for Greek citizens to have to take a position on such a matter, answering with either a yes or a no," Varoufakis said.
35Athens has already pooled cash reserves from schools, hospitals and local government to fund the state's debt payments.
36And Sakellaridis confirmed the government this month used a reserve account with money in a currency unit used by the IMF to repay a loan to the Fund.
37Sakellaridis also insisted Greece would not accept a one-off bank deposit levy to resolve the cash crisis - a similar measure had been used in eurozone member Cyprus two years ago.
