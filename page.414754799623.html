Kenneth G. Pigott, president and CEO of Lyric Opera of Chicago, dies at 71

Ukraine Cease-Fire Goes Into Effect, but Rebel Leader in Key Town Repudiates Accord

1Kenneth G. Pigott was that rarity among leaders of cultural institutions: a visionary board president who could speak knowledgeably about the art form he served and who took an active, influential role in moving the institution forward.
2The widely admired Lyric Opera of Chicago president and CEO led the eight-month international search that led to Anthony Freud's succeeding the retired William Mason as general director in 2011.
3He also was the key figure persuading superstar soprano Renee Fleming to become Lyric's first creative consultant, as of 2010.
4Over a lunch meeting with the diva in New York, Mr. Pigott initially proposed that she take over the directorship of the company to succeed Mason.
5To Mr. Pigott's chagrin, the diva turned down the offer, he told the Tribune.
6Mr. Pigott, 71, died Friday, Feb. 13, in Chicago after suffering cardiac arrest, according to a Lyric spokeswoman.
7He was a member of Lyric's board of directors for 13 years before becoming president and CEO in 2011.
8His business acumen, deep commitment to the art form and to Lyric's role as an integral part of Chicago's cultural fabric made him one of the institution's great MVPs - also one of its most unabashed cheerleaders.
9An attorney turned private investor, Mr. Pigott was a key member of the leadership team committed to significantly strengthening the opera company's financial base to broaden its services well beyond Lyric's core constituency of loyal audience members and well-heeled donors.
10Only by doing so, he believed, would Lyric weather the economic uncertainties of the early 21st century while retaining its status as an important international opera company.
11"Ken could rally the board and others to the Lyric donor cause. He had a magnetic personality, which, coupled with his knowledge and enthusiasm for Lyric and opera, made him truly a great leader. He knew opera far more than any of us that preceded him. He may have been the ultimate in leaders for Lyric and certainly (surpassed) me in his knowledge and involvement."
12One of Mr. Pigott's signal Lyric accomplishments was leading the strategic planning process - unanimously approved by the full board in early 2013 - that would combine "excellence, relevance and fiscal responsibility" moving forward, according to the opera company.
13Beyond his duties at Lyric, Mr. Pigott was managing partner of Vaduz Partners LLC, a private investment company specializing in information technology services.
14He also served on the boards of Opera America, the Ann & Robert H. Lurie Chicago Children's Hospital and the Merit School of Music, and was a major backer of Chicago's unsuccessful bid to win the 2016 Olympics.
15He graduated from Iowa State University and Harvard Law School and was a former partner in the Chicago law firm of Winston and Strawn.
16He and his family lived in Winnetka before moving to Chicago's Gold Coast.
17News of Mr. Pigott's death was sent in an email from Freud that was posted on Lyric's Facebook page Friday night.
18"We are shocked and deeply saddened to learn and to share the news of the death of Ken Pigott," Freud said in the email.
19"In the few hours since (his) passing, we have only begun to grasp the depth and breadth of this unimaginable loss. Ken was a truly unique man. (His) wisdom and passion transformed the lives of everyone at Lyric, and at the many organizations with which he was associated. He is irreplaceable."
20Besides his wife, Jane, Mr. Pigott is survived by seven adult children.
21Memorial services have yet to be planned, the Lyric spokeswoman said.
1Although the Ukrainian president and the pro-Russian rebel leader both announced that the cease-fire had gone into effect, the separatist leader said it did not apply to Debaltseve, where thousands of Ukrainian troops have been under siege and might be surrounded.
2The rebel leader, Aleksandr Zakharchenko, said the town, a critical railway hub, had not been mentioned specifically in the cease-fire agreement.
3That deal, which went into effect at midnight, had been brokered by Chancellor Angela Merkel of Germany and the French president, Francois Hollande, in Minsk, Belarus.
4Neither leader immediately commented on Mr. Zakharchenko's announcement about Debaltseve, which had been the scene of intense fighting in recent days as the rebels battled to take the town before any cease-fire.
5Just after the midnight deadline, Ukraine's president, Petro O. Poroshenko, went on national television saying he had ordered Ukraine's armed forces to halt their fire in the contested eastern portion of the country.
6In his earlier statement, Mr. Zakharchenko, the head of the self-declared Donetsk People's Republic, also said he had ordered his forces to halt combat all along the front line in eastern Ukraine, as required in the Minsk agreement, but not near Debaltseve.
7He also indicated that rebel forces would not allow the approximately 8,000 Ukrainian troops who are there to leave.
8The statement left a looming question about the future of the accord brokered Thursday with great effort by the European leaders, including 16 hours of overnight negotiations between Ms. Merkel, Mr. Hollande, President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia and Mr. Poroshenko.
9The cease-fire is the second such attempt to end a war that has claimed more than 5,400 lives since it began in April.
10Although Mr. Poroshenko said he had ordered the cease-fire, he added that Ukraine would not "turn the other cheek" if the other side violated the agreement, and he spoke bitterly of the two-and-a-half-day gap between the signing of the accord and the implementation of the cease-fire.
11"We were ready for an immediate cease-fire in Minsk," he said.
12"But our counterparties asked for 60 hours. We paid a very heavy price for these 60 hours because dozens of Ukrainian citizens, including women and children, were killed."
13Ukrainian forces also continued fighting until the cease-fire went into effect.
14Mr. Poroshenko urged the other leaders who had been involved in the talks to now ensure that the rest of the agreement was upheld.
15Within hours, reports emerged of possible cease-fire violations, including mortar fire near Debaltseve.
16But residents reached by phone in two cities with fighting on their outskirts, Donetsk and Mariupol, said all was calm.
17From the cease-fire's signing on Thursday until Saturday, the Ukrainian military reported 18 soldiers were killed and 63 wounded.
18At least four children were among the civilians who died in the recent fighting.
19The National Security and Defense Council of Ukraine issued a statement on Saturday evening carried by the Ukrainska Pravda newspaper saying Mr. Zakharchenko's comments signaled that, "In this way, the Donetsk People's Republic announces it does not recognize the Minsk accord and will continue military action."
20Mr. Poroshenko said Saturday he would ask the Ukrainian Parliament to declare a national state of martial law if the cease-fire broke down.
21Russian-backed militants and Mr. Putin have insisted that Debaltseve is surrounded; the Ukrainian government has not conceded that point.
22The only Ukrainian resupply road into the town is peppered with mines and in the range of artillery by both sides, and it is unclear who is in control.
23Even before Mr. Zakharchenko announced the accord did not apply to Debaltseve, anger had been swelling among Ukrainian soldiers at a negotiated lag between the signing of the agreement and its implementation.
24Fighting raged through the day on Saturday.
25Both sides had tried to land final blows before midnight.
26Shells killed two people in the rebel-held city of Donetsk.
27Soldiers interviewed at their sandbagged positions along the contested road, as well as ambulance crews and doctors, expressed rising anger over the agreement that led not to a hoped-for calm, but an escalation in fighting.
28The poorly guarded, 31-mile supply road into Debaltseve on Saturday was shrouded in an eerie mist and rimmed by bare trees, their limbs prickly with glistening hoarfrost.
29Through the day, ambulances rushed to a hospital carrying from the fight the wounded whose luck ran out hours before the accord was intended to take effect.
30"The cease-fire was a political decision by my government," Oksana D. Yarosh, a Ukrainian military surgeon, said in an interview outside the Artemivsk Central Regional hospital, where the most gravely injured are evaluated.
31She could not judge the decision to leave open the window for a final frenzy of fighting, she said, because it was unclear what other options existed.
32"I am a military doctor," she said.
33"I serve, and my service is trying to save lives and health. Personally, it has been very painful for me to see the boys with shrapnel wounds."
34One of her patients, a 32-year-old soldier, came in Thursday night with severe shrapnel wounds to his face, and his right elbow had been so severely wounded that his arm would probably have to be amputated below the joint, though she refrained from doing so immediately.
35"His life will never be the same," Dr. Yarosh said.
36"I think, 'How horrible is war.' And I think, 'How scary that we lose people in this way.' It's been difficult."
37The United States on Friday said that Russian artillery was aiding the separatists in the battle to encircle the town, site of a strategic railroad switching yard.
38On Saturday, the American ambassador to Ukraine, Geoffrey R. Pyatt, posted satellite images on his Twitter account to bolster the case that Russia had joined the separatists.
39One image seemed to show four gun lines of towed artillery and one of self-propelled artillery northeast of Debaltseve, in a landscape in snowy towns of gray.
40Another showed what Mr. Pyatt said were air-defense systems, and the third showed the black, streaky exhaust plumes of what was labeled a "Russian multiple rocket launcher deployment" near Shakhtar, Ukraine.
41A medic reached on Saturday by cellphone in Debaltseve, Albert Sardaryen, a 22-year-old who left his university to volunteer with an ambulance crew, said the city had been attacked by Grad rockets through the night Friday, but that the firing let up around 11 a.m. on Saturday.
42Soldiers were hunkered down in basements or bunkers, he said.
43The scale of the casualties was unclear, he said, because many of the wounded could not be taken from their front-line positions to his field hospital.
44The last ambulance left Debaltseve on Thursday evening, before the escalation that followed the cease-fire signing.
45"The situation is quite difficult," Mr. Sardaryen said.
46"We don't believe in the cease-fire. We are a little bit worried of being trapped. If there is no way out, all we can do is stay and fight here."
