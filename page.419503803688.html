Some 2,000 migrants rush past police across border to Macedonia from Greece

1Boris Grdanoski, Associated Press Migrants have a fight among themselves as tensions run high while waiting to board a train that will take them towards Serbia, at the railway station in the southern Macedonia's town of Gevgelija, on Saturday, Aug. 22, 2015.
2About 39,000 people have been registered as passing through Macedonia in the past month, twice as many as the month before.
3GEVGELIJA, Macedonia - Thousands of migrants on Saturday rushed past baton-wielding Macedonian police who were attempting to block them from entering Macedonia from Greece.
4Police fired stun grenades and several people were injured in the border clashes.
5The tumult started when police decided to allow a small group of migrants with young children to cross the frontier and crowds in the back squeezed them toward the shielded police wall.
6Many women, at least one pregnant, and children fell to the ground apparently fainting after squeezing past the cordon.
7Then thousands of others, including women with babies and men carrying small children, used the moment to run across a field not protected by barbed wire to enter Macedonia.
8Police fired stun grenades, but didn't manage to stop the rush.
9There were no immediate reports of the number and extent of injuries.
10Many children also lost their parents in the chaos and were left shouting "mama, baba."
11Several hundred, mostly elderly and children remained on the Greek side of the border when police restored order.
12It was the second day of clashes between the migrants and Macedonian police who are attempting to block them from heading north toward the European Union.
13On Friday, police fired stun grenades and clashed with the migrants, a day after Macedonia's government declared a state of emergency on the frontier to stop the human tide.
14At least 10 people were injured in the melee.
15Both Greece and Macedonia have seen an unprecedented wave of migrants this year, most fleeing wars in Syria, Afghanistan or Iraq.
16More than 160,000 have arrived so far in Greece, mostly crossing in inflatable dinghies from the nearby Turkish coast - an influx that has overwhelmed Greek authorities and the country's small Aegean islands.
17Some 45,000 crossed through Macedonia over the past two months.
18Few, if any, of the migrants want to remain in Greece, which is in the grip of a financial crisis.
19Most head straight to the country's northern border with Macedonia, where they cram onto trains and head north through Serbia and Hungary on their way to more prosperous EU countries such as Germany, the Netherlands or Sweden.
20Friday night, police allowed only small groups of families with children to cross the border by walking on railway tracks to a station in the Macedonian town of Gevgelija, where most take trains to the border with Serbia before heading further north toward EU-member Hungary.
21Those who could not cross spent the rainy and chilly night in the open with little food.
22They massed close to a razor wire separating them from machine-gun toting Macedonian policemen.
23Some raised their babies above their heads to try to persuade the policemen to let them through.
24"These men are heartless," said Yousef, a Syrian refugee who gave only his first name, as he held a little wide-eyed girl with curly hair in his arms and pointed toward the policemen.
25"They don't care about our tragedy."
26A police officer told The Associated Press that the force is only following the government's orders to block the refugees from entering the country.
27"Until we receive another order, the situation here will remain like this," said the officer, who refused to be named because he was not authorized to speak to reporters.
28As heavy rain poured, some migrants took off their shirts and booed and shouted insults at the policemen in camouflage fatigues.
29Others took shelter inside dozens of small tents or under a few trees on a muddy field.
30Last week, there were chaotic scenes at the Gevgelija train station involving hundreds of migrants trying to board the trains.
31On Saturday, Rama Kabul from Syria walked the railway track in the opposite direction from the station pleading with two Macedonian policemen pushing her back with riot shields to let her brother - who remained trapped behind the razor fence on the border - join her.
32"They took me out and left him there," Kabul said with tears in her eyes.
33"I just want to talk to him."
34Macedonian police said they started blocking the refugees on the 50-kilometer (30-mile) frontier "for the security of citizens who live in the border areas and for better treatment of the migrants."
35"Macedonian authorities should be protecting migrants, including children and those among them who may be fleeing war and persecution, not giving the police a green light to fire at them," Emina Cerimovic, research fellow at the rights group, said in a statement.
36Until now, the border has been porous, with only a few patrols on each side.
37Sealing it disrupts the Balkan corridor for migrants who start in Turkey, take boats to Greece or walk to Bulgaria, then make their way through Macedonia and Serbia before heading farther north.
