'They wanted $130,000 ... and then more': explosive Bali nine bribe allegations

1The former lawyer of Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran has outlined explosive allegations of corruption by the judges that sentenced the Bali nine duo to death, saying they asked for more than $130,000 to give them a prison term of less than 20 years.
2A file photo of ex-Bali nine lawyer Mohammad Rifan.
3But Bali-based attorney Muhammad Rifan says a deal fell through after the judges later told him they had been ordered by senior legal and government members in Jakarta to impose a death penalty.
4The judges, it is alleged, then asked for an even greater sum for a lighter sentence, money Mr Rifan did not have.
5The sensational claims by Mr Rifan that the initial trial of the pair was deeply corrupted were revealed by a joint investigation by Fairfax Media and the former host of SBS' Dateline program Mark Davis, a Gold Walkley-winning journalist.
6Michael Chan and Chinthu Sukumaran, the brothers of Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran, at Wijaya Pura in Cilacap on Sunday.
7It comes on the eve of the execution of the two Australians, who have been told they will die at midnight on Tuesday, or soon after.
8Mr Rifan says he has gone public with details of the alleged corruption after waiting for the judicial commission, the Indonesian body that safeguards the probity of judges, to investigate the alleged requests for bribes.
9The investigation was prompted after Mr Rifan made a surprise visit to Kerobokan prison on February 7 to meet with the condemned Australians, emerging to tell reporters there had been "interference" in the case.
10Bali nine lawyer, Julian McMahon, with three recent paintings by Myuran Sukumuran, among them one marked "Self Portrait, 72 hours just started" as Sukumuran began the countdown to his execution.
11Until now, he has declined to elaborate on the damning, if cryptic, comments.
12But he says he is now going public because the matter is extremely urgent given the Bali nine pair will soon be shot dead by a firing squad.
13In a recorded interview with Davis on Sunday, provided to Fairfax Media, Mr Rifan outlines how he first met with the judges to discuss the bribes and how the intervention from Jakarta, and his own miscalculation, led to the death sentence being applied to Chan and Sukumaran.
14His account provides a disturbing insight into the machinations of Indonesia's legal system, widely pilloried within the country as riven by corruption and wildly inconsistent rulings.
15"We met many times with the judges," he said.
16"We were talking about how long the penalty would be. Even though this is prohibited between lawyers and a judge, this is the reality. It's normal."
17"It was more than 1 billion rupiah (about $133,000 at the time) to get a verdict lower than 20 years - 15 or 16 or 17 years like that. So then we had a deal on that."
18But he says the deal unravelled one or two weeks before the pair were due to be sentenced, when the order allegedly came from upon high came to hand Chan and Sukumaran the death penalty.
19It is understood that the directive came from the attorney general's office and the supreme court.
20Following the intervention, Mr Rifan said the judges "started asking for a lot more money".
21"I just explained to them how much we had and they said the risk was now too big for them and that the [1 billion rupiah] was not enough."
22At this point, Mr Rifan says he made a fatal miscalculation.
23He believed the judges were bluffing.
24"I thought they were only joking. I thought they would return back to the 20 years if I didn't come up with more money."
25But, on February 14, 2006, Chan and Sukumaran were sentenced to death.
26One of the judges involved in the case, Wayan Yasa Abadi, denied in February there had been political interference or negotiations about bribes.
27"I can assure you there was none," he told Fairfax Media.
28"We protected ourselves from everybody. It was purely our decision."
29Another of the judges, Roro Setyawati, has said she had opposed the death penalty but was overruled by other judges on the panel.
30Mr Rifan accepts that his account of events should be properly tested by a thorough and impartial investigation by the judicial commission.
31In the meantime, he says, it was vital that the executions of the two Australian heroin smugglers be postponed.
32"This is an extraordinary situation because it is about lives. If they are dead they cannot be brought back again."
33The meeting between himself and the judges and the alleged interference by Jakarta are both "prohibited" under Indonesian law.
34"The judges are meant to be independent," he says.
35Fairfax Media has verified that an account by Mr Rifan has been separately outlined in writing.
36Meanwhile, Chan and Sukumaran have provided affidavits to the judicial commission.
37The commission allocated a file number to the case about two months ago, meaning it had formally authorised an investigation.
38However, Fairfax Media understands the judicial commission still has not interviewed the judges who heard the matter in Bali's district court, or anyone else involved in the matter.
39Mr Rifan says the inaction by the judicial commission is puzzling.
40He has made complaints about judicial irregularities before and "they were much more responsive".
41"They came to Bali straight away. They do the interview. They asked for the evidence, many things. They were very quick. But why in this case do they do nothing?"
42The handling of the legal cases of the Australians, as well as the other seven drug traffickers slated to be executed alongside them, has sparked criticism of irregularities and interference in the independence of the judiciary.
43Attorney general HM Prasetyo has consistently said that appeals before the courts should - or will - be rejected even as they were still being considered.
44Meanwhile, the country's supreme court and state administrative court rushed through verdicts - all adverse to the condemned drug felons - in unusually rapid time.
45Indonesia's president Joko Widodo announced in December he would reject the clemency applications of 64 drug convicts on death row before he had examined their cases individually or the felons had exhausted their legal appeals.
46He said the action was needed to fix the country's "drugs emergency".
47Mr Rifan says the judicial commission had rebuffed repeated requests to properly investigate the matter before Chan and Sukumaran are executed.
48"They don't care that some people are about to be shot," he says.
49"Maybe next month or in two months they will make a decision that the judges made a mistake, that their decision was not independent, that the penalty was invalid from the beginning.
