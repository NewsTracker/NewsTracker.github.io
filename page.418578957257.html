Jury reaches verdict in Colorado theater shooting trial

1James Holmes, pictured in 2012, is charged with killing 12 moviegoers and wounding 70 more in a shooting spree in a crowded theater in Aurora, Colo., in July 2012.
2Jurors have reached a verdict in the trial of Colorado theater shooter James Holmes.
3The jury made its decision on Thursday after deliberating for a day and a half.
4A court spokesman said the verdict will be announced at 4:15 p.m. local time.
5Holmes, 27, pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity in the 2012 shootings that killed 12 people and wounded 70 others.
6The verdict comes nearly three years to the day that Holmes slipped into a darkened midnight premiere of the Batman movie "The Dark Knight Rises" and opened fire.
7His attorneys argued that he was in the grips of a psychotic episode.
8Jurors heard nearly three months of testimony, including heartbreaking and sometimes gruesome stories from more than 70 survivors who took the stand.
9If the jury convicts Holmes, the trial will enter a sentencing phase in which the jurors will hear testimony and decide whether he should be sent to prison for life without the possibility of parole or sentenced to death.
10If they find him not guilty by reason of insanity, he would be committed indefinitely to a state mental hospital.
11Prosecutors focused on the findings of two state-appointed forensic psychiatrists who examined Holmes months and years after the shooting and found him severely mentally ill yet capable of knowing right from wrong and therefore legally sane under Colorado law.
12Dozens of investigators testified about the carnage Holmes inflicted and how he rigged his apartment into an elaborate booby trap he hoped would explode and divert first responders from the Aurora theater as he set about the July 20, 2012 attack.
13Prosecutors honed in on Holmes' elaborate planning of the massacre.
14They showed jurors a spiral notebook in which Holmes listed what weapons to buy, which auditoriums in the theater complex would allow for the most casualties, and even an estimated emergency response time to the theater.
15Defense attorneys portrayed Holmes as a struggling neuroscience graduate student so addled by mental illness that he was unable to tell right from wrong at the time of the shootings.
16They said he suffered schizophrenia, and they called two doctors who said Holmes was in the grips of a psychotic episode when he acted on delusions that propelled him to kill.
17They called a far shorter list of witnesses, such as doctors and jail guards, who described Holmes' bizarre behavior before and after the attack.
