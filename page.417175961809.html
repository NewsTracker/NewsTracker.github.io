3 bodies found near Mexico city occupied by vigilantes

1ACAPULCO, Mexico (AP) - Mexican police found at least three dismembered bodies wrapped in blankets near a southern city where residents have reported that 10 people disappeared during a vigilante takeover earlier this month and six more went missing in previous months.
2The bodies of the middle-age men were found Thursday in a cemetery near Chilapa, a city in Guerrero state that was occupied May 9-14 by vigilantes, who some residents claimed were linked to a local drug gang.
3It was not clear if the bodies were some of the men reported missing.
4Federal investigators have been sent in to search for the missing residents, but questions have arisen as to why the government allowed the armed vigilantes to take over the city of about 35,000 people for almost five days.
5Authorities appeared not to have resisted the takeover because "community police" groups have some legal standing in Guerrero and negotiations with such groups have worked in the past.
6"A decision was made to not act (against them) to avoid the spilling of blood," said a federal official who was not authorized to be quoted by name.
7"The government preferred to negotiate."
8Police found the three bodies after receiving an anonymous tip.
9The men were apparently killed about five days ago, said a Guerrero state official, who also was not authorized to be quoted by name.
10Claiming to be "community police" from surrounding towns, between 250 and 300 vigilantes took over Chilapa on May 9 purportedly to end widespread violence in the city that has resulted from a turf war between the rival Rojos and Ardillos drug gangs.
11The vigilantes disarmed local police and forced the police chief out, saying they suspected some officers of working for Los Rojos.
12After negotiations, the vigilantes returned the police weapons, a new chief was named and state and federal police were sent in to take charge of security.
13After the vigilantes left, some residents came forward to accuse them of kidnapping residents and said they appeared to be working for Los Ardillos.
14The confusion continued Thursday when Miguel Angel Godinez, the state prosecutor, said in an interview with Milenio television that there weren't any missing people, but rather 15 people were being held by the vigilantes.
15He said the leaders of the vigilante force are "responsible people" and they will have to say where the 15 people are.
16The Guerrero state government said Wednesday that it continued to negotiate with the leaders of the community police forces.
17It identified the head of the vigilantes as Jose Apolonio Villanueva, a communal farm leader.
18It said he was fed up with crime in the area and had demanded that marines be sent in.
19Community police forces have legal standing in some Guerrero towns, but they are designated by community assemblies.
20They are limited by law to policing their own towns, usually with low-caliber, single-shot rifles or shotguns.
21Most of the armed men who took over Chilapa had such weapons, but a few had assault rifles.
