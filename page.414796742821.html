1Under the agreement negotiated by the leaders of Ukraine, Russia, Germany and France, the warring sides were to begin withdrawing heavy weapons from the front line Tuesday.
2That plan already looks at risk, with the rebels saying they are not satisfied that conditions are in place for the process to go ahead.
3Associated Press reporters in Luhanske, a government-held town 9 miles northwest of the bitterly contested railway hub of Debaltseve, heard sustained and regular shelling Monday.
4Some of the artillery appeared to be outgoing, suggesting it was being fired by Ukrainian troops.
5Debaltseve, still in government hands, remains in contention despite the cease-fire.
6The rebels insist the town should revert to their control as it has been encircled by their fighters.
7A loaded Grad rocket launcher was seen pointing in the direction of Debaltseve, but it was not fired while AP journalists were present.
8Observers from the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe, who are supposed to monitor the implementation of the peace deal, said Sunday that separatists denied them access to Debaltseve.
9Despite the cease-fire that went into effect early Sunday, five Ukrainian troops were killed and 25 wounded in the past 24 hours, Ukrainian military spokesman Andriy Lysenko said Monday.
10In New York, UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon expressed concern over the continued hostilities around Debaltseve, urging "all parties to abide by the cease-fire without exception."
11The cease-fire had raised cautious hopes for an end to the 10-month-old conflict, which has already claimed more than 5,300 lives.
12But Ukraine and rebel officials have already traded multiple accusations of attacks since then.
13Separatist military official Eduard Basurin said in a televised news conference Monday that the government overnight lobbed artillery at Horlivka, a town under rebel control.
14Ukraine, however, blamed that attack on the rebels.
15The government-appointed police chief of the Donetsk region, Vyacheslav Abroskin, said the separatists shelled the town in order to derail the truce.
16Both the separatists and the Ukrainian government insist they are committed to the cease-fire negotiated in marathon talks last week.
17But the Russian news agency Interfax quoted Basurin as saying Monday that conditions are not yet ready to pull back heavy weapons Tuesday.
18"We will begin pulling back equipment from the line of contact if we receive a certain signal, which is if the Ukrainians also do the same thing," Basurin was quoted as saying.
19Ukraine and the West accuse Russia of arming and supplying manpower to the separatists and have imposed a range of economic sanctions to pressure Moscow into changing course.
20Russia denies all suggestions it is directly involved in the war in Ukraine, but the sheer amount of heavy weapons the rebels have belies that claim.
21On Monday, the European Union added 19 more people and nine organizations to its list of sanctioned Russian entities, including two Russian deputy defense ministers, eastern Ukraine-born Russian crooner Iosif Kobzon, who sang to the rebel leaders in Donetsk last year, as well as several separatist commanders.
22The Russian Foreign Ministry reacted angrily to the decision, calling it "clumsy" in the context of last week's cease-fire deal.
23The ministry dismissed the new EU sanctions as "running against common sense" and harming chances "to resolve an internal Ukrainian conflict."
1(AP) - A tractor-trailer driver threw items from his cab at police - including socks, shoes and a small refrigerator - as he led them on a 34-mile chase in western Pennsylvania, authorities said Sunday.
2Police in Westmoreland County said a man called emergency dispatchers just before 2 a.m. Saturday and said he planned to wreck his truck.
3State troopers tried to stop the vehicle near Washington Township but the driver disregarded the emergency lights and sirens, and a pursuit began along Route 70 and later the Pennsylvania Turnpike, police said.
4"During the pursuit, the driver threw numerous items at pursuing troopers from the cab" including a steel canister that struck a Greensburg state police unit and disabled it, police said.
5The driver also threw a mini refrigerator before the truck was stopped with spike strips shortly after 3 a.m. Saturday.
6The driver refused orders to leave the cab, police said, so troopers entered and used a stun gun to subdue him.
7He was taken to a hospital because officers believed he was under the influence of a controlled substance, namely Xanax, and for other injuries, police said.
8"It should be noted that during the pursuit the operator threw his shoes and socks at troopers. When the windows were broken to make entry into the cab, the operator's feet were cut during his resistance to arrest," police said.
9At the hospital, he was treated for cuts on his feet, and a blood sample was taken for testing.
10The driver, identified as Christopher Charles Boyer, 47, of Mifflintown, is charged with aggravated assault, fleeing or attempting to elude, reckless endangering, resisting arrest and other counts.
11Court documents indicate he had requested representation by the public defender's office, which rang unanswered Sunday.
12State police told the Pittsburgh Tribune-Review that Boyer was the only occupant of the truck, which hauls sand used in the hydraulic fracturing drilling process, known as fracking.
13Police did not disclose his employer.
