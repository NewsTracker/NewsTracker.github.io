4JERUSALEM (AP) - It's a mystery to many: Benjamin Netanyahu's campaign gained steam when he ruled out the creation of a Palestinian state, and he seems determined to continue settling occupied land with Jews.
5So why would Israelis again back policies that promise friction of all kinds - and will dramatically dilute the Jewish character of their hard-won state by making it eventually inseparable from the millions of Palestinians in the West Bank?
6The answer lies in the details of a conundrum so complicated that the dynamics of democracy seem hardly able to contain it.
7Tellingly, perhaps, the campaign did not even focus on the grand strategic issue that is now presented as having been decided: Instead the opposition pledged to address bread-and-butter issues, like the high cost of living.
8The outcome may be different under circumstances that force the Palestinian issue to the table.
9If international boycott initiatives start exacting an economic price on a country that appreciates its high standard of living, or if the Europeans who are Israel's top trading partners take off the gloves.
10Or if the Palestinians rebel, creating a major security headache, or the United States steps in with peace proposals backed by muscle.
11Another game-changer would be if the moderate opposition united behind a truly compelling candidate after a succession of leaders who were simply not perceived by enough people as ready for prime time.
12The last Labor Party leader to achieve an effective brand of gravitas was one-time military chief Ehud Barak, who won in 1999.
13Very few Israelis see the 48-year-old West Bank occupation as purely a nationalist conceit, greedy and anti-Palestinian, though that narrative certainly has currency in the region and around the world.
14From a Palestinian perspective, the West Bank and Gaza Strip combined are just about a fifth of historic Palestine - the bare minimum that is acceptable from their perspective in order to formally accept the existence of Israel.
15But what Israelis see is a pre-1967 border that is basically just a cease-fire line from the 1948-49 war that established the country.
16Without the West Bank, Israel is only about 10 miles (15 kilometers) wide at its narrowest point.
17The strategic highland looms over Israeli cities, visible on a clear day from the outskirts of Tel Aviv and surrounding Jerusalem on three sides.
18They fear that if their army clears out it will be replaced not by peaceful Palestinian moderates but more menacing forces like Hamas - which took over the Gaza Strip soon after Israel handed it fully to the Palestinian autonomy government of Mahmoud Abbas in 2005.
19The fact that Islamic State militants are menacing the region does not help, and was exploited by Netanyahu during a campaign in which he portrayed the militants in one TV ad as charging toward Jerusalem, with naive Israeli leftists even helpfully pointing the way.
20On several occasions Israeli governments have offered the Palestinians statehood on close to all of the West Bank and Gaza.
21A quarter century's worth of futile negotiations appears to stem from Israel's refusal to accept a return of Palestinian refugees and their descendants, who potentially number in the millions, and from the tremendous difficulty of sharing the holy city Jerusalem.
22The idea of a border running right through the it - with Palestinian police controlling entrances to the Old City a stone's throw from downtown pubs and hotels - is unfathomable to many Israelis.
23Because so few think a peace deal is likely, the opposition runs away from the issue.
24If elected they might offer types of concessions: perhaps a unilateral pullout from some areas; maybe a push for an interim deal which today the Palestinians refuse to contemplate; in any case, a freeze on settlements.
25But the issue is so complicated that they have found their position difficult to sell.
26Many Israelis console themselves with the islands of Palestinian autonomy established in the 1990s in the West Bank: perhaps they might suffice to make the "demographic issue" go away.
27Israel's fractured politics leave little room for maneuver.
28A look at the electoral map shows a huge proportion of parliament that is almost guaranteed to go to sectarian and ethnic interests.
29More than a third of the new parliament will be occupied by parties that target certain groups and have a near-guaranteed vote that has little to do with the Palestinian issue: Israeli Arabs, Russian immigrants, traditionally-inclined Sephardic Jews and different shades of religious Jews.
30Even the major parties, which theoretically stand for ideology, to a degree appeal to specific groups.
31This is especially true of Likud, which has a tremendously loyal base among working-class Israelis who hail from the Arab world and tend to be hawkish.
32They still resent the leftist establishment that founded Israel for the reception they got as immigrants a half-century ago.
33Many speak of the party as a "home" that is "in our blood" and cannot conceivably be "betrayed."
34On top of that, the religious sector, aligned with the right, has the country's highest birthrate by far, and so can be expected to constantly add to its automatic storehouse of votes.
35Netanyahu, known popularly as Bibi, is a brilliant campaigner who not only does and says what it takes to get elected but seems comfortable doing so.
36In the final week of the campaign he realized that the perception of victory, in Israel's fragmented political space, would depend more on his party than his bloc.
37So he tacked to the right, taking votes from his nationalist ally, the Jewish Home.
38On Tuesday, that meant issuing dire warnings that Israeli Arab citizens were streaming to the polls, which shocked many Israelis and brought charges of racism.
39A few days earlier, he had sounded the alarm against an international conspiracy supposedly amassed to "topple" him.
40He also energized his base by declaring that if re-elected he would never allow a Palestinian state - walking away from a 2009 declaration that itself had reversed his previous position.
41But so profound is the cynicism surrounding Israeli politics that many people view the zigzags as little more than political sport.
42Also effective was Netanyahu's controversial appearance two weeks ago on Capitol Hill, where he argued against President Barack Obama's supposedly impending nuclear deal with Iran.
43Mortified, the Israeli opposition could not disagree with the substance of his speech - but warned of a crisis with the United States.
44The relatively polite response from the U.S. administration was, to Netanyahu's fans at least, another sign that he has things well in hand.

3Uninsured women can face out-of-pocket health care costs of $20,000 or more during the course of a pregnancy, according to federal estimates - and a woman who learns she's pregnant today would not be able to get Obamacare for eight months, when the open enrollment period opens from November to January.
4High costs may lead some women - who don't get insurance through an employer yet make too much to qualify for Medicaid - to go without prenatal care altogether, advocates say.
5Some women may still be on plans that don't cover maternity care, even though the Affordable Care Act forced most insurers to do so, and unable to make a switch.
6"Expectant mothers shouldn't have to choose between seeking prenatal care and paying the rent," said city Controller Scott Stringer, who was set to release a report on the issue Thursday.
7Women who do not get prenatal care are three to four times more likely to die of complications than women who do, according to federal Centers for Disease Control and Prevention figures.
8"For expectant mothers, for newborns, this care is critical," said Christina Postolowski, a health policy manager at advocacy group Young Invincibles.
9Despite mounting pressure, the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services last month said pregnancy wouldn't be added to the federal exchange as a qualifying event.
10However, because New York runs its own marketplace, New York State of Health, local lawmakers believe they can change the policy for New York women.
11Stringer is calling on the exchange's director to modify its rules.
12Assemblywoman Aravella Simotas and state Sen. Liz Krueger are also drafting a bill to expand the list of qualifying events under state law to include pregnancy.
13"I don't think anybody can tell you that a pregnant woman should be denied health care because they missed an arbitrary enrollment period. Healthy expectant mothers and babies are what we should be focusing on," said Simotas (D-Queens).
14"I was pregnant in 2012, and I can't tell you how important it was to get prenatal care. It gave me peace of mind for my own health and that of the baby's."
15Because California and other states have already expanded to include events like returning from active National Guard duty, Krueger (D-Manhattan) said she does not expect a challenge from the federal government.
16However, insurers say it could lead to higher costs and premiums for everyone.
17Setting up a short window to enroll in health insurance stops people from signing up only when they become sick or injured and are about to get expensive care.
18"If individuals are incentivized only to sign up for coverage when they need care, then that has a significant impact on affordability for everyone," said Clare Krusing, a spokeswoman with America's Health Insurance Plans.
19Stringer said a change could actually save money in the long run, however - because prenatal care can lower the likelihood of a baby's admission to a neonatal intensive care unit or other medical needs.
