Labor board blocks effort to unionize college athletes (Yahoo Sports)

1CHICAGO (AP) -- The National Labor Relations Board on Monday blocked a historic bid by Northwestern University football players to form the nation's first college athletes' union, dealing a blow to a labor movement that could have transformed amateur sports.
2In a unanimous decision, the board said the prospect of union and nonunion teams in college could lead to different standards at different schools - from how much money players receive to how much time they practice - and create competitive imbalances on the field.
3The new ruling annuls a 2014 decision by a regional NLRB director in Chicago who said scholarship football players are employees under U.S. law and thus entitled to organize.
4But Monday's decision did not directly address the question of whether the players are employees.
5Some observers said the ruling effectively ends any chance to establish labor unions in college athletics.
6''This puts the nail in the coffin of organizing college players,'' said Ronald Meisburg, a former attorney for the NLRB and onetime board member.
7Tim Waters, of the United Steelworkers union, which helped bankroll the union drive, disagreed.
8''It is a bump in the road,'' Waters said.
9The face of the union-building effort, former Northwestern quarterback Kain Colter, also expressed disappointment.
10But he said the push for unionization had already pressured the NCAA to take athletes' grievances more seriously.
11''It turned out to be the right thing to do, and I don't regret it,'' Colter said.
12The labor dispute goes to the heart of American college sports, where universities and conferences reap billions of dollars by relying on amateurs who are not paid.
13In other countries, college sports are small-time club affairs, while elite youth athletes often turn pro as teens.
14The biggest factor in how it ruled, the board said, was the NLRB's jurisdiction, which extends only to private schools like Northwestern, the sole private institution in the Big Ten.
15The board repeatedly cited the need for standardization of rules and policies in sports and said giving the green light to just one team to collectively bargain would disrupt that uniformity.
16NLRB rules do not offer the losing side the option to appeal.
17But Ramogi Huma, a former linebacker at UCLA who worked closely with Colter, said he has not given up on bringing unions to college football.
18''The door's not closed,'' he said.
19The board seemed to leave open the possibly of taking up the unionization issue again if it involved other schools or if conditions change for Northwestern football.
20But Meisburg said the way the ruling highlights the challenges of organizing sports at private and state schools means the board is unlikely to consider another union petition from a college team.
21''I don't see those institutional problems going away,'' Meisburg said.
22Northwestern became the focal point of the labor fight in January 2014, when Colter announced plans to form the first U.S. labor union for college athletes.
23He appeared at a news conference for the College Athletes Players Association.
24Three months later, regional NLRB Director Peter Sung Ohr issued his decision, saying Northwestern football players should be able to unionize.
25A month later, players cast secret ballots on whether to unionize.
26Those ballots were sealed during the appeal and will now be destroyed without being counted.
27While NLRB decisions sometimes split along party lines, the three Democrats and two Republicans on the board all agreed.
28Under U.S. law, an employee is regarded as someone who receives compensation for a service and is under the direct control of managers.
29In Northwestern's case, Ohr concluded coaches are the equivalent of business managers and scholarships are a form of pay.
30On Monday, Waters criticized the NLRB for sidestepping the most sensitive question: Are scholarship players employees?
31''It's like they had a hot potato tossed into their laps, and they took a year and a half of deliberations and said, 'We're going to toss it back,''' he said.
32The board's decision was welcomed by the NCAA, which has been fighting lawsuits from former athletes over everything from head injuries to revenue earned from their likenesses in video games.
33In a statement, the Indianapolis-based NCAA portrayed the board's ruling as recognition that it's trying to improve conditions for athletes.
34''This ruling allows us to continue to make progress ... without risking the instability to college sports that the NLRB recognized might occur,'' it said.
35Northwestern's vice president for university relations, Alan Cubbage, also welcomed the ruling.
36The NCAA recently cleared the way for the five biggest conferences, including the Big Ten, to add player stipends to help athletes defray some of their expenses.
37Northwestern, the Big Ten and the NCAA all argued against the unionization effort, saying that lumping college athletes into the same category as factory workers would change amateur athletics for the worse.
38The specific goals of pro-union players included guaranteeing coverage of sports-related medical expenses for current and former players and reducing head injuries.
39AP College Football Writer Ralph D. Russo in South Bend, Indiana, and AP Sports Writer Jay Cohen in Chicago contributed to this report.
