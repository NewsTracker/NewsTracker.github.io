Boko Haram: changing media strategy for a wider conflict?

WHO gives green light to 15-minute Ebola test

1Lagos (AFP) - From grainy, amateurish footage and rambling, almost inaudible speeches to slick, expertly edited productions, Boko Haram's media strategy has undergone a transformation in recent months.
2The Nigerian militants this week published two videos that contrasted starkly with earlier efforts, using visual cues and a style resembling similar messages from the Islamic State jihadists.
3The marked change in both the form and content of the propaganda has led some to speculate on possible closer ties with the rebels in Syria and Iraq and whether an alliance could be forming.
4But experts said there was no concrete proof of direct links as yet, even if foreign jihadis operating in the Sahel region may have had previous dealings with IS militants.
5"The core leadership of Boko Haram and ISIS don't really have contact but a lot of their affiliates do for business purposes," said Yan St-Pierre, from the Modern Security Consulting Group.
6Boko Haram's control of the Lake Chad area -- on an established arms trafficking and smuggling route -- had forced foreign militants to enter talks with them, he told AFP.
7As a result, Boko Haram, an essentially home-grown rebel group with local aims at its inception, was increasingly open to international influence, he added.
8When Boko Haram first began distributing video messages, the quality was poor.
9The images, shot on commercially available cameras, were burnt onto CDs or copied onto memory sticks.
10They were then given to intermediaries and handed to media organisations in northern Nigeria, including AFP.
11The videos -- often ranting monologues from the group's leader, Abubakar Shekau, and up to an hour long -- were frequently corrupted and could not be played.
12Some improvements were seen from 2012, but the insurgents, who were known to have a media wing, still regularly sent uncompressed files of up to two gigabytes.
13With the Internet connection slow and intermittent in northern Nigeria, even the transmission of shorter, edited clips was time-consuming.
14Since December 2013, however, when militants overran a military base in Maiduguri, the use of more varied shots have become commonplace, including executions and harsh Sharia punishments.
15On Tuesday, Boko Haram published a video for the first time via Twitter using an Arabic-language account after several posts on YouTube.
16Both methods bypass the traditional media filter and allow the group more "control" of its message.
17Tuesday's Hausa-language video -- with English and Arabic subtitles and showing a more measured Shekau in sharp focus -- was accessible on video-sharing sites often used by jihadists.
18A short "trailer" on Thursday of Boko Haram fighters in action featured music, graphics and slow motion shots that recalled the Middle Eastern militants' propaganda.
19Boko Haram has also released branded photographs on the increasingly followed account, purportedly of an attack on the northeastern city of Gombe last Saturday, as well as statements.
20Aaron Zelin, a senior fellow at the Washington Institute for Near East Policy, said it was possible that Boko Haram's technical proficiencies had developed normally.
21"But it seems striking to me how much of a difference there is in the past four to five weeks from how it was doing previously," he said.
22"It seems to me there's some level of outside influence or trying to connect them to something."
23- Dog whistle?
24St-Pierre described the use of foreign jihadi symbols in Boko Haram videos as an "open application" for a more formal alliance to IS group or Al-Qaeda by factions using the name Boko Haram.
25Zelin suggested there was "heavy smoke but no specific fire" about foreign ties, against a backdrop of rumours that some Nigerian militants have pledged allegiance to Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi.
26Shekau has mentioned the IS group leader previously but has not pledged allegiance.
27Max Abrahms, a specialist in terrorist groups, from Northeastern University in Boston, Massachusetts, said he was less concerned with the technological advances.
28"Terrorist groups are simply keeping in tandem with current times. We shouldn't be surprised," he said by phone.
29But he cautioned against seeing any IS group influence on Boko Haram as one-way traffic.
30Shekau's repeated tirades against popular jihadist targets the United States and Israel, could be a dog whistle to gain the support -- or even mobilisation -- of extremists further afield.
31"Boko Haram has gained a lot of steam even with a very childish propaganda arm," he said in a separate email.
32"What social media seems to do is bring in some members from far-flung corners of the world, most of whom will remain sympathisers from afar."
1The test is a little less accurate than the so-called gold standard of lab assessment, but it doesn't need electricity or highly trained personnel to use it, WHO spokesman Tarik Jasarevic said.
2"Yesterday WHO assessed and listed (the) first antigen rapid test kit as being eligible for procurement to Ebola affected countries," Jasarevic said.
3"This is the first rapid antigen test that gives the results in 15 minutes."
4He described it as "a breakthrough" because it takes so little time.
5"Where possible, obviously results from this antigen rapid test should be confirmed by testing by blood sample using normal PCR tests," he added, referring to a DNA analysis to detect the disease.
6The test, manufactured by the U.S. firm Corgenix, "can correctly identify 92 percent of Ebola infected patients," he said.
7It entails putting a drop of blood on a paper strip, which then shows positive or negative, like a pregnancy test.
8The WHO's evaluation means that the test meets benchmarks for quality, safety and performance, Jasarevic said.
9He gave no details as to where and when the test will be introduced but did indicate it will likely be bought by a U.N. agency.
10Health watchdogs are keen for a fast test because the current PCR test, which looks for telltale genetic signatures, can take up to 24 hours.
11A simple but reliable test would help doctors in the field to quarantine people likely to have the virus and airports to test passengers before they get on a flight.
12"The new antigen test is not a game-changer, but it is another useful tool in the fight against Ebola," said Ben Neuman, a virologist at Britain's University of Reading.
13"The new test could help to quickly confirm outbreaks in remote areas without the need to send samples to a testing clinic and wait for results," he told the Science Media Center in London.
14"The new test isn't about saving the lives of infected people, but it can help in the long run by making it easier and quicker to detect Ebola outbreaks."
15Other prototype fast-track tests have been devised by scientists in Britain and France.
16As of Sunday, the WHO said 23,253 people had been infected with Ebola and 9,380 had died, the vast majority of them in Guinea, Sierra Leone and Liberia.
