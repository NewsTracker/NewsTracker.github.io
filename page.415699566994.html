Black box found at Germanwings plane crash site

1The victims included two babies, two opera singers and 16 German high school students and their teachers returning from an exchange trip to Spain.
2It was the deadliest crash in France in decades.
3The Airbus A320 operated by Germanwings, a budget subsidiary of Lufthansa, was less than an hour from landing in Duesseldorf on a flight from Barcelona when it unexpectedly went into a rapid descent.
4The pilots sent out no distress call and had lost radio contact with their control centre, France's aviation authority said, deepening the mystery.
5While investigators searched through debris from Flight 9525 on steep and desolate slopes, families across Europe reeled with shock and grief.
6Sobbing relatives at both airports were led away by airport workers and crisis counsellors.
7"The site is a picture of horror. The grief of the families and friends is immeasurable," German Foreign Minister Frank-Walter Steinmeier said after being flown over the crash scene.
8"We must now stand together. We are united in our great grief."
9It took investigators hours to reach the site, led by mountain guides to the craggy ravine in the southern French Alps, not far from the Italian border and the French Riviera.
10Video shot from a helicopter and aired by BFM TV showed rescuers walking in the crevices of a rocky mountainside scattered with plane parts.
11Photos of the crash site showed white flecks of debris across a mountain and larger airplane body sections with windows.
12A helicopter crew that landed briefly in the area saw no signs of life, French officials said.
13"Everything is pulverized. The largest pieces of debris are the size of a small car. No one can access the site from the ground," Gilbert Sauvan, president of the general council, Alpes-de-Haute-Provence, told The Associated Press.
14"This is pretty much the worst thing you can imagine," said Bodo Klimpel, mayor of the German town of Haltern, rent with sorrow after losing 16 tenth graders and their two teachers.
15The White House and the airline chief said there was no sign that terrorism was involved, and German Chancellor Angela Merkel urged reporters not to speculate on the cause.
16"We still don't know much beyond the bare information on the flight, and there should be no speculation on the cause of the crash," she said in Berlin.
17"All that will be investigated thoroughly."
18Lufthansa Vice-President Heike Birlenbach told reporters in Barcelona that for now "we say it is an accident."
19In Washington, the White House said American officials were in contact with their French, Spanish and German counterparts.
20"There is no indication of a nexus to terrorism at this time," said U.S. National Security Council spokeswoman Bernadette Meehan.
21Merkel, French President Francois Hollande and Spanish Prime Minister Mariano Rajoy were to visit the site Wednesday.
22French Interior Minister Bernard Cazeneuve said a black box had been located at the crash site and "will be immediately investigated."
23He did not say whether it was the flight data recorder or the cockpit voice recorder.
24The two devices -- actually orange boxes designed to survive extreme heat and pressure -- should provide investigators with a second-by-second timeline of the plane's flight.
25The voice recorder takes audio feeds from four microphones within the cockpit and records all the conversations between the pilots, air traffic controllers as well as any noises heard in the cockpit.
26The flight data recorder captures 25 hours' worth of information on the position and condition of almost every major part in a plane.
27Germanwings is low-cost carrier owned by Lufthansa, Germany's biggest airline, and serves mostly European destinations.
28Tuesday's crash was its first involving passenger deaths since it began operating in 2002.
29The Germanwings logo, normally maroon and yellow, was blacked out on its Twitter feed.
30Lufthansa CEO Carsten Spohr called it the "blackest day of our company's 60-year history."
31He insisted, however, that flying "remains after this terrible day the safest mode of transport."
32Germanwings said 144 passengers and six crew members were on board.
33Authorities said 67 Germans were believed among the victims, including the 16 high school students and two opera singers, as well as many Spaniards, two Australians and one person each from the Netherlands, Turkey and Denmark.
34Contralto Maria Radner was returning to Germany with her husband and baby after performing in Wagner's "Siegfried," according to Barcelona's Gran Teatre del Liceu.
35Bass baritone Oleg Bryjak had appeared in the same opera, according to the opera house in Duesseldorf.
36The plane left Barcelona Airport at 10:01 a.m. and had reached its cruising height of 38,000 feet when it suddenly went into an eight-minute descent to just over 6,000 feet, Germanwings CEO Thomas Winkelmann told reporters in Cologne.
37"We cannot say at the moment why our colleague went into the descent, and so quickly, and without previously consulting air traffic control," said Germanwings' director of flight operations, Stefan-Kenan Scheib.
38At 10:30, the plane lost radio contact with the control centre but "never declared a distress alert," Eric Heraud of the French Civil Aviation Authority told the AP.
39The plane crashed at an altitude of about 2,000 metres at Meolans-Revels, near the popular ski resort of Pra Loup.
40The site is 700 kilometres south-southeast of Paris.
41"It was a deafening noise. I thought it was an avalanche, although it sounded slightly different. It was short noise and lasted just a few seconds," Sandrine Boisse, the president of the Pra Loup tourism office, told the AP.
42Authorities faced a long and difficult search-and-recovery operation because of the area's remoteness.
43The weather, which had been clear earlier in the day, deteriorated Tuesday afternoon, with a chilly rain falling.
44Snow coated nearby mountaintops.
45French Interior Ministry spokesman Pierre-Henry Brandet said the crash site covered several acres, with thousands of pieces of debris, "which leads us to think the impact must have been extremely violent at very high speed."
46Search operations were suspended overnight and were to resume at daybreak, though about 10 gendarmes remained in the desolate ravine to guard the crash site, authorities said.
47Winkelmann said the pilot, whom he did not name, had more than 10 years' experience working for Germanwings and its parent airline Lufthansa.
48Florian Graenzdoerffer Lufthansa Spokesman for North Rhine Westphalia said the company had to cancel seven flights out of Dusseldorf because a number of crew members felt they were unfit to fly following news of the accident.
49"I can't tell you any details because this is a personal decision and in our business we have an agreement if a crew feels unfit to fly ... then we respect this," Graenzdoerffer said.
50The aircraft was delivered to Lufthansa in 1991, had approximately 58,300 flight hours in some 46,700 flights, Airbus said.
51The plane underwent a routine check in Duesseldorf on Monday, and its last regular full check took place in the summer of 2013.
52The A320 plane is a workhorse of modern aviation, with a good safety record.
53The last time a passenger jet crashed in France was the 2000 Concorde accident, which left 113 dead.
