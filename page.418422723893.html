Greece's Tsipras to sell austerity deal to reluctant party

1A man holds a tag queue position as he waits next to others pensioners outside the main gate of the national bank of Greece to withdraw a maximum of 120 euros ($134) in central Athens, Friday, July 10, 2015.
2Greece's Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras will seek backing for a harsh new austerity package from his party Friday to keep his country in the euro - less than a week after urging Greeks to reject milder cuts in a referendum.
3ATHENS, Greece (AP) -- Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras sought his left-wing party's backing on Friday for a new budget austerity package that is harsher than what he urged Greeks to reject in a vote just last week, but would provide the country will longer-term financial support.
4Government ministers signed off on the sweeping new measures, which include pension cuts and tax increases that are likely to inflict further pain on a people that have just emerged from a six-year economic depression.
5If approved, Greece would in return get a three-year package of loans worth nearly $60 billion as well as some form of debt relief.
6The package would be far larger than the 7.2 billion euros creditors had been offering to Greece during the previous five months of fruitless negotiations.
7Greece's latest proposal was sent to rescue creditors who will meet this weekend to decide whether to approve them.
8The new package of loans would be Greece's third bailout program since it lost access to financing from bond markets in 2010.
9In an unusual move, Tsipras is first seeking authorization from parliament to negotiate with the creditors based on the proposal in a vote Friday.
10He is essentially asking his Syriza party to sign off on measures that to many feel like a U-turn since more than 60 percent of voters opposed more austerity in last Sunday's referendum.
11Tsipras convened his party's lawmakers for closed-door discussions Friday morning before the parliamentary debate.
12The proposal was being debated at committee level Friday afternoon, and it was unclear when the full assembly debate would start.
13Votes are usually held at midnight, but they can go later.
14The coalition government has 162 seats in the 300-member parliament and pledged backing on a deal from a large section of opposition lawmakers.
15But failure to deliver votes from his own government could topple Tsipras' coalition.
16The proposals are to be discussed by eurozone finance ministers on Saturday, ahead of a summit of the European Union's 28 leaders Sunday.
17Though German officials would not be drawn on the merits of the Greek proposals, French President Francois Hollande said they are "serious and credible."
18France's Socialist government has been among Greece's few allies in the eurozone during the past months of tough negotiations.
19Jeroen Dijsselbloem, who chairs eurozone finance ministers' meetings, said the proposals were "extensive" but would not say whether he considered them sufficient.
20On Friday, Dijsselbloem will hold a conference call with the leaders of other key creditors, the EU's executive Commission, the European Central Bank and the International Monetary Fund.
21They are then expected to send their assessment of the proposal to the eurozone finance ministers later Friday.
22As the government inched closer to a deal to ensure Greece doesn't crash out of Europe's joint currency, some Greeks were furious at the deep spending cuts in the proposals.
23"If this is Europe, then we don't want this Europe," said Aristidis Dimoupulos, a marketing professor in Athens.
24"If this is the eurozone, we don't care if we go out or in. If in this life we'll be slaves, it's better to be dead."
25"I don't know. The chances are fifty-fifty" for a deal, said Athens resident Omiros Fotiadis.
26Meanwhile, in Greece, banks remained shut since the start of last week and cash withdrawals were restricted to 60 euros ($67) per day.
27Although credit and debit cards work freely within the country, many businesses are refusing to accept them and insisting on cash-only payments.
28All money transfers abroad, including bill payments, require special permission.
29Alternate Finance Minister Dimitris Mardas said the banks would be gradually restored to operation.
30They are currently due to remain closed through Monday, at which time he said a new order would be issued expanding what transactions can be carried out.
31Experts say it is unlikely that, even in the event of a deal, the limits on cash withdrawals and transfers will be lifted completely for some time.
32Rallies backing and opposing the government were planned in central Athens for Friday evening.
33Syriza had resisted a new loans-for-austerity deal, arguing the country is too weak to endure it, with a quarter of the labor force out of work and a growing number living in poverty.
34But it was forced to resume talks with the creditors as the Greek banks faced the prospect of collapse within days if the country did not receive a new rescue package.
35Athens finally issued its proposals late Thursday.
36In return for the new package, the government said it would seek debt relief - a notion gaining ground internationally despite reluctance in Germany.
37"The realistic proposal from Greece will have to be matched by an equally realistic proposal on debt sustainability from the creditors. Only then will we have a win-win situation," European Council President Donald Tusk said.
38On Thursday, German Finance Minister Wolfgang Schaeuble also said the possibility of some kind of debt relief would be discussed over coming days.
39But in a note of caution, he added: "The room for maneuver through debt re-profiling or restructuring is very small."
40Anna Psaroudakis in Athens, Angela Charlton in Paris and Mike Corder in the The Hague, Netherlands, contributed to this report.
