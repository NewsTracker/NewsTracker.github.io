2BONNE TERRE, Mo.
3(AP) - A Missouri death row inmate has been executed for the shooting death of a sheriff's deputy, after the U.S. Supreme Court and the state's governor declined to spare the 74-year-old who attorneys said had a diminished mental capacity because of a brain injury.
4Cecil Clayton was put to death Tuesday by lethal injection after Gov. Jay Nixon denied a clemency request and the nation's high court turned aside appeals claiming Clayton was mentally incompetent.
5The Missouri Supreme Court, in a 4-3 ruling, already had declined to intervene, with the court's majority concluding last weekend there was no evidence Clayton wasn't capable of understanding his circumstances.
6The U.S. Supreme Court was also divided, with four judges saying they would have granted a stay.
7As the execution began at 9:13 p.m., Clayton appeared to breathe heavily for about a minute, and the sheet over his right leg quivered slightly.
8His mouth slowly went agape, but there was no other movement before he was pronounced dead eight minutes later.
9In his final statement before the injection began, Clayton said only, "They brought me up here to execute me."
10The claim of Clayton's diminished mental capacity stemmed from a 1972 sawmill accident that his attorneys argued cost him about 8 percent of his brain, including one-fifth of the frontal lobe portion governing impulse control and judgment.
11Combined with his reported IQ of 71 and reading skills of a fourth-grader, Clayton's attorneys insisted psychiatric evaluations over the past decade concluded that Clayton didn't understand the significance of his scheduled execution or the reasons for it, making him ineligible to be put to death under state and federal law.
12"Cecil Clayton had - literally - a hole in his head," Elizabeth Unger Carlyle, one of his attorneys, said in a statement.
13"Executing him without a hearing to determine his competency violated the Constitution, Missouri law, and basic human dignity. ... The world will not be a safer place because Mr. Clayton has been executed."
14James Castetter, a brother of the victim, Christopher Castetter, told reporters after the execution that "the great state of Missouri did not kill an innocent man."
15"Cecil Clayton's actions are what put him to death," said James Castetter, who sat next to three other siblings who also watched Clayton die.
16"We know this execution isn't going to bring Chris back, but it destroys an evil person that would otherwise be walking this earth."
17Mike O'Connell, a Department of Corrections spokesman, said Clayton was not offered a sedative, honoring a request by Clayton's attorneys.
18Clayton, who was Missouri's oldest death row inmate, "was cooperative" when escorted to the execution chamber and was strapped to the gurney, O'Connell said.
19Attorney General Chris Koster said in a statement that Clayton "paid the ultimate price for his terrible crime."
20In their 11th-hour appeals, Clayton's attorneys argued that his deteriorating mental health left him convinced his conviction was a plot against him and that God would rescue him from a death sentence at the last minute, "after which time he will travel the country playing the piano and preaching the gospel."
21Clayton was convicted of gunning down Christopher Castetter, a sheriff's deputy in rural southwest Missouri's Barry County.
22Castetter was 29 and a father of three when he went to a home near Cassville on Thanksgiving Eve 1996 to check on a suspicious vehicle report.
23Authorities said Clayton shot Castetter once in the forehead while the deputy was in his car, which was found against a tree, its engine racing and wheels spinning.
24Clayton's brother had testified that the sawmill accident led to Clayton's breakup from his wife, alcohol abuse and violent outbursts.
25The lethal injection, Clayton's attorneys said, was "sure or very likely to cause excruciating or tortuous pain and needless suffering" in light of his dementia.
26"If Missouri proceeds with its scheduled execution of Mr. Clayton, it will be conducting an unregulated experiment on a human subject, as there are no studies that support (the prison system's) use of Missouri's lethal injection protocol on an individual suffering from severe brain damage," the appeals on Clayton's behalf argued.
27Clayton's claims of mental incompetence mirrored those of Ricky Ray Rector, who was executed in 1992 in Arkansas for the shooting death 11 years earlier of a police officer.
28Rector was 40 when he was put to death, having failed to sway then-Gov. Bill Clinton - campaigning for what at the time later became his first presidential term - that he was left brain-damaged by a self-inflicted bullet wound prior to his arrest.
29The execution was Missouri's second this year after the state's record 10 in 2014.
30It was also the first Missouri carried out in the evening after decades of having them just after midnight.
