18Mark A. Drumbl is the Class of 1975 Alumni Professor at Washington & Lee University, School of Law, where he also serves as director of the Transnational Law Institute.
19He is author of "Reimagining Child Soldiers in International Law and Policy."
20The views expressed are his own.
21(CNN)Images last week from an ISIS video appearing to show a child executing a hostage were horrific.
22The very idea of the "cubs of the caliphate," as the Islamic State of Iraq and Syria dubs them, is stomach-churning.
23But ISIS is far from the first or only group to treat children in such a wretched way.
24There are tens of thousands of child soldiers under age 18 around the world, from South America to Africa to Southeast Asia to recent conflicts in the Balkans.
25The Kony2012 video that went viral, for example, featured children in the Lord's Resistance Army in northern Uganda.
26Boko Haram, in Nigeria, also dreadfully abuses children.
27Children associated with armed forces and armed groups typically serve as porters, sentries, spies, cooks or cleaners.
28Many are sex slaves.
29Some, however, carry weapons on their own, exercise authority over others and commit atrocities against adults and children alike.
30There is, though, an oft-overlooked point that also highlights one of the difficulties we face if we are going to tackle the problem of child soldiers.
31Despite the dominant image of these soldiers as boys, it is estimated that globally as many as 40% of child soldiers are girls.
32This point, as much as any other, underscores why it is time to rethink how we view child soldiers.
33If we are going to develop effective responses to child soldiering, the international community needs to move past two dominant images.
34These two dominant images cast child soldiers either as passive, helpless victims who have been abused into serving as tools of war or, on the other hand, as bloodthirsty fighters with considerable autonomy who kill with sadism and zeal.
35As I have outlined in my book, both images are unhelpfully crude generalizations.
36Children who end up in armed groups arrive there from along a number of paths and for divergent reasons.
37And children are, of course, individuals.
38Hence, they act in different ways once they are in these armed groups.
39Some children are abducted, drugged, brainwashed and brutalized.
40Many refuse to kill, some kill to survive, others murder to thrive.
41Some are brought to armed groups by their own families.
42Others join willingly, often traveling long distances of their own volition.
43They deceive their loved ones in order to enlist.
44This latter point was underscored most recently by the case of three British schoolgirls who are alleged to have left the United Kingdom last month in an effort to join ISIS.
45The girls -- all midteens -- appear to have shown significant enterprise in traveling from London to Turkey and then to the border with Syria.
46Why do these misconceptions about child soldiers matter?
47Well, in the case of girl soldiers, the fact is that existing policies stemming from stereotypes serve them particularly badly.
48By focusing on boys, the specific challenges of reintegrating girl soldiers can be overlooked, notably girls who suffer sexual violence before and after recruitment.
49Should a civilian who has been tortured, raped or had a limb amputated by a child be denied a remedy to what would otherwise be a war crime?
50If someone's family has been wiped out by a group of child soldiers, should he or she be refused justice because of the age of the perpetrators?
51The international community is reluctant to prosecute child soldiers.
52While this move may be readily understandable, is it productive to insulate such children from involvement in restorative processes such as truth commissions, community service or traditional reintegration ceremonies?
53How should a trained soldier fight a child in the event that nightmare comes to pass?
54What are the appropriate rules of engagement?
55Rules must of course respect international law and seek to protect children, but they must also recognize the realities of self-defense.
56While mental health therapies are important after conflict, it is also important not to underestimate the resilience of young people and their ability to bounce back from adversity if policymakers are equally willing to invest more in their education, training and conflict resolution, among other things.
57The crude images of psychological devastation (the victim) or psychological deviance (the demon) divert attention away from what former child soldiers may most acutely need.
58With this in mind, if the international community really wants to assist, then it must move beyond clicktivism, shock-value and convenience.
59It must be willing to tackle difficult truths head on.
60Long-term prevention and healing require coherent answers to tough questions.
61We can't simply deny or wish away awkward realities.
62Once a conflict ends, child soldiers have just as much right as adults to be reintegrated into communities.
63Society owes them a chance to contribute to peaceful transition and build their lives.
64But how is it best to do that?
65For certain child soldiers, a healthy path to transition may involve them fulfilling some kind of obligation to society.
66Dismissively (and patronizingly) telling them their violent actions weren't their fault isn't helpful.
67Neither, of course, is locking them away in prison -- this is especially the case for children who have suffered horrid brainwashing as is the case for ISIS and Boko Haram.
68All this suggests the shortsightedness of bunching child soldiers -- and the challenges they create -- into a single category that doesn't take fully into account the radically different nature of their individual experiences in conflict.
69Instead, we should recognize the diversity among child soldiers -- not helpless victims or demented perpetrators, but as individuals with promise, potential, entitlements and reciprocal obligations.
70Until we make these changes to our thinking, we will hinder our own efforts at preventing something we all ultimately want to stop: children ending up in war.
