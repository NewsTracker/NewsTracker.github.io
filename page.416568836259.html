James Holmes' Colorado theater shooting jurors weep at photos of victims

1Jurors in Colorado sobbed at the sight victims' photographs on the first day of James Holmes' murder trial as District Attorney George Brauchler said in his opening statement that the gunman entered the Aurora movie theater in 2013 with the intent to murder as many people as possible to make himself feel better.
2The remarks by Brauchler marked the start of a long-awaited, lengthy and emotionally wrenching trial to determine if he'll be executed, spend his life in prison, or be committed to an institution as criminally insane.
3Holmes, 27, pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity to 166 counts, including first-degree murder, attempted murder and an explosives offense, after the mayhem he caused in suburban Denver on July 20, 2012.
4It remains one of America's deadliest shootings, and that Holmes was the lone gunman has never been in doubt.
5He was arrested at the scene, along with an arsenal of weapons on his body and in his car.
6His fate depends on whether a jury agrees that he was unable to know right from wrong because of a mental illness or defect three years ago, when he slipped into the midnight Batman premiere, unleashed tear gas and marched up and down the aisles, firing at people who tried to flee.
7'Through this door is horror.
8Through this door are bullets, blood, brains and bodies.
9Through this door, one guy who thought as if he had lost his career, lost his love life, lost his purpose, came to execute a plan,' said Brauchler, standing before a scale model of the theater.
10'Four-hundred people came into a boxlike theater to be entertained, and one person came to slaughter them,' the prosecutor said.
11Brauchler immediately went on the offensive, challenging the defense's insanity claims.
12The prosecutor declared that two separate mental health evaluations carried out by forensic psychiatrists found James Holmes to be sane.
13It was the first public word on what different psychiatrists determined after examining the former neuroscience student.
14'Both of them said the same thing.
15That guy was sane,' said Brauchler.
16Citing the results of two exams administered to Holmes, including a full IQ text, the prosecutor said that psychiatrists have concluded that the promising neuroscience graduate student at the University of Colorado School of Medicine has a superior intellect.
17'He is smart,' Brauchler said of Holmes calmly sitting at the defense table in front of him.
18Relying on a large TV screen hanging on the wall opposite the jury box to present snippets of evidence, including passages from Holmes' journal and clips from videotaped interviews with the suspect, George Brauchler methodically detailed how the aspiring researcher spiraled out of control over the course of a year.
19Quoting from transcripts of Holmes' text message and email exchanges with his parents and his ex- girlfriend, Brauchler described a socially awkward, uncommunicative man who was struggling with his studies and had his heart broken by his first love.
20Brauchler also quoted extensively from Holmes' journal, referred to as 'the notebook,' in which the defendant started meticulously planning a mass shooting back in 2012.
21In his diary, Brauchler said Holmes talked about his long-standing hatred of mankind and laid out his plan to attack a movie theater.
22The prosecutor said early on, Holmes ruled out an attack at an airport because he did not want to be associated with terrorism.
23After setting his heart on a movie theater just 4 miles from his home, Holmes drew detailed maps to pick the perfect theater at the multiplex: some had too many exits, others had too few.
24Eventually, he narrowed it down to theaters 9 and 10.
25He then began buying up weapons, additional magazines and enough body armor to cover himself from head to toe.
26A month before the massacre, Holmes drove several times to a remote, unsupervised gun range to practice firing weapons, including on moving targets.
27In preparation for the mass killing, Holmes dyed his hair red and purchased contact lenses to make his pupils look bigger.
28He would later tell a psychiatrist that he changed his appearance so he would be remembered because someone who looks different stand out more.
29He noted that the hue of his hair had nothing to do with the character of Joker from the Batman franchise, as initially thought.
30Prosecutor Brauchler described for the jurors how each victim was killed in the theater at the hands of James Holmes and showed them poster-size images of the casualties, leaving some of them in tears.
31Inside the silent courtroom, the district attorney detailed how Holmes fired on men, women and children, including 6-year-old Veronica Moser-Sullivan who was struck four times.
32Brauchler said he would show a photo of Moser's body later in the trial, but didn't show it during opening statements.
33He said they should only have to look at that photo once.
34Holmes watched the screen with the photographs but showed no visible reaction.
35There was a box of tissues at the feet of Holmes' parents but they didn't use them during the opening statements.
36Defense attorneys were beginning their opening statement.
37Brauchler described how Holmes shot some people in the head and face, riddled others with multiple bullets and hunted down those who tried to escape.
38'I shot at people trying to get away,' Brauchler quoted Holmes as telling a psychiatrist after the fact.
39But according to DA Brauchler, that tragic night in July had a silver lining.
40'The greatest mass murder in our memory was intended to be far worse but four providential things kept that from happening,' Brauchler said.
41One was a neighbor who decided not to open the door of Holmes' apartment filled with booby traps where a stereo was blasting and call the police instead.
42The second was the fact that Holmes 'fumbled' one of the two canisters of tear gas used inside the theater.
43A jammed rifle was the third.
44The fourth providential thing, Brauchner noted, were the 'heroes' who rushed to the rescue of the victims in the blood-spattered theater.
45Brauchler concluded his emotional two-hour presentation, interspersed with personal stories of each of the 12 slain theater goers, with an impassioned plea addressed to the men and women of the jury.
46Holmes, 27, appeared calm throughout the prosecutor's opening statement on Day 1 of a trial to determine if he'll be executed, spend his life in prison, or be committed to an institution as criminally insane.
47His clown-like red hair grew out long ago, and the bushy beard he wore before the trial was gone, too.
48Instead, Holmes appeared with a neat haircut and trimmed beard, wearing a button-down shirt.
49Discreetly, so that others can't see, Holmes was restrained by a harness under his light-blue shirt and khaki pants, tethered to the floor of the courtroom where his trial is expected to take months.
50His fate depends on what the jury learns about his mental state three years ago, when he slipped into a midnight Batman premiere, unleashed tear gas and marched up and down the aisles, firing at people who tried to flee.
51Holmes acknowledges killing 12 people and wounding 70 more inside the packed theater, but has pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity.
52At around noon Monday, Judge Carlos Samour Jr, addressed the jury, explaining what will be expected of them and detailing what will happen during the course of the trial.
53Before issuing the instructions, the presiding judge told the 12 jurors, plus 12 alternates, he has ordered the media not to identify them by name or contact them or their friends or family members.
54Judge Samour reminded the jurors tasked with deciding Holmes' fate that the burden lies on the prosecution to prove beyond reasonable doubt that the gunman was sane during the commission of the crime.
55The 24 people will not know if they are jurors or alternates until deliberations are about to begin.
56At that point, the remaining alternates will be dismissed.
57Prosecutors were to spend two hours making their opening arguments on the first day of the trial proper, before a presentation of the same length by public defenders Daniel King and Katherine Spengler.
58The defense on Monday was still fighting the judge's decision to let the jury see girsly images of the crime scene, including a 90-minute silent video of the inside of the theater.
59Witnesses said Holmes threw smoke bomb-type devices before opening fire randomly with weapons, including an AR-15 military-style rifle, a 12-gauge shotgun and a .40-caliber pistol.
60Unlike most other states, Colorado puts the burden on prosecutors, who must convince jurors beyond a reasonable doubt that Holmes was sane.
61Colorado has already spent millions seeking that verdict, managing an outsized number of victims, witnesses and more than 85,000 pages of evidence.
62Experts say Holmes faces long odds.
63Insanity defenses are successful in only 25 per cent of felony trials nationally, even less so in homicides.
64'Lay people tend to think of people with mental illness as extremely dangerous, and that also influences jurors, especially if someone has killed someone,' said Christopher Slobogin, who teaches law and psychiatry at Vanderbilt Law School.
65Winning a trial on mental-health grounds is rare, but then again, so are jury trials for mass shooters.
66Most are killed by police, kill themselves or plead guilty.

69Based on that, Holmes 'faces some pretty long odds,' he said.

71His victims included a 6-year-old girl, two active-duty servicemen, a single mom and an aspiring broadcaster who had survived a mall shooting in Toronto.
72Several died shielding friends or loved ones.
73Nearly three years passed as both sides filed hundreds of motions in legal debates over capital punishment and insanity pleas.
74Prosecutors will argue that the once-promising doctoral candidate in neuroscience plotted and planned for months, amassing guns, ammunition, tear gas grenades and enough chemicals to turn his dingy apartment into a potentially lethal booby trap that could have caused even more carnage.
75They'll ask jurors to find him guilty, and if so, sentence him to death rather than life without parole.
76If jurors decide instead that Holmes was insane at the time of the shooting, he would be committed indefinitely to a state psychiatric hospital.

79Defense attorneys have objected to the results of the second one.
