At busy crossing, pedestrians need passports to enter Mexico

1SAN DIEGO (AP) " Walking into Mexico at the nation's busiest border crossing with the United States is no longer an uninterrupted stroll for foreigners.
2Starting late Wednesday, pedestrians going to Tijuana from San Diego at the San Ysidro crossing must choose between a line for Mexicans who get waved through, and a line for foreigners who must show a passport, fill out a form and " if staying more than a week " pay 322 pesos, or roughly $20, for a six-month permit.
3About a dozen foreigners stood in line Wednesday night, directed by English-speaking agents to six inspection booths where they got passports stamped.
4It took about 10 minutes from start to finish.
5Travelers have long followed similar protocol at Mexican airports, but the new border procedure marks a big change at land crossings that weren't designed to question everyone.
6Pedestrians and motorists have generally entered Mexico unencumbered along the 1,954-mile border with the United States.
7"This is about putting our house in order," said Rodulfo Figueroa, Mexico's top immigration official in Baja California state, which includes Tijuana.
8The changes, which have been in the works for years, come as Donald Trump has surged to the top of the Republican field in the U.S. presidential race.
9He has insisted that Mexico sends criminals to the U.S. and pledges to build a border wall at Mexico's expense.
10For Mexico, it is a step toward closing an escape route for American criminals who disappear in Mexico.
11Border inspectors will tap into international criminal databases.
12Motorists will see no change, and if lines get too long, officials will also wave pedestrians through.
13More than 120 Americans expelled from Mexico this year while living in Baja California had arrest warrants in the U.S., according to Figueroa, delegate of the National Migration Institute.
14Some ordered to leave last year were on the FBI's most-wanted list.
15But authorities say benefits extend beyond stopping unwanted visitors.
16A recent hurricane stranded twice as many Americans in Cabo San Lucas than U.S. authorities thought were there, Figueroa said, and registering as a foreigner would have made it easier to identify those who needed help.
17Figueroa said Mexico can initially process about 1,000 foreigners daily, up from about 50 currently.
18"If the line becomes clogged up, we will just let everybody through," Figueroa said.
19"If we can't check everybody, we won't."
20Figueroa said San Ysidro is believed to be the first U.S. land crossing to have a separate line for foreigners to show passports and that it will serve as a model for others as they are upgraded.
21Aurora Vega, a spokeswoman for the National Migration Institute, referred questions to other departments.
22Officials at the Foreign Relations Department and Mexican Embassy in Washington had no immediate comment.
23About 25,000 pedestrians (and 50,000 motorists) cross daily at San Ysidro to work, shop and play but it is unclear how many are foreigners in Mexico.
24U.S. Customs and Border Protection says about one-third entering San Diego are U.S. citizens, one-third are U.S. legal residents and the rest are from other countries, largely Mexico.
25An unknown number have dual citizenship or residency in the U.S. and Mexico.
26Both countries have long wrestled with logistical hurdles of stopping people going to Mexico by land.
27The U.S. occasionally stops motorists and pedestrians as they leave " mainly to check for guns and cash " but it doesn't have a system to record exits like at airports, seen by many as a significant shortcoming in border security.
28Previous efforts to question more foreigners entering Mexico met resistance in Tijuana, whose economy partly relies on Americans who visit restaurants, beaches, doctors and dentists.
29Lines to enter the United States at San Ysidro have exceeded four hours.
30Roberto Arteaga, who has made tacos, shined shoes and sold tickets for private bus and van rides in Southern California during 28 years as a street vendor near the border crossing, says requiring passports and imposing a fee for longer stays sends the wrong message.
31"We should be welcoming," he said during a lull in business Tuesday.
32"This will hurt Tijuana's economy."
33Other crossers said the move was overdue.
34"Anything to keep the country safer is much better for everyone," Cynthia Diaz of Oceanside, near San Diego, said as she stood in line to return to the U.S with her niece, who visited Tijuana for a root canal.
35"It's safer for us on the other side too."
