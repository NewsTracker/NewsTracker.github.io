Bodies of teens from Germanwings crash arrive in hometown

1Photo: Hearses carrying coffins with remains of victims of the Germanwings crash drive past the Joseph-Koenig-Gymnasium high school in Haltern.
2The bodies of German schoolchildren killed in the Germanwings plane that prosecutors say was deliberately crashed in the French Alps have arrived in their grieving hometown.
3Tearful friends and loved ones lined the route in the north-western town of Haltern, as the procession of hearses - 14 white vehicles carrying the teenagers and two black cars with their teachers - rolled past their high school.
4Many of the onlookers tossed white roses as church bells rang out.
5The victims' remains arrived from Dusseldorf, where a special flight operated by Lufthansa touched down late Tuesday repatriating the remains of a total of 44 Germans killed in the March 24 disaster.
6The pupils had been returning from an exchange trip to Spain when their flight from Barcelona to Duesseldorf came down in the Alps.
7Investigators say that 27-year-old German co-pilot Andreas Lubitz, who had a history of severe depression, intentionally downed the plane.
8A total of 72 Germans were on board the doomed Airbus A320.
9Lufthansa is the parent company of budget airline Germanwings.
10Last week the families of some of the Haltern victims angrily complained to Lufthansa after they were told the repatriation would be delayed due to problems with the issuing of death certificates.
11Lufthansa later said the flight would go ahead as initially planned, with further victims to be transferred to their home countries in the coming weeks.
12A member of a team of therapists assigned to the families after the crash, Sybille Jatzko, said the loved ones should have been given a representative to collect information on the repatriation and other logistical and bureaucratic issues.
13"That is one thing that clearly went very wrong" in the crash's aftermath, she told the daily Berliner Zeitung newspaper, noting that many of the grieving parents simply lacked the strength to contend with the paperwork.
14Brice Robin, the French prosecutor who is leading the probe into the crash, was due to meet Thursday with relatives of some of the other victims to discuss the identification and repatriation of remains.
