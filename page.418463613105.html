2Greece faces 'difficult' bailout talks BRUSSELS - Greece's financial future remained up in the air Sunday, after European finance ministers adjourned a meeting to decide whether to accept economic reforms proposed by the country in exchange for a bailout.
3In a statement, Jeroen Dijsselbloem, president of the Eurogroup of finance ministers, said the talks were not finished and that the discussions would continue at 11 a.m. local time Sunday.
4"We had an in-depth discussion on Greek proposals. The issue of credibility and trust was discussed and also, of course, the financial issues involved," he said.
5Leaving the European Union building where the talks were being held, Finland's Finance Minister Alexander Stubb told reporters that the discussions were "progressing well."
6It followed reports that Germany's hard-line finance minister is demanding that the cash-strapped nation make more concessions to get a new bailout or temporarily withdraw from the 19-nation eurozone.
7European finances ministers met here Saturday to debate Greece's latest plan to impose new austerity measures at home in exchange for a new three-year loan totaling $59 billion and relief from prior loans that Greece is unable to repay.
8The finance ministers' meeting will be followed Sunday by a summit of European leaders to determine whether Greece should get new financing.
9As Greece's teeters on the verge of bankruptcy, German Finance Minister Wolfgang Schauble suggested the new austerity plan approved by Greece's parliament early Saturday morning doesn't go far enough, German newspaperFrankfurter Allgemeine Sonntagszeitungreported.
10Schable said Greece should take a "time out" from the eurozone for at least five years to get its financial house in order, the newspaper said.
11Schauble, a key player in the talks and the leading voice for making Greece adhere to tough terms to get a third bailout, predicted "exceptionally difficult negotiations."
12Under Schauble's approach, Greece would remain a member of the European Union while it restructures its debt, the German paper reported.
13A German finance ministry position paper said Athens' latest plans "leaves out important central reform areas to modernize the country and to bring economic growth and sustainable development over the long term," FAS reported.
14In an alternative ministry proposal, the newspaper added, Greece would rapidly improve its proposals and transfer $56 billion of assets to a fund to pay down its debt.
15The German Finance Ministry declined to comment on the reports.
16As ministers arrived for the critical Saturday meeting, their tone was guarded.
17"We are not there yet," said Dutch finance minister Jeroen Dijsselbloem, the eurozone's top official on the Greek bailout plan.
18"Both on substance and there is of course a major issue of trust. Difficult meeting."
19Christine Lagarde, head of the International Monetary Fund, said before the talks got underway that "We have to make a lot more progress."
20The emergency talks convened just hours after Greece's parliament approved a motion for pension cuts and tax increases so it can receive the new loan, avert bankruptcy and keep its banking system from collapsing.
21The new austerity plan being reviewed by the 19 eurozone finance ministers alongside officials from the International Monetary Fund, European Central Bank and European Commission - Greece's creditors - resembles the demands the creditors previously made.
22It was overwhelmingly rejected by Greek voters in a national referendum last Sunday.
23The creditors have lent Greece nearly a quarter-trillion dollars since 2010, as Greece has struggled through a prolonged economic slump.
24Talks on a new financing plan bogged since the election of Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras' leftist Syriza party in January.
25It vowed to resist new austerity measures but capitulated Friday after the lenders refused to make further concessions.
26Moody's rating service estimated that Greece's banks could run out of money by Sunday and that even if some kind of deal is reached by then, the country- with huge deficits and a 25% jobless rate - faces a long road back to financial stabilization.
27"Regardless of a political agreement on Greece this weekend, we believe that implementation risks will still remain high and the conditions under which external finance is provided will be harder to absorb because of the political and social challenges in the country," the credit firm said in a note sent to investors on Friday.
