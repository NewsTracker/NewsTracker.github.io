13A week after the devastating earthquake, life is limping back to normal in Nepal.
14PAUWATHOK, Nepal (AP) - At the entrance of this destroyed mountain village, a wooden sign stands, cobbled together from debris of homes flattened by Nepal's devastating earthquake.
15Its message: "WE NEED HELP. PLEASE HELP."
16A steep winding road leads up to the ruins of the small village of Pauwathok, perched on a ridge about 3,600 feet (1,100 meters) above sea level.
17It's just 30 miles (50 kilometers) east of Kathmandu, Nepal's capital.
18But villagers say not one government official, not one soldier has visited since the massive quake struck a week ago, underscoring just how unprepared and overwhelmed Nepal's government has been.
19Early Saturday, a convoy of covered trucks approached Pauwathok.
20The trucks were apparently transporting aid and escorted by Nepalese police carrying automatic weapons.
21Hungry residents ran toward the road.
22The trucks were not stopping.
23"Are we invisible to you?!" a voice among the crowd screamed as the trucks rode slowly up a hill and out of sight.
24One week after the strongest tremor to hit impoverished Nepal in eight decades, aid has been slow in reaching those who need it most.
25In many places it has not come at all.
26U.N. humanitarian officials said Saturday they are increasingly worried about the spread of disease.
27They said more helicopters are needed to reach isolated mountain villages like Pauwathok, which were hard to access even before the quake.
28The true extent of the damage from the magnitude-7.8 earthquake is still unknown as reports keep filtering in from remote areas, some of which remain entirely cut off.
29The U.N. has estimated the quake affected 8.1 million people - more than a quarter of Nepal's 28 million people.
30The government's latest number, reported Sunday, is 7,040 dead, with little hope of finding survivors.
31Nepal has been shaken by more than 70 aftershocks, and its people remain on edge.
32One brief aftershock Sunday afternoon shook this village's only paved road, triggering screams from residents who began to run, then stopped when the tremor eased.
33Pauwathok is located in the district of Sindupalchok, where more deaths have been recorded than anywhere else in Nepal - 2,560, compared to 1,622 in Kathmandu.
34The U.N. says up to 90 percent of the houses in Sindupalchok have been destroyed.
35Rajaram Giri said he was sitting under a large tree when the big quake hit April 25.
36Pauwakthok was immediately engulfed in clouds of red dust.
37When it cleared, Giri saw only ruins.
38Only a handful of the village's roughly 80 homes remain habitable.
39Mostly made of brick and mud and sticks, they were either completely leveled or damaged beyond repair.
40Some families now sleep under makeshift shelters of debris that they constructed from their own ruined homes - torn roofs and boards, whatever was left.
41Others sleep under tarpaulins - obtained only after residents sat in the middle of the road Friday and blocked an aid convoy that eventually relinquished 30 of them, said Giri, who took one for his family.
42"We only have the clothes on our backs," he said.
43"The rest has been buried under the rubble."
44Volunteers have begun stepping into the void.
45Late Saturday, Pauwathok's appeal for help was finally answered.
46A truck full of rice, noodles and medical supplies pulled up.
47Everyone crowded round.
48The truck had been organized by a teacher who went for help to Kathmandu, where he collected donations from charities and friends.
49They brought the aid themselves.
50"The government has not been able to cater to the needs of everyone, so it's better if everybody can pitch in," said Supral Raj Joshi, who helped bring the aid.
51"It's not that the government isn't trying, it's that they've not been able to reach everywhere."
52Six other civilians, several of whom worked with street kids in Kathmandu, also came to deliver medical aid Saturday.
53They dressed the wound of an elderly lady, Tilamaya Bharti, who had lost part of a finger when a stone crushed it during the tremor.
54They also gave Bharti painkillers - the first she'd had since doctors in a nearby town conducted a hasty amputation last week and sent her away with nothing to ease the pain.
55While the situation in Pauwathok is grave, some remote parts of Nepal appear to have suffered even more.
56David O'Neill of U.K. International Search and Rescue said a team from his group had driven as far as it could, then walked for hours into six remote villages to assess them.
57The team reported that the villages had been so badly hit, 80 percent of their inhabitants had died.
58"Everything has been flattened," O'Neill said.
59The people there apparently died not in the original quake, but in a major aftershock the next day, he said.
60O'Neill spoke in Chautara, a destroyed town in Sindhupalchok.
61His team had hoped to reach the remote areas by helicopter, but none were available, so they were returning to Kathmandu.
62"We definitely need more helicopters," Ertharin Cousin, executive director of the U.N.'s World Food Program, told The Associated Press in the village of Majuwa, west of the capital.
63Aid agencies have been using Majuwa as a staging ground to get supplies deeper into mountainous areas.
64"This is one of the poorest places on Earth," Cousin said.
65"If the global community walks away, the people of this country will not receive the assistance that is required for them to rebuild their lives."
66That need remains great.
67Nepal Information Minister Minendra Rijal said the government has supplied only 29,000 tents to 400,000 displaced people.
68"The earthquake has caused unimaginable destruction," said Rownak Khan, a U.N. Children's Fund senior official in Nepal.
69"Hospitals are overflowing, water is scarce, bodies are still buried under the rubble and people are still sleeping in the open. This is a perfect breeding ground for disease."
70Medicine, medical equipment, tents and water supplies are needed now, he said.
71"We have a small window of time to put in place measures that will keep earthquake-affected children safe from infectious disease outbreaks - a danger that would be exacerbated by the wet and muddy conditions brought on with the rains," Khan said.
72Laxi Dhakal, a Nepal Home Ministry official, said hopes of finding survivors had faded.
73"Unless they were caught in an air pocket, there is not much possibility," he said.
74In Jalkeni, a village not far from Pauwathok, the road is lined with mounds of broken wood and stone, the remains of destroyed homes.
75On top of one mound sat a woman cradling a young girl.
76The mound - surrounded by a pile of dusty rocks, a broken TV and shredded clothes - used to be Sunita Shrestha's two-story home.
77"No one has come to help us yet," said Shrestha, as the sun beat down.
78"I don't know if they ever will."
79Associated Press writers Binaj Gurubacharya in Kathmandu and Katy Daigle in Majuwa, Nepal, contributed to this report.
