16With their country struggling to stave off financial collapse, Greek officials restarted talks with skeptical creditors on a new rescue deal, but showed up Tuesday without the concrete proposals their European counterparts had demanded.
17Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras' situation is urgent: Without a deal, Greece's banks could go bust within days, the first step in the country's potential exit from the euro currency union.
18Its banks won't reopen until Thursday at the earliest after the European Central Bank refused to increase assistance.
19Eurozone finance ministers, including Greece's newcomer Euclid Tsakalotos, met in Brussels ahead of a full summit of leaders later Tuesday.
20The meetings were unlikely to be decisive, though, after officials said Tsakalotos had not brought written proposals with him.
21Rather, they said, he made a presentation and discussed key issues.
22The three officials spoke only on condition of anonymity because they were not authorized to speak publicly.
23One big sticking point in the talks is Greece's demand that the terms of its bailout loans be made easier.
24European officials are split on the issue, with lead eurozone lender Germany still reluctant.
25The International Monetary Fund last week called for European states to accept longer repayment rates and lower interest rates on their loans to Greece.
26Many economists say that Greece's debt burden, at almost 180 percent of annual GDP, is unsustainable for a country its size.
27Getting a new rescue deal for Greece is urgent and becoming more so by the day.
28Greek banks are running out of cash even after the government shut them last week and placed limits on how much depositors can withdraw or transfer.
29Normal commerce is now impossible in Greece.
30Small businesses, lacking use of credit cards or money from bank accounts, were left to rely on cash coming from diminishing purchases from customers.
31But Greeks are holding on tight to what they have.
32And suppliers are demanding that businesses pay cash up front.
33Giorgos Kafkaris, a 77-year-old pensioner, was among Greeks standing in line to withdraw cash at an Athens ATM on Tuesday.
34Tsipras made a big win in Sunday's bailout referendum, when 61 percent of Greek voters rejected measures creditors had proposed last week in exchange for loans.
35In a sign he may be willing to compromise, Tsipras appointed a new finance minister to lead talks with creditors.
36Tsakalotos, a 55-year-old economist, has replaced Yanis Varoufakis, who constantly clashed with his peers.
37"I won't hide from you that I am very nervous and very anxious," Tsakalotos said after being sworn in Monday.
38"I am not taking over at the easiest moment in Greek history."
39Investors were cautious on Tuesday, with stock markets down and the euro 0.7 percent lower.
40Greece's stock market remains closed since last week amid the bank closures.
41Greece has been granted two bailout programs worth a total of 240 billion euros ($266 billion) in loans from other eurozone countries and the International Monetary Fund.
42But the spending cuts and tax increases demanded as a condition for the loans have hit growth, causing an economic depression and pushing unemployment to 25 percent.
43The government, meanwhile, has been slower than hoped in making the economy more competitive and selling state assets to raise money.
44James Nixon, chief European economist at Oxford Economics, said there's "a narrow trajectory from here that sees an emboldened Greek parliament accepting the need for reform in return for a debt write-down."
45Costas Kantouris in Athens contributed to this report.
