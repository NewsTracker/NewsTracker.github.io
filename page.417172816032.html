More people moving from Australia to New Zealand than in the other direction for first time in 24 years

1The late New Zealand prime minister Robert Muldoon once quipped that the annual exodus of Kiwis to Australia raised the average IQ of both countries.
2Australians called that sour grapes - but now New Zealand is having the last laugh.
3Last month, for the first time in 24 years, more people headed east across the Tasman Sea than in the opposite direction.
4Long viewed as Australia's poor relation, with lower wages, less sunshine and a duller cultural life, New Zealand is booming.
5Paul Bloxham, chief economist in the region for the bank HSBC, has dubbed it the "rock star economy".
6As its fortunes have soared, Australia's have dived, thanks to the tailing off of a decade-long mining boom fuelled by Chinese demand for raw materials.
7Particularly painful has been a slump in the price of iron ore, Australia's most lucrative export.
8Much to the mirth of New Zealanders, their larger neighbour has also been plagued by political instability, with former Labor prime ministers Kevin Rudd and Julia Gillard serially stabbing each other in the back and the conservative incumbent, Tony Abbott, narrowly avoiding being ditched by his own party.
9By contrast, Mr Abbott's Kiwi counterpart, John Key, is serving his third term.
10Before being elected in 2008, Mr Key made a campaign advertisement in Wellington's Westpac Stadium, pointing to the 34,500 empty seats as equivalent to the number of New Zealanders leaving every year.
11He vowed to turn that around - and now he has.
12In April, according to new government figures, 100 more people crossed The Ditch - as the Tasman Sea is known colloquially - in an easterly direction than those going west.
13Some were Australians seeking better job prospects and a lower cost of living, while others were New Zealanders moving home.
14It's a big turnaround for the two close neighbours, whose citizens have the right to live and work in each other's countries, but one that could be seen for some time.
15Two years ago, a net 34,000 New Zealanders moved to Australia.
16That fell to 11,000 last year and to 1,900 in the latest statistics.
17Some say the Kiwis began to fall out of love with Australia when former prime minister John Howard abolished their long-standing right to housing, health and unemployment benefits.
18Fairly or unfairly, the perception for many in Australia was that New Zealanders had availed themselves liberally of such benefits - so liberally that those arriving in Sydney were known as "Bondi bludgers [scroungers]".
19While the New Zealand government has welcomed the population boost, not all locals are quite so enthusiastic.
20Aucklanders complain that the influx of people is driving house prices in the city through the roof.
