10In recent days, Putin called his Navy's Northern Fleet to full combat readiness in exercises in Russia's Arctic.
11Russia's Defense Ministry says 76,000 soldiers, 41 warships and 15 submarines are taking part in the exercises, which stretch from the Arctic to the Pacific.
12NATO scrambled jets Tuesday from Estonia and Lithuania to intercept a group of 11 Russian aircraft.
13Eight fighters and three military transports were among the Russian aircraft in international airspace over the Black Sea.
14All except one had switched off their transponders, according to a Pentagon spokesman.
15The Russian planes landed in Kaliningrad two and a half hours after they were first detected by Estonian radar.
16Kaliningrad, the tiny Russian enclave in the Baltics, is also where the Kremlin announced this week that it plans to deploy state-of-the-art missiles - known as Iskander missiles - along the border with Poland and Lithuania.
17NATO officials expressed concern about the sudden or "snap" exercises the Russian military launched over the past two years.
18"The recent Russian practice of calling snap exercises is of serious Russia has conducted about a dozen snap exercises over the past two years. Russia's takeover of Crimea was done under the guise of a snap exercise," a NATO military officer told Fox News.
19NATO has not conducted these kinds of exercises since the end of the Cold War.
20The U.S. military says Russia has been jamming the airspace over Eastern Ukraine, while continuing live fire drills on Ukraine's border.
21The U.S. Army, meanwhile, is flexing its muscles with a year-long training exercise in Eastern Europe dubbed Operation Atlantic Resolve.
22It comes as the U.S. Army's European command announced it was sending dozens of Stryker combat vehicles and 600 troops on an 1100-mile convoy through six countries in the Baltics starting March 21.
23"This road march is significant because it demonstrates to the Russians that the American military can maneuver across their front," said retired Army Major General Robert Scales, a Fox News Contributor.
24Some 750 U.S. military vehicles, including tanks and artillery, began arriving last week in Riga, Latvia.
25"We're going to demonstrate that same resolve as we lean forward and demonstrate to Russia and to Putin that we are not broken in our efforts to stand together through this type of aggression," Major General John R. O'Connor, in charge of moving the armored vehicles into Riga, told NATO TV.
26Russia announced earlier this week it would deploy long-range, nuclear-capable strategic bombers to Crimea and state of the art missiles to Kaliningrad, but the Pentagon said it had not seen any movement of these weapon systems.

2In a delay that some say may have cost lives, the World Health Organization resisted calling the Ebola outbreak in West Africa a public health emergency until the summer of 2014, two months after staff raised the possibility and long after a senior manager called for a drastic change in strategy, The Associated Press has learned.
3GENEVA (AP) - In the aftermath of the world's biggest outbreak of Ebola, the World Health Organization acknowledged it was too slow to act, blaming factors including a lack of real-time information and the unprecedented nature of the epidemic.
4But an investigation by The Associated Press has revealed the U.N. health agency knew from the start how unusual the outbreak was.
5WHO officials privately floated the idea of declaring an international health emergency in early June, more than a month before the agency maintains it got its first sign the outbreak merited one - in late July - and two months before the declaration was finally made on August 8, 2014.
6WHO blamed its slow response partly on a lack of real-time information and the surprising characteristics of the epidemic.
7In fact it had accurate field reports - including scientists asking for backup - and it identified the unprecedented features of the outbreak.
8The agency was also hobbled by a shortage of funds and a lack of clear leadership over its country and regional offices.
9Politics appear to have clouded WHO's willingness to declare an international emergency.
10Internal emails and documents suggest the U.N. health agency was afraid of provoking conflict with the Ebola-stricken countries and wary that a declaration could interfere with the economy and the Muslim pilgrimage to Mecca.
11An Ebola-infected WHO consultant in Sierra Leone violated WHO health protocols, creating a rift with Doctors Without Borders that was only resolved when WHO was thrown out of a shared hotel.
