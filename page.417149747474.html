Lonely man finds a home in Islam, and then in Islamic State

1This image provided by New Zealand 3 News/MediaWorks via APTN shows Mark Taylor, who converted to Islam and is now thought to be in Syria, as he reads the Quran in his native New Zealand.
2Taylor, 42, began visiting a mosque in the city of Hamilton some years ago, and resurfaced 2014 in Syria.
3Authorities estimate only a half-dozen New Zealanders have traveled to Syria to fight with the Islamic State group.
4It's unclear how they've become radicalized, although Taylor's case might provide a clue: He's acknowledged listening to the sermons of the late U.S.-born al-Qaida preacher Anwar al-Awlaki.
5WELLINGTON, New Zealand (AP) - Before he left New Zealand, Mark Taylor spoke with a local television network.
6"I've been lonely all my life," he said.
7"Been rejected by people. Used by people. Abused by people."
8He said that by converting to Islam, he felt he'd become part of a community.
9"Because the good thing about being Muslim is you can walk into virtually any mosque and pray," he told TV3 News, "and people would welcome you."
10When Taylor, 42, began visiting a mosque in the city of Hamilton about four years ago, worshippers there saw him as lonely, a little lost and possessing a childlike view of the world.
11They didn't see any anger or radicalism in the security guard who said he was a former Army soldier.
12And so they were not only appalled but also worried for him when he resurfaced last year in Syria, describing himself as an adventurer and posting social media messages under the Twitter handle "Kiwi Jihadi."
13Authorities estimate only half-a-dozen New Zealanders have traveled to Syria to fight with Islamic State.
14When Taylor, who also goes by the names Muhammad Daniel and Abu Abdul-Rahman, arrived in Hamilton, mosque elder Mustafa Farouk said he and others tried to help.
15Farouk said they allowed Taylor to park the old truck he lived in near the mosque and offered to find him accommodation and mental-health services.
16But he said Taylor wanted to remain independent and declined their offers.
17If Taylor had radical thoughts, Farouk said, he kept them to himself.
18"The only thing he ever talked about was wanting to get married," Farouk said.
19"He didn't seem like he could pose any danger to anyone at all. More that he might need someone to look after him, really."
20How Taylor managed to leave New Zealand - despite being closely monitored - has raised questions about the effectiveness of the country's intelligence and immigration agencies.
21He had been arrested in 2009 in proximity to al-Qaida militants in the Pakistani region of Waziristan.
22He told authorities he was there looking for a bride, and was later released.
23Diplomatic cables leaked to Wikileaks show Australian authorities recommended in 2010 that Taylor be placed on a watch list because of his "demonstrated connections" with al-Awlaki.
24In 2011, New Zealand Prime Minister John Key said a number of unspecified restrictions had been placed on Taylor.
25But Farouk said he managed to leave a couple of years ago for Indonesia, where he lived and worked before traveling to Syria.
26New Zealand authorities, citing privacy concerns, declined to answer questions from the AP about how Taylor managed to depart, or to say whether he had served as a soldier.
27His Twitter profile says he is "in Dawlah Islam, Islamic state," though his current status is unclear.
28In April, he posted a video calling on Islamic State supporters in New Zealand and Australia who have had their passports confiscated to commit terrorist acts at home, "even if it means you have to stab a few police officers or soldiers."
