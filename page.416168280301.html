Hillary Rodham Clinton's campaign to focus on economic security, opportunity

1CHICAGO: Hillary Rodham Clinton's presidential campaign will center on boosting economic security for the middle class and expanding opportunities for working families, while casting the former senator and secretary of state as a "tenacious fighter" able to get results, two senior advisers said on Saturday.
2They provided the first preview of the message that Clinton planned to convey when she launches her long-anticipated campaign on Sunday with an online video.
3Until now, the former first lady has offered only hints of what would drive her if she were to run a second time for the White House.
4Clinton, who lost the 2008 nomination to Barack Obama, will skip a flashy kickoff rally in favor of conversations with voters about the economic needs of middle class families and the next generation.
5Clinton appears unlikely to face a formidable primary opponent, though a handful of lower-profile Democrats such as former Maryland Gov. Martin O'Malley have said they are considering campaigns.
6Should she win the nomination, Clinton would face the winner of a Republican primary field that could feature as many as two dozen candidates.
7Senators Rand Paul of Kentucky and Ted Cruz of Texas have already announced their candidacies.
8Senator Marco Rubio of Florida is expected to enter the race in Miami on Monday, and former Florida governor Jeb Bush, the brother and son of former presidents, is also likely to run.
9The strategy described by Clinton's advisers has echoes of Obama's successful 2012 re-election campaign.
10He framed the choice for voters as between Democrats focused on the middle class and Republicans wanting to protect the wealthy and return to policies that led to the 2008 economic collapse.
11The advisers said Clinton will argue that voters have a similar choice in 2016.
12Clinton also intends to sell herself as being able to work with Congress, businesses and world leaders.
13That approach could be perceived as a critique of Obama.
14He has largely been unable to fulfill his pledge to end Washington's intense partisanship and found much of his presidency stymied by gridlock within Congress.
15The Clinton advisers spoke on condition of anonymity in order to discuss her plans ahead of Sunday's announcement.
16People familiar with the plans say Clinton will travel to Iowa and other early-voting states to hold small events with residents in the days after the video's release.
17By campaigning heavily in the early-voting states, which influence the rest of the state-by-state battle for party nominations, Clinton hopes to avoid making the same stumbles she did in 2008, when she entered the race as a US senator and a heavy favorite only to be upset by Obama in Iowa's lead-off caucuses.
18In New York on Saturday, at the final event put on by "Ready for Hillary," a group not connected with her campaign that's worked for the past few years to stoke excitement for it, enthusiastic supporters joined elected officials and local party leaders to celebrate the launch to come.
19"After she left the State Department she could have slipped into grandmother-hood, but people want to call her back into public service," said Jarret Berg, 29, a Democratic staffer in the New York legislature.
20"It's time for her."
21Clinton is not expected to roll out detailed policy positions in the first weeks of her campaign.
22Advisers said she planned to talk about ways families can increase take-home pay, the importance of expanding early childhood education and making higher education more affordable.
23It's not yet clear whether that will include a noticeable break with Obama on economic policy.
24Republicans have hammered Obama's approach as anti-business and insufficient in the wake of the recession.
25The White House says the economy has improved significantly in recent years.
26The unemployment rate fell to 5.5 per cent in March, but manufacturing and new home construction slowed, cheaper gas has yet to ignite consumer spending and participation in the labor force remains sluggish.
27Clinton will enter the race as the overwhelming favorite for her party's nomination.
28Still, her team has said her early strategy is designed to avoid appearing to take that nomination for granted.
29The early events will include discussions at colleges, day care centers and private homes, and stops at coffee shops and diners.
30After about a month of such events, Clinton will give a broader speech outlining more specifics about her rationale for running.
31Clinton's husband, Bill, and daughter, Chelsea, are unlikely to appear at her early events.
32Bill Clinton, the former two-term president, said recently that he wanted to play a role as a "backstage adviser" in his wife's campaign.
