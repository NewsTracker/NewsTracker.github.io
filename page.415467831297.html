West Indies win to close on quarters

Cyclone Pam: Aid flights arrive in storm-ravaged Vanuatu

1The UAE crumbled to 26-5 in Napier as Windies captain Jason Holder took 4-27.
2But Nasir Aziz and Amjad Javed exploited some ragged bowling to add a record-tying 107 for the seventh wicket and lift the associates to 175 all out.
3Johnson Charles and Jonathan Carter struck fifties as the Windies eased home by six wickets in 30.3 overs.
4In reaching their target inside 36.2 overs, West Indies went through on net run rate, although their passage was not confirmed until Pakistan beat Ireland by seven wickets in Sunday's final Pool B contest in Adelaide.
5West Indies' performance did little to suggest they will be a match for Brendon McCullum's unbeaten New Zealand side in Wellington on 21 March.
6The inconsistency that has blighted their campaign was again in evidence as a wayward spell from Kemar Roach helped the UAE get back into the game after Holder and Jerome Taylor had blown away their top order with some high-class pace bowling.
7There was also a show of dissent from former captain Darren Sammy when he reacted badly to being pulled from the attack after one over and earned himself a talking-to from Holder.
8By that point, the UAE were in the midst of an impressive recovery in which Aziz and Javed equalled their own team's record for the highest World Cup seventh-wicket stand, set against Ireland in Brisbane in February.
9Aziz top-scored in his first one-day innings, while Javed reached 50 for the first time with seven fours and a huge six, when he planted Taylor into the stands over long-on.
10Their partnership was finally ended in the 41st over and the Windies wrapped up the tail efficiently as Taylor dismissed captain Mohammad Tauqir for his third wicket of the day.
11With Chris Gayle sidelined by a back injury, Charles was handed an opportunity at the top of the West Indies order, and he kick-started the run chase with a four and a six in the first over on his way to a 34-ball half-century.
12Although Dwayne Smith and Marlon Samuels once again posted low scores, and the experiment of moving Andrew Russell up the order did not pay off, Carter found some fluency to guide them home with his maiden ODI fifty.
13West Indies captain Jason Holder: "The ball came out really well for me at the start. The pitch was a bit slow but it swung and bounced a bit for me.
14"I'm extremely pleased with the way the guys came out and played. We needed to win by a certain margin. We had to show positive intent. We knew we had to finish the game inside 36 overs and we did it.
15"New Zealand would be a big test. They are playing some very good cricket."
16UAE captain Mohammad Tauqir: "When West Indies put us in to bat first, they took good advantage of the bowling conditions.
17"At 46-6 it was difficult to recover but it was a good effort by Amjad Javed and Nasir Aziz. Amjad been very consistent throughout the tournament and has been a positive for us.
18"It was a decent performance for us in this tournament, a good learning experience and it has been very enjoyable."

1Baldwin Lonsdale, visibly weary and red-eyed from lack of sleep, said in an interview that he and other top government officials were preparing to return home later Monday from Sendai, in northeastern Japan, where they were attending a disaster conference.
2Lonsdale said that the limited information he was able to get from home showed six people confirmed dead on Port Vila after the category 5 typhoon smashed across the Vanuatu archipelago.
3He said information from other islands was not available because most communication links were still not working.
4"This is a very devastating cyclone in Vanuatu," Lonsdale said.
5"I term it as a monster, a monster. It's a setback for the government and for the people of Vanuatu. After all the development that has taken place, all this development has been wiped out."
6The president said because of the break in communications, even he could not reach his family.
7"We do not know if our families are safe or not. As the leader of the nation, my whole heart is for the people, the nation."
8Officials in Vanuatu had still not made contact with outlying islands and were struggling to determine the scale of devastation from the cyclone, which tore through early Saturday, packing winds of 270 km/h and leaving a trail of destruction and unconfirmed reports of dozens of deaths.
9Two people have been confirmed dead in the capital, Port Vila, with another 20 injured there, said Paolo Malatu, co-ordinator for the National Disaster Management Office.
10Power remains out across the tiny Pacific archipelago and people on many of the outer islands have no access to running water or outside communications.
11Earlier, Chloe Morrison, a World Vision emergency communications officer, said Vanuatu's disaster response office told her agency that at least eight people died.
12She had also heard reports of entire villages being destroyed in more remote areas.
13The confusion over the death toll is due largely to a near-total communications blackout across the country.
14With power lines and phone circuits down, officials in the capital had no way of knowing what the scope of the damage was on the outer islands, where the storm scored a direct hit.
15"We haven't been able to communicate outside Port Vila," Malatu said.
16"At this point, the damage is severe and we haven't had figures of how many houses destroyed. ... It's really bad, it's really bad."
17"The damage to homes and infrastructure is severe," Malatu said.
18"The priority at the moment is to get people water, food and shelter."
19He said there was major damage to many government buildings, and bridges were down outside the capital, making travel by vehicle impossible even around the main island of Efate.
20Lonsdale appealed for more help.
21"Tarpaulins, water containers, medical needs, gathering tools, construction tools, all these are very important right now," the president said.
22The first shipments of aid landed in Vanuatu on Sunday as authorities declared a state of emergency and global relief agencies geared up to help.
23Aid flights, including a New Zealand military Hercules aircraft carrying eight tonnes of supplies and an initial team, began to land on Sunday as Port Vila's airport partially reopened.
24Australia sent two military aircraft including one with medical experts, search and rescue teams and emergency supplies while a UN team was also due in Port Vila on Sunday with members drawn from as far away as Europe.
25Oxfam's country manager Colin Collett van Rooyen said Vanuatu's outlying islands were particularly vulnerable.
26"We are talking about islands that are remote and really small, with none of what we would call modern infrastructure," he said.
27"We anticipate that that will go higher," he added, referring to the death toll.
28Sune Gudnitz, regional head of the UN Office for the Co-ordination of Humanitarian Affairs, said he was particularly worried about the densely populated volcanic island of Tanna in the south.
29Nothing had been heard from the island, which has a population of about 29,000 people, he said.
30Australia has promised $5 million A in aid, while Britain, which jointly ruled Vanuatu with France until independence in 1980, has offered up to $2.95 million US in assistance.
31The World Bank said it was exploring a swift insurance payout to the government.
32"We will also be deploying humanitarian supplies to provide support for up to 5,000 people in the form of water, sanitation and shelter," Australian Foreign Minister Julie Bishop told reporters in Perth.
33Officials are planning to head to the outer islands Monday in helicopters, small planes and military aircraft to get a better sense of the destruction, Malatu said.
34Debris is scattered over a building in Port Vila, Vanuatu in the aftermath of Cyclone Pam.
35"People are really upset and it's really hard, just because for the last couple of years, we haven't received a really big cyclone like this one," said Isso Nihmei, Vanuatu co-ordinator for the environmental and crisis response group 350.
36"Most people right now, they are really homeless."
37Vanuatu (in red) is a sprawling country of 83 islands and 260,000 people.
38Structural damage across Port Vila was extensive, Nihmei said, with the majority of homes severely damaged or destroyed.
39Some residents began cleaning up what was left of their wrecked houses and checking on family members.
40Relief workers, meanwhile, were trying to get victims to temporary shelters as fast as possible, Nihmei said.
41Hannington Alatoa, head of the Vanuatu Red Cross Society, said flyovers by New Zealand and Australian relief teams showed much of the country had been "flattened."
42At least half of the population, or about 130,000 people, has been affected, Alatoa said in Sendai, Japan, where he and other Vanuatu officials were attending a UN conference on disaster risk reduction.
43He had no accurate information on the numbers left homeless.
44"No trees, no foliage, no iron structures standing on the western part of Tanna," Altoa said, referring to one of Vanuatu's southern islands.
45"Trees blocked the roads. ... People are in great need of water."
46Highlighting the difficulty of getting information from some areas, Alatoa said he has been unable to contact his family from Sendai.
47"I tried yesterday to call them from my hotel room, but I didn't get through," he said.
48"It's difficult. I'm praying that they are able to cope with the situation they have on the ground right now."
49Residents awoke to much calmer weather Sunday after many hunkered down in emergency shelters for a second straight night.
50Many people who have ventured out from 23 emergency shelters around Port Vila have found their homes damaged or blown away altogether, Morrison of World Vision said.
51Teetering trees and downed power lines have made parts of the capital hazardous.
52She said communications have been so problematic that her aid group hasn't yet been able to account for many of its own 76 staff members on the islands.
53For anybody who wasn't in a secure shelter during the cyclone "it would have been a very, very tough time for them," she said.
54Vanuatu has a population of 267,000 people spread over 65 islands.
55About 47,000 people live in the capital.
56UNICEF estimated that 54,000 children were among those affected by the cyclone.
57The cyclone has already caused damage to other Pacific islands, including Kiribati and the Solomons.
58Authorities in New Zealand are preparing for Cyclone Pam, which was forecast to pass north of the country on Sunday and Monday.
