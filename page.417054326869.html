US 'deeply concerned' by Egypt's death penalty decision for Mohamed Morsi

1The US is "deeply concerned" about an Egyptian court decision to seek the death penalty for the former president Mohamed Morsi, a State Department official said on Sunday.
2The US criticism follows condemnation from Amnesty International and Turkish president, Recep Tayyip an, after the court ruling on Saturday against the deposed leader and 106 supporters of his Muslim Brotherhood in connection with a mass jail break in 2011.
3The ruling against Morsi is not final until 2 June.
4All capital sentences are referred to Egypt's top religious authority, the Grand Mufti, for a non-binding opinion, and are also subject to legal appeal.
5"We are deeply concerned by yet another mass death sentence handed down by an Egyptian court to more than 100 defendants, including former president Morsi," the State Department official said, speaking on condition of anonymity.
6"We have consistently spoken out against the practice of mass trials and sentences, which are conducted in a manner that is inconsistent with Egypt's international obligations and the rule of law," he said.
7The official, noted, however, that Saturday's death sentence ruling was "preliminary".
8Morsi and his fellow defendants were convicted on charges of killing and kidnapping policemen, attacking police facilities and breaking out of jail during the uprising.
9Morsi, who became Egypt's first freely elected president in 2012 after the ousting of president Hosni Mubarak in a popular uprising, has said the court is not legitimate, describing proceedings against him as part of a coup by former army chief Abdel Fatah al-Sisi in 2013.
10Despite US lawmakers' concerns that Egypt is lagging on democratic reforms, Egypt remains one of Washington's closest security allies in the region.
11Relations cooled after Morsi was overthrown by the military nearly two years ago, but ties with Sisi, his successor, have steadily improved.
