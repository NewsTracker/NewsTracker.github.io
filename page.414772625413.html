1When Republicans swept into power last November, they promised a new era of productivity and discipline that would break four years of gridlock.
2"America's New Congress," they called it.
3But far from striking a bold contrast with the last two terms of stalemate, congressional Republicans have quickly run into familiar obstacles, including partisan paralysis and party infighting.

5With money for the Department of Homeland Security set to run out at midnight Friday, Republican leaders in Congress struggled to convince their followers to fund the huge department now and fight President Obama's immigration policies later.

7But as members of Congress go home for their first extended break since Republicans took control Jan. 6, they have few other achievements.
8Only two bills have become law - one a leftover from last year that funds a terrorism insurance program important to real estate developers, the other a noncontroversial measure to address mental health problems among veterans.
9That compares with six new laws at this point in 2007, when Democrats came to power in both chambers for the final two years of President George W. Bush's tenure.
10The new Republican majority, said one lawmaker granted anonymity to speak openly about their work, is like the dog that caught the car - still figuring out what to do next.
11Rather than begin the year with an agreed-upon strategy or comprehensive agenda for the party in power, the 114th Congress opened last month with a loosely defined set of legislative priorities.
12Even the Keystone bill was passed only after an exhaustive process in the Senate.
13During the course of the debate, more amendments were discussed and dispensed with than in the entire previous year when Democrats were in charge.

18When lawmakers return to Washington in a little more than a week, they'll once again face something Republicans had hoped to leave behind them - the possibility of a partial government shutdown.
19The shutdown threat stems from a legislative trap Republicans set for themselves last December after Obama moved to shield up to 5 million people in the country illegally from the possibility of deportation.
20At the behest of conservatives eager to undo the new policy, lawmakers agreed not to fund the Department of Homeland Security, which handles immigration among other duties, past Feb. 27.
21Republican leaders struggled for most of the last six weeks to find a way forward before the Homeland Security money runs out.
22They initially hoped a few Senate Democrats who have qualms about Obama's immigration plans might join them on a bill that would fund the department and overturn the president's executive action.
23But Democrats have remained united in blocking any such plan.
24Republicans, with 54 senators, would need six Democrats to break ranks to end a filibuster of the bill.

33House Majority Leader Kevin McCarthy (R-Bakersfield) wrote to colleagues Friday.
