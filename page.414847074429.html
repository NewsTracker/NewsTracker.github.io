1Measured by volume, the right-wing backlash has diminished severely, as great roaring waves of furious anger have given way to irregular ripples of discontent.
2But measured by its content, very little has changed.
3Conservatives are talking much less than they used to about Obamacare, but they are saying more or less the same things, treating the law as a costly and obvious failure.
4What's most striking is how oblivious most of them remain not only to measures of the law's success, but even to the broad parameters of its objectives.
5To take a typical example, here is Stephen Moore, chief economist at the Heritage Foundation, making his case, such as it is, that Obamacare has failed to meet its cost targets.
6Perhaps the most revealing aspect of Moore's column is the fact that, five years after its passage, the chief economist of the most influential conservative think tank in the United States lacks even a passing familiarity with its fiscal objectives.
7Obamacare has two fiscal goals.
8The first is to pay for its expanded coverage with a combination of spending cuts and higher taxes, so that the net effect is to reduce the deficit.
9The second, and more ambitious, goal is to change the incentives of the health-care system to gradually bring down health-care inflation (a goal health-care wonks have called "bending the curve").
10Moore's column, which I am excerpting in its entirety, makes clear he does not understand either target.
11If there were a contest for the biggest lie in Washington over the past 30 years, it would be hard to compete with President Obama's boast that he would put 30 million more Americans on Medicaid and Obamacare subsidies, and this would reduce the budget deficit.
12That's got to be right up there with President Clinton declaring, "I did not have sexual relations with that woman."
13A new Congressional Budget Office report has blown the lid off the Obama whopper fib.
14Keep in mind what Moore is claiming here.
15Obama promised that the ACA would reduce the deficit, and according to Moore, the Congressional Budget Office has a new report showing this promise was false.
16If true, this would be enormous news.
17In fact, no such finding exists.
18CBO originally estimated that Obamacare would reduce the deficit.
19It correspondingly estimated for the next several years that repealing the law would increase the deficit.
20CBO stopped issuing cost estimates of the overall law.

22The Congressional Budget Office reports that through the first four months of fiscal 2015, federal spending is rising at an 8.2 percent clip.
23Most components of spending are relatively flat and national defense outlays - the most important function of government - are falling.
24Still, one area accounts for almost the entire budget blowout: Obamacare.
25Medicaid spending is up a stratospheric 23 percent so far this year thanks to massive new enrollments.
26In addition, the $7 billion in Obamacare "exchange subsidies" so far this year brings the rise in costs of the "Affordable" Care Act to nearly 30 percent.

58Note that the colloquial definition of welfare is income support for working-age people who aren't working, as opposed to government subsidies in general.

60Medicaid, unlike traditional welfare, is not a program that gives people income support as a substitute for wage income.
