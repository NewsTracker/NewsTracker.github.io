Damaged heritage sites reopened in Nepal

1Nepal has reopened most of the cultural heritage sites that were damaged in a pair of devastating earthquakes, hoping to lure back foreign tourists.
2The April 25 and May 12 quakes killed more than 8,700 people and damaged hundreds of thousands of buildings in Nepal, including old temples, palaces and other historical structures that are popular with tourists.
3Six of the seven UNESCO World Heritage sites closed after the quakes have now been reopened, Tourism Minister Kripasur Sherpa said.
4"We are urging people to come to Nepal for holiday to help Nepal rebuild," Sherpa said in Bhaktapur, a city filled with ancient temples that is among the reopened heritage sites.
5The sites were closed due to the damage from the quakes, but also over concerns about the safety of tourists.
6Before the quakes, about 1,000 foreigners a day visited Bhaktapur, located just east of Kathmandu.
7Tourists fled Nepal after the first quake, but it also is currently rainy season, a time when relatively few foreign visitors are usually around.
8Bhesh Narayan Dahal, chief of Nepal's Department of Archaeology, said that measures have been taken to ensure that tourists are safe, and that there are plans to provide safety helmets for visitors in some places.
9Some 741 heritage structures were damaged in the quakes, and it will take at least $18 million to rebuild and restore them, Dahal said.
