Greek debt crisis: Greece's banks in a battle to win back trust

1People are given priority tickets by a National Bank branch manager as they wait to enter the bank in Athens on Monday.
2Reopening the doors of Greek banks Monday was just the beginning.
3Restoring trust in the banks, so that customers will be willing to deposit their money again, is one of the most important tasks that Greek and eurozone officials will face as they try to get the economy moving again.
4But undoing the damage wrought in recent months will take money and time, as well as agreement among quarreling eurozone nations.
5And even if Greece and its creditors can reach a new bailout deal, it might not be until next year that Greek banks are stable enough that the government can lift restrictions on movements of money abroad, analysts said.
6Customers at a Eurobank branch in Athens shortly after it reopened on Monday.
7Greeks' nervousness about keeping their money in the banks is understandable as long as it is only emergency loans from the European Central Bank that are keeping those lenders from collapsing.
8And, unlike in the United States, where federal deposit insurance protects individual bank accounts up to $US250,000, there is no adequately financed system to insure eurozone depositors against losses if a bank fails.
9European leaders could do a lot to bolster confidence if they declared that they would protect deposits up to 100,000 euros, in line with new European Union rules that are in the process of being phased in, said Nicolas Veron, a senior fellow at Bruegel, a research organisation in Brussels.
10"If you had such an announcement, you could imagine the capital controls being lifted very quickly," Veron said, referring to the Greek restrictions on money transfers.
11If there is no such declaration - and so far there has not been - ''there is every likelihood the capital controls will have to be kept in place," he said.
12Some elements of a rescue plan for Greek banks are beginning to take shape.
13The European Central Bank, in its role as overseer of eurozone banks, will conduct a review of the financial condition of the four largest Greeks banks around the end of the summer.
14That is the first step in determining what kind of help they need to survive.
15Greek banks passed a similar review last year, but that was before they became collateral damage in Greece's political and economic turmoil.
16As businesses have suffered from slumping sales and many people have received only portions of their salaries or pensions, borrowers have fallen behind in loan payments.
17More than 1 in 3 outstanding loans in Greece was delinquent at the end of March, and the number has probably grown since then.
18At the European Central Bank's behest, the tentative bailout agreement between eurozone creditors and Athens provides for up to 25 billion euros that could be used to replenish bank capital depleted by the surge in problem loans.
19The agreement also requires the Greek parliament to pass laws to bring its rules on bank restructuring into line with European directives.
20The lawmakers are supposed to take up that measure Wednesday, as part of the next package of creditor demands.
21But as the bailout negotiations resume, assuming they do, there are likely to be disagreements among eurozone members about how much help to give Greek banks.
22Countries like Germany might argue that Greek bankers should be responsible for their own problems.
23Some countries may balk at guaranteeing deposits in Greece.
24For Greeks, the experience of Cyprus, which shares a language and culture, is not reassuring.
25When Cyprus banks were on the verge of collapse in 2013, eurozone leaders insisted on a "bail-in" that included seizing money from individuals and businesses with large deposits.
26Much of the money in Cyprus belonged to wealthy foreigners.
27But in Greece, wealthier people and many businesses have already taken their money out.
28The depositors left behind are Greeks of more modest means or businesses that need to have money in local banks to operate.
29They are rightly suspicious of whether their money is adequately protected.
30"The vital fabric of the Greek economy is in those deposits," Veron said.
31"Bailing them in would be economically counterproductive."
32If the Greek banks can be cleaned up by recapitalising the ones that can be salvaged and closing down any that cannot, they might be able to borrow money at reasonable rates on the open market and begin to operate normally.
33But that process is likely to take well into the fall, and perhaps next year.
34The European Central Bank is wary of moving too quickly, and if restrictions on money movements are removed before confidence has returned, there is a risk of a bank run.
35"The awareness that capital controls are hampering the recovery of the Greek economy is there," Mario Draghi, the European Central Bank's president, said last week.
36"The idea is to move as fast as we can, but also with a due degree of caution."
37In the meantime, Greek banks are dependent on the central bank for the cash they need to operate.
38Last week the central bank increased its emergency lending to Greek banks by 900 million euros, allowing the banks to reopen.
39The central bank's policy board, the Governing Council, is expected to confer by telephone this week to discuss possible further increases - if the government continues to stick to conditions dictated by creditors.
