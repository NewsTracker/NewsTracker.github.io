Theater gunman's writings alternate between ramblings, plans

1FILE - In this June 4, 2013, file photo Aurora theater shooting suspect James Holmes appears in court in Centennial, Colo.
2Prosecutors in the Colorado theater shooting trial say they are moving closer toward the heart of their case: whether Holmes was legally insane when he committed one of the worst mass shootings in U.S. history.
3Colorado theater shooting gunman James Holmes' notebook full of scrawled writings and stick-figure drawings was made public Wednesday, a day after it was introduced as evidence in his trial.
4The writings on about 30 pages in the brown, spiral school notebook alternate between sometimes incoherent ramblings and Holmes' methodical plans to attack the movie theater and maximize the number of people killed.
5It provides the most comprehensive look yet into what Holmes described as his "broken" mind.
6Prosecutors, however, are attempting to use its planning entries to undermine Holmes' claim that he was legally insane at the time of the July 2012 attack, which killed 12 and injured 70 others.
7It's unclear when Holmes wrote the entries.
8Just before the shooting at a premiere of the Batman movie "The Dark Knight Rises," he mailed the notebook to a University of Colorado psychiatrist who treated him.
9It was found unopened in a campus mailroom days later, after one of Holmes' attorneys called the school asking for it back.
10The notebook had $400 worth of burnt $20 bills wedged in its pages.
11A judge gave all 24 jurors copies of the writings this week.
12The notebook opens with drawings of stick figures, one standing, the rest presumably dead, and a series of equations that appear to try to evaluate whether a "murderer" gains value by killing people.
13"Can a person have both no value AND be ultimately good AND/OR ultimately evil?"
14Holmes writes.
15"Unknown."
16Holmes details various mental issues, diagnosing himself as suffering from schizophrenia, generalized anxiety disorder, psychosis and several other illnesses.
17He writes that he goes catatonic for up to five hours in the middle of the day, pulls his hair, feels isolated and obsesses about various parts of his body.
18"The real me is fighting the biological me," Holmes writes.
19He also details an obsession with mass killing: "And, finally, the last escape, mass murder at the movies. Obsession onset (more than) 10 years ago. So, anyways, that's my mind. It's broken. I tried to fix it."
20One section is titled "Crazy Thoughts."
21Holmes jots down notes about the Garden of Eden, the myths of Prometheus and Faust, and writes various, almost nonsensical essays on philosophical issues.
22The section ends with the word "Why?" scribbled hundreds of times over eight pages in ever-larger writing.
23Over three pages under the heading "The Shrinks," Holmes lists the names of psychiatrists or doctors he has visited and speaks of medication prescribed.
24He writes: "Prevent building false sense of rapport. Speak truthfully and deflect incriminating questions. Oddly they don't pursue or delve farther (sic) into harmful omissions."
25He concludes with: "No consequence, no fear, alone, isolated, no work for distractions, no reason to seek self actualization. Embrace the hatred, a dark knight rises."
26Holmes lists various possible methods of violence, from attacking an airport to serial killing.
27He diagrams and ranks by stars the individual theaters at the Aurora Cineplex for attack purposes, using sight lines, the number of exits and audience capacities.
28"Can lock double doors, inflicting mass casualties," reads one entry.
29"Maximum casualties, easily performed w/ firearms although primitive in nature. No fear of consequences, being caught 99 percent certain."
30In a section titled "Case the Place," Holmes writes: "Targets random. The cruel twists of fate are unkind to the misfortunate."
31Holmes diagrammed various theaters at the Aurora complex, marking exit doors, evaluating his own visibility, even locating the best parking spots.
32He listed pros and cons for attacks on each theater.
33He also drew a raw map of the theater complex and a nearby National Guard facility and police station and wrote: "ETA Response (about) 3 mins."
