Obama, Netanyahu make dueling appeals on Iran to US Jews

1WASHINGTON (AP) - President Barack Obama and Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu made dueling appeals to the American Jewish community Tuesday as they sought to rally support for their opposing positions on the Iranian nuclear deal.
2Obama met privately for more two hours with Jewish leaders at the White House, making a detailed case for the nuclear accord and urging opponents - including some in the room - to stick to the facts in making their own arguments, according to participants.
3He singled out the tens of millions of dollars being spent by critics, most notably the pro-Israel group American Israel Public Affairs Committee.
4The president's meeting came hours after Netanyahu participated in a live webcast aimed at Americans Jews.
5The prime minister railed against the agreement to curb Iran's nuclear program in exchange for billions of dollars in sanctions relief, calling it a "bad deal" that leaves Tehran on the brink of a bomb.
6"The nuclear deal with Iran doesn't block Iran's path to the bomb. It actually paves Iran's path to the bomb," Netanyahu said in his remarks.
7Organizers said about 10,000 people participated in the meeting.
8Netanyahu, one of the fiercest critics of the nuclear accord, also disputed Obama's assertion that opponents of the diplomatic deal favor war.
9He called that assertion "utterly false," saying Israel wants peace, not war.
10Participants in Obama's meeting with Jewish leaders said attendees who oppose the deal raised with the president their concern over being painted as eager for war.
11They said while Obama appeared sympathetic to their concerns, he continued to argue that if Congress rejects the agreement, he or the next president would quickly face a decision on taking military action to prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon.
12The back-to-back sales pitches from the leaders came on the eve of a foreign policy address Obama was to deliver as he seeks to bolster support for the deal in Congress.
13A White House official said Obama would frame lawmakers' decision to approve or disapprove of the deal as the most consequential foreign policy debate since the decision to go to war in Iraq.
14The official said Obama would also argue that those who backed the Iraq war, which is now widely seen as a mistake, are the same ones who oppose the Iran deal.
15The official insisted on anonymity because the official was not authorized to preview the president's address by name.
16Participants in the meeting were also granted anonymity in order to comment on their private discussions with the president.
17The White House is preparing for the likelihood that lawmakers will vote against the deal next month and is focusing its lobbying efforts on getting enough Democrats to sustain a veto.
18Only one chamber of Congress is needed to sustain a veto.
19Obama spokesman Josh Earnest said Monday that the White House is confident it can sustain a veto "at least in the House."
20The president got a boost in the Senate Tuesday with Sens.
21Barbara Boxer of California, Tim Kaine of Virginia and Bill Nelson of Florida announcing their support for the deal.
22However, the administration lost the backing of three prominent Jewish Democrats - New York Reps.
23Steve Israel and Nita Lowey and Florida Rep. Ted Deutch.
24Obama, who has long been criticized for his lack of engagement with Congress, has gotten personally involved in selling the deal to lawmakers and other influential groups.
25Those who have met with him say he has a detailed understanding of the complex agreement, which is perhaps his top foreign policy priority.
26"It was pretty solid evidence of a couple of things: first of all, just how engaged the president is on this issue, and second, how important it is to him," said Andrew Weinstein, a South Florida community leader who attended the meeting.
