Nigerian leader visits Cameroon as Boko Haram attacks

1FILE- In this file photo taken Friday, May 29, 2015, former General and Nigerian President, Muhammadu Buhari, arrives for his Inauguration at the eagle square in Abuja, Nigeria.
2Nigeria's new President Muhammadu Buhari makes his first official visit to one-time enemy Cameroon on Wednesday, July 29, 2015 to ease tensions and bolster support for a regional army to fight the Boko Haram Islamic uprising that is spilling across borders, his spokesman and Cameroon's defense minister said.
3Nigeria's new President Muhammadu Buhari is set to make his first official visit to one-time enemy Cameroon Wednesday to ease tensions and bolster support for a multinational army to fight the Boko Haram uprising that has spilled across borders.
4The long-anticipated diplomacy comes eight weeks after Buhari visited neighboring Niger and Chad.
5All three countries are contributing to the force.
6Hard feelings date back to a 1980s land dispute.
7More recently, Nigeria accused Cameroon of doing little to prevent Boko Haram from using their territory as a refuge.
8Cameroon saw Buhari's failure to visit earlier as a snub, and its president, Paul Biya, didn't attend his May inauguration.
9"The two countries are intertwined and have no choice but to have a relationship to deal with Boko Haram," said Chris Fomunyoh, an analyst with the Washington-based National Democratic Institute.
10On Monday, Boko Haram killed at least 29 people in two Christian villages in northeast Nigeria, while in Cameroon, suicide bombings claimed 60 lives over the past week.
11Some 20,000 people have died in the 6-year-old uprising.
12"Those are the last kicks of a dying monster," said Cameroon's Defense Minister Edgar Alain Mebe Ngo'o Tuesday.
13The insurgency has consistently rebounded after major setbacks, however.
14Multinational troops earlier this year forced the extremists out of towns they had held, but now, Nigerian politicians say Boko Haram is again seizing territory.
15A Nigerian presidential statement said talks would focus on "full activation and deployment" of an 8,700-strong multinational army, delayed for months by lack of funding dependent on a U.N. Security Council resolution.
16Associated Press reporters Edwin Kindzeka Moki in Yaounde, Cameroon and Haruna Umar in Maiduguri, Nigeria contributed to this report.
