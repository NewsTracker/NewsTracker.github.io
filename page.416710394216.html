Girls Freed From Boko Haram Extremists Receive Food, Medical Care

1YOLA, Nigeria (AP) -- Hundreds of girls and women, many bewildered and traumatized, are being registered, fed and given medical care in their first day out of Nigeria's war zone.
2The group of 275 women and children are among the nearly 700 freed in the past week by the Nigerian military from Boko Haram extremists and the first to be transported to the safety of a Malkohi refugee camp here in Yola in the country's northeast.
3Woman and children rest at a refugee camp in Yola, Nigeria, after being rescued from captivity by Boko Haram fighters.
4Officials on Sunday are still registering the 61 women and 214 children, almost all girls.
5Many critically malnourished babies and children have been put on intravenous drips in the clinic and 21 have been hospitalized for gunshot wounds and fractured limbs, said a camp official.
6Through interviews the officials are trying to determine where the women and children are from.
7It does not appear that any of those released are from the group of more than 200 schoolgirls kidnapped by Boko Haram a year ago from the town of Chibok, said an official.
8"Based on registration we have carried out so far, none of them is from Chibok," said Zakari Abubakar, Malkohi camp team leader for the National Emergency Management Agency.
9Almost all are from Gumsuri village near Chibok and which had been attacked many times in the past year, said Abubakar.
10The women and children were rescued by the military from the Sambisa Forest, the last stronghold of the Islamic extremists, and had to travel for three days on the open backs of military trucks to reach the safety of Malkohi Camp.
11Nigerian military said it has rescued more than 677 girls and women in the past week.
12The Nigerian military said the women and girls were freed when soldiers destroyed more than a dozen insurgent camps.
13Saturday night when the women arrived at the camp, they appeared exhausted and too distressed to realize they were safe, or to be questioned about their experiences under Boko Haram.
14They lined up for tea, water and a stew of baobab leaves.
15They were taken captive when Boko Haram had seized a large swath of northeastern Nigeria last year, declaring it an Islamic caliphate.
16Nigerian troops ran away before their advance, complaining they were not given enough ammunition or food to fight, and leaving civilians with no defense against an uprising that killed as many as 10,000 people last year.
17Some 1.5 million people have been driven from their homes.
