Clinton offers energy plans, declines comment on Keystone XL oil pipeline

1Hillary Rodham Clinton is detailing new energy proposals in Iowa to address climate change.
2She calls global warming one of the "most urgent threats of our time" and says people are "just not paying attention" if they don't acknowledge it.
3But she's still not taking a position on the Keystone XL oil pipeline.
4The Democratic presidential contender is proposing that every home in the United States be powered by renewable sources by 2027.
5Her plan calls for installation of 500 million solar panels over four years.
6Clinton laid out clean-energy ideas during a tour of a regional bus station in Des Moines, Iowa, on Monday.
7When asked about the Keystone XL oil pipeline opposed by environmental activists, she would not comment except to say she wants a State Department review of the project to run its course.
8Addressing this issue Sunday, Clinton touted a program that would combat climate change at least in part by changing the tax code to promote renewable energy.
9"This is not complicated folks," the former secretary of state told more than 200 people at Iowa State University.
10"I'm just a grandmother with two eyes and a brain. And I know we're facing huge problem."
11Climate change has become a key issue in the Democratic presidential primary, where Clinton is the heavy favorite.
12Billionaire Tom Steyer has led an effort to promote the issue.
13Clinton proposes, through tax incentives, to increase the amount of power derived from renewable sources to support every home in the United States within 10 years.
14For instance, Clinton said she supports renewing the wind energy tax credit as part of over time shifting the U.S. energy system from one based on fossil fuels.
15"We need to get the incentives fixed in our tax system which as you know are too heavily weighted toward fossil fuels," Clinton said during a day of campaigning in central Iowa.
16Clinton also hinted that her plans would impose changes on the coal industry, though she also pledged the government's help for workers to make the transition.
17"We can make a transition over time from a fossil fuel economy, predominantly, to a clean renewable energy economy, predominantly," Clinton said later during an event at a central Iowa rural home.
18Weaning the country off of coal is a tricky political position in key places on the political battleground map.
19Southeast Ohio and western Pennsylvania are regions of states that have been pivotal in recent elections.
20And they remain the home of key coal-producing areas.
21Crediting coal-miners for having "created an industrial revolution," Clinton said "it is important that we help them transition to a new economy."
22Clinton's plan also includes the goal of installing 500 million solar panels within four years.
23It would also increase capacity to the nation's power grid with a combination of renewable energy sources such as wind, solar, hydroelectric and geothermal.
24The plan's estimated cost is about $60 billion over 10 years, and would be paid for by eliminating tax breaks for the oil and gas industry, Clinton spokesman Brian Fallon said.
25Former Maryland Gov. Martin O'Malley, also seeking the 2016 Democratic presidential nomination, noted Sunday that he unveiled a climate change plan in Iowa that addresses not just consumer energy use, but industrial and transportation, as well.
26As governor, O'Malley doubled Maryland's renewable fuel production, and reduced greenhouse gases in the state by 10 percent during his two terms.
27Beamout reported from Ames, Iowa.
