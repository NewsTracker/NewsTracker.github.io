G7 states vow to wipe out Ebola but offer little concrete action

1KRUEN, Germany, June 8 (Reuters) - Leaders of the Group of Seven industrial nations pledged on Monday to wipe out Ebola but offered little in terms of concrete action, disappointing non-governmental organisations.
2G7 leaders said in a communique at the end of a two-day summit in the Bavarian Alps that they would offer help to at least 60 nations, including in West Africa, over the next five years to help prevent outbreaks from turning into epidemics.
3More than 11,000 people have died in the Ebola outbreak in West Africa since the first reported case in March 2014.
4The G7 said the crisis showed it was necessary to enhance the world's ability to prevent, detect and respond to such emergencies.
5The G7 nations said they would work together to combat future epidemics and boost or establish strategies to quickly deploy teams of experts with a variety of skills via a common platform, but their communique was thin on detail.
6Florian Westphal, General Director of Medecins Sans Frontieres (MSF) Germany, said the leaders had done little to ensure epidemics would not spiral out of control in future.
7"No concrete measures have been decided to strengthen emergency response, meaning the world is no better prepared to face major health crises and save thousands of lives," he said.
8Charity Oxfam was also disappointed, saying fighting pandemics like Ebola required "more than simply putting the emergency services on standby".
9The G7 countries said they would put money into preventing and controlling neglected tropical diseases to meet a goal of eliminating them by 2020, and committed to supporting research.
10Antibiotic resistance, which can be caused by misuse and overuse, is a growing and deadly problem.
11Doctors and health experts have warned for decades that rising rates of resistant bacteria are leading to tens of thousands of deaths, threatening to nullify modern medical advancements.
12The G7 leaders said they would promote "prudent use" of antibiotics and committed to researching issues including the development of new antibiotics, alternative therapies and vaccines.
