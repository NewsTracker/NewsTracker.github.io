Human waste on Everest causes pollution, threatens to spread disease: official

Concerns over drug prompt delay of Georgia woman's execution

1Human waste left by climbers on Mount Everest has become a problem that is causing pollution and threatening to spread disease on the world's highest peak, the chief of Nepal's mountaineering association said Tuesday.
2The more than 700 climbers and guides who spend nearly two months on Everest's slopes each climbing season leave large amounts of feces and urine, and the issue has not been addressed, Ang Tshering told reporters.
3He said Nepal's government needs to get the climbers to dispose of the waste properly so the mountain remains pristine.
4Hundreds of foreign climbers attempt to scale Everest during Nepal's mountaineering season, which began this week and runs through May. Last year's season was cancelled after 16 local guides were killed in an avalanche in April.
5Climbers spend weeks acclimatizing around the four camps set up between the base camp at 5,300 metres (17,380 feet) and the 8,850-meter-high (29,035-foot-high) summit.
6The camps have tents and some essential equipment and supplies, but do not have toilets.
7"Climbers usually dig holes in the snow for their toilet use and leave the human waste there," Tshering said, adding that the waste has been "piling up" for years around the four camps.
8At the base camp, where there are more porters, cooks and support staff during the climbing season, there are toilet tents with drums to store the waste.
9Once filled, the drums are carried to a lower area, where the waste is properly disposed.
10Dawa Steven Sherpa, who has been leading Everest cleanup expeditions since 2008, said some climbers carry disposable travel toilet bags to use in the higher camps.
11"It is a health hazard and the issue needs to be addressed," he said.
12Nepal's government has not come up with a plan yet to tackle the issue of human waste.
13But starting this season, officials stationed at the base camp will strictly monitor garbage on the mountain, said Puspa Raj Katuwal, the head of the government's Mountaineering Department.
14The government imposed new rules last year requiring each climber to bring down to the base camp 8 kilograms (18 pounds) of trash - the amount it estimates a climber discards along the route.
15Climbing teams must leave a $4,000 deposit that they lose if they don't comply with the regulations, Katuwal said.
16More than 4,000 climbers have scaled Mount Everest since 1953, when it was first conquered by New Zealand climber Edmund Hillary and his Sherpa guide, Tenzing Norgay.
17Hundreds of others have died in the attempt, while many have succeeded only with help from oxygen tanks, equipment porters and Sherpa guides.

2A day later, they decided to temporarily halt executions until they could more carefully analyze the pentobarbital, which is supposed to be clear.
3The cloudy drug bolstered death penalty opponents, who have been vocal in their opposition after three botched executions in other parts of the country.
4Gissendaner, who was convicted of murder in the February 1997 slaying of her husband, had originally been set for execution last week on Feb. 25, but it was postponed because of a threat of bad weather.
5Attorneys for Gissendaner said in a filing with the U.S. Supreme Court that a lawyer for the state called them around 10:25 p.m. Monday to say the execution would be postponed several days because the state's pharmacist had looked at the drug an hour earlier and determined it was cloudy.
6The state's lawyer called back about five minutes later to say the prison wasn't sure which drugs they had checked, "this week's or last week's," and that they were considering going forward, the filing says.
7The lawyer called a third time, saying "this particular batch (of drugs) just didn't come out like it was supposed to" and they weren't going to proceed, according to the court filing.
8About 11 p.m., the state told reporters an independent lab checked its potency and it was acceptable, but it later appeared cloudy and the execution was postponed "out of an abundance of caution."
9The back and forth was detailed in Gissendaner's emergency motion for a stay.
10Corrections spokeswoman Gwendolyn Hogan said "there was never any confusion within the department about the drugs which were to be used," but also indicated the department was not part of the conversations between the attorney general's office and defense attorneys.
11Georgia Attorney General Sam Olens said in a statement it is "essential that executions are carried out in a constitutional manner" and that he approved of the decision to temporarily suspend executions.
12The cloudiness could be contamination by bacteria or some impurity, said Michael Jay, a professor at the University of North Carolina's Eshelman School of Pharmacy.
13If the particles were big enough, they could clog blood vessels when injected or could lodge in the lungs, Jay said.
14It could also make the drug less potent, making an inmate very sleepy but not kill them, he said.
15"If it's a solution that's supposed to be clear and it's not clear, it should never be injected," Jay said.
16"So they did the right thing by not injecting it."
17Compounded drugs have a shelf life of 30 days, Jay said.
18Corrections officials declined to say when the department obtained the drug or when it was set to expire.
19Georgia and other death penalty states have bought execution drugs from compounding pharmacies in recent years after pharmaceutical companies stopped selling to U.S. prisons.
20And many of them have adopted laws to hide the identity of their drug providers, saying those laws are necessary to protect their suppliers and maintain a source of the drugs.
21Critics say executions should be as transparent as possible.
22The cloudy drug "raises significant concerns about Georgia's ability to carry out executions in compliance with the Constitution, but we don't know what really happened," said Megan McCracken of the University of California, Berkeley, School of Law's Death Penalty Clinic.
23"Because of the secrecy surrounding Georgia's procedures, it is impossible to say if this is indicative of a larger problem," she said.
24Richard Dieter, executive director of the Death Penalty Information Center, which tracks data on executions, said he's never heard of an execution being halted at the last minute because of a problem with the drug itself.
25Missouri and Texas are also actively using compounded pentobarbital in a one-drug formula for executions, and South Dakota has also used this method, McCracken said.
26Other states have been using a three-drug combination starting with the sedative midazolam.
27But executions in those states are mostly on hold pending a U.S. Supreme Court ruling in an Oklahoma case sparked by a botched execution there.

30Owen and Gissendaner then met up and set fire to the dead man's car.
