Turkish Cannes winner joins fight against domestic censorship

1In a year when Turkey's film industry is winning unprecedented plaudits abroad, it's also battling censorship at home, prompting dozens of the nation's most prominent directors to boycott its main festival.
2More than 100 filmmakers, including the most recent winner of Cannes's Palme d'Or, Nuri Bilge Ceylan, signed a letter on Sunday alleging censorship at the Istanbul Film Festival, the country's foremost showcase for film.
3The action came after programmers canceled the screening of "Bakur," or "North," a locally-made documentary that looks at the lives of Kurdish militants engaged in armed struggle against the Turkish state.
4The filmmakers' protest comes even as they receive record support from the government, which says it aims to increase the value of film and television exports to $2 billion by 2023, the centennary of the founding of the Turkish Republic.
5Last year, 31 percent of the 108 domestically produced films received budgets from the Ministry of Culture, including "Winter's Sleep," Ceylan's three-hour, Chekhov-inspired epic that won top prize from the Cannes jury.
6Such support makes yesterday's censorship a puzzling step back for the government, according to Cem Doruk, 35, a producer who had two films in the festival before he joined colleagues in removing them from competition in protest.
7"Given the prestige the Turkish industry now has worldwide, it's embarrassing that they'd risk that so a few hundred cinephiles can't see this film," Doruk said in an interview in Istanbul on Sunday.
8"It's fair to say that before the ruling party came to power, there wasn't anything like this much support for film in Turkey. An industry that used to sustain perhaps five independent films a year can now sustain 50, and that's partly down to their support."
9Conflicts with ethnic Kurds have kept Turkey's southeast in varying levels of military conflict since the early 1980s, and the subject was largely taboo until recent years in cinema as well as in the media.
10Restrictions have been gradually lifted as Turkey's government started talks with Abdullah Ocalan, the imprisoned leader of the Kurdistan Workers' Party, or PKK, to end fighting that's left some 40,000 people dead.
11The PKK is considered a terrorist group by Turkey, the European Union and United States.
12According to its directors, "'Bakur' tells the story of how the PKK, a group that built its struggle mostly on national identity, turned to become a women's movement."
13They say the documentary "challenges the audience to develop a different point of view as it sheds light on this mysterious world."
14The ministry warned the festival hours before it was due to show "Bakur" that the film didn't have the necessary certification, prompting it to cancel the screening and those of other films without documentation, the festival said in a statement Sunday.
15The government doesn't typically require such documents before commercial release, and other films shown didn't have to produce them, the protesting filmmakers allege, saying that they would retract their films in solidarity.
16The warning shows that there are still "two topics the government gets jumpy about," Doruk said.
17"That's the Gezi Park protests and the Kurdish issue."
18The Gezi demonstrations started as a protest over the uprooting of trees near Istanbul's central square in the summer of 2013 and rolled out country-wide, sustained by a groundswell of broader dissatisfaction with the government and a harsh police crackdown.
19Efforts to censor a documentary about the protests sparked a similar reaction from participants at last year's Antalya film festival.
20A ministry of culture website shows that 332 feature films have been given financial assistance in the last decade, helping to spark a revival in Turkey's film industry after two decades in abeyance.
21Last year Ceylan won two prizes at Cannes, making him one of Turkish cinema's most decorated directors.
22Newcomer Kaan Mujdeci picked up the special jury prize in Venice.
23"It's clear that this act was motivated by the film's content," Ayse Cetinbas, producer of "Bakur," said in an interview on Sunday.
24The Ministry of Culture didn't respond to a request for comment.
