5Mark Servidio was online in New York at 3 a.m. to order two Apple Watches - Sport models with black bands in large for him and small for his wife.
6Six hours later, he was at the 9 a.m. opening of the Apple store on New York's Upper East Side.
7The 30-year-old software developer wanted to check out what he just paid $349 and $399 for.
8He'd booked an afternoon appointment, but he decided to try out his luck after seeing there was hardly a line.
9Store employees were able to accommodate him quickly.
10Servidio ended up trying some of the models he didn't buy, just to see what they'd feel like.
11An employee explained some features, though the watches Servidio was allowed to try on were all running in demonstration mode.
12Servidio admits he doesn't really need an Apple Watch.
13But he's curious about what it can do and thinks it might make checking messages and other notifications less intrusive than pulling out his phone.
1411:26 a.m. EDT.
15S&P Capital IQ analyst Angelo Zino predicts Apple will sell 10 million watches in 2015.
16"Our view is it's going to take time for the consumer to adopt wearable technology," Zino said in an interview.
17"We're very positive on the long-term trends for wearables and we think Apple is going to be a clear leader in the category."
18He thinks initial sales are going to be below expectations, though.
19Because the smartwatch is a new category of product, Apple will have to work harder to show customers what it can do, Zino says.
20The company is encouraging customers to make appointments to try the watch and some of its features, before ordering it online.
21That's a departure from its approach to the iPhone and iPad, which have been sold on a first-come, first-served basis to customers who often line up at Apple stores on the first day those products are available.
22"They had to change their sales strategy," Zino said of the watch.
23"It's not a standardized product, so you need to make consumers aware of the customization that's available, and you can't take the approach you've taken with other products."
2411:11 a.m. EDT.
25Employees of the Lenox Square Mall's Apple store in Atlanta clapped and cheered as customers streamed through its glass doors just before 10 a.m. About 20 people had lined up outside the second-floor storefront.
26Marcus Martin, a self-described "gadget" guy from Columbus, Ohio, was first in line with his wife and smiled broadly after leaving the store with an order placed.
27He said it was a long-anticipated gift for his birthday on Saturday.
28"It's very user-friendly, and that was a question I had in my mind," he said.
29He especially likes the watch's ability to turn off when a user's arm swings down, hoping that feature will save battery life.
30Steve Kaip and his 15-year-old son Jason also were among the first in the store.
31On a spring break trip to Atlanta from their home in Novi, Michigan, they're planning to place an order when they get home, preserving their "early adopter" status.
32"I don't wear a watch today, but I'm considering wearing a watch if it did more than just tell time," Steve Kaip says.
33"The calendar function letting you know an event is coming up with a buzz on your wrist, instead of wherever your phone is, that's good."
