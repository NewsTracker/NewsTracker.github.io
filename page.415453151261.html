1Russell Brand a no-show for SXSW premiere The documentary follows bad boy British entertainer Russell Brand's turbulent ride from stand-up comic to movie star, addict and activist.
2AUSTIN - BRAND: A Second Coming, the new documentary on the turbulent life of British actor/comedian Russell Brand, offers all the elements one would expect from such a work: Brand being hilarious on stage, Brand strung out on drugs, Brand flirting with crowds of eager women, Brand naked on a police van waving a red flag.
3But the most revealing moments in BRAND, which had its world premiere at South by Southwest Film Festival Friday night, are the small snippets when he looks directly at the camera and offers glimpses into his stormy psyche.
4The film, by award-winning filmmaker Ondi Timoner, follows Brand from his small-town roots and struggling stand-up comic act to global movie star, through his harrowing descent into hardcore drug and sex addiction, the breakup of his marriage to pop star Katy Perry, and his resurgence through rehab and transcendental meditation.
5Brand shot to stardom in films such as Forgetting Sarah Marshall and Get Him to the Greek and his live concert performance Messiah Complex, before seemingly evacuating the limelight a few years ago for a life as a political activist and professional rabble-rouser.
6Comedian Russell Brand joins residents and supporters from a housing estate in East London as they demonstrate against U.S. investment company plans to evict 93 families on Dec. 1, 2014, in London, England.
7BRAND was SXSW Film Festival's opening night film.
8Fans and the media lined up early outside the Paramount Theater in downtown Austin alongside a red carpet, hoping to catch a glimpse of cast and crew.
9But notably missing: Brand himself, who canceled his SXSW appearance just hours before the premiere.
10In a colorfully-worded, 414-word statement posted on his website, Brand described struggling with the movie project for more than seven years and as many directors before teaming up with Timoner.
11He apologized to event organizers for bailing out, saying watching the film again would be too "painful."
12"My life was hard enough the first time 'round and going through it again was painful and sad," Brand said in the statement.
13"I know Ondi is an artist and I'm told the film is good but for me watching it was very uncomfortable."
14Timoner, a two-time winner of Sundance Film Festival's Grand Jury Prize for documentaries for earlier films Dig!
15and We Live in Public, said she heard Brand wasn't coming and immediately thought of Get Him to the Greek, where Brand's character, an out-of-control British rock star, struggles to reach a concert in Los Angeles.
16"This is a painful experience for him to have this film come out," Timoner told USA TODAY before the premiere.
17"Probably, it's a little bit terrifying because it's so personal and so raw. But at the end of the day, I know people are going to love him for this."
18The film at times depicts Brand, 39, in an unflattering light, showing his self-comparisons to Jesus and using his fame to serially sleep with women.
19In one particularly difficult scene, a friend's home video footage shows a young Brand squatting in a corner of a room in London smoking drugs from an aluminum square.
20Shortly after, Brand enters rehab.
21Brand struggles with his mom's cancer diagnosis and a mostly absent father who bonds with his grown son by taking him to hotel parties with prostitutes.
22But Timoner also peels back Brand's many layers to reveal a thoughtful, complex person in constant pursuit of life's deeper meaning.
23Brand appears to have an epiphany when he attends a glitzy fashion show in Paris with then-wife Perry, then travels to Kenya and witnesses children foraging through garbage piles alongside feral pigs.
24"This is hell," he says.
25"And we're creating it."
26The incident launches him onto a path toward activism that leads to his YouTube show The Trews and frequent visits to "Occupy" street protests.
27Throughout the film, Brand's comedic vigor pokes through, whether describing his drugged-out, naked protest atop of a police van on a cold London day or his hilarious barbing with the befuddled on-air staff during a live MSNBC interview.
28The film shows Brand shuttling between concert shows and TV interviews, to visits to addiction clinics to hug clients, and to marathon meditation sessions.
29"He's an artist of genius proportions," comedian Rosie O'Donnell says in the film, "only with the desire to serve."
30Brand never truly answers if he'll return to Hollywood or continue calling for cultural and political revolution.
31Or both.
32In one scene, he surveys a group of adjoining buildings in London that he envisions some day turning into a non-profit online studio and cafe run by recovering addicts.
33"Nothing will ever be enough," Brand tells the camera.
34"Ever."
