3HONG KONG (AP) - Decades ago the Chinese New Year holiday, also known as Spring Festival, had little impact outside of China.
4But as the country gained outsized economic influence, the holiday, which has enormous cultural significance in the Chinese-speaking world, has become more prominent.
5This is how it ripples around the world.
6Chinese factories shut down for the holiday and then some, with hundreds of millions of migrant workers heading to their hometowns, part of the world's largest mass movement of people.
7In the lead up to the holiday, factories run flat out to fill orders before shutting.
8The holiday itself runs from Feb. 18 to 24 this year, but workers start setting off as much as two weeks earlier on packed trains and buses.
9After the holiday they may take the same amount of time to return, or not.
10The holiday is a prime occasion to switch jobs.
11It all means an annual headache for retailers and importers overseas who rely on China.
12Shipping companies warn customers that China's transport and logistics networks are at capacity and their shipments must be at ports two weeks ahead of the holiday to stand a chance of getting on a boat before the country shuts down.
13This year, shipping delays are compounded by a slowdown at U.S. West Coast ports.
14Stock market trading shudders to a halt as mainland China shuts for an entire week and financial hubs such as Hong Kong and Singapore take a break as well, albeit shorter.
15Numerous other countries including South Korea and Vietnam also observe Lunar New Year holidays.
16Muslim majority Malaysia and Indonesia, with large Chinese minorities, take holidays too.
17Trading volumes "drop off considerably" about three working days before the start of the holiday, said Andrew Sullivan, managing director at Haitong Securities in Hong Kong.
18This year, Friday was "the last day that you can sell in Hong Kong and get your money before Chinese New Year" under trading settlement rules, he said.
19Foreign investors also tend to wind down trading in Asia as the holiday nears, Sullivan said.
20The festival is traditionally the most important time of the year for family reunions, but as China has become prosperous, an increasing number of wealthy Chinese are opting to travel abroad.
21That translates into big business for global luxury brands.
22Many British department stores, for example, are pulling out all the stops to woo mainland Chinese shoppers.
23(Designer handbags, watches and jewelry can be up to 30 percent cheaper in Europe because of high luxury taxes in China.)
24Harrods is selling its own brand of red envelopes traditionally used to give "lai see," or lucky money.
25Selfridges and luxury brand Burberry are each offering cards and envelopes personalized with Chinese calligraphy.
26Designer label Vivienne Westwood has launched a collectors' necklace featuring a sheep pendant.
27Shoppers at Fortnum & Mason paying with UnionPay cards - China's homegrown payment network - will get bonus gifts.
28Chinese spending in Britain last February jumped 23 percent over the same month in 2012, said Gordon Clark, manager at Global Blue, a Switzerland-based firm that tracks luxury retail spending worldwide.
29Chinese shoppers spend an average 739 pounds ($1,137) per transaction in Britain each February, mostly on luxury jewelry, watches and designer clothes.
