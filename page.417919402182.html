Greece to offer more in debt talks, official says

1Greece may bring more to the table in negotiations for a debt deal with its creditors, a close aide to the prime minister said on Saturday, as time runs out for the austerity-battered country to avoid default and help its ailing banks.
2Back from a visit to Russia, Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras will meet his negotiating team in Athens and may speak to European Commission chief Jean-Claude by phone to try to break the deadlock before an emergency summit on Monday.
3His government urgently needs to secure a cash-for-reforms deal to avoid defaulting on a $1.8 billion International Monetary Fund loan at the end of June that could eventually turf it out of the eurozone.
4With nervy depositors pulling billions out of Greek banks, Athens may have to impose capital controls within days to staunch the flow.
5It was not immediately clear how far Greece's leftist government, which won a January election vowing to lift its people out of austerity, is willing to bend in order to secure an agreement or what kind of additional offers it could make.
6While Greece has dug in its heels over demands for pension cuts and some tax increases, its leaders have continued to sound upbeat about the chances of reaching a deal - optimism that is not widely shared among Europe's leaders.
7"We will try to supplement our proposal so that we get closer to a solution," State Minister Alekos Flabouraris told Greek Mega television in a morning news show.
8"We are not going there with the old proposal. Some work is being done to see where we can converge, so that we achieve a mutually beneficial solution."
9The European Central Bank has kept Greek lenders afloat and on Friday raised the ceiling on so-called emergency liquidity assistance the banks rely on to keep their doors open.
10Greece has repeatedly warned Europe about the dangers of cutting the country adrift from a bailout program.
11In that vein, Flabouraris said he was confident the European Central Bank would not stop funding Greek lenders as this would set off a domino effect and topple lenders in other vulnerable parts of Europe.
12EU chief Juncker has been seen as more sympathetic to Greece's position, although he has reportedly warned Athens not to rely on him to avert a collapse in the debt talks.
13"With deposits fleeing the Greek banking system as a result of his brinkmanship, and the European Central Bank Governing Council ready to scale back its emergency lending authority for Greek banks, Tsipras faces the choice on Monday between taking the deal offered by the creditors - or not having the Greek banks open properly on Tuesday morning," Jacob Funk Kirkegaard, a senior fellow at the Peterson Institute For International Economics told Reuters.
14Greeks are exasperated with austerity measures imposed on them under two bailouts by the IMF, the European Commission and the ECB.
15Wages and pensions have been slashed and one in four Greeks is jobless.
16But after months of fractious negotiations, patience among its creditors is running dry, and some European leaders are now openly contemplating the possibility of Greece becoming the first nation to be dumped out of the euro zone.
17In an article in the Irish Times, Greece's Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis criticized the behavior of his peers at a meeting of European finance ministers on Thursday, where he had presented his government's latest proposals.
18"An impartial spectator of our eurogroup deliberations would come to the safe conclusion that it is a strange forum, one ill-equipped to forge good, hard decisions when Europe truly needs them," he wrote.
19"The pressing question our Irish friends must answer prior to Monday's extraordinary summit meeting on Greece is this: Is it more likely that the euro zone will become a better union to belong to if Greece is thrown to the wolves, despite the type of proposals tabled at Thursday's eurogroup meeting?"
20While some members of Tsipras's Syriza party are not averse to quitting the euro, opinion polls suggest the majority of Greeks want to remain in the currency bloc they joined in 2001.
21To that end, Athenians are planning to hold another rally in the capital on Monday calling for Greece to stay in the euro.
22A Facebook invitation for the event showed 5,700 people had already signed up to going by Saturday afternoon.
23Greek businesses have also called on Tsipras to solve the crisis.
24Andreas Andreadis, head of Greek tourism association SETE, told German magazine Boerse Online in remarks published on Saturday that a "Grexit" would have "devastating" consequences for the country, adding Greece would "fall back to the level of a developing country."
25A pipeline project penned on Friday in Moscow may help Greece pay its debt.
26Russian President Vladimir Putin said after Friday's talks with Tsipras that a prospective Russian natural gas pipeline should help Greece service its debt.
27Speaking of the pipeline deal at a meeting with top executives of global news agencies, including The Associated Press, which began nearly three hours behind schedule at around midnight, Putin said he saw no support for the Greeks from the European Union.
28"If EU wants Greece to pay its debts it should be interested in growing the Greek economy ... helping it pay its debts," he said.
29"The EU should be applauding us. What's wrong with creating jobs in Greece?"
30Tsipras' visit gave rise to speculation that the Greeks may be seeking Russian loans -- and ahead of the talks, Putin's spokesman said Russia would consider a loan if the Greeks asked for one.
31"We would do this because they are our partners and this is a normal practice between countries who are partners," spokesman Dmitry Peskov told The Associated Press.
32But when Tsipras met with Putin at the sidelines of a major economic forum in St.Petersburg, the possibility of a loan wasn't discussed, Peskov told journalists.
33Instead, they spoke about "the necessity of developing investment cooperation."
34Russia and Greece signed a deal Friday to build an extension of a prospective gas pipeline that would carry Russian gas to Europe through Turkey.
35Russia promised Greece hundreds of millions of dollars in transit payments yearly if it agreed to build the pipeline.
36Construction of the pipeline is expected to start next year and be completed in 2019.
37Russian Energy Minister Alexander Novak said Russia and Greece would be equal partners in the project, with Russia's half owned by the state bank VEB.
38The talks were held after both leaders addressed investors and Russian government officials at Russia's biggest annual economic forum.
39Putin said little about Greece, although he slipped in a joke about its predicament.
40"When Mr. Tsipras spoke, he said the problem of Greece was not a Greek problem but a European one. Well, that's right. If you owe someone a lot, then it is already not your problem but the problem of the one you owe - and that's an absolutely correct approach," Putin said.
