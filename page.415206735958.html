Concern grows for civilians as Iraq tightens noose on Tikrit

1On Monday, around 30,000 Iraqi security forces and allied fighting units launched the biggest anti-IS ground operation yet in Iraq, closing in on Tikrit from at least three directions.
2A senior commander said operations were focused on cutting supply lines to stop reinforcements and weapons from reaching the jihadists, who have controlled the city since June.
3The next step will be to "surround the towns completely, suffocate them and then pounce on them," Lieutenant General Abdel Amir al-Zaidi told AFP.
4Iraqi forces have yet to retake Ad-Dawr and Al-Alam, towns south and north of Tikrit respectively, but some units were already on the edge of the city, military sources said.
5Zaidi said the operation launched Monday had already achieved results by securing areas further out in Salaheddin province and forcing IS fighters to regroup in urban areas.
6"The first phase of the battle to liberate Salaheddin was successfully completed -- and in record time -- by clearing the areas in the east of the province," he said.
7The Iraqi forces' advance has been slowed by suicide car bombs, roadside bombs and sniper fire, as IS fighters retreated to urban positions but seemed unable to fight back in open areas.
8Government forces, Shiite militias and volunteer units have been supported by Iraqi jets and helicopters, as well as Iran.
9"This is the most overt conduct of Iranian support," General Martin Dempsey, chairman of the US Joint Chiefs of Staff, told senators Tuesday.
10"Frankly, it would only be a problem if it resulted in sectarianism," he said.
11Sectarianism-fuelled revenge killings have been a feature of past operations against IS however and rights groups expressed concern Wednesday.
12"Shiite paramilitary militias have often carried out reprisal sectarian attacks against Sunni civilians who are not involved in the hostilities," said Amnesty International's senior crisis response adviser Donatella Rovera.
13"We are concerned about the possible recurrence and increase of such attacks in the ongoing operations," she told AFP.
14Some leaders and fighters have described the operation as an opportunity to avenge the June 2014 massacre by IS of hundreds of new, mostly Shiite, recruits from the nearby base of Speicher.
15Some Sunni tribes have been accused of taking part in the massacre, considered the worst of its kind since IS swept through Iraq's Sunni heartland and beyond in June last year.
16Prime Minister Haider al-Abadi said as he announced the Tikrit operation on Sunday that residents should turn against IS.
17In a speech to parliament the next day, he also said that "in this battle, there is no neutral party," arguing that any Iraqi choosing neutrality was de facto siding with IS.
18"Abadi's statement that there can be no neutrality is worrying," said Joe Stork, deputy Middle East and North Africa director at Human Rights Watch.
19"Any person who is not participating in hostilities directly must be regarded as a civilian, and protected against deliberate or indiscriminate attack," he told AFP.
20Many civilians fled the cities conquered by IS last year but the group has in some recent cases prevented residents from leaving.
21A former army officer who gave his name as Abu Ahmad fled the town of Al-Alam with his wife and five children on Sunday but he had to pay a smuggler.
22"We left with a 'guide', a guy who knows the roads. We were five families, and paid him $1,000 each," he told AFP by telephone from Kirkuk.
23The Kurdish-controlled city of Kirkuk is only 100 kilometres (60 miles) away but the route they took was a huge loop through the desert that saw them cover eight times that distance.
24"In the time it took us to get here, I could have gone to Hajj and back," he joked, referring to the pilgrimage to Mecca in Saudi Arabia.
25He said he decided to leave because he was afraid that homes would be shelled, that Iraqi forces "would not distinguish between armed men and innocent people" and that militias would kill civilians in revenge for Speicher.
26Tikrit, a Sunni city located 160 kilometres (100 miles) north of Baghdad on the Tigris, is of both strategic and symbolic importance in Baghdad's fight against IS.
27It is the hometown of former president Saddam Hussein, the remnants of whose Baath party have collaborated with IS.
28Commanders have also said Tikrit is a stepping stone for an even more ambitious operation aimed at retaking Mosul, the large northern city which has been the main Iraq hub of IS.
1A visitor to Yellowstone National Park left with a bill worth more than $2,700 when a wild buffalo charged the length of three football fields to ram into his stopped Nissan Xterra.
2Tom Carter posted the footage of the unusual encounter on YouTube last week.
3The scene appears fairly tranquil as Carter and his passenger, a friend and park tour guide, were animal watching and enjoying the view of the clear road in the middle of the snow-covered mountains.
4But in the far distance a herd of buffalo can be seen running up the paved roadway.
5Carter spotted them about 300 yards away, stopped the vehicle and turned off the engine.
6Then he started recording and saw the lead animal not slowing down as it got closer and closer to the vehicle.
7Carter told the Daily News a group of cars were actually traveling behind the animals and keeping pace with them at about 10 mph.
8He said he doesn't believe the motorists were being malicious, but that it probably agitated the animals and caused them to run away frightened.
9The Dallas resident said he expected the buffalo to pass them by, but at the last moment he realized he wouldn't be that lucky.
10"Don't run into our ca-" Carter says in the video as the buffalo makes direct impact with the front of the SUV.
11He shared images of the front-end damage that included a crushed hood and broken grill in another video.
12The car belonged to his friend who, unfortunately, does not have bison insurance.
13She was left with a bill of $2,787.82, he said.
14"I gave her a copy of the video to show (the auto mechanic)," he said.
15"They realized it wasn't her fault."
16Carter, 60, who was a tour guide of the park in the 1970s and has written many hiking guidebooks, said he never saw buffalo act that way before.
17The animal appeared to be fine after the collision, shaking its head and moving on.
18Carter then told the other drivers to stop chasing them.
19He and his friend were not injured in the accident.
20"It took on something its own size," Carter said.
