Independent candidate rides hatred of politics in Mexico

3Now he is trying to beat the odds in another way, running as an independent for governor of Nuevo Leon, a wealthy and strategic state bordering Texas.
4Mexicans' disgust with corrupt, aloof, high-living politicians has a name, and it's El Bronco.
5The horseback-riding, boot-clad, tough-talking Jaime Rodriguez lives up to his nickname.
6As mayor of a suburb of the northern industrial city of Monterrey, he survived two assassination attempts that left his car bullet-ridden, defying, he says, the fierce Zetas cartel.
7Now Rodriguez is trying to beat the odds in another way, running as an independent for governor of Nuevo Leon, a wealthy and strategic state bordering Texas.
8The June 7 midterm election is the first time the country has allowed unaffiliated candidates, thanks to an electoral reform last year.
9But the law allows him only a fraction of the campaign financing the government gives political parties.
10Although it is a state race, Rodriguez has captured the national imagination with his unorthodox manner and unrefined speech.
11He explains the challenges of his uphill race this way: "Sometimes God slaps you upside the head to make you get with program."
12El Bronco says his nickname, and his blunt style just "show people that I'm the same as them, that I'm nobody different, that I'm just another guy who wants things to change, and things to be better."
13Midterm elections, in which 500 members of congress, 17 state legislatures, nine governors and more than 300 mayors will be chosen, are usually viewed as a referendum on the president's performance half-way through his six-year term.
14But this year it seems to be a referendum on the parties.
15In an opinion poll carried out by the lower house of congress earlier this year, 75 percent said they had little or no confidence in any party.
16The margin of error was 3.9 percentage points.
17"This breath of fresh air could be just the shock the parties need, at least in Nuevo Leon," Luis Carlos Ugalde, former head of the Mexico's national election commission, told local media.
18Critics point out that Rodriguez spent 33 years in the ruling Institutional Revolutionary Party, or PRI, and quit eight months ago to take advantage of the new law.
19But voters see him as an alternative in political system rife with cronyism and corruption.
20Current Nuevo Leon Gov. Rodrigo Medina has taken out full-page newspaper ads to deny widespread claims of corruption, allegations Rodriguez says he will pursue if he's elected.
21"I think that in Nuevo Leon, we are seeing the first Mexican Spring," Rodriguez told The Associated Press, comparing his quixotic quest for the governorship to pro-democracy movements like the Arab Spring of 2010 or the Prague Spring of 1968.
22That might be an overstatement.
23But Rodriguez's support harkens back to 2000, when another plainspoken cowboy candidate, Vicente Fox, managed to topple the PRI's 71-year rule and win the presidency for the opposition National Action Party.
24Fox rode a wave of discontent with the authoritarian and corrupt rule of the PRI.
25But the last 15 years have convinced many voters that National Action and other opposition parties mirror the PRI in terms of corruption scandals and accusations of politicians getting rich off public funds.
26This time around, it's all political parties that Mexicans appear to hate.
27People are so fed up that parties have trouble giving away pens and T-shirts on the street, and youthful brigades have organized on the Internet to rip down campaign posters from lampposts and trees in some Mexico City neighborhoods.
28Last week, Fernando Elizondo of the Citizen Movement party ended his gubernatorial campaign and threw his support behind Rodriguez.
29A day later, a poll by the newspaper Reforma showed Rodriguez with 31 percentage points, five ahead of his nearest opponent, Ivonne Alvarez of the PRI.
30The poll had a margin of error of 1.8 percentage points.
31Rodriguez's style is all wild west, with campaign videos showing him riding horseback or describing bullets raining down on his truck in an assassination attempt.
32When former President Felipe Calderon unfavorably compared the charismatic Rodriguez to deceased former Venezuelan President Hugo Chavez, El Bronco shot back: "I think Calderon must have been either drunk, or hung over."
33El Bronco's public support for "self-defense" vigilantes in Michoacan - ranchers and farmers who armed themselves to drive out a drug cartel in 2013 - led ruling-party candidate Alvarez to run an ad showing a motley group of vigilante-style gunmen holding assault rifles and a baby, with a narration: "Nothing is more important than your children. Who do you want caring for them?
34And in what appears to be a counteroffensive by the PRI, leaked official documents quote people suggesting, without firm proof, that Rodriguez has ties to the Zetas, the cartel he battled as mayor.
35He denies those allegations.
36El Bronco says he wants peaceful change, but tells people in Nuevo Leon, a state shattered by drug violence, that he can feel their pain.
37His own 22-year-old son was killed in 2009 in what initially appeared and accident, but which may have been a kidnapping.
38As an independent candidate, he is excluded from the generous government financing given to political parties, so he has conducted his campaign largely using social media, and he passes the hat at raucous campaign rallies.
39Political parties get essentially all their financing from tax money, and they are criticized for their bloated budgets for government cars, trips, bodyguards, advisors, offices and meals, angering people in a country where the minimum wage is under $5 per day.
40Rodriguez says he wants change things all that.
41And despite inequitable financing rules, he pulled together hundreds of thousands of signatures to run as an independent candidate.
42"I have two emotions that guide me," Rodriguez said.
43"My family deserves someone who can protect them, and the other that, in this country we need somebody who will do things differently."
