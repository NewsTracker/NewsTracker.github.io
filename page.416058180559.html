Turkish academics to fight Twitter and Facebook ban in court

1Turkey has taken a tough stance on social media under President Tayyip Erdogan and the ruling AK Party he founded.
2It temporarily banned both sites last year in a bid to prevent the circulation of leaked recordings during a corruption scandal.
3The government has also introduced legislation making it easier for such bans to be imposed.
4In the second half of 2014, Turkey filed more than five times the number of content-removal requests to Twitter than any other country, data from the micro-blogging company shows.
5Both Twitter and video-sharing service YouTube were inaccessible for hours on Monday after a Turkish court ordered the removal of images of a prosecutor held at gunpoint by far-left militants.
6Facebook said it had complied with the court order and appeared to have avoided the ban.
7Representatives of both Twitter and Facebook have said they would launch an appeal.
8Ankara University law professor Kerem Altiparmak said he and another legal academic, Yaman Akdeniz, had filed an appeal to challenge the restriction in Turkish courts.
9"It does not matter that the ban is lifted now. We think it is against the law and are appealing," he said, vowing to take the case to the European Court of Human Rights if necessary.
10Social media companies set a bad precedent for free speech by agreeing to remove content, he said, adding: "Both Twitter and YouTube are now hostage as they implement all the decisions taken by the courts."
11A spokesman for Erdogan said on Monday that a prosecutor had demanded the ban because some media organizations had acted "as if they were spreading terrorist propaganda" in sharing the images of the hostage-taking.
12The prosecutor in the photos, Mehmet Selim Kiraz, was killed in a shoot-out between hostage-takers and police last Tuesday.
13"The laws are being used as weapons by authorities," said Mehmet Ali Koksal, a lawyer specializing in IT cases.
14"The government says 'I'll shut you down if you don't remove so and so'. If this were between two people it would be called blackmail."
15During the corruption scandal last year, Erdogan vowed to "wipe out" Twitter.
16Many Turks used software such as a virtual private network (VPN) to skirt restrictions.
