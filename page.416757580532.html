Advance team for 80 medical volunteers arrives in Nepal

1Advance team for 80 medical volunteers arrives in Nepal NEWPORT, Del.
2Doctors, nurses and volunteers with the Delaware Medical Relief Team spent Sunday evening sorting and packing medical supplies as the group prepares to send teams to Nepal to assist in relief efforts.
3NEWPORT, Del.
4- After 23 hours in the air and a landing on a potholed runway in Katmandu, three Delaware doctors have arrived in Nepal to prepare the way for as many as 80 volunteers from their state during the next three months.
5Nepal's government has urged foreign rescue workers in the quake-hit capital to return home as the Himalayan nation shifts to recovery mode after its 7.8-magnitude earthquake April 25.
6Still, members of the Delaware Medical Relief Team say they're hearing from non-governmental organizations and hospitals on the ground that medical supplies are desperately needed.
7"Our goal is to really serve the underserved," said team co-founder Reynold Agard as about 30 members and other volunteers met Sunday night at a warehouse here to organize and catalog boxes, trash bags and shipping containers full of supplies.
8An internist at Premier Physicians in Wilmington, Agard will bring his wife and two sons, both aspiring doctors, when he leaves on the journey.
9"The most important information on your gravestone is not the date of your birth or the date of your death," he said.
10"It's the dash in between."
11The still-rising death toll from the quake, Nepal's worst in more than 80 years, has reached more than 7,500 and at least 14,000 more are injured.
12The Delaware scout team, which arrived Monday, is attending a meeting Tuesday with other medical relief workers that the United Nations organized.
13The doctors - Vinod Kripalu, an internist; Kripalu's wife, Chetana, a family doctor; and Reha Dharmaraj, a doctor from India who is doing an externship at Trinity Medical Associates in Wilmington - also will firm up access to housing, food, drinking water, and medical supplies and equipment.
14The first contingent of 20 volunteers who have committed to seven- to 10-day shifts, including a News Journal reporter and photographer, could depart later this week.
15Some team members are determined to serve the hard-hit villages outside of Katmandu that have been cut off from medical care.
16Well-connected team leaders recently approached the Indian Army about riding on a military transport to establish a makeshift clinic in the villages.
17Details still are being discussed.
18This is the first relief trip for the Delaware Medical Relief Team since 2010 when dozens of volunteers spent nearly four months in the port city of Jacmel to help victims of the Haiti earthquake.
19The team raised and spent $250,000 for that effort.
20Nepal fundraising to-date totals nearly $40,000, but volunteers are optimistic they can meet or exceed Haiti's total.
21Joining the team Sunday were 13 of the Haiti volunteers.
22One, Guitho "Max" Mede, offered to translate for the medical team after the earthquake.
23He is now studying English at the University of Delaware.
24Mede, 27, said he could empathize with the Nepalese people on their long road to rebuilding.
25"Together, we are one people," he said.
26Other Delaware residents have responded to the humanitarian crisis by hosting fundraising events, donating supplies and - in the case of one mother and daughter - renting a jeep and driving across the border from India.
27After more than three days of driving to travel 450 miles, Amy Kaufman and her daughter, Holly, of Dagsboro, Del., also arrived Monday in Katmandu.
28The pair were in India on a non-emergency charity mission for their nonprofit group, the Shauna Rose Kaufman Foundation, when the earthquake hit.
29In email, Amy Kaufman said they had raised about $10,000 for food and medical supplies and planned to travel Tuesday to a village just outside of the city that was destroyed.
30On Tuesday, the U.S. Agency for International Development announced $11 million in aid for emergency shelter materials, medical supplies, safe drinking water, improved sanitation and hygiene kits for the most critical districts in Nepal, which has 31 million people living in a country slightly larger than Arkansas.
31By contrast, Arkansas' population is slightly less than 3 million.
32The American aid agency's acting administrator, Alfonso Lenhardt, also is in Nepal visiting earthquake-hit areas.
33He said the additional aid brought the total U.S. government commitment to about $26 million.
34On her own, Kaufman rented a jeep and packed it full of supplies after her driver backed out, claiming conditions were too "unsanitary" to keep traveling.
