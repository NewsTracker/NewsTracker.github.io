President Barack Obama to end historic Kenya visit with national address

1US President Barack Obama told Kenyans that their country is at a crossroads and urged them to "choose the path to progress" by continuing to root out corruption, eliminate income inequality and be more inclusive of women and girls.
2At the end of a historic visit to the land of his father's birth, Mr Obama said Kenya has come so far in just his lifetime, but can go even further.
3"You can choose the path to progress, but it requires making some important choices," he said in a speech to several thousand Kenyans packed into an indoor arena in Nairobi.
4Thousands more Kenyans lined the president's motorcade route to the arena and the speech was broadcast live on local TV.
5Mr Obama traced the history of Kenya's evolution, from colonialism and isolation to independence and global engagement.
6But he said challenges remained and he urged Kenyans, particularly its future leaders, to deal with corruption and tribal conflict, create opportunity for all, improve education and health care, treat women better and confront the threat of terrorism.
7"When it comes to the people of Kenya, particularly the youth, I believe there is no limit to what you can achieve," he said.
8"Because of Kenya's progress - because of your potential - you can build your future right here, right now.
9The president's late father was born and is buried in Kenya, and its people have waited for years for the chance to welcome Mr Obama back as president.
10He made history by becoming the first sitting American president to visit Kenya when he arrived late on Friday.
11Kenya has one of the fastest-growing economies on the continent and is the commercial hub of East Africa, but is struggling to overcome challenges to its prosperity posed by widespread corruption and the threat of al-Shabab militants based in neighbouring Somalia.
12Mr Obama will also highlight efforts to support African youth by visiting a regional centre for his Young African Leaders Initiative, a programme to help cultivate the next generation of African leaders.
13Kenyan President Uhuru Kenyatta said on Saturday at a news conference with Mr Obama that the US president will be remembered in Africa for focusing on the continent's young people.
14Nearly 1 in 3 Africans are aged 10-24, and about 60% of Africa's total population is younger than 35, according to the US government.
15In Ethiopia, another Horn of Africa nation that will be getting its first visit by a sitting US president, Mr Obama plans meetings with the president and prime minister.
16He w ill also speak to the continent from the headquarters of the African Union, which plays a role in peace and security on the continent.
17It will be the first time an American president addresses the AU.
