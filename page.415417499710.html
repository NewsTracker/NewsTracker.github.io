MH17: New evidence on downing of passenger plane over Ukraine

Utah doctor found guilty of murder in death of ex-wife

1Chervonyi Zhovten: Villagers in eastern Ukraine have said they saw a missile flying directly overhead just before a Malaysian airliner, Malaysian Airlines MH17, was shot out of the sky on July 17 last year, providing the most detailed accounts to date that suggest it was fired from territory held by pro-Russian rebels.
2The accounts from four villagers of Chervonyi Zhovten, which was then, and is now, controlled by the rebels, are significant because they indicate the rocket was in the early stages of its flight path.
3That would mean it must have been launched from rebel ground nearby, challenging the suggestion of Moscow and the separatists that the plane was brought down by the Ukrainian military.
4At the time, the nearest Ukrainian-held area was about six kilometres away.
5Ukraine and its Western allies have said it was the rebels who shot down the airliner, using a Russian-made Buk anti-aircraft missile system.
6All 298 people on board were killed, including 27 Australians.
7Until now, videos, photographs and accounts from residents have pointed to a Buk battery being delivered to the rebel-held town of Snizhne, seven kilometres north of Chervonyi Zhovten, on July 17, and then driven away from the area some time later.
8Its precise location at the time the plane was shot down has never been confirmed.
9Now one of the villagers has said that a missile battery was positioned in a field near Chervonyi Zhovten on the day the Malaysia Airlines Boeing 777, en route from Amsterdam to Kuala Lumpur, crashed to earth.
10A former rebel fighter corroborated this.
11A top rebel commander, Andrei Purgin, said the separatists did not have any weapon capable of downing a plane at cruise altitude.
12"You can come up with whatever you want. The most that we, the rebels, were operating is the PZRK [shoulder-launched missile system]. The ceiling for the PZRK is up to four kilometres," he said.
13Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov said it was up to a Dutch-led international investigation to determine what happened.
14"The Russian side awaits that information impatiently", he said.
15The downing of the plane was a turning point in the conflict in Ukraine, in which about 6,000 people have been killed since last April.
16It persuaded many Western governments that the Russian-backed rebels were a dangerous threat and stiffened their resolve to impose new sanctions on separatist leaders and on Moscow.
1Salt Lake City pediatrician John Brickman Wall, 51, was convicted of killing the woman amid a bitter custody dispute.
2He faces up to life in prison.
3The jury of three women and five men deliberated for about seven hours before reaching the verdict late Thursday night.
4Prosecutors alleged Wall attacked 49-year-old Uta von Schwedler with a knife, dosed her with an anti-anxiety drug Xanax and drowned her in her bathtub.
5Defense attorneys countered that the theory was unbelievable, and it was more likely von Schwedler killed herself.
6Her death initially was treated as a suicide.
7But family and friends pushed for more investigation, saying the researcher showed no signs of wanting to end her life.
8The couple's oldest son, Pelle Wall, said publicly that he thought his father killed his mother.
9After the verdict was read, the 21-year-old college student thanked police for years of investigation and "uncommon persistence."
10He spoke at the courthouse in Salt Lake City surrounded by more than two dozen supporters.
11"We have spent the last three and half years seeking justice for my mother, and today that quest is finally at an end," he said.
12The victim's sister, Almut von Schwedler, said the Wall children will never be the same, but the verdict is the first step toward closure.
13"Johnny never succeeded to destroy Uta's joy for life, but he ended up taking her life," she said.
14"Revenge was certainly not what drove us in those dark days, and dark and desperate days we had many of."
15Prosecutor Matthew Janzen said Pelle Wall played an important part in the case, one of many pieces that formed the largely circumstantial case.
16As the verdict was read, John Wall sat with his hands folded and shoulders bent, staring down and blinking rapidly as his frown deepened.
17The defendant's sister, Wendy Wall, maintained her brother's innocence.
18"This verdict will not bring Uta back," she said in a statement.
19"Now, to that tragedy has been added the conviction of an innocent man."
20The evidence in the case was unusual: A medical examiner thought the shallow cuts on von Schwedler's wrists and leg looked like she was defending herself from an attack, but he couldn't explain the fatal level of Xanax in her system.
21For the prosecution, spilled antihistamine pills on the floor, a house in disarray and bloodstains in von Schwedler's bed showed she was attacked.
22The defense said the home revealed signs of a troubled woman who died trying to calm herself with medication.
23"The state just can't throw up a whole bunch of theories," John Wall's lawyer Fred Metos said during closing arguments Thursday.
24He added self-injury is a rare side effect of Xanax.
25Prosecutors said von Schwedler studied childhood leukemia and recently made a discovery that could help find new treatments for the disease.
26She had no prescription for Xanax, but John Wall filled a large one for his mother months before his ex-wife's death.
27"He resented her efforts to see the children, to talk with the children, to text the children, to email the children," prosecutor Nick D'Alesandro said during closings.
28"He just couldn't stop talking about how much he hated her."
29The night of their mother's death, the former couple's four children were staying at John Wall's house.
30The next morning, he wasn't at home when they woke up.
31Instead, he went to a car wash and had the interior of his car cleaned, including a pinkish stain, according to prosecutors.
32He came to work with a scratch on his face and injury to his eye; he said the family dog scratched him while he slept.
