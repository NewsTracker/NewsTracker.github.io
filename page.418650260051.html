Merkel says Greece must act fast as banks prepare to reopen

1Women withdraw money from an ATM outside a National Bank branch in Athens, Greece, July 19, 2015.
2ATHENS (Reuters) - Greece will reopen its banks on Monday for the first time in three weeks, but with restrictions on withdrawals still in place, German Chancellor Angela Merkel said the government would have to move fast on any new bailout terms.
3The cautious reopening of the banks, and an increase in value added tax on restaurant food and public transport from Monday, are aimed at restoring trust inside and outside Greece after an aid-for-reforms deal last week averted bankruptcy.
4Greeks will be able to withdraw 420 euros a week at once instead of just 60 euros a day but capital controls will remain.
5"That's not a normal life so we have to negotiate quickly," Merkel said in extracts from an interview with German public broadcaster ARD.
6Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras is trying to turn a corner after he reluctantly agreed to negotiate a third bailout, allowing the European Central Bank to top up emergency credit lines but prompting a rebellion in his leftist Syriza party.
7The head of Greece's banking association Louka Katseli urged Greeks to do the opposite of what they have done for months and put cash back on their bank accounts rather than withdraw more.
8"Tomorrow when the banks reopen and normality is restored, let's all help our economy. If we take our money out of chests and from our homes - where they are not safe in any case - and we deposit them in the banks, we will strengthen the liquidity of the economy," she told Skai television on Sunday.
9Merkel said it would be possible to talk about changing the maturities of Greece's debt or reducing the interest Athens has to pay after the first successful review of the new bailout package to be negotiated.
10Berlin, the biggest contributor to eurozone bailouts, would do all it could to bring talks to a successful conclusion but would "negotiate hard" to ensure Athens stuck to agreements, she said.
11"That certainly won't be easy because there are things that we have discussed with all of the Greek governments since 2010 that have never been done but that have been done in other countries like Portugal and Ireland," she said.
12Acceptance of the bailout terms that meant the banks could reopen marked a turnaround for Tsipras after months of difficult talks and a referendum that rejected a less stringent deal proposed by the lenders.
13He sacked party rebels in a government reshuffle on Friday and is seeking a swift start to talks on the bailout accord with European partners and the IMF before elections which Interior Minister Nikos Voutsis said were likely in September or October.
14But while opinion polls suggest the prime minister's popularity remains high, on the streets of Athens some were skeptical that the bank reopening would change much in a recession-hit country with over 25 percent unemployment rate.
15"The banks opening tomorrow won't change anything for me," said 31-year old hotel worker Joanna Arvanitaki.
16"I never used to withdraw 60 euros a day - 60 euros is what I had a week for my expenditure."
17German Economy Minister Sigmar Gabriel said this deal could succeed where previous ones failed because the European Union now emphasizes growth and investment rather than just austerity.
18It would depend on reforms being enacted and "convincing the population that this is a path that allows Greece to assert itself rather than becoming a permanent alms-receiver," he said in extracts from a television interview.
19French President Francois Hollande, who pushed hard for a deal, said the Greek crisis had weakened Europeans' faith in the European project and revived calls for the creation of a euro zone government.
20"What threatens us is not an excess of Europe but its insufficiency," he wrote in an op-ed in the Journal du Dimanche weekly newspaper.
21Gabriel rejected accusations Germany had been too hard on Athens and criticized Finance Minister Wolfgang Schaeuble for suggesting Greece could quit the euro zone temporarily.
22For her part, Merkel said the euro zone had turned its back on the idea.
23But in a sign of the challenge for euro zone leaders to convince their electorates of the merits of the deal, more than half of Germans think the planned deal with Greece is bad and many would have preferred it left the euro zone, a YouGov survey seen by German newspaper Welt am Sonntag showed.
24The deal struck at a euro zone summit last week allowed the European Central Bank to top up emergency credit lines which the Greek banking sector needs to survive.
25"The banks are ready to open and we don't expect any major problems on Monday," an official at the Central Bank of Greece told Reuters.
26As well as getting a weekly limit instead of a daily one, customers will also be able to access their safety deposit boxes and withdraw money without a credit card.
27Deposit boxes are not affected by the capital restrictions and clients can therefore take whatever they want from them, bank officials said.
28But restrictions on transfers abroad and other capital controls remain in place.
29"We are expecting queues in our branches in the first two or three days. Many people will ask to open their safe deposit boxes," an official at EFG Eurobank (EURBr.AT), the country's third-largest bank by assets, said.
