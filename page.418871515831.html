Greece advances in loan talks, under fire for euro exit plan

1A man passes a store front of souvenir shop in the Plaka tourist district of Athens, Tuesday, July 28, 2015.
2Emissaries from Greece's international creditors are holding preparatory talks with Greek officials as the aim is to thrash out the terms of the deal before Aug. 20, when Greece must make a debt payment that it cannot afford without new loans.
3ATHENS, Greece (AP) - Greece pushed ahead with talks on a new rescue loan Tuesday, but its government came under increasing pressure over claims it had a top-secret plan to prepare for a euro exit that involved accessing citizens' personal tax data.
4Emissaries from Greece's international creditors held a second day of preparatory talks with Greek officials, ahead of higher-level negotiations later this week on the country's new multi-billion euro lifeline.
5The talks in Athens aim to thrash out the terms of the deal - worth about 85 billion euros ($94 billion) over three years - before Aug. 20, when Greece must make a debt payment that it cannot afford without new loans.
6It will be the country's third bailout in more than five years.
7So far, Greece has received about 240 billion euros from the European Union and the International Monetary Fund.
8In return, it was forced to implement painful spending cuts, tax increases, and wide-ranging market reforms.
9The austerity deepened a sharp recession, and caused unemployment to swell to a peacetime record.
10This week's talks will include an array of issues such as pensions and labor market reforms, where the government is being asked to cut early retirement, raise retirement ages, streamline the pension system and ease restrictions protecting workers from mass layoffs.
11Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras has reluctantly accepted the reforms in principle - even though he has repeatedly said he doesn't agree with them - abandoning the staunchly anti-austerity policies that brought his Syriza party to power six months ago.
12The U-turn was necessary after talks with bailout creditors came very close to collapse and Greece was threatened with exit from the euro currency union unless it agreed.
13Parliament has already approved two batches of reforms, drastically increasing sales tax on key consumer goods, and reforming the banking and judiciary systems.
14But that caused a rift within Tsipras' party, and about a quarter of his lawmakers refused to back the reforms, which were passed with the help of pro-European opposition parties.
15Early elections now seem likely after the bailout deal is signed.
16Adding to Tsipras' troubles, his former finance minister, Yanis Varoufakis, has claimed that he tried to surreptitiously clone his own ministry's tax records with the help of a childhood friend, as part of a contingency plan requested by Tsipras before the January elections.
17The idea, according to Varoufakis, was to set up a parallel banking system that would allow transactions to continue - in euros, but with a provision for immediate conversion to a new currency - were Greek banks to be closed down.
18The alleged scheme never received Tsipras' go-ahead, and Greek banks did close down for three weeks with severe limitations on withdrawals that remain in force, although domestic transfers were still allowed.
19The head of the finance ministry's IT services denied that the ministry systems had been tampered with, adding that adequate protection measures are in place.
20The main opposition New Democracy party on Tuesday formally asked a parliamentary committee investigating Greece's bailouts to examine Varoufakis, with a view to determining whether Tsipras was aware of his alleged acts.
21Also, a prosecutor forwarded to parliament two separate lawsuits against Varoufakis by Greek citizens claiming he severely harmed the national interests by failing to adequately negotiate with bailout creditors.
22Under Greek law, all lawsuits against lawmakers, irrespective of their merits, must be examined by parliament as members of the house enjoy immunity from prosecution.
23Lawmakers would then vote on whether to lift Varoufakis' immunity.
