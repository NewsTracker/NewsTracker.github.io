Winter weather wrath? Pooches' paws feel the pain, too

Obamacare faces another Supreme Court struggle

1LOS ANGELES (AP) - People aren't the only ones suffering from a seemingly endless winter of bone-rattling cold, record-busting snow and ice-slick sidewalks.
2Pooches' paws feel the pain, too.
3While millions of residents from the Rocky Mountains to the East Coast shovel snow deep into the season and hunker down awaiting relief, their dogs are either missing out on walks or left vulnerable to injury with each salt-coated step.
4Rock salt and shards of ice can cut feet or get wedged between toes, de-icing chemicals can burn paw pads and frostbite can happen.
5That's led to a late-season boost in sales of doggy boots, which can be an annoyance for canines but allow owners to protect pets that are like family.
6Malia Ebel of Concord, New Hampshire, has four dogs - two that will wear boots and two that won't or can't.
7Either way, when it dips below zero, Ebel cancels the crew's two daily walks.
8"My two little dogs won't go out the front door without them when it's snowy," she said of the dog boots worn by her Cavalier King Charles spaniel mixes.
9Ebel trained 13-year-old Seymour and 12-year-old Sanders when they were young to wear boots, which are a necessity instead of a fashion choice.
10"My dogs don't have a problem with the snow; it's the salt that hurts their feet," she said.
11"So it's great that their feet are protected and they can walk on the street all winter. In a winter like this, there has been so much snow and they've had to salt the roads very consistently."
12The persistent winter has pushed Boston close to its 20-year-old snowfall record with more than 100 inches and seemingly froze Niagara Falls in place.
13While people throw up their hands at each new storm, the weather is giving a boost to pet clothiers.
14At the Barker & Meowsky Paw Firm in Chicago, the number of boots sold each day in the last six weeks was four times higher than a typical day this winter, company President Alice Lerman said.
15"Some days all we sold were boots," she said of the pet boutique that sells clothing, furniture and carriers for cats and dogs.
16Boots called "Muttluks," fleece-lined boots that resemble furry Mukluks for people, were good sellers, as were Pawz disposable booties that look like balloons and come in 10-packs.
17But the boots that sold out every day were a new product called Saltsox.
18They slip on easily, stay dry and come shaped like a dog's foot so they won't fall off as often, Lerman said.
19There are even less intrusive options: Musher's Secret wax was designed for sled dogs and forms a shield on paws to keep ice and salt out.
20Bag Balm moisturizer and Vaseline also can be used in a pinch.
21Boots are a begrudging necessity for Wendy Olcott's golden retriever, Sunny.
22Living in Contoocook, New Hampshire, her 12-year-old dog learned to wear boots as a puppy for their long walks.
23But that doesn't mean Sunny likes them.
24"Sunny didn't like wearing the boots initially, and she still, all these years later, doesn't like wearing them," she said.
25But walks go faster with the footwear because the dog doesn't have to stop to get the snow out from between her toes, Olcott said.
26Shelters also are struggling this winter.
27At the Worcester Animal Rescue League in Massachusetts, keeping about 60 dogs and cats and two bunnies warm and clean has made laundry a never-ending chore, Executive Director Allie Tellier said.
28Worcester received 92.1 inches of snow through mid-February.
29Some days, workers and volunteers can't get to the shelter, and whoever is closest has to trudge in to feed the animals.
30When it's 20-below, even the housebroken dogs won't do their business outside, leading to messes, Tellier said.
31But when the hardier dogs go out, many come back holding up frozen paws.
32"We are equipped to handle snow and winter, but this has been harsh," Tellier said.
1The outcome of a dispute over tax subsidies that make insurance affordable for millions could hinge on the votes of Chief Justice John Roberts and Justice Anthony Kennedy.
2Justice Elena Kagan called the challenge a part of the "never-ending saga" of Republican-led efforts to kill the Affordable Care Act, known as Obamacare.
3Aside from Roberts and Kennedy, the same liberal-conservative divide present during the earlier case was in play in the packed courtroom - with the same lawyers representing each side in the case, as they did in 2012.
4Millions could be affected by the court's decision.
5The justices are trying to determine whether Obamacare makes people in all 50 states eligible for federal tax subsidies to cut insurance premiums or only those in states that set up their own insurance markets.
6Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg said the law allowed for flexibility and giving subsidies only to some states would be "disastrous."
7If the court rules against the Obama administration, up to 7.5 million people in at least 34 states would lose the tax subsidies aimed at helping low- and moderate-income people buy private health insurance, according to consulting firm Avalere Health.
8White House spokesman Josh Earnest said there are "no contingency plans that could be implemented that would prevent the catastrophic damage" to the law from a ruling favoring the challengers.
9Chief Justice Roberts, who supplied the decisive tie-breaker vote in the 5-4 ruling in 2012 upholding the law, did not indicate how he might vote.
