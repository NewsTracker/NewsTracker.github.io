Up-front losses would be large for Greece, long-term consequences uncertain for Europe

1FRANKFURT, Germany - With aid negotiations off and ATMs running out of money, it's not speculation any more.
2Greece could leave the euro.
3And soon.
4What would that mean for the Greek people and for the European Union's 16-year-old shared currency - the crown jewel of a six-decade-old project in binding Europe's countries closer together?
5For Greece, the short-term pain and turmoil could be extreme whereas the currency union would likely survive the initial shock.
6The longer-term costs - and any possible benefits - could take years to become apparent.
7Ironically, a few experts think one of the most devastating outcomes for the euro would be if Greece leaves and, against all expectation, thrives.
8That would undermine claims for the euro being a key to prosperity.
9On Sunday Greeks are asked to vote on the painful demands made by creditor countries for desperately needed bailout loans.
10The vote could be the turning point.
11A "no" could mean no more loans, leaving Greece little choice but to print its own currency after running out of euros needed to pay government wages and pension and to refloat troubled banks.
12Here is a look at some of the possible damage - and any benefits - of a Greek-euro divorce.
13FORTRESS EUROZONE?
14There's a widespread view among analysts that the eurozone would not collapse in the short term if Greece left.
15There would be turmoil.
16Stocks would fall.
17Borrowing costs for the weaker eurozone members, such as Portugal or Italy, might rise for a while.
18But, this view has it, the European Central Bank could handle it.
19The ECB is already pouring 1.1 trillion euros ($1.2 trillion) of monetary stimulus into the economy through regular bond purchases and stands ready to do more.
20Since the Greek crisis started in 2009, the governments that use the euro have come up with crisis backstops.
21Those include tougher banking supervision and a pot of money to bail out troubled governments.
22Other voices say the short-term implications are scarier.
23U.S. Treasury Secretary Jacob Lew has persistently warned that the global economy, currently enjoying an uneven recovery, doesn't need the uncertainty of a Greek euro exit, or "Grexit."
24For the long term, a Greek exit would disrupt the euro's "Hotel California" principle: that, as the Eagles put it, you can "check out any time you like, but you can never leave."
25It is supposed to be permanent.
26But if it turns out that one country can leave, investors might think, so can others.
27They might demand more interest for the risk of lending to countries such as Portugal and Italy.
28A market crisis could drive another country out.
29Some think such higher rates would be beneficial, by forcing countries to shape up their finances.
30The euro would in fact be stronger.
31Greece was an anomaly, a backward economy that didn't belong in the club in the first place, the thinking goes.
32A number of economists, however, think a Greek exit could cause long-lasting damage to the euro.
33As analysts at Standard & Poor's put it, "the permanence of the monetary union will have been proved false, and this could throw into question assumptions underpinning more than two decades of economic and political policy."
34Ben May, chief European economist at Oxford Economics, says that the worst blow Greece could deal to the eurozone "would be if Greece left and its economy quickly started to grow strongly."
35The example would not be lost on other weak members of the currency union.
36"For perennial slower growers such as Italy and Portugal an exit, devaluation and default strategy would now become a credible alternative option."
37There's little doubt that leaving the euro means imminent disaster for Greece.
38"In the short term, which may in fact last a couple of years, the process of exit would be extremely messy," said Zsolt Darvas, senior fellow at the Bruegel research institute in Brussels.
39The start would be a banking collapse that would make normal commerce impossible.
40Greece's new currency would plunge, meaning default on its bailout loans denominated in euros.
41The economy would plummet - some say by 10 or 20 percent.
42That's on top of a fall of 25 percent in the six years, about the drop the U.S. suffered during the Great Depression.
43And there's more.
44Greece imports energy and medicine, so those essentials could double in price.
45Greek companies that owe money to foreigners would be unable to pay.
46Many would go bankrupt.
47With no lenders and tax revenue plunging, the government would have to slash spending even more sharply than under the hated bailout deal.
48Greece might even need humanitarian aid.
49Inflation could run out of control, especially if the central bank has to print money to rescue banks and fund the government.
50The larger question is whether Greece would benefit much from a sharply devalued national currency, which in theory helps exports.
51To do that, you have to have something valuable to export and sell - and to be able to do so efficiently.
52Olives and nice vacation beaches aren't enough.
53Economist Darvas says the demands of Greece's lenders have pushed the country to reform some aspects of its economy, making it somewhat more competitive.
54Labor contracts are more flexible now, for instance.
55Greece has risen from 108th before the crisis on the World Bank's ease of doing business index to 62nd - not great, but progress.
56If it cuts excessive regulation and bureaucracy, that might improve further.
57But that might prove more difficult if Greece was no longer a part of the euro - and did not face external pressure from fellow eurozone states to modernize its economy.
