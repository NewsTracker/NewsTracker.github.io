Britain pulls out spies as Russia, China reportedly crack Snowden files

1US intelligence leaker Edward Snowden speaking during a dinner with US ex-intelligence workers and activists in Moscow in late 2013.
2London: Britain has pulled out agents from live operations in "hostile countries" after Russia and China cracked top-secret information contained in files leaked by former US National Security Agency contractor Edward Snowden, the Sunday Times reported.
3Security service MI6, which operates overseas and is tasked with defending British interests, has removed agents from certain countries, the newspaper said, citing unnamed officials at the office of British Prime Minister David Cameron, the Home Office and security services.
4Mr Snowden downloaded more than 1.7 million secret files from security agencies in the US and Britain in 2013, and leaked details about mass surveillance of phone and internet communications.
5Whistleblower Edward Snowden in front of the Christ the Saviour Cathedral in central Moscow in late 2013.
6The US wants Mr Snowden to stand trial after he leaked classified documents, fled the country and was eventually granted asylum in Moscow in 2013.
7He went to Russia via Hong Kong and, although he claimed in 2013 that the encrypted files remained secure, Britain believed both Russia and China had cracked documents that contain details that could allow British and American spies to be identified, the newspaper said, citing officials.
8However, an official at Mr Cameron's office was quoted as saying that there was "no evidence of anyone being harmed".
9A spokeswoman at Mr Cameron's office declined to comment.
10A Home Office source told the newspaper that Russian President Vladimir Putin did not grant Mr Snowden asylum for nothing.
11"His documents were encrypted but they weren't completely secure and we have now seen our agents and assets being targeted," the source said.
12A British intelligence source said Mr Snowden had done "incalculable damage".
13"In some cases the agencies have been forced to intervene and lift their agents from operations to stop them being identified and killed," the source was quoted as saying.
14British security agency GCHQ declined to comment and representatives from other British intelligence services did not respond to requests for comment.
15The Russian and Chinese governments were not immediately available for comment.
16The revelations about the impact of Mr Snowden on intelligence operations comes days after Britain's terrorism law watchdog said the rules governing the security services' abilities to spy on the public needed to be overhauled.
17Conservative lawmaker and former minister Andrew Mitchell said the timing of the report was "no accident".
18"There is a big debate going on," he told BBC radio.
19"We are going to have legislation bought back to Parliament ... about the way in which individual liberty and privacy is invaded in the interest of collective national security.
20"That's a debate we certainly need to have."
21Mr Cameron has promised a swathe of new security measures, including more powers to monitor Britons' communications and online activity in what critics have dubbed a "snoopers' charter".
22Britain's terrorism laws reviewer David Anderson said on Thursday the current system was "undemocratic, unnecessary and, in the long run, intolerable".
