Man's death after arrest exposes tensions in Baltimore

1BALTIMORE (AP) - The death of Freddie Gray, a Baltimore man critically injured while in police custody, has sparked demonstrations across the city that touch on the fears many from his neighborhood say they feel about their everyday interactions with police.
2"He was a typical Sandtown kid," said Sean Price, who grew up in Baltimore's Sandtown neighborhood where Gray lived.
3"He wasn't perfect but neither is anybody. This isn't anything new. Freddie Gray is just a microcosm of what happens every day in Sandtown, in Baltimore."
4The Sandtown neighborhood in West Baltimore surrounds blocks of red-brick public housing called the Gilmor Homes.
5Trees are sparse amid abandoned lots overgrown with grass and crumbling, burned-out row homes, their doors and windows boarded up.
6Gray was arrested April 12 after police "made eye contact" with him and another man in an area known for drug activity, police said, and both men started running.
7Gray was handcuffed and put in a transport van.
8Exactly what happened in the van and how he was injured are still unknown.
9But he died a week later in a hospital of what police described as "a significant spinal injury."
10In an interview with Baltimore station WJZ-TV on Wednesday, Police Commissioner Anthony Batts said a second man who was in the police van at the same time as Gray has said the driver of the van didn't drive erratically.
11"He didn't see any harm done to Freddie at all," Batts said.
12"What he has said is that he heard Freddie thrashing about."
13Danielle Hall, a friend of Gray's, said the relationship between law enforcement and the neighborhood is so contentious that many believe it's safer to run from police than stand still.
14"People run every day from the police. Why wouldn't you run when every time you turn, you're getting harassed? ... Why stop when you already know what they'll do to you? Rough you up, throw you on the ground?"
15The six officers involved in the arrest have been suspended with pay.
16Five officers voluntarily gave statements the same day as Gray's arrest, while one officer invoked his right to remain silent, police and an attorney representing one of the officers said Wednesday.
17For days, demonstrators have poured into the streets, carrying signs, chanting "hands up, don't shoot" and calling for transparency and accountability for the police department.
18A statement released Wednesday by the Baltimore police union comparing protesters of Gray's death to a "lynch mob" drew criticism on Twitter by users who called it racially insensitive and inappropriate, given that the demonstrations have been peaceful.
19As demonstrators marched onto a highway onramp and faced a line of officers blocking their way Wednesday night, Pastor Wesley West of Faith Empowered Ministries linked arms with Gray's cousin, Carron Morgan, and they faced the crowd.
20After West led chants for justice and peace, and called for a moment of silence, protesters headed away from the interstate.
21Morgan, 18, turned and shook the officers' hands.
22"At the end of the day, I don't think all cops are bad," he said, "just that some are corrupt."
23At another demonstration Wednesday evening outside of the Western District station house near where Gray was arrested, Mark Hill said anger in the community is reaching a boiling point, but the frustration is longstanding.
24"It's getting charged out here because people are really getting tired," Hill said.
25"There's a fear in the community of what police might do to you."
