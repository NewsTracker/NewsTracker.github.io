2Mr Fraser, who died on Friday aged 84, was the man who precipitated the most notorious event in Australian political history: the 1975 dismissal of the Whitlam government by Governor-General John Kerr.
3Remarkably, and baffling for many, Whitlam and the man he branded "Kerr's cur" built a relationship after politics based on their shared values and vision of what sort of nation Australia might become.
4Eric Sidoti, director of the Whitlam Institute and former executive director of the Human Rights Council of Australia, said Mr Whitlam showed him the Fraser inscription last July, during one of his regular visits before the Labor icon's death in October.
5"He pushed the book across to me and said `read the inscription'," Mr Sidoti said.
6"It struck me at the time that it just captured so much about how big they both were - they were big men with big hearts.
7"It was an absolute lesson in rising above bitterness."
8The 1975 dismissal scored deep divisions into Australian society and even now many people "maintain the rage" as Mr Whitlam urged them to do 40 years ago.
9But Whitlam biographer Jenny Hocking says both men were able to separate the political and the personal.
10Mr Whitlam formed the view that Mr Fraser's blocking of supply was part of politics and parliamentary strategising, Professor Hocking says.
11"I think you can safely say Whitlam never forgave John Kerr but he could with Fraser because he put that in the box of political activity," she said.
12By the mid 1980s there was a thaw in the relationship and Fraser and Whitlam made their first joint public appearance when they shared a stage in 1991 at a Melbourne rally against concentration of media ownership.
13"At the time this was seen as a very public reconciliation and it was a sign of how their politics had moved together," Prof Hocking said.
14Mr Fraser quit the Liberal Party in 2009, saying it had become conservative rather than liberal, but Prof Hocking says Mr Fraser underwent a shift in his politics and drew closer to Mr Whitlam - though not on all points.
15Prof Hocking asked Mr Whitlam once about his relationship with Mr Fraser.
16"He said to me: `Well, Malcolm has improved'."
17Mr Fraser shared views with Mr Whitlam more than many realise: the Fraser government carried on with some of the key reforms introduced by the Whitlam government in areas including Aboriginal land rights and multiculturalism - to the discomfort of some in the Liberal ranks.
18When the Whitlam Institute established its Gough Whitlam Oration series in 2011, the first lecture was by Julia Gillard; the second, in 2012, was delivered by Mr Fraser.
19Mr Fraser spoke on Politics, Independence and the National Interest and said of his old rival: "As political antagonists we had substantial differences, but as Australians we had shared interests and concerns".
20While Prof Hocking characterises their relationship as "a political rapprochement", Mr Sidoti believes it was a true friendship.
21In recent years, the two would meet whenever Mr Fraser was in Sydney.
22"Their relationship was quite remarkable and very genuine," he said.
