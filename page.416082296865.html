The first wearable you'll actually want to wear - and the answer to obsessively checking your mobile: Dailymail.com's verdict on the Apple Watch

1For the last week, I've been one of the lucky few outside Apple to have one.
2While it's not the first smartwatch on the market, it is the first you'll actually want to use - and will change the way you communicate.
3The Apple Watch - while it's not the first smartwatch on the market, it is the first you'll actually want to use - and will change the way you communicate, at least if you're a phone addict.
4As a phone addict, I can rarely go ten minutes without checking my handset.
5However, after a day or two with the Apple Watch, a strange thing happened - I completely stopped checking my phone.
6The haptic engine means when an alert comes in, you simply receive a 'tap' on the wrist - and a single glance down shows you exactly what you need to know.
7It's a huge change in the way we communicate, making it more personal, and more socially acceptable to stay in touch - a quick tap on your wrist to reply means you are unlikely to annoy whoever you're with (until you take a call on the watch and start talking into your wrist, admittedly).
8It's a beautifully made device, and I went for the 42mm stainless steel Apple Watch, the larger of the two sizes available (the other being 38mm).
9I also got to try out three of the bands - the brown leather loop strap, the Milanese loop, and the white sport band.
10While in pictures it looks a little chunky, on the wrist its rounded edges make it incredibly comfortable - and both the Milanese loop and the brown leather loop strap are held in place by magnets, making them easy to adjust.
11The first challenge I faced, probably along with many other buyers, is that I don't usually wear a watch.
12Even the act of looking down at my wrist again took a few days to get used to.
13Most of the time you wear it, the watch screen is off.
14But thanks to some smart software and sensors, when you move your wrist to look at it, the display appears.
15This works surprisingly well, and only very occasionally did I find myself looking down and not seeing the display pop on.
16The initial watch face can be easily swapped, and customised to your heart's content to show everything from the sun's position in the sky to a dancing Mickey Mouse.
17The screen is superb - bright with deep rich colours, and Apple has taken full advantage of this with some of its face designs, which feature blooming flowers and jellyfish gliding behind the time.
18You can change colours and the information displayed - known as 'complications', a very un-Apple like term used by the watch industry.
19I found I changed faces more than I expected - it's rather nice being able to have a clear display with your diary during the day, but switch to Mickey at weekends.
20The real key to the watch is the haptic engine, which is how it tells you you have a text message, email, or even need to change direction when you're using the maps app.
21Using an accompanying app, you can manage these notifications - and this is the key to using the watch - getting them at the right level so they aren't annoying.
22You can also use Apple's VIP email function to do the same for emails, so you only get alerts when certain people send you a message.
23The Milanese loop (left) and the white sports strap - two of the bands we tested with the stainless steel version of the Apple Watch.
24These cost $699 ( 599) and $599 ( 519) respectively - although you can buy extra straps separately.
25Some of the 'glances' on the Apple watch, showing your fitness data (left) and your location in the Maps app (right).
26Rather than the annoying loud buzz and vibration of other gadgets, the haptic engine is extremely subtle - and feels almost like you're being tapped on the wrist.
27It's an extremely personal way to notify people - and nobody around you is aware of it.
28Every time you take the Watch off your wrist, it locks, and you'll need to put in your passcode before it will do anything once you put it back on.
29Getting around the Watch also takes a little getting used to.
30It is completely alien the first few times you try it, with the combination of touchscreen, the digital crown and the contacts button.
31However, after a day or two, it becomes second nature, and the digital crown in particular is an astonishingly well made piece of hardware, feeling just the right amount of solid as you use it to scroll through longer lists without obscuring the screen.
32With the current, very limited crop of apps, the digital crown is only really used to scroll through long lists - such as email, messages and music.
33However, once more apps appear it is expected it to play a larger role in how the watch works.
34One of the easiest ways to communicate using the Watch is via the messages app.
35This allows you to use Siri to dictate replies, and to send new animated emoji (which you can even customise, changing their colour).
36You can even bypass the interface all together, and just raise the watch and say 'Hey, Siri' to reply.
37However, Apple has also added some new features to allow you to communicate with other Watch owners by sending drawings, taps and even your heartbeat.
38It's great fun, and the doodling app is particularly good.
39There's also a built in microphone and speaker, meaning you can make calls on the Watch.
40While being British means I was unable to test this in public without being extremely embarrassed at talking ostentatiously into my wrist, it actually works well - and calls are surprisingly clear.
41While for longer messages you'll probably want to turn to your phone or computer, for short replies, the Apple Watch is more than adequate - particularly as you can set your own stock responses, and they will also adapt to the message, taking a guess at what you are most likely to say.
42Being able to quickly tap out a reply without opening your phone is a huge timesaver and also socially far less annoying for everyone around you.
43There are also some new gestures to learn with the watch.
44Swiping up from the bottom of the screen shows you glances - small pieces of information such as the weather - your heart rate and the music controls.
45But there are times when the information takes a while to update and appear, and this is something Apple needs to improve with app updates.
46Bear in mind the company has until 24 April when customers actually have the watch in their hands to do this.
47Despite this, some of the glances are simply jaw-droppingly good.
48For instance, the Transit App which, here in New York, shows me how long I'll have to wait for the next subway train to take me home.
49Swiping down shows you your notifications, and a red dot appears when you have them waiting.
50Much has been made of the battery life of the watch, and in use, it's surprisingly good.
51During my week with the Watch, it didn't once run out of power - even when using the fitness tracker function to track my morning dog walks.
52However, you will need to charge it every night, using a magnetic charger you pop onto the back of the phone - so it can't track your sleep.
53The main screen of the watch, which shows icons.
54You can tap them with a finger, or zoom in using the scroll wheel.
55Apple says over 1,000 apps have now been submitted for the Watch.
56One of the key features of the Watch, according to Tim Cook at least, are its fitness functions, and these work very well.
57It'll track your movement as standard, but there's also a fitness app which will also keep an eye on your heart rate and offer more advanced capabilities.
58It can also remind you every hour to stand up if you haven't - which can feel a little Big Brother-esque at first, but I actually rather grew to like it - even if it did mean I drank even more coffee than normal.
59The Watch also includes Passbook, which is incredibly useful.
60I was able to to check in for flights using it - although be warned it won't fit under the scanners used at many boarding gates - and it also includes Apple Pay, so in the US at least, you can use your watch to pay with a tap.
61I found it worked flawlessly on our Apple Pay enabled office vending machines.
62Once you've paired your iPhone with the Watch, a new app appears on your phone to let you see your data.
63On the watch, there's also a fitness app.
64Additionally, you can set stand goals - this means the watch will tell you every hour if you haven't stood up - a welcome reminder to take a break (and you can turn it off for those long days in the office).
65It can feel a little Big Brother-esque at first, but I actually rather grew to like them - even if it did mean I drank even more coffee than normal.
66Measuring your heartrate does mean you need to keep the watch relatively tight to your skin.
67On the back of the Watch's case, a ceramic cover with sapphire lenses sits above a sensor that uses infrared and visible-light LEDs and photodiodes to detect the wearer's heart rate.
68While using the Milanese loop, this was initially an issue, especially if running.
69However, I found swapping to the rubber sports band made a huge difference - and it's extremely comfortable to wear, even when playing squash.
70Apple Watch uses this sensor, along with an accelerometer and the GPS and Wi-Fi in a connected iPhone, to measure physical movement such as steps, distance and calories.
71Will the Apple Watch change your life?
72Well, possibly more than many realise until they start using it.
73After a day or two with the watch, a strange thing happened - I completely stopped checking my phone.
74While Apple may be the firm that really created phone addiction, with the Watch they may have cured it.
75Apple undoubtedly has another hit on its hands.
76As more and more apps appear, and developers actually get to spend time with it, I fully expect it be used in ways we can't even imagine now.
77There were only a few apps available for us to try, but some like travel app Transit and Citymapper show what the Watch is capable of - and expect an explosion in the number of apps once developers get their hands on the hardware.
78Much like the iPhone, apps could propel this to become a new computing platform - and even with the features it already has, the Apple watch is the most elegant way to communicate digitally.
79There are a huge range of bands available for the Watch - with Apple hoping many owners will buy more than one, and they can easily be swapped.

99Gestures: Following last year's unveiling, Apple admitted the Watch is unlike any device it has ever made.
100It said it has 'reinvented all-new ways to select navigate and input that are suited to a smaller device worn on the wrist'.
101The Watch is also said to be able to sense force adding 'a new dimension to the user interface'.
102Force Touch uses electrodes around the flexible display to distinguish between a light tap and a deep press, and trigger instant access to a range of contextually specific controls - such as an action menu in Messages, or a mode that lets you select different watch faces.
103Digital Crown: On mechanical watches, the crown has historically been used to set the time and date and to wind the mainspring.
104Apple has adapted this and its 'digital' crown can be used to magnify content on the small display.
105Rotating the digital crown replaces the typical pinching motion used on iPhones and iPads and allows wearers to zoom and scroll without obstructing the screen.
106Pressing the crown will also return wearers to the home screen.
107Watch OS: The Watch OS software is tweaked it to better suit the watch's power, display size and functions.
108Apple has also invented a new typeface to make the screen easier to read.
109Sapphire display: Apple Watches have a laminated Retina display made from a 'single crystal of sapphire.' While on the Sport collection the screen has the added protection of Ion-X glass.
110Glances: The Glances feature shows info users would like to see, similar to Google Now, and is accessed by swiping the screen up from the bottom.
111For instance, wearers can glimpse the weather forecast, check out what's next on their calendar, or find their location on a map.
112They can then swipe through different Glances, or tap on one to go directly to its corresponding app for more details.
113Music can also be controlled on an iPhone through the Apple Watch.
114Vibration and notifications: The Watch has a built-in is a 'taptic engine' that responds to a subtle vibrations users feel on their wrist for notifications.
115Any notification that appears on the connected phone will appear on the Watch.
116This is made from a linear actuator, or motor, inside the Apple Watch that produces haptic feedback.
117The wearer can then respond to these messages directly or swipe down to see the Notification Centre.
118This haptic feedback could also be used with Apple Maps, for example, to help people navigate.
119It will produced gentle vibrations to signal when to turn left or right.
120The Watch additionally understands questions in messages and offers pre-selected answers, and messages can be dictated to the iPhone.
121And this taptic engine means people can get someone's attention with a gentle tap or send their heartbeat remotely.
122There is no keyboard on the Watch, and messages can only be sent through dictation, or emoji.
123Siri is also built into the Apple Watch.
124Heart rate sensor: On the back of the Watch's case, a ceramic cover with sapphire lenses sits above a sensor that uses infrared and visible-light LEDs and photodiodes to detect the wearer's heart rate.
125Inductive charging: The Watch uses Apple's MagSafe technology with inductive charging.
126This means it can be charged wirelessly by placing a connector near the back of the watch where magnets cause it to snap into place automatically.
127MagSafe is the name of the connector Apple uses with its MacBook range.
