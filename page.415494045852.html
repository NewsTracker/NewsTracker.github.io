54 dead after bus plunges down mountainside in Brazil

Benjamin Netanyahu plays the hardline settlement expansion card to capture right-wing voters

1The dead include at least eight young children, three adolescents, 24 women and 14 men, the government of Santa Catarina state said in a statement earlier Sunday when the death toll stood at 49.
2There was no breakdown by age or gender given on the five additional casualties reported later.
3The bus plunged nearly 400 metres down a mountain near the city of Joinville on Saturday.
4Ten people were transferred to nearby hospitals with severe injuries, and only one patient has been reported in stable condition.
5Officials earlier warned that the death toll could rise because more bodies could be found under the bus and in the woods.
6Police officers and emergency personnel started working on removing the bus on Sunday from the mountainous area in Serra Dona Francisca.
7Later, Brazilian President Dilma Rousseff said the number of people confirmed killed had risen to 54, and she sent her condolences to their families.
8"In this hour of pain and suffering, I want to extend my sympathies to the families and friends who lost their loved ones," Rousseff said in a statement.
9The bus crashed on a curvy road that winds through a verdant green region popular for hiking and biking in Santa Catarina state, located about 950 kilometres southwest of Rio de Janeiro.
10Although the cause of the accident is under investigation, authorities suspect the driver may have lost control on a curve, causing the bus to run off the road.
11The group was travelling from Uniao da Vitoria for a religious event near the coast in Parana state.
12The bus company could not be reached for comment.
1Jerusalem: Warning his supporters there was a real danger Tuesday's election could bring a leftist government to power in Israel, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu vowed to continue with his controversial settlement expansion program in a last-ditch appeal to right-wing voters.
2Speaking at a rally made up mostly of settlers and national religious voters in Tel Aviv, the prime minister promised his government would never divide Jerusalem and that - in contravention of international law - it would keep building settlements in occupied East Jerusalem and the West Bank.
3"As long as the Likud is in power, there will be no concessions or withdrawals [from the occupied territories]," he told the cheering crowds.
4Unofficial estimates indicated the turnout was smaller than a rally of left-wing voters a week earlier.
5Mr Netanyahu's Likud Party is trailing by at least four seats in the latest polls behind the rival Zionist Union, a centre-left alliance of the Labor Party, headed by Isaac Herzog and Hatnua, headed by Tzipi Livni, who was justice minister in the ruling coalition before Mr Netanyahu called early elections last December.
6His campaign's focus on security issues and in particular, Iran's nuclear program, has left many voters cold - they say that cost of living and affordable housing are their top priorities.
7The last opinion polls before the March 17 election were published on Friday - they estimate the Zionist Union would win between 24 and 26 seats in the 120-seat Knesset, compared to 20-22 seats for Mr Netanyahu's Likud Party.
8Likud's poor polling promoted a surge of frenzied campaigning from Mr Netanyahu, including what many have interpreted as a "paranoid" Facebook post in which he accused a conspiracy of foreign governments and left-wing parties of trying to topple his government.
9"Leftist activists and the foreign and international media are conspiring to get Tzipi [Livni] and Buji [the nickname of Isaac Herzog] elected via illegitimate means, using innuendo and foreign money," Mr Netanyahu wrote on his Facebook page.
10He warned: "Those foreign organisations understand that if Tzipi and Buji are in charge, they will give up everything. They'll withdraw to the 1967 boundaries and they will divide Jerusalem."
11He repeated those accusations at the Tel Aviv rally: "Our rivals are investing a huge effort to harm me and the Likud, to open a gap between my party, the Likud, and [our competition], and if we don't close this gap, there is a real danger that a left-wing government will rise to power," Mr Netanyahu said.
12The leader of the far-right Jewish Home Party, Naftali Bennett, also addressed the rally, saying these elections would decide whether "Israel remains a Jewish State".
13But it is not just voters who care about economic issues who have turned against the ruling Likud Party, it is also security experts who have despaired at Mr Netanyahu's insistence on focusing on Iran above all else.
14"You and you alone turned the United States from an ally into an enemy," Shabtai Shavit, a former chief of the Mossad spy agency, said of Mr Netanyahu's actions, including his widely-condemned Iran speech to the US Congress earlier this month.
15Another retired Mossad chief, Meir Dagan, has also been outspoken in his criticisms of the prime minister's security credentials, saying he had a "lack of vision and loss of direction".
16Whichever party wins, it will have to form a coalition in order to lead the government.
