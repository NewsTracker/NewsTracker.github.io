Mexico's 1st Independent Candidate Leads in Exit Polls

1Mexico's ruling party has lost seats in congress but may gain a majority from its coalition with the small but growing Green Party that was everywhere during the campaign, according to official voting trends released late Sunday by the electoral institute.
2Voters also elected Mexico's first independent governor after a reform allowing unaffiliated candidates, a move seen as a jolt to all political parties.
3Independent Jaime Rodriguez, known as "El Bronco," had at least a 6-point lead in the race in Nuevo Leon state, according to exit polls conducted by the television network TV Azteca and other media.
4The margin of error in the Azteca survey, however, was 3.8 percentage points.
5The ruling party candidate, Ivonne Alvarez, acknowledged the vote was close.
6Rodriguez's popularity in a state that is home to the business hub of Monterrey was attributed to voters' disgust with all political parties, each with its own corruption scandals.
7"Nuevo Leon has shown that through the ballot box, you can change things," Rodriguez said in addressing his supporters, adding that he will wait for the official count to confirm his victory.
8"We're going to give the parties six years of vacation."
9His support harkens back to 2000, when another plainspoken cowboy candidate, Vicente Fox, managed to topple the PRI's 71-year rule and win the presidency for the opposition National Action Party.
10The voting, marred by sporadic outbursts of violence, was seen as a litmus test for President Enrique Pena Nieto and his Institutional Revolutionary Party.
11The PRI lost seats, according to the official early count.
12But a surge in Green Party representatives, from 27 in the current congress to as many as 48, could give the PRI-Green coalition 251 of 500 votes, a majority the party has lacked until now.
13Protesters burned ballot boxes in several restive states of southern Mexico in an attempt to disrupt elections, but officials said the vote was proceeding satisfactorily despite "isolated incidents."
14"Considering the challenges we faced in this election, the balance has been positive," electoral institute head Lorenzo Cordova said in a national address after polls closed.
15Thousands of soldiers and federal police guarded polling stations where violence and calls for boycotts threatened to mar elections for 500 seats in the lower house of Congress, nine of 31 governorships and hundreds of mayors and local officials.
16The elections drew a turnout of between 47 and 48 percent, the electoral institute said.
17Midterm elections usually draw light turnout, but attention was unusually high this time as a loose coalition of radical teachers' unions and activists vowed to block the vote.
18The teachers' demands include wage hikes, an end to teacher testing and the safe return of 42 missing students from a radical teachers' college.
19Those students disappeared in September, and prosecutors say they were killed and incinerated by a drug gang.
20Protesters burned at least seven ballot boxes and election materials in Tixtla, the Guerrero state town where the teachers' college is located.
21Soon after, there was an exchange of rock-throwing between protesters and hundreds of people who said they intended to defend their right to vote.
22There were no immediate reports of injuries.
