1A jury in the New York suburbs found Lacey Spears, of Scottsville, Kentucky, guilty of second-degree murder in the death last year of 5-year-old Garnett-Paul Spears.
2The defence portrayed Spears as a caring mother and her son as sickly, but the prosecution argued Spears reveled in the attention his illness brought her.
3Video showed Spears twice taking him into a hospital bathroom with a connector tube and him suffering afterward.
4"The motive is bizarre, the motive is scary, but it exists," Assistant District Attorney Patricia Murphy said in closing arguments Thursday.
5"She apparently craved the attention of her family, her friends, her co-workers and most particularly the medical profession."
6She suggested that Spears, 27, eventually killed the boy because she feared he would start telling people she was making him ill.
7Spears' actions were "nothing short of torture," she said.
8Several doctors testified there was no medical explanation for the spike in Garnett's sodium levels that led to his death.
9But defence lawyer Stephen Riebling said there was no "direct evidence" of a crime and drew out from witnesses that Spears seemed devastated by her son's death.
10He said the hospital video was edited to eliminate tender scenes between mother and son, including one in which she puts two pairs of socks on him.
11"If she's planning on killing him, why does she care whether his feet are cold?" he asked the jury.
12He also said the hospital was negligent and dehydrated the boy, an assertion Murphy called "ridiculous."
13The evidence included two feeding bags found in Spears' apartment that were heavily tainted with salt, including one Spears asked a friend to hide.
14One bag had the equivalent of 69 McDonald's salt packets in it, a forensic toxicologist testified.
15Also in evidence were many of Spears' postings on Facebook, Twitter, MySpace and a blog and her online research into the dangers of sodium in children.
16Spears, an Alabama native, was living with her son in Chestnut Ridge, New York, when he died.
17She moved to Kentucky afterward and was living there when she was arrested.
18There was no mention at the trial of a disorder known as Munchausen by proxy, in which caretakers secretly harm children to win sympathy.
19Some experts believe that disorder fits Spears' actions.
20Westchester County's Journal News reported in May that the boy's father had never met him but read that he was dying in the hospital.
21The father, Chris Hill, who lives in Athens, Alabama, sent Spears a friend request on Facebook.
22On Jan. 23, 2014, the day the boy died, Hill posted a message on her Facebook page, copying a photo of the boy and saying: "Even though I don't get to see him, I feel like he's been with me this hole time. IM not going to lie. I cried for hours when I found this out, and it will continue to hurt till the day I die."

2(AP) - One of three men arrested on charges of plotting to help the Islamic State group wage war against the U.S. was ordered transferred from Florida to New York on Tuesday.
3A federal judge in Jacksonville ordered Abror Habibov's transfer during a detention hearing.
4He is scheduled to be in New York by March 11.
5Habibov was arrested in Jacksonville last week and charged with attempt and conspiracy to provide material support to a terrorist organization.
6The 30-year-old Habibov ran cellphone repair kiosks at malls in Florida, Georgia and Virginia, and federal prosecutors say he helped fund 19-year-old Akhror Saidakhmetov's efforts to join the Islamic State group in Syria.
7Saidakhmetov and another man, 24-year-old Abdurasul Hasanovich Juraboev, were arrested in Brooklyn.
8According to federal prosecutors, Saidakhmetov is a citizen of Kazakhstan, and worked for a time with Habibov at the kiosks.
9Habibov, a native of Uzbekistan whose visa had expired, is being without bail in Florida.
10One of the kiosks he operated was in a suburban Jacksonville mall, but there were no state business records with Habibov listed as an owner.
11The kiosk had been removed from the floor as of Tuesday.
12Both Saidakhmetov and Juraboev threatened attacks in the U.S. and planned to travel to Syria to join the Islamic State group.
13According to the federal complaint, Habibov was mainly a source of planning and money.
14On Tuesday, Habibov appeared in court dressed in jeans, Adidas sneakers and a black sweatshirt.
15His black hair was mussed and he had a beard.
16His attorney, Maurice Grant, a federal public defender, declined comment outside the courtroom.
17Inside, Habibov waived his right to hearings to confirm his identity and to a preliminary hearing in Jacksonville so that he could be transferred back to New York.
18Habibov answered "yes" when asked if he understood all he was waiving, and through an Uzbek interpreter asked the court to ensure he would be able to make phone calls in New York.
