FIFA leaders return to Zurich, ready to pick election date

1GENEVA (AP) " FIFA leaders this weekend return to the scene of a criminal investigation which sent football's governing body into crisis eight weeks ago.
2On Monday in Zurich, the FIFA executive committee should pick an election date to choose a successor for Sepp Blatter, president for 17 years.
3FIFA has been dogged by allegations of corruption for much of Blatter's time at the top of world football.
4It is the committee's first meeting since the newly re-elected Blatter announced on June 2 that he would leave office within months, under pressure from American and Swiss federal investigations of bribery and corruption in the award of broadcasting and hosting rights for international competitions.
5Some around the executive table want Blatter gone sooner " ideally on Monday.
6Still, Blatter wants to hang on and shape overdue reforms of FIFA now being demanded even by long-loyal World Cup sponsor Coca-Cola.
7ARREST DEVELOPMENTS?
8Will Swiss police intervene again and arrest or question members of the FIFA executive committee and their international entourages?
9At least one FIFA official will be absent.
10Marco Polo Del Nero, Brazil's delegate and president of its football confederation, has told FIFA he needs to stay at home.
11On May 28, he unexpectedly left Zurich one day after the arrest of seven football and marketing officials in dawn raids at a luxury Zurich hotel.
12He missed the FIFA congress that re-elected Blatter a day later.
13In the separate Swiss case, some of the 10 FIFA voters in the 2018-2022 World Cup bid contests who Swiss prosecutors said in May are expected to be interviewed.
14One of the 10 voters, FIFA senior vice president Issa Hayatou, returned to his native Cameroon after the congress saying that no Swiss police had approached him during his stay.
15The Confederation of African Football told The Associated Press this week that there has been "no request done by Swiss police so far" to arrange meetings this time in Zurich.
16CAF said its FIFA delegates will "always be available to speak, be it to Swiss police or other investigative authority within FIFA or outside."
17The Swiss Attorney General's office declined to give details to the AP on Friday of planned activity around Monday's executive session.
18It is unclear how much progress American and Swiss investigators have made since May 27.
19Just two of the 14 men indicted in May " suspended FIFA vice president Jeffrey Webb of the Cayman Islands and Traffics Sports USA president Aaron Davidson " are in the United States, and Webb was extradited only on Wednesday.
20Swiss prosecutors are sifting through massive amounts of data seized from FIFA plus 81 "suspicious acts" of possible money laundering reported by banks in Switzerland.
21BLATTER'S LAST STAND?
22The FIFA president wants to stay for a few more months but could a UEFA-driven move to oust him succeed on Monday?
23While UEFA and its president Michel Platini will not publicly discuss strategy, it is no secret that some senior European officials want Blatter gone quickly.
24They may have support in other continents.
25FIFA rules require that Hayatou " hardly a modernizer " would step up as interim president until the election.
26If UEFA can't oust Blatter, it certainly wants an early election.
27A four-month campaign after the deadline for applications is required by FIFA rules that were passed in Blatter's previous presidential term.
28Would-be candidates also need time to gather nominations from five of the 209 FIFA member associations.
29UEFA has briefed for a Dec. 16 ballot, though that clashes with the Club World Cup played Dec. 10-20 in Japan.
30Blatter suggested February more likely, giving him a few extra weeks.
31One logical option is after the Ballon d'Or ceremony on Jan. 12, which already brings FIFA's hierarchy and international media to Zurich where the election should be held.
32Asked by the AP this week about their preferred option, FIFA's six confederations either did not respond or did not state one.
33Former Brazil great Zico and Liberia football federation president Musa Bility said they will seek nominations.
34Diego Maradona's apparent interest was an attention-grabbing headline but only that.
35Platini is the obvious favorite and Africa's plans should be clearer after Hayatou chairs a scheduled CAF executive meeting on Aug. 6 in Cairo.
36The recent FIFA rule that would-be candidates must have an active role in football for two of the past five years excludes outsiders.
37Prince Ali bin al-Hussein of Jordan has kept a low-key silence since getting 73 votes in his defeat to Blatter on May 29.
38Monday's meeting should also set an agenda for the election congress.
39Blatter wants this to include a slate of reforms detailed in his June 2 speech: Term limits, vetting officials for integrity, publish salaries and bonuses.
40He later added increasing the size of the executive committee.
41The FIFA leader would devote his last few months to leaving a better legacy, working alongside Domenico Scala, FIFA's audit and compliance panel chairman and election monitor.
42An obvious question: Why should the man who presided over such a tarnished organization be allowed to shape its recovery?
43On Friday, one of FIFA's most significant sponsors, Coca-Cola, finally spoke out against Blatter.
44It called for "eminent, impartial leaders" to lead reform, following a similar plea by Transparency International.
45The name of Kofi Annan, the former United Nations secretary general from Ghana and a noted football fan, is increasingly raised.
46Monday's meeting and debate could be shaped by a meeting of Blatter with the six continental confederation presidents at 5 p.m. (1500 GMT) on Sunday.
47It was on May 28 with this group that Platini took Blatter aside and urged him to go immediately.
48Blatter refused, was re-elected and is still there.
