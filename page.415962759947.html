Kenya mourns 148 dead in university attack by militants

1The call by Elizabeth Namarome Musinai at dawn Thursday was one of several her family received as the attack and hostage drama unfolded at Garissa University College, where gunmen from the al-Shabab militant group killed 148 people.
2Then, about 1 p.m., a man got on the line to demand that Kenyan President Uhuru Kenyatta be contacted within two minutes and told to remove troops from neighboring Somalia, where they are fighting al-Shabab extremists.
3He phoned back promptly.
4When told the president had not been contacted, he said, "I am going to kill your daughter."
5Three gunshots followed, and he hung up.
6When Elizabeth's father, Fred Kaskon Musinai, called the man back, he said he was told: "She is now with her God."
7Musinai said he is still hanging on to hope that Elizabeth somehow survived, although she is not on the list of wounded, which now numbers 104.
8He has traveled from his home in Kitale to Nairobi, where the dead are being brought to a morgue for families to identify and claim.
9Survivors and relatives gave other harrowing accounts of the siege by Islamic extremists as Kenya on Friday mourned the victims of the attack, the deadliest since the 1998 bombing of the U.S. Embassy in Nairobi that killed more than 200 people.
10At the Nairobi morgue, screaming and crying family members were assisted by Red Cross staffers, who tried to console them.
11Archbishop John Njue, who conducted Good Friday services in the capital, cited the "murdered" students and said, "This is a tremendous challenge in our country."
12Pope Francis condemned the attack as an act of "senseless brutality" and called for those responsible to change their violent ways.
13In a telegram of condolence, Francis also urged Kenyan authorities to work to bring an end to such attacks and "hasten the dawn of a new era of brotherhood, justice and peace."
14The gunmen singled out Christians at the university, killing them on the spot.
15But Muslims also were among the dead, as were women, even though the attackers had said at one point that they, too, would be spared.
16The masked attackers - strapped with explosives and armed with AK-47s - battled troops and police before the violence ended after about 13 hours.
17In announcing an updated figure of 148 people killed by the gunmen, Interior Minister Joseph Nkaissery said 142 of the dead were students, three were policemen and three were soldiers.
18Police worked at the Garissa campus Friday, taking fingerprints from the bodies of the four slain gunmen and of the students and security officials who died, for identification purposes.
19Security forces guarded the gates of the school.
20Its slogans on the wall outside said "Oasis of Innovation" and "A World Class University of Technological Processes and Development."
21Elsewhere in Garissa, soldiers blocked a group of women that approached a military-controlled site where students were awaiting evacuation, prompting several women to collapse, shrieking, in the dust for several minutes.
22A bystander said the son of one of the women had died in the attack.
23Survivor Helen Titus said one of the first things the al-Shabab gunmen did when they entered the campus was to head for a lecture hall where Christians were in prayer.
24"They investigated our area. They knew everything," said Titus, a Christian, who was being treated in Garissa for a bullet wound to the wrist.
25The 21-year-old English literature student told The Associated Press that she smeared blood from classmates on her face and hair and played dead at one point.
26The gunmen also told students hiding in dormitories to come out, assuring them that they would not be killed, Titus said.
27"We just wondered whether to come out or not," she said.
28Many students did, and the gunmen shot them anyway.
29Esther Wanjiru said she was awake at the time of the attack.
30Asked if she lost anyone, she said: "My best friend."
31Another survivor, Nina Kozel, said she was awakened by screaming and that many students ran to the fences and jumped over them.
32Some suffered bruises, she said.
33Many were unable to escape and hid in vain under beds and in closets in their rooms, she added.
34"They were shot there and then," she said, adding that the killers shouted "God is great" in Arabic.
35Those who surrendered were either selected for killing, or freed in some cases, apparently because they were Muslim, she said.
36A spokesman for the group, Ali Mohamud Rage, said it was responsible for the attack.
37Al-Shabab has struck several times in Kenya, including the siege at the Westgate Mall in Nairobi in 2013 that killed 67 people, to retaliate against Kenya for sending troops to Somalia in 2011 to fight the militants and stabilize the government in Mogadishu.
38Somali President Hassan Sheikh Mohamud called for stronger collaboration between his country and Kenya to defeat al-Shabab.
39The U.N. Security Council expressed outrage - a word it rarely uses - in condemning the attack.
40A statement approved by all 15 council members paid tribute to Kenya's role in fighting "terrorism" - especially its role in the African Union's mission in Somalia against al-Shabab.
41The White House said President Barack Obama called Kenyatta to express condolences "for the lives lost during the heinous terrorist attack."
42Obama, who is scheduled to visit Nairobi in July, "emphasized his support for the government and people of Kenya," according to a statement.
43It added that he and Kenyatta would discuss how to strengthen counterterrorism cooperation.
44Some Kenyans were angry the Kenyan government didn't take sufficient security precautions.
45The attack came six days after Britain advised "against all but essential travel" to parts of Kenya, including Garissa.
46A day before the attack, Kenyatta dismissed that warning as well as an Australian one pertaining to Nairobi and Mombasa, saying: "Kenya is safe as any country in the world. The travel advisories being issued by our friends are not genuine."
47Previous travel warnings have hurt the country's tourism industry.
48Babu Owino, the chairman of the Students Organization for Nairobi University, said the government's behavior shows it is not serious in fighting extremist attacks.
49A small group of demonstrators walked down a main road in Garissa with signs that read "We are against the killing of innocent Kenyans!!!! We are tired!!" and "Enough is enough. No more killing!! We are with you, our fellow Kenyans."
50"We feel very sorry for them and we condemn the attack," demonstrator Abdullahi Muktar said of the victims.
51Odula reported from Nairobi, Kenya.
52Abdi Guled in Mogadishu, Somalia, and Edith M. Lederer at the United Nations contributed to this report.
