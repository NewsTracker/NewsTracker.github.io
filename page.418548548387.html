Why Germany refuses to write off Greece's debts

1The International Monetary Fund says Greece's debts are too big to pay and need to be partly forgiven.
2Germany says that's out of the question.
3German officials, led by Chancellor Angela Merkel and Finance Minister Wolfgang Schaeuble, say that as part of a new rescue package for Greece they will consider giving it more time to pay its debts and perhaps lower interest rates.
4But no outright cut on what it owes.
5If pressed, Germany officials concede the IMF may be right in saying that Greece's debt pile is too big to pay, no matter how many years it gets to pay it.
6Still, they exclude any write-offs.
7The German position - joined by several other countries, including Finland and the Netherlands - seems to leave Greece in a tight spot: The debt might not be payable, but must not be reduced.
8Here are five reasons why Germany may be taking that stance.
9It's taxpayers' money, and these are elected officials in a country where bailing out Greeks for previous profligacy is not popular.
10It particularly unloved among members of Merkel's and Schaeuble's conservative party.
11Countries that have been through economic crises, like Spain, are also taking a tough position on Greece since they did the hard work of reducing their own debts through painful spending cuts.
12The European Union treaty forbids countries from taking on each other's debts and, says Schaeuble, rules are rules.
13That was a basic provision installed way back before the euro was founded, in part to ensure that the shared currency wouldn't mean Germany paying for other people's mistakes.
14Of course, the European Union found a way to get around it to do the earlier bailouts, by citing an emergency relief clause that's also in the treaty.
15Give one country a break, and others may wonder why they can't have one, too.
16Ireland, Portugal, Cyprus and Spain all got bailouts and imposed tough cuts along with pro-business reforms; those countries are beginning to see recoveries.
17Handing the left-wing Syriza government in Greece its chief demand, debt forgiveness, would encourage other anti-austerity political movements, such as Podemos in Spain.
18Spain, Portugal and Ireland all have general elections in the next nine months.
19Future eurozone governments may look at the debt writedown and feel less compelled to keep debt under control, an issue economists call "moral hazard."
20In other words, bailouts encourage risk-taking.
21German Finance Ministry spokesman Martin Jaeger says that the European approach of making countries' debt sustainable by getting budgets in shape has worked well elsewhere.
22He said Germany still believes that approach can work in Greece.
23Why promise Greece a haircut before it does more to help itself?
24Since its first bailout in 2010, Greece has been promising reforms of its wage-bargaining system, expensive pensions, shaky tax collection, clogged legal system and sweetheart deals for trades with restricted entry.
25Somehow it all never quite gets done.
26Negotiations for a third Greek bailout package of about 85 billion euros will include conditions such as spending restraint, pension reform and pro-business changes that would ultimately improve Greece's ability to pay.
27They also include a promise to ease the terms of Greece's debt.
28The IMF's warning Tuesday that Greece needs debt relief going "far beyond what Europe has been willing to consider so far."
29It isn't proposing to write off its own loans to Greece, saying it represents many poorer countries around the world.
