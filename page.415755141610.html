Germanwings co-pilot Andreas Lubitz was '100% fit to fly'

1And at some point, investigators said Thursday, he became intent on ending his life.
2Andreas Guenter Lubitz locked himself in the cockpit and said nothing as the pilot tried to break down the door Tuesday, authorities said.
3Flight data recordings indicate that he breathed normally as he steered the plane into the mountainside.
4Lubitz intended to kill himself, Marseille Public Prosecutor Brice Robin said.
5Authorities refused to label it a "suicide" since he was also responsible for the deaths of so many others.
6Lubitz had logged only 100 hours with that type of plane, an Airbus A320, before the incident.
7He had flown a total of 630 hours since he was hired by Germanwings in September 2013.
8Lubitz passed all his medical exams, and showed no signs of bipolar disorder, substance abuse, personality disorder or other conditions, according to The Aviation Business Gazette.
9"He was 100 percent fit to fly without any restrictions," Carsten Spohr, CEO of Germanwings' parent company Lufthansa, told reporters Thursday.
10"His flight performance was perfect. There was nothing to worry about."
11Flying was Lubitz's dream, according to LSC Westerwald, the German flying club of which he was a member since he was about 14 or 15 years old.
12The man from Montabaur, about 40 miles from Frankfurt, showed his passion for flying early, earning a gliders license as a teen, records show.
13Lubitz graduated from the demanding German preparatory school Mons-Tabor High School in 2007, a year before he began training with Lufthansa in Bremen, Germany, and Phoenix, Ariz., Spohr said.
14He took a "several-month gap" in training in 2009, but earned his student pilot license the next year after flying more than 1,000 hours, Spohr said.
15He spent 11 months as a Lufthansa flight attendant as he waited to become a Germanwings pilot, Spohr said.
16The same month Lubitz was hired, the Federal Aviation Administration recognized him for joining the "prestigious" ranks of other FAA-certified airmen.
17He continued to live with his parents in Montabaur, Mayor Gabriele Wieland told German newspaper Bild.
18Four police cars guarded the two-story house in a wealthy sub-division Thursday.
19The curtains were drawn, preventing the swell of reporters outside from seeing in.
20Lubitz also had a home in Dusseldorf, the intended destination of the doomed plane.
21Robin would not speculate why Lubitz wanted to die, telling reporters that he's "not in the head of the co-pilot."
22Yet Lubitz, a "rather quiet" but friendly guy, showed no signs of depression when acquaintance Peter Ruecker saw him last fall.
23"He was happy he had the job with Germanwings and he was doing well," Ruecker, who watched Lubitz learn to fly, told the Associated Press.
24"He gave off a good feeling."
25Lubitz had a girlfriend and "a lot of friends," Ruecker said.
26"He wasn't a loner."
27He had plenty of interests besides flying, according to his now-defunct Facebook page.
28He frequented night clubs and listened to pop music.
29In 2011, he ran the Lufthansa half marathon in Frankfurt in 1 hour and 48 minutes.
30Those who knew him, either from the flying club or from the neighborhood, said they were "shocked" because Lubitz was a "normal guy," Reuters reported.
31"I got to know him, or should I say reacquainted with him, as a very nice, fun and polite young man," flying club chairman Klaus Radke said.
