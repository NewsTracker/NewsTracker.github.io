Air strikes trigger final push on Iraq's Tikrit

1Iraqi forces buoyed by the first US-led coalition air strikes on Tikrit made a final push Thursday to flush diehard jihadists out of Saddam Hussein's hometown.
2Washington had been reluctant to get directly involved in a battle in which Iran-backed militias have taken the lead but the Pentagon seemed keen to reassert itself as Baghdad's chief partner in the war against the Islamic State group.
3The tussle for influence over Iraq came as Washington and Tehran began fresh nuclear talks and also as Iran's regional rival Saudi Arabia launched air strikes in Yemen.
4The US-backed air campaign against Shiite minority rebels in Yemen was announced by the Saudi ambassador in Washington and condemned by Tehran.
5The operation to retake Tikrit was launched on March 2 but had failed to dislodge a relatively small number of IS fighters who have hemmed themselves in with thousands of bombs for a last stand in the city centre.
6"The assault on the last (IS-held) pocket of Tikrit started from the southern front, in Awja," said a brigadier general from the military headquarters in Salaheddin province, of which Tikrit is the capital.
7He said Iraqi forces were also attacking from the west and north and were repairing a bridge over the Tigris that IS recently blew up to pile further pressure from the east.
8The forces involved in the fighting include the volunteer Popular Mobilisation units, Shiite militia groups, the army's counter-terrorism force as well as interior ministry units.
9Iraqi air strikes had, by Baghdad's own admission, not been efficient and accurate enough to break the back of IS resistance.
10"Now the operation to take Tikrit really begins," one US defence official told AFP.
11Lieutenant General James Terry, who oversees the command in charge of the US war effort, said precision strikes would save "innocent Iraqi lives while minimising collateral damage to infrastructure."
12The exact number of civilians trapped inside Tikrit is unclear but a Red Crescent spokesman last week said "no more than 30,000, probably quite a bit less."
13Iraqi Defence Minister Khaled al-Obeidi said Iraqi Sukhoi fighter jets also pounded Tikrit Thursday.
14And a spokesman in Paris announced the French air force took part in the coalition raids that began late Wednesday.
15"The ongoing Iraqi and coalition air strikes are setting the conditions for offensive action to be conducted by Iraqi forces currently surrounding Tikrit," Terry said.
16The US military said the first wave of coalition air raids consisted of 17 strikes that struck buildings, bridges, checkpoints, staging areas, berms and a command post.
17Washington had expressed strong reservations over the leading role played in the Tikrit operation by Shiite militia groups, some of which have been accused of serious abuses.
18Iran's top commander in charge of external operations, Qassem Suleimani, has been ubiquitous on the Salaheddin front lines and is perceived by many Iraqis as the brain behind Iraq's ground operations.
19But the Pentagon insisted Washington remained Baghdad's most precious partner in the war to reclaim the vast regions of Iraq IS conquered last summer.
20"Reliable, professional, advanced military capabilities are something that very clearly and very squarely reside with the coalition," spokesman Colonel Steven Warren said Wednesday.
21Lloyd Austin, a top US general, told US lawmakers Thursday that Shiite militias "have pulled back" from the front and that special forces and police were "clearing" Tikrit.
22A US condition for the strikes was that Iraq's government be "in charge" of all forces in the assault, he said.
23The four-star general said the offensive had been flawed because some of the forces were previously "not supervised".
24Tikrit holds both strategic and symbolic importance.
25It was the hometown of executed dictator Saddam Hussein, remnants of whose Baath party collaborated with IS last summer.
26And it is seen as a key stepping stone to recapturing Iraq's second city Mosul, the jihadists' largest hub.
27The International Crisis Group warned that, so far, advances against IS paradoxically risked undermining what is left of the Iraqi state.
28In a brief entitled "Defeating the Iraqi state, one victory at a time", the think tank said it was crucial to re-empower local elites, build credible local forces and provide services in recently reconquered areas.
29"This will only be possible if Iran... and the US... pursue this objective instead of investing respectively solely in militias and disintegrating state institutions such as the army as the expedient answers to the jihadi threat," it said.
