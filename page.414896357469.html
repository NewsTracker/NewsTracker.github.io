WHO approves 15-minute Ebola test

1The World Health Organization (WHO) has approved the first rapid test for Ebola.
2Corgenix's test is less accurate than the standard test but is easy to perform giving results within 15 minutes.
3The WHO has assessed and listed the ReEBOV Antigen Rapid Test Kit, developed by Corgenix Medical Corporation, as eligible for procurement to Ebola affected countries.
4The test was evaluated under WHO's Emergency Assessment and Use, a procedure established to provide a minimum quality, safety and performance assurance for diagnostic products in the context of the Ebola emergency.
5"This test is easy to use and perform, it does not require electricity, and can give results within 15 minutes," WHO spokesman Tarik Jasaravic said.
6The test by a German pharmaceutical company is easy to use and store.
7It comes at a time when Ebola infections in western Africa are on the rise again.
8However, procurement and roll-out of the test kits will not begin immediately since the US company is still working out the test's costs and needs a week or two more to finish administrative procedures with the US Food and Drug Administration, Jasarevic said.
9Ebola is currently being tested in laboratories largely through the detection of the virus's nucleic acid (genetic material), using commercial or in-house tests.
10Nucleic acid tests (NATs) are more accurate but are complex to use and require well-established laboratories and trained personnel, the WHO pointed out in its press release.
11Where possible, results from ReEBOV Antigen Rapid Test Kit should be confirmed by testing a new blood sample using an approved Ebola NAT.
12More than 23,000 people, mainly in West Africa, have been infected in the outbreak and 9,300 have died.
13Doctors Without Borders has published a report on the Ebola outbreak which has claimed over 10,000 lives.
14The frontline aid agency has condemned the slow global response, particularly from the World Health Organization.
15There is no way to tell when exactly the Ebola outbreak will be over in Africa, says Margaret Harris from the WHO.
16International organizations learned too late about the spread of the disease, she noted.
17Just over two weeks after the release of its last known Ebola patient, Liberian officials have confirmed a new case.
18The hardest-stricken of three African nations has already lost over 4,000 lives to the deadly virus.
