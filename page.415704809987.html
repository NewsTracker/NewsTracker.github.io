Afghan police official fired for mob-killing Facebook comment

1Hundreds of demonstrators marched in the capital on Tuesday for a second day to denounce police for allowing a crowd to beat the woman to death with planks.
2The woman, named Farkhunda, was killed and set on fire last week in front of police officers after she was accused of burning a Koran, Islam's holy book.
3Her death has triggered a public outcry in a country where mass demonstrations of support for women's rights are rare.
4Protesters in Kabul painted their faces red on Tuesday to represent Farkhunda's bloodied face as filmed by mobile phones as she was beaten to death.
5"We have come here to register a very deep sense of anger and frustration with the process of reform," said Afghanistan's former spy chief, Amrullah Saleh, who was at the protest.
6Interior Minister Noor ul-Haq Olomi said on Tuesday that 20 officers, including the police chief of the district, had been dismissed over the case.
7"It is clear there was a failure to carry out police duties," Olomi said in a statement, promising a full investigation and prosecutions if warranted.
8Hashmat Stanekzai, the spokesman for Kabul's police chief, was also fired on Tuesday for statements made on his personal Facebook page the day after the killing, Olomi said.
9Stanekzai called the woman an "unbeliever" and said people like her hoped to gain U.S. or European citizenship with shows of blasphemy.
10Foreign aid donors have spent billions of dollars on Afghanistan's police force and trying to entrench the rule of law and many are frustrated at a persistent culture of impunity and abuse among the security forces.
11The country's top investigator has said there is no evidence Farkhunda burned the Koran.
12Her brother says a cleric made the accusation at his mosque after Farkhunda told a woman not to pay for good-luck amulets because they were un-Islamic.
13The president has ordered an inquiry into her killing and Olomi said 28 people have been arrested for participating in the mob attack.
14More than a dozen years after the fall of the hard-line Taliban regime that routinely executed people for violating their interpretation of Islamic law, Afghanistan remains a deeply conservative nation.
15Some Afghans - including a mullah in Kabul - expressed approval for last week's killing.
