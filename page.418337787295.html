10CAIRO (AP) - After a series of stunning militant attacks, Egypt's government is pushing through a controversial new anti-terrorism draft bill that would set up special terrorism courts, shorten the appeals process, give police greater powers of arrest and imprison journalists who report information on attacks that differs from the official government line.
11The draft raised concerns that officials are taking advantage of heightened public shock at last week's audacious attacks to effectively enshrine into law the notorious special emergency laws which were in place for decades until they were lifted following the 2011 ouster of autocrat Hosni Mubarak.
12Rather than reviewing security policies since the attacks, officials have largely been focusing blame on the media for allegedly demoralizing troops and on the slowness of the courts.
13The 55-article bill has not been officially made public but was leaked to the Egyptian press over the weekend.
14A judicial official who vetted the draft confirmed its contents to The Associated Press on Monday.
15The bill is currently in a review process, leaving it unclear when it will be issued or whether changes could be made.
16Since Egypt has not had a parliament for more than two years, laws are issued by the president, Abdel-Fattah el-Sissi, after going through the Cabinet.
17In the absence of parliament, any debate is largely through media or behind closed doors.
18The leaked copy of the bill brought an outcry and demands for changes in the draft from Egypt's journalists union and from senior judges.
19"The disaster is that now they have decided to punish those who are carrying the news and not those responsible for the catastrophes," Khaled Balshi, of the Press Syndicate, told the AP.
20The government "seeks a press that fits it, a la North Korea."
21The New York-based Committee to Protect Journalists has said that at least 18 journalists were behind bars in Egypt as of June 1 and accused the government of using the pretext of national security to crackdown on freedoms.
22Four more journalists were arrested last week, the group said.
23"Imposing jail time for publishing news about any topic, including government's account of public events, contradicts Egypt's own constitution and defies any standard of free press. Journalists' basic function in any democratic society is to vet government behavior and Egypt is not an exception," Sherif Mansour of CPJ wrote to AP in an email.
24The bill "pushes further the government ability to, and its practice of, crushing critical and independent voices inside the country."
25Still, the draft law was hailed by Egypt's political parties and other public figures as a much-needed tool to combat violence that has been rising since the military ousted Islamist President Mohammed Morsi two years ago.
26The Cabinet approved the draft Wednesday.
27That was two days after a car bomb in an upscale Cairo neighborhood killed the top public prosecutor Hisham Barakat.
28That same Wednesday, Islamic militants launched a multi-pronged attack attempting to seize a northern Sinai town, hitting the military with suicide attacks and battling soldiers for hours.
29In the next required step, the State Council - a judicial body that must vet bills and advises the government on legal issues - reviewed the draft and made a few changes most at the request of the government, said the judicial official, who is a member of the council.
30Those amendments toughened some provisions, including introducing a prison sentence for anyone who records or broadcasts any court proceedings in a terrorism case without court permission.
31The official asked for anonymity because he was not authorized to talk to the press.
32The Cabinet will now consider whether to make any changes sought by representatives of the judiciary, whose opinions are non-binding.
33At Barakat's funeral, a visibly angry el-Sissi shouted that courts must act faster.
34"We have left it all to you, to justice. We have left with you, for two years," he shouted.
35"Please, please, we want prompt courts and prompt and fair laws ... The trials and the laws won't work in this manner and under these circumstances. This may work with regular people, but not these people. Only prompt laws will work."
36His anger was matched by TV reporters calling for the quick implementation of death sentences issued against Islamists, including Morsi.
37The government also aggressively pushed back against foreign media, which officials and the pro-state media accused of exaggerating the degree of the Sinai attacks.
38The military spokesman warned against using foreign media reports.
39The draft bill states that "anyone who intends to publish news or statements about terrorist attacks in way that differs from official statements issued by concerned authorities is punishable by imprisonment of no less than two years."
40An earlier version stipulated the news must be untrue to be punished, but the word "untrue" was dropped, the judicial official said.
41The bill would create special courts to hear terrorism cases.
42It would also eliminate one of the two layers of appeal that are currently allowed in criminal cases, and it would shorten the deadlines for filing appeals to 40 days from 60.
43It would also create a new category of detention - "restraining" - meaning police can detain someone solely on the basis of suspicion without evidence of a crime for a 7-day-period, which prosecutors can renew an unlimited number of times.
44The draft also grants the president the right to declare curfews and evacuate residents from an area for a period of six months, with the approval of the Cabinet.
45It says law enforcers cannot be questioned over use of force if it is to protect themselves from imminent danger and is proportional.
46A rights activist who specializes in legal reforms, Mohammed Zaree, said the vague terms in that provision would ensure police impunity for abuses.
47"To say that we have legal vacuum is to mislead the public. Egypt doesn't need new laws," Zaree said.
48"To increasingly depend on exceptional laws has been the main reason for decreased efficiency in security agencies."
49Egypt's Supreme Judicial Council, made up of the 13 top judges, expressed to the government its opposition to creating special terrorism courts and to reducing the time for appeal.
50Speaking to the AP, one judge who is not on the council but is high ranking in the judiciary, said the creation of special courts denies the defendants their right to a natural judge.
51He called the bill "half-cooked," saying it was clumsily written.
52He agreed to express his concerns to the AP on condition his name not be used because judges traditionally refrain from talking to the press.
53He also bristled at the implication that rising terrorism was linked to delays in court proceedings.
54"As if the judiciary is to blame. This is not the causal relation," he said.
55"Do they want to get to the truth or to take revenge from a suspect?"
