Q&A: In Alabama, state court vs. federal courts. Who wins?

Ferguson, Missouri police menaced African-American citizens for revenue, US federal investigation concludes

1Who wins?
2ATLANTA (AP) - The Alabama Supreme Court is in a showdown with a federal judge who ruled that the state's same-sex marriage ban unconstitutional.
3The state's justices say they remain the authority on state law, at least until the U.S. Supreme Court settles the question of whether the U.S. Constitution gives gay couples nationwide a fundamental right to marry.
4So until then, who prevails in Alabama, no stranger to disputes between state and federal authorities?
5-WHAT HAVE THE COURTS ACTUALLY SAID?
6U.S. District Court Judge Callie "Ginny" Granade, who sits in Mobile, Ala., ruled that Alabama's ban violates the U.S. Constitution's "equal protection" and "due process" clauses.
7Four federal appeals courts made similar rulings, based on the U.S. Supreme Court's 2013 ruling striking down the federal Defense of Marriage Act.
8That case stopped short of explicitly striking down state laws confining marriage to heterosexuals.
9The Alabama Supreme Court declared Tuesday that marriage between a man and a woman remains "the fundamental unit of society," and ordered county officials to stop issuing licenses to gays and lesbians.
10The 11th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals declined Alabama's request to delay Granade's decisions, and the U.S. Supreme Court, which expects to rule later this year, likewise declined to intervene.
11- BUT DOESN'T FEDERAL POWER TRUMP THE STATES?
12Alabama's justices don't dispute that the U.S. Supreme Court has the last word on constitutional questions, but the relationship between the lower federal courts and state courts is more complicated.
13The U.S. Constitution doesn't say whether state courts must adhere to federal court rulings.
14It simply created U.S. Supreme Court and authorized Congress to create other federal courts as necessary.
15"State courts may interpret the U.S. Constitution independently from, and even contrary to, federal courts" short of the Supreme Court, the Alabama ruling states.
16University of Alabama law professor Ronald Krotoszynski says that's technically correct.
17But he notes that Alabama stands alone in its reaction among the many states where federal judges have declared same-sex marriages legal.
18"Does the (Alabama) court have the power to do this? Yes," the professor said.
19"Was it wise for the court to exercise its power this way? I'd say no. ... This is just not a standard kind of move in the inter-relationship between state and federal courts."
20- SO WHAT SHOULD LOCAL OFFICIALS DO?
21For now, they're following their state Court.
22After Granade's rulings, 48 out of 67 Alabama counties had issued marriage licenses to same-sex couples, according to the Human Rights Campaign, a gay-rights advocacy group.
23But less than a day after the state court ruling, it could not find a single probate judge in Alabama issuing licenses to same-sex couples.
24- WHAT HAPPENS TO THE SAME-SEX COUPLES WHO ALREADY MARRIED IN ALABAMA?
25The state Supreme Court decision did not explicitly invalidate the licenses that probate judges have issued in recent weeks.
26But it did describe their licenses as "purported."
27- IS THIS JUST ANOTHER EXAMPLE OF ALABAMA FLOUTING THE FEDS?
28From the Civil War to the civil rights movement, the Alabama government has a long history of thumbing its collective nose at federal authorities.
29But disputes between state and federal courts aren't unique to Alabama.
30Virginia state courts in recent years have upheld convictions under a state law outlawing sodomy among minors, despite the U.S. Supreme Court's decision throwing out a Texas anti-sodomy statute.
31California state courts have repeatedly applied state bankruptcy codes at odds with federal bankruptcy rulings.
32Gay rights advocates could find a gay or lesbian couple to apply for a marriage license and get denied.
33Then they could ask Granade to order their county's judge to issue a license.
34Also, a probate judge could join a federal appeal.
