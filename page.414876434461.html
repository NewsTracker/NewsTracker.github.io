Germany rejects Greek olive branch on eve of bailout talks

1ATHENS, Greece (AP) - Germany quickly rejected a conciliatory bailout proposal by Greece on Thursday, dampening hopes that emergency talks might break a deadlock threatening the country with default and an exit from the euro.
2The Greek government asked to extend its rescue loan agreement by six months, in order to give all sides more time to hash out a more permanent deal.
3That goes much of the way toward satisfying an ultimatum from the 19-country eurozone.
4It held back, however, on offering to continue in full a series of budget cuts and reforms that the eurozone has required since 2010 in exchange for loans, but that Greece blames for devastating its economy.
5Germany was quick to say the plan was not good enough.
6German finance ministry spokesman Martin Jaeger said it "is not a substantial proposal for a solution."
7He said it amounts to a request "for bridge financing without fulfilling the demands of the (bailout) program," namely the budget measures.
8It does not, he added, correspond to what the eurozone countries had demanded of Greece before talks broke down on Monday.
9The European executive Commission was somewhat more upbeat.
10Spokesman Margaritis Schinas said Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker "sees in this letter a positive sign which, in his assessment, could pave the way for a reasonable compromise in the interest of financial stability in the euro area as a whole."
11He said Juncker held talks with Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tspiras and Dutch Finance Minister Jeroen Dijsselbloem, who will head Friday's emergency meeting of eurozone finance ministers in Brussels.
