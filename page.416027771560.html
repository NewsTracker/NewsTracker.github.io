Kenya launches air strikes on Al-Shabab

1Relatives stand to pray as they wait to go and view their loved ones who were killed during an attack on a university, during a ceremony at Chiromo funeral home in Nairobi, Kenya on April 6, 2015.
2Al-Shabab gunmen killed scores of people at a university in northeastern Kenya on Thursday.
3Kenya launched air strikes against fighters from the armed group Al-Shabab in Somalia following an attack on a college in eastern Kenya that killed 148 people, a military spokesman said Monday.
4In the first major military response to last week's attack by Al-Shabab on Garissa university, warplanes attacked two camps of the Al-Shabab armed group on Sunday afternoon and early Monday morning, said Col.
5David Obonyo of the Kenyan military.
6Al-Shabab, which is based in Somalia, claimed responsibility for the college attack Thursday in the Kenyan town of Garissa.
7Four gunmen died in the assault, which ended after a 15-hour siege.
8The group has killed more than 400 people in Kenya since April 2013.
9The air strikes occurred in the Gedo region of Somalia, Obonyo said.
10"This is part of continuing operations, not just in response to Garissa," he said.
11Jets pounded the camps in Gondodowe and Ismail, both in the Gedo region bordering Kenya.
12Cloud cover made it difficult to establish how much damage the bombings had caused or to estimate the death toll.
13Kenyan President Uhuru Kenyatta had vowed harsh measures against Al-Shabab following the attack on Garissa, which is near the border with Somalia.
14Kenya has troops in Somalia as part of an African Union force to attack al-Shabab and shore up the beleaguered Somali government.
15Kenya has carried out air strikes before, but it has struggled to stop the flow of al Shabaab militants and weapons across its porous border with Somalia.
16Al-Shabab said it attacked students at Garissa College University as a reprisal for Kenya sending troops into Somalia.
17Nairobi decided in 2011 to provide troops to the African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM), with United Nations approval.
18This peacekeeping mission, which includes a military component that draws troops from Ethiopia, Djibouti, Burundi, Sierra Leone and Uganda, was put together to root out Al-Shabab from its strongholds in south and central Somalia.
19The mission has counted a number of significant successes in recent years, such as the recapturing of important port cities along the Somali coast and the killing of several Al-Shabab leaders.
20In March, AMISOM seized Kuday, an island off the port city of Kismayo that Al-Shabab fighters used to smuggle contraband charcoal that it sells to Gulf states to sustain its operations.
21The killing that same month of Adnan Garaar, who allegedly helped plan the Westgate attack, by a U.S. drone also delivered an important blow to the organization, the Pentagon reported.
22Much of the capital of Mogadishu is back in the Somali government's hands, AMISOM reported, but car bombs and suicide attacks continue to rock the capital.
23Al-Shabab, Somalia's largest armed group, started out as one of many factions fighting against the U.N.-backed Transitional Federal Government based in Mogadishu.
24It morphed into what the United States labeled a "terrorist" organization after staging attacks on prominent foreign and national targets.
