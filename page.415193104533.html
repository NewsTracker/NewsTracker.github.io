Aussie rugby star chases NFL dream with 49ers

Ukraine mine blast 'traps dozens'

1(CNN)Australian rugby star Jarryd Hayne is swapping the rugby pitch for the gridiron.
2Several months after announcing his retirement from Australia's National Rugby League (NRL) and the Parramatta Eels, the 2014 Player of the Year has signed a three-year contract with the San Francisco 49ers.
3Despite never having played professional American football, the 27-year-old had also attracted interest from a number of other teams, including Super Bowl runners-up Seattle Seahawks and Detroit Lions.
4Ultimately, however, he chose San Francisco as his destination, not least because new coach Jim Tomsula has experience working with overseas players, having previously helmed the Rhine Fire in the NFL Europe league.
5"He was probably the one coach that knew the transition better than anyone," Hayne said in a press conference.
6The 6' 2", 226 lb New South Wales native ran a 4.53 second 40-yard dash in a December workout. NFL analyst Daniel Jeremy tweeted his support, saying that he believed Hayne has "an excellent chance" of becoming an excellent punt/kickoff returner.
7Despite this, the Australian said he was aware of the chances he was taking.
8"This is a huge risk," NFL.com reported Hayne as saying.
9"I'm taking a massive leap of faith."
10As is his new team.
11Along with a reported guaranteed $100,000 payday, the 49ers -- Super Bowl runners-up in 2013 -- are taking a punt on an untested prospect, albeit one who will not disrupt their roster, or salary cap.
12In 2014, Hayne won the NRL's Dally M awards for Player of the Year, Best Fullback, Top Tryscorer and Representative Player.
13He has previously won Dally M recognition, most notably in 2009 as Player of the Year.
14In nine years with the Eels, he scored 103 tries.
15He made the decision to switch sports in October last year, saying at the time: "I'm always telling people to chase their dreams and follow their hearts -- if I don't live by that I'm not being honest with myself."
16Despite his lack of hands-on football experience, his new team are confident that his background means that he will be a quick learner.
17"Hayne comes from an athletic family as his father, Manoa Thompson, is a former professional rugby league player who represented Fiji on the national rugby team," the 49ers said in a statement.

2Ukraine's officials say as many as 33 people may have died.
3There are fears the rebels lack the resources to deal with the disaster, the BBC's Tom Burridge reports.
4Zasyadko was the scene of Ukraine's worst mine disaster nearly eight years ago, when 101 people were killed.
5Ukrainian President Petro Poroshenko has demanded access to the mine for government rescuers and police.
6The mine is close to a front line where the pro-Russian rebels have been battling Ukrainian government troops.
7However, Ukraine's mine unions head Mykhailo Volynets told a Ukrainian TV channel there had been no fighting in the area in recent days.
8While Ukraine's economy has significantly shrunk since fighting began in April, many coal mines continue to operate in the conflict zone.
9Mining safety standards are poor, and there are frequent accidents.
10Confusion surrounded the death toll in the hours after the blast, which occurred more than 1,000m (3,200ft) underground at 05:20 local time (03:20 GMT).
11In the Ukrainian parliament in Kiev, speaker Volodymyr Hroisman announced that 32 people had been killed and called for a minute's silence.
12However, he later retracted the figure, saying instead that one person was dead and the fate of about 30 others was unknown.
13The rebels said 23 miners were confirmed dead.
14But the Ukraine-controlled Donetsk regional administration said later on Wednesday that 33 people died, and nine bodies had already been recovered.
15It is known that, at the time of the blast, 230 people were in the shaft.
16About 200 have now been accounted for.
17The sister of one miner who was in the pit at the time of the explosion, Alexei Novoselsky, stood at the entrance to the mine, in tears, Reuters reports.
18"Tell me, are there survivors?" she asked a passing rescue worker.
19"Why are you concealing the truth?"
20A welder at the mine, who gave his name as Oleg, said: "I've been down the pit for 23 years, and this is the fourth explosion that I can recall.
21"If they didn't get them out straight away, then later they will only retrieve bodies. An explosion is a terrible thing."
22The mine is one of Ukraine's biggest coal producers.
23At the end of last year, the mine was reportedly still supplying coal to coke and chemical works owned by Ukraine's wealthiest businessman, Rinat Akhmetov, in Donetsk region.
24In the old coal-mining area of Donbas, the Zasyadko mine is one of the newest.
25It started operations in 1958 and is now said to be part-owned by Yukhym Zvyahilsky, a local leader with money and influence who was once an acting prime minister of Ukraine and is now an MP.
26The mine is also one of the most dangerous.
27It is deep and it has particularly hazardous levels of methane.
28The year 2007 was particularly bad.
29It saw three accidents, the worst of which claimed the lives of 101 miners.
30In normal circumstances Ukraine has an established rescue procedure.
31There are many skilled local rescuers helped by teams that are brought in quickly from other parts of Ukraine.
32But these are not normal times.
33The war in eastern Ukraine caused a lot of material damage to infrastructure, power supply and road links across the Donbas.
34It also ruptured the existing links with central authorities.
35It is unlikely that the rebels will talk to Kiev and allow the rescue teams in but will they turn to Russia for help?
36Whatever the death toll of this accident, it adds to the depressing statistics in this part of Ukraine where the UN has reported more than 6,000 people killed by the conflict that is tearing it apart.
