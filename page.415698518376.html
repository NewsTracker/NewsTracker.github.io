Arab-Israeli leaders reject Netanyahu's apology for offending Arabs in election-day comment

1The spat has touched on longstanding claims of discrimination by Israel's Arab minority, which makes up 20 percent of the Jewish state, and signaled that the rift will not be healed anytime soon.
2An Arab advocacy center in Israel said the country's national elections brought an "unprecedented level of racist incitement" against the minority community.
3In the heat of a close race last week, Netanyahu posted a video on his Facebook page where he implored his hard-line supporters to head to the polls, saying that "left-wing organizations" were bussing Arabs to the polls "in droves."
4The comments drew accusations of racism from Arab voters and a White House rebuke.
5The footage of the apology, filmed by Likud, shows Netanyahu saying to a gathering of Arab officials on Monday: "I know that what I said a few days ago offended some of Israel's citizens, offended Israeli Arabs. I had no intention of doing so. I am sorry for this."
6But Arab politicians from the Joint List - a new coalition of mostly Arab parties - said they were not invited to the gathering at the prime minister's residence.
7"Why didn't he call us? Why didn't he invite us?" said Ayman Odeh, head of the Joint List.
8There was no immediate comment from Netanyahu's Likud Party.
9Arab politicians said his election victory was illegitimate, claiming Netanyahu won the vote by pandering to anti-Arab fears.
10"We call on Netanyahu to return the mandate he received on the basis of incitement and fear-mongering," the Joint List said in a statement.
11Netanyahu has secured a majority of backers in the new parliament and is expected to be formally tapped on Wednesday to form the next government.
12Opinion polls conducted on the eve of elections showed Netanyahu's conservative Likud party trailing behind his main challenger, the center-left Zionist Union.
13His sudden turnaround victory came as Netanyahu made his remark about Arab voters in the final hours of voting.
14His comments on Arabs, along with a declaration that he would not allow the establishment of a Palestinian state on his watch, have infuriated the White House.
15And his attempts to backtrack on both positions have been greeted with skepticism.
16"We just don't know what to believe at this point," State Department spokeswoman Marie Harf said Monday.
17The controversy comes amid the backdrop of the Arab-Israeli community's newfound political might.
18The Joint List made unprecedented gains in the March 17 election, earning enough votes to make it the third-largest party in Israel's 120-seat parliament.
19The Joint List said election results showed that more than 90 percent of Arab-Israeli voters supported the coalition in the elections.
20Arab-Israeli politician Ahmed Tibi told Israel Radio Tuesday that Netanyahu's attempt to defuse the dispute was "not an honest apology."
21Emilie Moatti, a spokeswoman for the Joint List, said the Arab dignitaries who gathered at Netanyahu's home where the apology was filmed were all Likud supporters, with no Joint List representatives invited.
22Yunis Meri, chair of the municipal council in the Arab-Israeli village of Fureidis, and a Likud supporter, said he hugged the prime minister at the gathering.
23"What he said before the elections, he put it behind him," Meri said.
