Potential candidates to replace Blatter as FIFA president

1FILE - In this Friday, May 29, 2015, file photo, UEFA president Michel Platini, left, and FIFA senior vice president Issa Hayatou look on during the 65th FIFA Congress held at the Hallenstadion in Zurich, Switzerland.
2European soccer leader and former French star Michel Platini and Jordan's Prince Ali bin al-Hussein are the likely candidates to succeed Sepp Blatter.
3But in an election where Africa and Asia hold almost half the votes, Cameroon's Issa Hayatou and others may enter the race.
4FILE - In this Friday, May 29, 2015, file photo, Prince Ali bin al-Hussein, right, is embraced by UEFA President Michel Platini, left, after al-Hussein announced his withdrawal in the FIFA president election at the 65th FIFA Congress at the Hallenstadion in Zurich, Switzerland.
5FILE - In this Friday, May 29, 2015 file photo, FIFA president Sepp Blatter after his election as President greeted by UEFA President Michel Platini, right, at the Hallenstadion in Zurich, Switzerland.
6Blatter has been re-elected as FIFA president for a fifth term, chosen to lead world soccer despite separate U.S. and Swiss criminal investigations into corruption.
7The 209 FIFA member federations gave the 79-year-old Blatter another four-year term on Friday after Prince Ali bin al-Hussein of Jordan conceded defeat after losing 133-73 in the first round.
8Now that Sepp Blatter says he will be resigning, the race opens to succeed him as FIFA president.
9The job has been held by nine men since FIFA's founding in 1904, but just two over the past four decades: Joao Havelange of Brazil from 1974-98 and Blatter since then.
10Blatter announced his decision to resign Tuesday, just four days after he was elected to a fifth four-year term through 2019.
11An election is expected to be held by an extraordinary FIFA congress between December and March.
12The rules call for a vote by secret paper ballot.
13Africa has 54 votes, Europe 53, Asia 46, North and Central America and the Caribbean 35, Oceania 11 and South America 10.
14Long expected to run for the FIFA job this year, Platini announced last August: "Now is not my time, not yet."
15He pushed last week for Blatter to quit before the election.
16Because Europe holds only about one-quarter of the votes, Platini would need to build alliances to win.
17Platini, who turns 60 on June 21, captained France to the 1984 European Championship title and won the European Cup with Juventus in 1985.
18He has been UEFA president since January 2007, when he won 27-23 against Sweden's Lennart Johansson, who had held the job since 1990.
19Platini presided over the expansion of the European Championship from 16 nations to 24 starting at next year's tournament in France, pushed for Euro 2020 to be played in 13 cities across Europe rather than in one country, and as a member of the FIFA executive committee said he voted for Qatar to host the 2022 World Cup.
20Prince Ali, 39, would represent a two-generation change in leadership.
21Educated at the Royal Military Academy Sandhurst in Britain and Princeton in the United States, he was commissioned as an officer in the Jordanian military in December 1994 and was awarded the Brunei Medal for serving in Brunei.
22He has been president of the Jordan Football Association since 1999 and served on the FIFA executive committee from 2011 until last week, when he was replaced by Sheikh Ahmad al-Fahad al-Sabah of Kuwait.
23Prince Ali lost to Blatter 133-73 in Friday's vote, with opposition coalescing behind him after Luis Figo of Portugal and Michael van Praag of the Netherlands withdrew.
24Ali has promised to increase the World Cup field from 32 nations to 36.
25Hayatou, a Cameroonian who turns 69 in August, has been the president of the Confederation of African Football since 1988 and has been on the FIFA executive committee since 1990 - its second-longest-serving member.
26A former runner, basketball and soccer player, Hayatou ran against Blatter for FIFA president in 2002 and lost 139-56.
27He has been a member of the International Olympic Committee since 2001.
28Hayatou was reprimanded by the IOC in 2011 for receiving $20,000 in cash in 1995 from FIFA's former marketing partner ISL.
29Hayatou denied wrongdoing, but the IOC said he was guilty of a conflict of interest.
30British newspaper The Sunday Times claimed in June 2014 that banned former Qatari official Mohamed bin Hammam targeted Hayatou and other African soccer leaders with cash, luxury trips and other gifts to support Qatar's successful bid to host the 2022 World Cup.
31Hayatou has denied the allegations.
32Hayatou undergoes dialysis for kidney problems.
33The 67-year-old Dutchman was chairman of Ajax from 1989-03, following his father Jaap, who chaired the team's board from 1964-78.
34A former referee, he became chairman of the Royal Dutch Football Association in 2008 and has been on the UEFA executive committee since 2009.
35He was nominated to run against Blatter but withdrew a week before the election and supported Prince Ali.
36Van Praag had vowed to retain Blatter an adviser and head of a "Sepp Blatter Foundation" to help underprivileged children.
