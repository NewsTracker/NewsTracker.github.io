Families of Germanwings crash victims to meet investigators

1A painting is put up in a terminal of the airport commemorating the people who died in the Germanwings plane crash in France in Duesseldorf, Germany, Wednesday, June 10, 2015.
2150 people died in the plane crash on March 24.
3Families of victims of the Germanwings plane crash are meeting with investigators in Paris Thursday, amid lingering questions about why the co-pilot intentionally destroyed the Airbus A320 and how relatives may be compensated for their loss.
4The meeting, which is to also focus on the long-stalled return of the victims' belongings, comes in the same week that the first remains were returned to families after the March 24 crash.
5All 150 people on board were killed.
6The families were holding their own meeting at a Paris hotel before heading off to see Marseille Prosecutor Brice Robin, who is leading a French investigation into the crash, and other French officials.
7German lawyer Peter Kortas, whose firm represents the relatives of 34 victims, said negotiations with Germanwings began several days ago over compensation.
8Families were also looking for answers about delays in returning the victims' remains.
9"In this moment everything else is not as important as the fact that the bodies, (the) remains be returned to their families," he said.
10"It's already more than two and a half months since the crash happened, so it's finally necessary to get to closure."
11"The loss of the relatives should be compensated with also a suitable amount of money," he said.
12"There are two points in these negotiations: First, the material loss for the material damages, and it is also about damages for pain and suffering."
13French and German prosecutors say co-pilot Andreas Lubitz intentionally flew Flight 9525 into the French Alps en route from Barcelona to Duesseldorf.
14German authorities have said he suffered depression in the past and had researched suicide methods.
15The first burial is expected Friday.
16Victims had a total of 19 nationalities but nearly half were German, and 47 were Spanish.
17In Germany Wednesday, a convoy of hearses brought home the remains of a group of students and their teachers from a high school in the town of Haltern who were among the dead.
18Hundreds of people, some hugging and crying, lined the streets to pay their respects.
19A convoy of hearses drives on the highway in Duisburg, Germany, Wednesday, June 10, 2015, taking home 16 school children who died in the Germanwings plane crash in March.
20The coffins, that arrived at the airport in Duesseldorf Tuesday evening, are brought to their families in the city of Haltern.
