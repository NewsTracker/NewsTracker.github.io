7ZURICH (AP) - The FIFA election is won and done.
8Sepp Blatter's problems, however, are far from over.
9The prize for the 79-year-old president of soccer's governing body is four more years in office and some big issues ahead.
10American and Swiss federal investigations, which rocked FIFA this week, could yet creep closer to Blatter's door.
11Blatter, who defeated Prince Ali bin al-Hussein on Friday in the FIFA presidential election, is also set for open conflict with UEFA and its president, former protege Michel Platini.
12"At the end of my term I will give this FIFA to my successor in a very, very strong position, a robust position," Blatter said in his acceptance speech.
13Q: What next for Blatter?
14A: Carry on regardless, is one answer.
15And keep fighting UEFA.
16Blatter was defiant this week in shifting blame for the American corruption case on the actions of individuals out of his control.
17His candidate speech to voters Friday simply offered more of the same leadership style.
18Blatter offered no big ideas in his campaign - he did not even bother with a manifesto - but offered one pledge in his acceptance speech.
19He put Europe on notice by promising a "better representation of the confederations" on his executive committee, where UEFA has eight of 25 voting members.
20Translation: Dilute UEFA's influence after it supported Prince Ali and two other election candidates from Europe who withdrew last week.
21The FIFA vs. UEFA fight resumes Saturday morning when Blatter chairs a meeting of his 27-member executive committee.
22It must decide how to allocate qualifying slots for the 2018 World Cup.
23Europe's 13 places - not including host Russia - should be safe.
24Q: When will Blatter finally answer questions about the election and the federal investigations?
25A: FIFA has called a news conference for 11:30 a.m. (0930 GMT; 5:30 a.m. EDT) on Saturday after the executive committee session.
26It will be Blatter's first formal meeting with international media in 15 days.
27The FIFA president will want to talk about his election victory.
28But he will also be quizzed on the American case which left two of his vice presidents and a newly elected member of his executive committee in Zurich prison cells during Friday's election.
29They are among seven soccer officials fighting extradition to the U.S. after being arrested at a luxury downtown hotel early Wednesday.
30The key question for Blatter?
31How and why was $10 million paid from a FIFA account as apparent bribes in the 2010 World Cup hosting contest.
32The money ended up with Blatter's longtime executive committee colleagues Jack Warner of Trinidad and Tobago and Chuck Blazer of the United States to vote for South Africa's successful bid.
33Q: What about the American and Swiss corruption investigations?
34A: They are a rare part of the FIFA game that Blatter cannot control.
35A 164-page U.S. indictment detailed racketeering, money-laundering and wire fraud charges involving 14 soccer and marketing officials.
36Four more, including Blazer, were named as having made guilty pleas.
37Blatter was not named in the indictment, nor were there many links directly to FIFA.
38Still, U.S. federal authorities have promised it is just the beginning of their case.
39More ties to FIFA could be revealed when questioning - and plea bargaining - begins of indicted officials facing 20-year prison sentences.
40FIFA itself provoked the Swiss investigation by filing a criminal complaint to the attorney general's office about potential wrongdoing in the 2018 and 2022 World Cup hosting contests won by Russia and Qatar, respectively.
41Swiss authorities began questioning 10 FIFA voters on Thursday.
42Though FIFA invited the case, they may come to regret where it leads.
43Any serious findings will revive pressure for a World Cup re-vote - though the sporting and legal implications would be immense.
44Q: What do Blatter's critics inside FIFA do now?
45A: For Prince Ali, his spell on the Blatter-chaired FIFA executive committee is over.
46He was maneuvered out of his FIFA vice presidency by Asian Football Confederation leaders, and then focused only on his FIFA presidential run.
47Over to you, Europe.
48"Enough is enough," Platini said Thursday, after asking Blatter to resign.
49"People no longer want him anymore and I don't want him anymore either."
50The rift with Blatter won't end soon and could be widened next Friday.
51Platini has called his member federations to discuss their tactics in Berlin ahead of the Champions League final on June 6.
52The high-stakes options are pulling out from FIFA or boycotting the 2018 World Cup.
53Platini does not want that, but said all options are open for his members to discuss and decide.
54Q: Where and when does the FIFA show go next?
55A: First, the unfinished business on Saturday at FIFA headquarters.
56Blatter and some confederation presidents, like Platini, should also soon go to two FIFA tournament finals.
57On June 20, FIFA protocol requires Blatter to attend the Under-20 World Cup final in Auckland, New Zealand.
58Then to Vancouver, Canada, for the Women's World Cup final on July 5.
59Blatter will probably take care on that trip to avoid setting foot on U.S. territory.
60Federal agencies would surely want to meet and speak with a visiting FIFA president.
61Blatter should next meet the currently hostile European soccer family in two months for what is rather a home venue for him - St. Petersburg, Russia.
62The home city of Blatter's loyal ally, President Vladimir Putin, hosts the 2018 World Cup qualifying tournament draw on July 25.
63Leaders of all 53 European members of FIFA should also be there for a showcase event that will put Blatter on center stage of world soccer.
