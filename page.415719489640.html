Rescuers struggle to recover bodies in Germanwings French Alps crash

1Germanwings budget flight 4U9525, carrying 144 passengers including 16 German teenagers returning home from a school trip, plunged for eight minutes before hitting the side of a mountain in the French Alps Tuesday with no survivors.
2There was no response to desperate attempts by air traffic controllers to hail the pilots.
3The accident's cause remains a mystery but authorities have recovered a black box from the Airbus A320 at the crash site, where debris was believed to be scattered over four acres of remote and inaccessible mountainous terrain, hampering rescue efforts.
4More than 300 policemen and 380 firefighters have been mobilized.
5Lieutenant Colonel Jean-Marc Menichini said a squad of 30 mountain rescue police would resume attempts to reach the crash site by helicopter at dawn Wednesday, while a further 65 police were seeking access on foot.
6Five investigators had spent the night at the site.
7It would take "at least a week" to search the remote site, he said, and "at least several days" to repatriate the bodies.
8Video images from a government helicopter on Tuesday showed a desolate snow-flecked moonscape, with steep ravines covered in scree.
9Debris was strewn across the mountainside, pieces of twisted metal smashed into tiny bits.
10The plane was "totally destroyed", a local MP who flew over the site said, describing the scene as "horrendous".
11"The biggest body parts we identified are not bigger than a briefcase," one investigator said.
12A crisis cell has been set up in the area between Barcelonnette and Digne-les-Bains along with an emergency flight control centre to coordinate chopper flights to the crash site.
13French President Francois Hollande, his German counterpart Angela Merkel and Spanish Prime Minister Mariano Rajoy were expected to reach the scene around 2:00 pm (1300 GMT).
14The 144 passengers were mainly German and Spanish.
15The high school in the small German town of Haltern attended by the 16 students on the plane was set to hold an event Wednesday to honour the victims.
16"This is certainly the darkest day in the history of our city," said a tearful Bodo Klimpel, the town's mayor, Tuesday.
17"It is the worst thing you can imagine."
18Spain, meanwhile, declared three days of mourning and was to hold a minute of silence across the country at noon Wednesday.
19Opera singers Oleg Bryjak, 54, and Maria Radner, 33, were also on board, flying to their home city of Duesseldorf.
20Radner was travelling with her husband and baby, one of two infants on board the plane.
21Debris of the plane.
22Budget airline Germanwings said the Airbus, travelling from Barcelona to Dusseldorf, plunged for eight minutes but the crew made no distress call before crashing near the ski resort of Barcelonnette.
23The rapid descent was "unexplained", Marseilles prosecutor Brice Robin said.
24One of the plane's black boxes has been found, but it was unclear whether it was the flight data recorder or the cockpit voice recorder.
25Investigators will continue searching for the second black box Wednesday.
26Weather did not appear to be a factor in the crash, with conditions calm at the time, French weather officials said.
27Lufthansa, the parent company of Germanwings, said it was working on the assumption the crash was an "accident".
28"Anything else would be speculation," Lufthansa vice president Heike Birlenbach told reporters in Barcelona.
29She said the 24-year-old Airbus A320 had undergone its last routine check on Monday.
30Germanwings executive Thomas Winkelmann said the pilot had "more than 10 years of experience" and some 6,000 flying hours on an Airbus jet under his belt.
31It was the first fatal accident in the history of Germanwings, and the deadliest on the French mainland since 1974 when a Turkish Airlines plane crashed, killing 346 people.
32Search and rescue personnel being lowered close to the crash site.
33Locals described the difficult terrain that awaited rescue teams.
34"Ground access is horrible ... it's a very high mountainous area, very steep and it's terrible to get there except from the air during winter," local resident Francoise Pie said.
35Another local official, Gilbert Sauvan, told AFP: "The only possible access was by helicopter and people had to be winched down because the choppers couldn't land."
36Germanwings said 67 Germans were believed to have been on board, while Spain said 45 people with Spanish-sounding names were on the flight.
37Two Colombians, two Argentines, and two Australians were among the dead, according to their governments, while Hollande said Turks may also have been aboard.
38Two Japanese were "very likely" on board, their government said.
39Belgium and Denmark said at least one of their nationals was on board, while Mexico said three of its citizens were believed to be among the victims and Britain said its nationals were also on board.
40A Swedish third division football team booked on the fatal flight had changed flights at the last minute.
41"May they rest in peace," Dalkurd FF goalkeeper Frank Pettersson wrote on Twitter.
42The world's worst air disasters remain the March 27, 1977, collision of two Boeing 747s on the runway at Tenerife in the Canary Islands, killing 583 people, and the August 12, 1985 crash into a mountainside of a Boeing 747 belonging to Japan Airlines, killing 520 people.
