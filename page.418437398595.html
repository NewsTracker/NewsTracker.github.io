Greek lawmakers back bailout reform plan

1ATHENS - Greece's parliament backed the government's reform plan to win a third bailout early Saturday, but with the government suffering significant losses from lawmakers dissenting over the plan's austerity measures.
2The motion, which sought to authorize the government to use the proposal as a basis for negotiation with international creditors over the weekend, passed with 251 votes in favor, 32 against and eight voting "present" - a form of abstention - in the 300-member parliament.
3Those who voted "present" or were absent, as well as two of those who voted against, were members of Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras' left-wing Syriza party, raising questions about the stability of his government.
4The dissenters included two ministers - Panagiotis Lafazanis, who holds the energy portfolio, and Dimitris Stratoulis, who holds the social security portfolio - and prominent party member and parliament Speaker Zoe Konstantopoulou.
5"I support the government, but I don't support an austerity program of neoliberal deregulation and privatizations which & would prolong the vicious circle of recession, poverty and misery," Lafazanis said in a statement released to the press explaining his "radical and categorical" objection to the proposal.
6Former Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis, who resigned earlier in the week, was absent for family reasons, spending the weekend with his daughter, who was visiting from Australia.
7Although he sent a letter saying he would have voted in favor had he been present, it could not be counted among the "yes" votes under parliamentary rules.
8All opposition parties except the Nazi-inspired Golden Dawn and the Communist Party voted in favor.
9The proposed measures, including tax hikes and cuts in pension spending, are certain to inflict more pain on a Greek public who just days ago voted overwhelmingly against a similar plan.
10But the new proposal, if approved by Greece's international creditors, will provide longer-term financial support for a nation that has endured six years of recession.
11Without a deal, Greece faces the immediate prospect of crashing out of Europe's joint currency, the euro.
12It would be the first nation to do so.
13If the proposal is approved, Greece would get a three-year loan package worth nearly $60 billion ( 53.5 billion) as well as some form of debt relief.
14That is far more than the 7.2 billion left over from Greece's previous bailout that had been at stake in the country's five-month negotiations until last month.
15Speaking earlier in the debate that began just before midnight Friday, Tsipras acknowledged that the reforms his government has proposed are harsh and include measures far from his party's election pledges, but insisted they are Greece's best chance to emerge from its financial crisis.
16Tsipras said his government had made mistakes during his six-month tenure but said he had negotiated as hard as he could.
17"There is no doubt that for six months now we've been in a war," he said, adding that his government had fought "difficult battles" and had lost some of them.
18"Now I have the feeling we've reached the boundary line. From here on, there is a minefield, and I don't have the right to dismiss this or hide it from the Greek people," he said.
19But he insisted the latest proposal contains measures that would help the economy and, if approved by Greece's creditors, would unlock sufficient financing for the country to emerge from its protracted crisis and see its massive debt tackled.
20Defense Minister Panos Kammenos, who heads the junior coalition member Independent Greeks, said he was advocating a vote in favor of the proposal even though it goes against his party's principles.
21The party holds 13 seats in parliament.
22"I want to state clearly, I am not afraid of 'Grexit,'" he said, referring to the possibility of Greece leaving the euro.
23"I am afraid of one thing: national division and civil war."
24He said he feared failure to get a deal with creditors would eventually lead to civil strife.
25Greece's latest proposal was sent to rescue creditors who were to meet this weekend to decide whether to approve it.
26Eurozone finance ministers were to meet Saturday afternoon, followed by a summit of the 28-nation European Union on Sunday.
27The country has relied on bailout funding since losing access to financing from bond markets in 2010.
28The new measures overturn many of the election promises of Tsipras' Syriza party, which had vowed to overturn bailout austerity, and come less than a week after 61 percent of voters opposed similar reforms, proposed by creditors, in last Sunday's referendum.
29The coalition government has 162 members, and got the backing of a large section of opposition lawmakers.
30Greece's major creditors - the International Monetary Fund, the European Central Bank and other eurozone nations - were already combing through the proposals before sending them to the other 18 eurozone finance ministers Saturday.
31French President Francois Hollande described the measures as "serious and credible," though Germany refused to be drawn on their merits.
32France's Socialist government has been among Greece's few allies in the eurozone during the past months of tough negotiations, with Germany taking a far harder line.
33Jeroen Dijsselbloem, the Dutch finance minister who chairs the meetings of the eurozone finance ministers known as the Eurogroup, said the proposals were "extensive" but would not say whether he considered them sufficient.
