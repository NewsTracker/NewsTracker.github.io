13The World Health Organization says a quick assessment of Nepal's worst-hit districts has found some hospitals damaged or destroyed but most are coping well with no extra staff or beds required.
14However, they are in need of essential medicines, equipment and materials.
15The WHO says the Nepalese health system took measures to prepare for such emergencies.
16The agency says it is focused on preventing the possible spread of diarrhoeal diseases among at least 2.8 million displaced people, especially those living in 16 makeshift camps in Kathmandu.
17So far, the number of cases has not exceeded expectations and no camps have reported an increase in disease or any outbreaks.
18A day after two people were pulled alive from underneath the earthquake rubble, Nepali rescuers in orange jumpsuits and wearing masks and hardhats are working amid piles of debris - bricks, broken slabs of concrete, wooden floorboards, a mangled ceiling fan.
19Most of the time they find themselves in a dark warren of wrecked buildings, some of which are still standing only because their fall has been broken by other buildings in the packed quarters.
20Some break bricks with sledgehammers, some build small walls with bricks under a building's exposed foundation, which has been partially stabilized with wood and cement.
21At a nearby command center, exhausted soldiers and rescuers sit in the shade, some sleeping, near tools and other equipment laid out in neat rows with a sign that reads "logistics."
22Members of the British army Gurkha engineers are back in their native Nepal to help restore water supply in the capital.
23One of them, the 34-year-old Cpl.
24Besh Gurung, says he has been away from Nepal for 13 years and now is glad "I could serve my countrymen when they really needed something so necessary like clean drinking water."
25Nearly 2,000 people in tented camps and the Nepalese soldiers stationed in the former royal palace area are the immediate beneficiaries.
26Gurkhas have been part of the British military for over two centuries, and have fought in the Falklands, Iraq and Afghanistan.
27Thousands of young men from the poverty-stricken Himalayan hills still apply to join the force every year.
28Nepal's government is giving out 100,000 rupees ($1,000) to families of each of those killed in Saturday's earthquake, and another 40,000 rupees ($400) for funeral costs, according to the state-run Radio Nepal.
29Meanwhile, Nepal Rastriya Bank, the central bank, has ordered all private banks to open for at least a few hours Friday as well as over the weekend to meet the demand.
30The latest death toll figure stands at 6,198 in Nepal alone, plus 61 elsewhere in the region.
31Most of the people who were sleeping outside their homes for fear of aftershocks have moved back indoors.
32There were only a few dozen tents left at the Tudikhel grounds in the heart of Kathmandu, with less than a thousand people still there.
33The area used to be crowded with more than 30,000 people in the first days after the quake hit.
34Almost all the gas stations are open Friday in Kathmandu and there are no more lines.
35The popular Hermann's bakery at Lalitpur, a Kathmandu suburb, is selling fresh bread and croissants, which were quickly snapped up.
36Krishna Maharjan, who brought green onions and cauliflower from his farm on a bicycle, says he and his fellow farmers on the outskirts of Kathmandu are now delivering produce to the city.
37"We are trying to get as much fresh food to the people as possible. I feel it is our small contribution, but that's what we can do and every little bit helps," he says.
38Farmers who miss the planting season that is expected to start late May onwards will be unable to harvest rice - the country's staple food - again until late 2016.
39The U.N. Food and Agriculture Organization says this, together with likely losses of food stocks and wheat and maize harvests, would severely limit food supplies and incomes in the South Asian country, where around two-thirds of people rely on agriculture for their livelihood.
40The U.N. humanitarian chief, Valerie Amos, is in Nepal for a three-day visit to meet victims and local leaders.
41She plans to visit areas outside the Kathmandu Valley, according to the deputy spokesman for the U.N. secretary-general, Farhan Haq.
42The U.N. humanitarian office says about 24,000 people are living in 13 camps in Kathmandu.
43More than 130,000 houses are reportedly destroyed.
44The main challenges are the "inaccessibility of some remote areas, the lack of helicopters, poor communication and security concerns."
45Police say a woman in her 20s has been rescued five days after Nepal's massive earthquake.
46A police official identified the woman as Krishna Devi Khadka and said she was rescued Thursday evening in an area near Kathmandu's main bus terminal.
47The official asked not to be identified because he wasn't authorized to speak to the media.
48Earlier Thursday, a 15-year-old boy, Pemba Tamang, was pulled, dazed and dusty, from the wreckage of a seven-story building elsewhere in Kathmandu.
49An Indian seismologist says more than 70 aftershocks have been recorded in the Himalayan region since Saturday's earthquake.
50J.L. Gautam says the 73 aftershocks recorded as of Thursday morning by India's monitoring stations ranged from magnitude 3.2 to 6.9, the strongest occurring Sunday.
51Gautam is the director of seismology at the Indian Meteorological Department in New Delhi.
52India monitors seismic activity from stations in the Himalayan foothills in northern India in the towns of Gangtok, Dehradun and Shimla and others in Shillong and Guwahati in northeastern India.
53Gautam said the aftershocks will continue for "a few weeks, or months, or even years."
54He says, "These aftershocks are quite normal after a powerful earthquake of such magnitude."
55France has announced the death of a third French citizen in the quake.
56The first government flight carrying French nationals from Nepal arrived in Paris overnight.
57According to EU officials, some 9,000 EU citizens are still in Nepal following Saturday's earthquake.
58More than 1,000 of them have not been reached yet.
59The official death toll in Nepal has reached 5,489.
60It does not include the 19 people killed at Mount Everest - five foreign climbers and 14 Nepalese Sherpa guides - when the quake caused an avalanche at base camp.
61Relief aid group Oxfam says congestion at Kathmandu airport, blocked roads, fuel shortages and difficult terrain are slowing down the pace of aid delivery.
62The group says it is looking at ways to transport essential goods overland from India.
63It says challenges include getting aid to remote mountain villages, many of which are connected to the outside world by a single dirt road that may now be blocked by landslides.
64According to the U.N., Saturday's earthquake has displaced about 2.8 million Nepalese.
65Over 70,000 homes are believed to be destroyed and another 530,000 damaged in 39 out of the country's 75 districts.
66Nepalese police say the boy who was pulled from rubble five days after the earthquake is a 15-year-old who made it alive by "good faith."
67Nepalese police officer L.B. Basnet says he spoke to the boy who gave his name as Pemba Tamang.
68Basnet says: "''He thanked me when I first approached him. ... He was really responsive. He told me his name, his address, and I gave him some water. I assured him we were near to him."
69The rescue operation was led by a Nepalese team with American responders from the U.S. Agency for International Development assisting them.
70Basnet wore a headlamp and was dressed in blue camouflage uniform.
71He crawled into the hole to get the boy out.
72A teenage boy has been pulled out from the rubble of a Kathmandu building five days after the earthquake.
73Waiting crowds roared and cheered as the boy was carried out in a stretcher.
74His face was covered in dust, and medics put an IV drop into his arm.
75A blue brace was placed around his neck.
76He appeared stunned, and his eyes blinked in the sunlight.
77A USAID team was working at the scene for several hours to try to free the boy.
78Team leader Andrew Olvera said before the rescue that the boy was not very deep, but he was trapped between two collapsed floors.
79A team of U.S. rescuers is working on pulling out a 15-year-boy who has been buried in the rubble of a collapsed home.
80They were inspecting another site in Kathmandu on Thursday when a Nepalese team with them said a 15-year-old boy was alive and trapped in a nearby building.
81USAID team leader Andrew Olvera says: "We have lights, we have generators, we have heavy breaching equipment, jackhammers, and we're trying to get him out."
82He says the boy is not too far down, but the floors have collapsed and "he's pancaked between them."
83The team has dropped medical supplies and IV fluid to team members who went down inside, including a U.S. firefighter medic who is part of the team.
84It's unclear if the boy was able to receive them.
85The team also stuck a rotatable camera on a pole into the hole to see how best to get him out.
86The site is dangerous.
87Olvera says it looks like it used to be a seven-story building with concrete slabs hanging down.
88European plane maker Airbus is providing helicopters, planes and satellite imagery for relief efforts in Nepal.
89Airbus Helicopters is offering aircraft for French rescue missions to locate and retrieve anyone trapped in difficult-to-reach areas, according to the French Foreign Ministry.
90Airbus' defense and space business is offering satellite imagery to help assess damage and help rescuers target and deliver aid.
91France-based Airbus and its charitable arm plan to use a new Nepal Airlines A320 to bring medical supplies and aid workers to Kathmandu.
92In quake-wracked Nepal, everyone who is able is joining the aid effort.
93The 32-year-oid Narayan Thapa was working at a tire factory in Gorkha town when the earthquake destroyed his village of Bungkot, around 45 kilometers (28 miles) away.
94"Everything is destroyed. Not even one home is standing. We think there is still one person dead under the rubble," Thapa said.
95Thapa and his friends are scrambling to find a four-wheel vehicle to carry the medicine and food they've cobbled together to the village.
96"People are hungry there, they are suffering. I'm doing whatever I can to help," he said.
97Among those in desperate need of supplies in Bungkot, with a population of about 300, are Thapa's parents, wife and daughter.
98"My friends are helping me. Everyone is doing whatever they can. We managed to send a little bit yesterday. My family - they're relying on me. There is no food. They are in trouble there," Thapa said.
