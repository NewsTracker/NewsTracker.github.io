Baltimore unrest puts spotlight on O'Malley's stint as mayor

1BALTIMORE (AP) - Martin O'Malley often casts Baltimore as the comeback city that overcame the ravages of drugs and violence when he was mayor.
2Now, weeks before the former Maryland governor expects to enter the 2016 presidential race and challenge Hillary Rodham Clinton in the Democratic primaries, Baltimore's turnaround has been marred by the unrest after the police-custody death of Freddie Gray.
3The turmoil has placed new scrutiny on O'Malley's "zero tolerance" law enforcement policies as mayor from 1999 to 2006.
4The record shows that murders and violent crime overall declined in O'Malley's years as mayor.
5But it was when a grand jury concluded that too many arrests were being made in black neighborhoods without merit, and when the city settled a lawsuit from people who said they were wrongly arrested for minor offenses - the sort of concerns driving some of the anger in Baltimore today.
6David Rocah, a staff attorney with the ACLU of Maryland, said the O'Malley administration left a legacy of "hyperaggressive and militarized policing" that, in his view, contributed to the outrage behind the riots.
7"I think the idea that you can arrest your way to public safety has always been deeply misguided and counterproductive," Rocah said.
8But O'Malley says those judging him in hindsight should remember the crime and despair of the Baltimore he inherited as mayor.
9"I don't think that any of us want to go back to the days of 1999," O'Malley said.
10"Our city is undoubtedly a safer place, and our city is becoming a better place, but our city still has a lot of progress to make."
11He spoke outside the Dawson Safe Haven Center, an after-school refuge for children that was once a home for a family of seven killed in a 2002 firebombing by a drug dealer.
12O'Malley called that episode "our Alamo."
13O'Malley has tried to build a following in Iowa and New Hampshire as an alternative to Clinton, the dominant front-runner.
14O'Malley has backed tougher regulations on Wall Street, opposed the Trans-Pacific Partnership trade deal and addressed student debt - issues that resonate with liberals.
15FILE - In this Dec. 7, 1999 file photo, Martin O'Malley.
16Still relatively unknown, even among Democrats, O'Malley frequently points to his time as mayor as a key part of his biography.
17A 2013 video by his team, shown at a New Hampshire Democratic dinner where he appeared, described Baltimore in the late 1990s as a "cauldron of crime, drugs and profound despair" and credited O'Malley with "an assault on hopelessness. He didn't make a campaign promise to make the city safer, he made a pledge. And he kept it."
18In the 1990s, more than 300 people were murdered each year in Baltimore.
19O'Malley advocated "stop and frisk" practices, cracked down on lower-level crimes such as public drunkenness and disorderly conduct, and brought in two police commanders from New York steeped in such policing.
20The number of homicides fell to 253 in 2002 and stayed below 300 during his two terms, while never dropping to his goal of 175.
21But the approach did lead to many arrests.
22In 2005, a Baltimore grand jury found excessive arrests in black neighborhoods and recommended retraining so officers would use better judgment.
23Judge Joseph McCurdy Jr. had tasked the panel with determining "what can be done to address the lack of confidence that exists between many members of the public and law enforcement."
24The ACLU and the NAACP sued in 2006 on behalf of 14 plaintiffs who said they were wrongly arrested as part of a policy that emphasized arrests for minor offenses under O'Malley's watch.
25The city agreed to the $870,000 settlement in 2010.
26O'Malley's successors moved away from zero-tolerance policing.
27But he hasn't shied away from his record.
28When the recent protests erupted, he cut short a trip in England and Ireland, returned to Baltimore and walked the streets to talk to former constituents and community leaders.
29Some stopped to shake hands or take pictures with him while others told him about their bad experiences with the police.
30A few heckled him.
31O'Malley told one person the police were also victims of violence.
32"I buried 10 police officers" as mayor, he said.
33"Half were black. Half were white."
34Asked about the zero-tolerance policy, O'Malley said, "What we had zero tolerance for was police misconduct. We worked at it every day."
35His advisers note he created a civilian review board for police conduct, expanded drug treatment and saw a decline in excessive force complaints and police-involved shootings.
36After two terms as mayor, he won two terms as governor with strong support in Baltimore.
37"The people of Baltimore were given ample opportunities to express at the ballot box their satisfaction or dissatisfaction with the direction that our city took to reduce violent crime, to reduce homicides, to make our city more livable," O'Malley said.
38Still, some think the riots erupted, in part, from years of frustration among residents who felt unfairly targeted.
39"He had some responsibility," said Marvin "Doc" Cheatham, a former president of the NAACP's Baltimore city branch.
40"But you have to lay blame also with the majority of the City Council, because the majority of them were in office when he was in office."
