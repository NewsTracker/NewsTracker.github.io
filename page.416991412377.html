Israel's Netanyahu finalises cabinet lineup for vote

1Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu was preparing on Thursday to present his proposed government for the approval of parliament, where his coalition commands a razor-thin majority.
2Netanyahu's new administration marks a shift to the right by giving increased prominence to Naftali Bennett's far-right Jewish Home, which opposes a Palestinian state and strongly backs settlement activity.
3The move looks likely to complicate Israel's already damaged relationship with the Palestinians and further strain ties with the international community.
4After two months of coalition horsetrading, Netanyahu was to present his government for a vote at the Knesset in a session which was to begin at 1600 GMT.
5But following a last-minute request by Netanyahu, the session was set to be delayed by two hours, a Knesset spokesman said.
6Netanyahu did not explain his request, but media was rife with reports of crises in negotiations between the premier and two senior members of his party, Silvan Shalom and Gilad Erdan, over the portfolios they might receive.
7The lineup is dominated by rightwing and religious parties and commands a slender majority of 61 of the parliament's 120 seats, with commentators warning it was vulnerable to the slightest head cold or whim of a disgruntled MP.
8Netanyahu's five-party alliance was built over weeks of intensive negotiations which saw him forced to hand out senior ministerial portfolios to coalition partners in a move which has fostered resentment in his own Likud party.
9On the eve of the March 17 election, Netanyahu promised there would be no Palestinian state on his watch, in remarks which triggered a diplomatic backlash.
10Although he has since sought to backtrack, reviving the peace process is unlikely to be a priority for his new cabinet, which features several ministers bent on expanding settlement construction on land that the Palestinians want for a future state.
11One is the justice minister designate, Ayelet Shaked of Jewish Home, who has drawn fierce criticism for her outspoken views on the Palestinians and her attempts to advance legislation criticised as anti-democratic.
12As the parliamentary vote approached, the allocation of portfolios among Likud members was beginning to take shape.
13Defence Minister Moshe Yaalon was to keep his portfolio, as was Yisrael Katz, who has served as transportation minister since Likud won the 2009 election.
14Katz would also receive the intelligence portfolio, which was formerly held by Yuval Steinitz, who would become energy minister and also continue to coordinate Israel's position on Iran and its nuclear programme, the party said.
15Netanyahu confidant Ofir Akunis will be a junior minister in the ministry of communications, which will be headed by the prime minister himself, a party spokesman said.
16Outspoken Miri Regev was named culture and sports minister, and hardliner Danny Danon, who was fired as deputy defence minister during the summer war with Gaza militants, received the science, technology and space portfolio.
17Yariv Levin was named tourism minister, with his responsibilities including coordination between the government and the Knesset.
18Haim Katz was named welfare minister, and Tzipi Hotovely was appointed deputy foreign minister, with Netanyahu keeping the portfolio for himself and Hotovely, who has called to annex the West Bank and make Palestinians Israeli citizens.
19Druze Likud lawmaker Ayoub Kara, who was briefly hospitalised earlier Thursday, told media he was to be deputy regional cooperation minister, with Netanyahu formally heading the ministry.
20Attorney General Yehuda Weinstein meanwhile paved the way for Arye Deri, head of the ultra-Orthodox Shas party, to be appointed economy minister.
21Deri was imprisoned in 2000 for two years for corruption and breach of trust, felonies he committed when he was interior minister.
22Weinstein said there was no formal legal obstacle for Deri to serve as minister, but warned it would be "difficult" to defend such an appointment, were it challenged in a petition to the High Court.
23The coalition groups Likud, which won 30 seats in the election, with the centre-right Kulanu (10 seats), Jewish Home (eight) and the two ultra-Orthodox parties, Shas (seven) and United Torah Judaism (six).
