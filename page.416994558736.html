Embattled from the outset, new Netanyahu government sworn in

1JERUSALEM, May 14 (Reuters) - Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's new rightist coalition government, hobbled by a razor-thin parliamentary majority, was sworn in late on Thursday amid wrangling within his Likud party over cabinet posts.
2The evening ceremony in the Knesset was postponed by two hours so Netanyahu could divvy up for Likud the remaining ministries, after others were assigned to its four partners.
3Some Israeli commentators called the manoeuvring "farcical".
4The coalition with conservative, far-right, and ultra-Orthodox Jewish parties controls 61 of parliament's 120 seats, auguring difficulty for the fourth-term Netanyahu in passing controversial policies or even surviving no-confidence votes.
5Blowback abroad is also expected.
6Prospects for resolving deadlocked U.S.-sponsored peace talks with the Palestinians are dim, Netanyahu is at loggerheads with Washington over its nuclear negotiations with Iran, and Western diplomats recoil at nationalist legislation championed by some in the coalition.
7Seconds into his speech unveiling the government, as he pledged that it would "safeguard security, work toward peace," Netanyahu was interrupted by opposition heckling and guffaws.
8But coalition lawmakers managed to get the new government approved by a 61-59 vote, to allow its inauguration by midnight.
9Netanyahu has retained four cabinet portfolios for himself, at the cost of leaving key Likud loyalists feeling bereft.
10That has stirred speculation he is holding the ministries in reserve for Isaac Herzog, head of the centre-left opposition, should they eventually join forces in a "national unity" government.
11"I am leaving the door open for broadening the government," Netanyahu said in his speech.
12"The country needs this."
13Herzog was combative when he took the lectern, however: "No decent leader would join the 'Netanyahu circus' that you put together at the last minute, on the strength of one seat, only to extend your rule," he said.
14Shortly after winning a March 17 election, Netanyahu appeared to be coasting toward a comfortable 67-seat majority.
15But in a surprise move last week, he was abandoned by long-time ultra-nationalist coalition ally Avigdor Lieberman.
16The guidelines of the new government make no mention of a two-state solution to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, after talks collapsed in April last year.
17Instead, they pledge in general terms to "advance the diplomatic process and strive for a peace agreement with the Palestinians and all our neighbours".
18U.S. President Barack Obama said on Tuesday he had not given up hope for peacemaking.
19Palestinian President Mahmoud Abbas said on Thursday he was willing to revive peace talks but the onus was on Netanyahu to change course and end Israel's West Bank occupation by 2017.
