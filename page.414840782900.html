Boko Haram leader vows to disrupt Nigeria elections

Fierce fighting in key Ukraine town

1Boko Haram leader Abubakar Shekau has vowed to disrupt Nigeria's general election in a new video released on social media.
2The release of the video came after the Islamist militant group was blamed for two suicide attacks which killed 38 people in the country's north-east.
3"This election will not be held even if we are dead," Shekau said in the video released on Tuesday, presumably referring to the poll scheduled for March 28.
4"Even if we are not alive Allah will never allow you to do it," he said in the Hausa language.
5The video appeared to be the first message released by the group on Twitter, a sign of its changing media tactics after previous messages were distributed to journalists on DVD.
6Shekau was shown in unusual clarity in front of a solid blue background, dressed in black and with an automatic weapon resting to his right.
7Nigeria's general election had been scheduled for February 14 but has been postponed by six weeks.
8Security services say they need more time to contain the violence in the north-east, Boko Haram's stronghold.
9Despite Shekau's threat, experts have said they doubt the Islamist rebels have the capacity to disrupt voting nationwide, although election officials have conceded that voting could prove impossible in parts of the north-east.
10In the video Shekau also claimed responsibility for a weekend attack in the north-east Nigerian city of Gombe, which the military said was repelled.
11He disputed the military's account of the clashes and claimed that his fighters overran troops and freed insurgents from prison.
12The Islamist leader has been declared a global terrorist by the United States, a label sanctioned by the United Nations.
13The US military will share communications equipment and intelligence with African allies to assist them in the fight against Boko Haram, the commander of US Special Forces operations in Africa said.
14West African military commanders have long complained that cross-border operations against Islamist groups - from Al Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM) in Mali to Boko Haram in Nigeria - have been obstructed by a lack of compatible communications equipment, which makes it hard to swap information and coordinate.
15Photo: The Chadian army patrols the border between Nigeria and Cameroon in January, as part of a military contingent against Boko Haram.
16Major General James Linder said that, as part of the annual US-sponsored "Flintlock" counter-terrorism exercises this year in Chad, the US would introduce technology to allow African partners to communicate between mobile phones, radios and computers.
17The RIOS system would allow soldiers in the field to transmit photos from a remote location in the Sahel immediately to a central command room and could also precisely pin-point the coordinates of personnel, a US military official said.
18Brigadier General Zakaria Ngobongue, the Chadian director of the Flintlock exercises, welcomed the US assistance in improving communications between regional armies.
19"We need support in terms of intelligence and logistics, above all in communication because the equipment we have comes from different countries, and their support will make our task much easier," he said.
20Washington's long-term goal was to enable African nations to be sufficiently trained and equipped to face their own security challenges, Mr Linder said.
21"By 2050, one-third of the global population will be on the African continent," he said.
22"The global economy and the global community need stable countries in Africa and that can only happen through African nation states themselves."
23Amid growing international alarm, the four nations of the Lake Chad region - Chad, Niger, Cameroon and Nigeria - plus neighbouring Benin have been preparing a joint task-force of 8,700 men to take on Boko Haram.
24The Sunni jihadist group has killed an estimated 10,000 people last year in its campaign to carve an Islamist emirate from northern Nigeria.

2He told the security council that, in total, nearly 2,500 servicemen had left.
3The Russia-backed separatists reported taking hundreds of soldiers captive as they continued their onslaught on the strategic railroad junction.
4Associated Press reporters saw dozens of Ukrainian troops retreating from Debaltseve with their weapons.
5Some were driving to the nearby town of Artemivsk in trucks while several others were on foot.
6One soldier spoke of heavy government losses, while another said they had not been able to get food for days because of the rebel shelling.
7"We're very happy to be here," said one soldier.
8"We were praying all the time and already said goodbye to our lives a hundred times."
9Mr Poroshenko said: "Debaltseve was under our control, it was never encircled. Our troops and formations have left in an organised and planned manner."
10He spoke at a Kiev airport as he travelled to eastern Ukraine to "shake the hands" of the soldiers who were pulled out of Debaltseve.
11Rebel spokesman Eduard Basurin said hundreds of Ukrainian soldiers had surrendered in Debaltseve.
12Russian state-owned television showed images of Ukrainian troops being escorted along a village road by the rebels.
13Kiev admitted that some soldiers had been taken prisoner in Debaltseve, but gave no details on how many were seized.
14Russian President Vladimir Putin, on a visit to Budapest, had called on Kiev to admit defeat in the contested town, saying "the only choice" of the Ukrainian troops was to "leave behind weaponry, lay down arms and surrender".
15Fighting around Debaltseve, which links the two major separatist cities of Donetsk and Luhansk, had raged on despite a ceasefire deal brokered by European leaders which went into effect on Sunday.
16Some retreating troops said they had not received any reinforcements from the government in Debaltseve and had been walking and retreating for a whole day.
17EU foreign policy chief Federica Mogherini has accused Russian-backed rebels in Debaltseve of acting "in clear violation of the ceasefire".
18"Russia and the separatists have to immediately and fully implement the commitments agreed to in Minsk, in line with yesterday's UN Security Council resolution, starting with the respect of the ceasefire and the withdrawal of all heavy weapons," he said.
19The separatists have raised a flag in triumph over the embattled town.
20Nato secretary-general Jens Stoltenberg accused the separatists of refusing to respect a ceasefire agreement and urged Russia "to end support for separatists and to withdraw forces and military equipment from eastern Ukraine".
21Russia has denied supplying the separatists with troops and weapons, a claim dismissed by Western nations and Ukraine, who point to Nato satellite pictures of Russian weapons in eastern Ukraine.
22The German government condemned the rebels' advance on Debaltseve.
23Chancellor Angela Merkel's spokesman Steffen Seibert described the onslaught as "a serious strain on the (Minsk ceasefire) agreement as well as hopes for peace in eastern Ukraine".
24Semyon Semenchenko, a volunteer battalion commander and a member of the Ukrainian parliament who was critical of the government's decision to retreat, said in a statement that 167 injured soldiers have been evacuated from Debaltseve.
25He mentioned a high death toll and said some bodies had been left behind but did not give specific numbers.
