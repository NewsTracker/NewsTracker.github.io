Cardinals fire scouting director Correa amid hacking scandal (Yahoo Sports)

1ST. LOUIS (AP) -- The St. Louis Cardinals said Thursday they have fired scouting director Chris Correa, the first known fallout from the hacking scandal that has resulted in a federal investigation into whether the team illegally got inside the player personnel database of the Houston Astros.
2The Cardinals declined to say why Correa was fired but confirmed he'd been let go a day earlier after a team-imposed leave of absence.
3The team is investigating the alleged hacking, as is the FBI.
4''At this time, it's still an ongoing investigation and there's really nothing more I can add at this point,'' general manager John Mozeliak said before a game against San Diego.
5Jim Martin, an attorney hired by the team in February, declined to say how long Correa had been on leave.
6Correa, who was promoted to director of amateur scouting during the offseason, shepherded some of the team's top draft picks this season to interviews at Busch Stadium just a few weeks ago.
7Martin said no other employees had been terminated and the investigation wasn't done.
8''I can't give you an end-point,'' Martin said.
9An attorney for Correa, Nicholas Williams, said in a statement that Correa ''denies any illegal conduct'' and instead pointed at the Astros.
10''The relevant inquiry should be what information did former St. Louis Cardinals employees steal from the St. Louis Cardinals organization prior to joining the Houston Astros, and who in the Houston Astros organization authorized, consented to, or benefited from that roguish behavior,'' said Williams, who didn't return a message left seeking additional comment.
11Jeff Luhnow, who headed the Cardinals' scouting and player development department and was a key proponent of the team's Redbird database, was hired as the Astros general manager in December 2011.
12At least one former Cardinals employee - Sig Mejdal, a former NASA employee and analytics expert - joined Luhnow in Houston.
13Asked about Williams' comment, Astros general counsel Giles Kibbe declined comment.
14''We look forward to the FBI concluding their investigation,'' he said.
15The hack was first reported in June 2014 after some of the trade talk was published online.
16The Astros rely heavily on analytics in their evaluation of players and use an online database called Ground Control to house proprietary information.
17The FBI has declined to confirm it is investigating the Cardinals.
18But a person familiar with the investigation has told the AP that federal authorities are looking at whether members of the team were to blame for what Major League Baseball called a ''breach'' of the Astros database.
19There has been no indication of how many employees might be under investigation and any motive for the hack remains unclear.
20Last month, Martin said Cardinals Chairman Bill DeWitt Jr. and Mozeliak were not targets of the investigation.
21DeWitt also used the term ''roguish behavior'' last month in a news conference in which the team confirmed the investigation.
