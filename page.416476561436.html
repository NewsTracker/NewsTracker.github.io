Greece heads for more crunch talks with European creditors

1An man walk past a kiosk with Greek flags for sale in central Athens, Thursday, April 23, 2015.
2Greece is running perilously short of cash amid an impasse in bailout talks with its international creditors.
3Eurozone finance ministers are to meet in Riga, Latvia on Friday but hopes of a deal on Greece there have diminished.
4RIGA, Latvia (AP) - Greece sought to downplay the scale of its differences with its bailout creditors as the eurozone's top official said Friday that time is running out for the country to secure a deal that will prevent its bankruptcy.
5Though Eurogroup president Jeroen Dijsselbloem downplayed any expectations of a deal at the meeting of 19 eurozone finance ministers in the Latvian capital of Riga, he said he hoped to be able to report some progress in the protracted discussions.
6He said there's a "great sense of urgency" from all sides to get a deal done, particularly for Athens so the country can get the financial support it needs "to make sure there's enough money available to keep the government running."
7Greece had an end-of-April date to agree to more reforms in exchange for rescue money its creditors had set aside.
8Without the money, Greece faces potential bankruptcy and a possible exit from the euro, a development that many in global policymaking circles feel could damage the world's economic recovery.
9Though Dijsselbloem noted that "April isn't over yet," he said the deadline was far more important for Athens than the eurogroup.
10Slovakia's finance minister, Peter Kazimir, showed little optimism that a deal would be agreed in the coming days and even ventured that Athens has until the end of June.
11"The end of June is the most important," he said.
12For weeks, the meeting in Riga was expected to be the one where Greece's immediate financial future would be sorted out.
13Greece's left-wing government, elected in January on a mandate to bring crippling austerity to an end, would present its reform plans to its creditors in the eurozone.
14If all worked out as planned, Greece would be handed the remaining money available in its bailout program - 7.2 billion euros ($7.7 billion) - to pay off upcoming debts to its creditors in the eurozone and the International Monetary Fund.
15Creditors have demanded reforms that include sweeping changes to pensions and labor rules.
16But the Greek government has ruled out many key demands, arguing it was elected to end the kind of stifling budget austerity that contributed to a 25 percent contraction in the economy.
17Its focus is geared far more on fighting corruption and increasing its tax take than more cuts.
18After weeks of tortuous discussions that have yielded little besides distrust, Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis is not expected to present any reform plans at the meeting.
19Though Varoufakis did not speak to reporters as he entered the meeting, he insisted in a blog post published Friday that the "current disagreements are not unbridgeable" and that there already is common ground, particularly on revamping the tax system and making pensions fit for purpose by, for example, limiting early retirement.
20"The Greek government wants a fiscal-consolidation path that makes sense, and we want reforms that all sides believe are important," Varoufakis said.
21"Our task is to convince our partners that our undertakings are strategic, rather than tactical, and that our logic is sound. Their task is to let go of an approach that has failed."
22Heading into the meeting, Slovakia's Kazimir showed his frustration at the lack of progress, saying he was "a little bit tired" by the failure of the Greek authorities to present a properly costed set of proposals that would help break the logjam.
23"I have almost no expectation today," he said.
24"Substance is missing, this is the crucial problem. Time is running and we have no time for diplomatic or political chit-chat."
