Review: The best new features coming from Apple, Google

1SAN FRANCISCO (AP) - New features promised for smartphones, tablets and watches could improve how we interact with technology.
2Although it's too soon to tell how well these will work in practice, here's a look at what I'm looking forward to most this fall.
3I like to do other things, such as checking email or Facebook, when I'm watching video.
4Laptops let me do this.
5With iPads, video stops as soon as I open another app.
6I need to pick up a second device to multitask.
7With the upcoming iOS 9 update, you can slide a window from the right of an iPad to launch a second app, such as a map when you're doing email.
8Best of all, you can have video running in a small window in the foreground while you do other things with an app that takes up the rest of the screen.
9You can even move the video off screen and still listen to its audio while you work.
10Samsung has tried this with some Android phones and tablets, with mixed success.
11Video sometimes stops anyway when you try to run it on the side, as apps weren't designed with this in mind.
12With Apple, it will be a core feature.
13Apple Maps has improved a lot for driving and walking directions since it ousted Google Maps in 2012 as the default service on iPhones.
14Transit directions are coming with iOS 9, though only in select cities at first.
15In the U.S., that will be Baltimore, Chicago, New York, Philadelphia, the San Francisco Bay Area and Washington.
16It's not everywhere, but it's a good start.
17On Android, Google Maps will get offline navigation, matching what Windows phones offer with Here Maps.
18You'll get turn-by-turn directions and details on local businesses even when cellular service is sketchy or nonexistent.
19You'll have to download a specific region of the map ahead of time, and it's not yet clear how easy or how wide of a region that will be.
20It's also not yet known whether this feature will extend to Google Maps on iPhones.
21Siri on Apple devices and Google Now on Android devices are supposed to get smarter by doing more based on context.
22Calling on Google's assistant while listening to a song might give you information on the artist.
23Doing so while reading an email on movie plans might get you reviews and trailers.
24Siri, meanwhile, should know whether you're going on a run or getting in a car.
25You might automatically get music on a run, but an audiobook in your car.
26Siri also promises to anticipate your needs.
27If you have a meeting coming up, she'll remind you to leave, factoring in current traffic.
28Google Now has been doing that for years, with mixed results.
29I get reminders to leave for the airport followed by directions back home, for instance.
30I look forward to seeing how well Siri and Google Now perform with the upcoming smarts.
31Android users will get more control over what permissions they give apps.
32Currently, you're shown a long list of permissions when installing new apps, such as letting the app access your location or the phone's microphone.
33You have to accept all of them or forgo the app.
34With the upcoming M version of Android, you give permission as apps need it.
35That's how it works on iPhones.
36So if an app needs access to your camera, you decide then whether to grant permission.
37If you decline, you can still use the app's other features.
38On Apple Watch, apps from outside parties have essentially been extensions of phone apps.
39All the computing is done on the phone, with bits of information transferred to the watch's screen wirelessly.
40That will change with the upcoming watchOS 2 system.
41For the first time, third-party apps will be able to directly interact with sensors and controls on the watch, something Apple apps can already do.
42That makes it possible for outside parties to write games, fitness and other apps that work without the phone.
43Apple Watch will also be able to play video for the first time - if you don't mind the small screen.
44Phones running Microsoft's system have a lot of innovations, including direct links from the home screen to specific tasks within apps.
45Windows phones suffer from a lack of apps, though.
46New tools will make it easier for app developers to convert their existing iOS and Android apps to the upcoming Windows 10.
47Windows phones could become a viable alternative - if developers see a reason to bother.
