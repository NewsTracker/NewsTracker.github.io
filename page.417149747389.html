A Georgian region is fertile ground for Islamic State

1In this April 7, 2015 photo a relative shows a photo of Muslim Kushtanashvili, left on the screen, in a house in the village of Duisi, about 70 km (43 miles) north of Tbilisi, in the Pankisi Gorge, Georgia.
2As many as 200 young men have gone to fight in Syria from Georgia's Pankisi Gorge, the native region of Islamic State commander Omar al Shishani.
3Last September, the U.S. Treasury Department placed Omar al Shishani on its list of "specially designated global terrorists."
4But to some in the Pankisi, the mountainous region of northeastern Georgia where he was born in 1986, the ginger-bearded commander is a hero and a role model.
5BIRKIANI, Georgia (AP) - In the summer of 2012, a former Georgian Army corporal who had served prison time for illegal possession of ammunition burned his photo albums and quit his native village.
6Tarkhan Batirashvili had wanted to become a policeman, but couldn't get hired.
7Now, this offspring of a Christian father and a Muslim mother was about to start a new chapter in his military career - one in which he would be credited with some of Islamic State's most stunning battlefield victories and rise to senior rank.
8Last September, the U.S. Treasury Department placed Batirashvili - who now calls himself Omar al Shishani and is believed by some to be Islamic State's chief of military operations - on its list of "specially designated global terrorists."
9To follow the path he blazed, as many as 200 of his young countrymen have left their villages.
10Batirashvili's father Temur is aghast.
11"It's monstrous what's going on in the valley, that they are deceiving these kids and they're leaving to fight in a foreign land," the 72-year-old man told visitors to his one-story stone house in the hamlet of Birkiani.
12"My son should not be in Syria."
13The Pankisi is home to an estimated 5,000-7,000 descendants of Muslim Chechens who settled here in the 19th century.
14In the village of Omalo, locals say, a green-tile roofed building is used by preachers from the Wahhabi branch of Sunni Islam to enlist volunteers for jihad.
15Residents say the recruiters promise money and provide ground transportation to Tbilisi airport more than 120 miles (200 km) away, as well as prepaid plane tickets to Turkey.
16On April 2, her 16-year-old grandson Muslim Kushtanashvili and schoolmate Ramzan Bagakashvili, 18, joined the valley's recruits to Islamic State.
17That Thursday, the teens left as usual for their school in Omalo, next door to the building used by the Wahhabis.
18They never came home.
19Muslim called friends later to say he was in Turkey.
20Then word came back the 10th grader had crossed into Syria.
21A legal minor, Muslim also had no passport, so his family can't understand why Georgian border guards let him fly out of the country.
22Interior Minister Vakhtang Gomelauri has vowed an investigation and punishment for those responsible, but Muslim's family suspect authorities are protecting the recruiters.
23Bagakashvili's mother agrees.
24"Here they are stirring up things, recruiting youths," Tina Alkhanashvili said.
25"You can't get authorities to watch them."
26Within weeks, Georgian officials reported three more boys from the valley had disappeared.
