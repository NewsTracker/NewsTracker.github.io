Nepal's prime minister: 'We were not prepared' for 2nd quake

1CHARIKOT, Nepal (AP) - Nepal has been overwhelmed by its second powerful earthquake in less than three weeks, its prime minister said Thursday as he visited this normally placid foothills town, now filling up with frightened villagers desperate for government help.
2Thousands of people coming from surrounding areas to seek help crowd the streets of Charikot, the administrative center of the isolated district hit hardest by Tuesday's magnitude-7.3 quake, which killed at least 110 people and injured more than 2,300.
3The magnitude-7.8 earthquake that hit April 25 killed more than 8,150 people, injured tens of thousands more and left hundreds of thousands homeless.
4"After the first quake, we were not prepared for a second one so big," Prime Minister Sushil Koirala told reporters after arriving in Charikot by helicopter.
5A Nepalese farmer carries a bundle of harvested wheat in Nalinchowk, near Kathmandu, Nepal, Thursday, May 14, 2015.
6More than half of s population work in agriculture.
7With tens of thousands of barns and storage sheds destroyed by the earthquakes, many small farmers now have no place to store their crops this year.
8With hundreds of thousands of people left homeless, he said the coming monsoon rains loomed large.
9"We need tents. Our people need shelter. With the rainy season, it will be difficult for people to survive in the open," he said.
10Nearly everyone is too afraid to sleep indoors and aftershocks are keeping people on edge in this town.
11Food has been handed out occasionally here, but nowhere near enough for the people who keep arriving.
12Many simply waited at the locked gates of the army's small aid distribution center, shaking the fence angrily when their frustration got the better of them.
13"We came here with such hopes and such difficulty, but now we're just waiting and waiting," said Navraj Nama, 25, who came to Charikot with his brother and elderly uncle after the second earthquake.
14He said 90 percent of their home village, Danda Khorka, had been damaged in the April 25 quake, and about 50 buildings collapsed when the second one hit.
15Nama's village is among those in desperate need of shelter, and the young farmer came here hoping to get tents or tarpaulins to carry back with him.
16None was available.
17Sabita Debi, who ran a tea shop with her teenage son, said she has been living in the open with her children since the first April 25 earthquake.
18Her damaged house leaning to one side, Devi said she had received one tarp sheet and some rice in the past two weeks.
19Her face is red and chapped from the long days spent in the sun.
20"The government is promising but nothing has come to us ... We keep running here and there when we hear food is being distributed," said 35-year-old Debi, dressed in a crumpled sari since she left her home two weeks ago.
21Ganga Karki Jungu, a social worker in Charikot, said the people were angry as "they have no roof, no food."
22He said the government was failing to coordinate the distribution of relief material.
23"In many villages, there is not a single house that has not developed cracks or not collapsed altogether. The buildings that remain are shaky."
24Jungu was among a group of social workers and political activists who met Prime Minister Koirala to press for urgent help.
25"The government has to build some permanent structures to provide housing on a large scale before the monsoon rains begin, " he told the prime minister.
26Koirala promised help.
27"We all have to work with a new determination to build a new Nepal. All our efforts will be to reconstruct homes and rebuild the lives."
28Sabita Devi said "if we don't get help soon, I don't know what is going to happen to us."
29Her husband was in the capital at the time of the first quake nearly three weeks ago.
30Devi said she heard from her relatives that he was safe there.
31"But there has been no word from him," she said.
32There is also a shortage of tarps and tents in Kathmandu, with some people even using cardboard boxes as temporary shelters.
33"We have nowhere to go. This is our home for now. We had just moved back into our rented rooms and again the earthquakes are back," said carpenter Raj Kumar, who was sharing a small tent with two other families in Kathmandu.
34Meanwhile, a search continued for a U.S. Marine helicopter carrying six Marines and two Nepalese soldiers that disappeared Tuesday while delivering aid.
35Officials in Kathmandu said the search for the helicopter was focused around Sunkhani, nearly 80 kilometers (50 miles) northeast of the capital.
36Tuesday's quake was less powerful than the one in April and shook a smaller, less populated area.
37It was centered between Kathmandu and Mount Everest.
38It hit hardest in deeply rural parts of the Himalayan foothills, hammering many villages reached only by hiking trails and causing road-blocking landslides.
39AP writers Binaj Gurubacharya in Kathmandu, Tim Sullivan in New Delhi and Robert Burns in Washington contributed to this report.
