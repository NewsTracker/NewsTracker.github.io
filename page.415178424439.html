Mess Hall competes for top title

Georgia woman's execution postponed again; first weather, now cloudy drugs

1The mess hall was being considered for the Maj.
2Gen. William Pendleton Thompson Hill Award.
3The award was established in 1985 to improve food service and recognize the best mess halls in the Marine Corps.
4Lt. Col.
5Richard Kohler, director of food services for the Marine Corps, and Master Gunnery Sgt. Bryan Velloza, senior enlisted advisor for food services for the Marine Corps, were accompanied by Jack Kleckner, a representative of the National Restaurant Association, to evaluate the mess hall for the corps-wide award.
6The dining facility opened in 2013 and has a drive-thru window, walk-in window and large dining area, which all-in-all can serve more than 2,000 Marines.
7In order for a facility to compete for the annual award, it must first be evaluated at the local level by base food services representatives.
8The representatives report the top three for each region to Headquarters Marine Corps.
9Headquarters Marine Corps then sends their W.P.T. Hill evaluation team to grade the mess halls on 11 categories highlighting sanitation, food acceptability, management and food preparation.
10Winners of the award will be given a trip to Chicago to participate in an honorary ceremony with presentation of trophies for the best mess halls in the military.
1JACKSON, Ga.
2(AP) -- Citing concerns about the drug to be used in a lethal injection, corrections officials in Georgia postponed the execution of the state's only female death row inmate for the second time in a week.
3The execution drug was sent to an independent lab to check its potency and the test came back at an acceptable level, but during subsequent checks it appeared cloudy, Georgia Department of Corrections spokeswoman Gwendolyn Hogan said.
4Corrections officials called the pharmacist and decided to postpone Kelly Renee Gissendaner's Monday night execution "out of an abundance of caution," she said.
5No new date was given.
6Gissendaner, 46, was scheduled to be executed at 7 p.m. at the prison in Jackson for the February 1997 slaying of her husband, Douglas Gissendaner.
7Pentobarbital is the only drug used in Georgia executions.
8For other recent executions, the state has gotten the drug from a compounding pharmacy, but officials did not immediately respond to an email late Monday asking if that was the source in this case.
9Georgia law prohibits the release of any identifying information about the source of execution drugs or any entity involved in an execution.
10A request for a stay by Gissendaner's lawyers was pending when corrections officials decided to postpone the execution.
11Her lawyers sought a delay until the U.S. Supreme Court rules in a case out of Oklahoma.
12Late Monday, her lawyers added additional arguments for the high court: that it should consider a stay because Gissendaner didn't kill her husband herself.
13They also argued she had been thoroughly rehabilitated.
14Previously, courts had found Gissendaner plotted the stabbing death of her husband by her boyfriend, Gregory Owen, who will be up for parole in eight years after accepting a life sentence and testifying against her.
15Gissendaner was originally set to die Feb. 25, but corrections officials delayed the execution until Monday because of projected winter weather conditions.
16Gissendaner would have been the first woman executed in Georgia in 70 years and only the 16th woman put to death nationwide since the Supreme Court allowed the death penalty to resume in 1976.
17About 1,400 men have been executed since then, according to the Death Penalty Information Center.
18The Georgia Board of Pardons and Paroles, the only entity authorized to commute a death sentence, denied clemency last week and upheld that decision late Monday.
19Gissendaner's lawyers had urged the board to reconsider and "bestow mercy" by commuting her sentence to life without parole.
20Kelly and Douglas Gissendaner had a troubled relationship, repeatedly splitting up and getting back together, even divorcing and remarrying.
21At the time of her husband's death, Gissendaner was a 28-year-old mother of three children, 12, 7 and 5 years old.
22And Owen was her on-again, off-again lover.
23Rather than divorcing her husband again, Gissendaner repeatedly pushed Owen to kill him, prosecutors said.
24Acting on her instructions, Owen ambushed her husband while she went out with friends, and forced him to drive to a remote area.
25Then he marched him into the woods and stabbed him multiple times, prosecutors said.
26Owen and Gissendaner then met up and set fire to the dead man's car in an attempted cover-up.
27Both initially denied involvement, but Owen eventually confessed and testified against his former girlfriend.
28Gissendaner's lawyers argued her sentence was disproportionate, given that she wasn't there when Owen killed her husband, and Owen got a life sentence with the possibility of parole.
29But Georgia's Supreme Court voted 5-2 Monday to deny her challenge, citing Owen's testimony that she pushed for murder rather than divorce so she could get her husband's insurance money.
30In their request Monday for reconsideration, Gissendaner's lawyers said the parole board did not have a chance to hear the overwhelmingly positive testimony of many corrections employees who declined to speak up for fear of retaliation.
31Her initial clemency petition already included testimonials from dozens of spiritual advisers, inmates and prison staff who described a seriously damaged woman transformed through faith behind bars.
32She has shown remorse and provided hope to struggling inmates while helping guards maintain control, they said.
33"The spiritual transformation and depth of faith that Ms. Gissendaner demonstrates and practices is a deep and sincere expression of a personal relationship with God," chaplain Susan Bishop wrote.
34"It is not a superficial religious experience."
35Two of Gissendaner's three children also asked the board to spare their mother's life, describing their own emotional journey from anger and bitterness to forgiveness.
36"The impact of losing my mother would be devastating. I can't fathom losing another parent," wrote her daughter, Kayla Gissendaner.
37"My mom has touched so many lives. Executing her doesn't bring justice or peace to me or to anyone."
38But it also has been "a long, hard, heartbreaking road" for Douglas Gissendaner's parents and sisters, according to a statement from them issued through the Gwinnett County district attorney's office.
39The family made it clear they wanted the execution to go forward.
40Several dozen people gathered outside the prison in support of Gissendaner, including some women who had been in prison with her.
41Michelle Collins, who did time for forgery, remembered Gissendaner persuading her to stop misbehaving and start caring about her future.
42"She looked around at us and said, 'At least y'all are going to get out of here again. Who are you to throw your lives away when I'm never going to get out of here?'" said Collins.
43"She gave me the will to do something good when I got out," Collins said, adding that she now makes good money working for a Fortune 500 company.
44"She told me to make sure I never came back and I never have."
45The crowd gathered outside the prison cheered loudly when they heard the execution had been postponed.
