U.S. Commander Says Some 12,000 Russian Soldiers In Eastern Ukraine

1The U.S. military estimates some 12,000 Russian soldiers are supporting pro-Moscow separatists in eastern Ukraine.
2U.S. Army Europe Commander Ben Hodges said the Russian forces are made up of military advisers, weapons operators and combat troops.
3Hodges also said some 29,000 Russian troops are in Crimea, which Moscow annexed from Ukraine last year.
4Hodges said in Berlin on March 3 that helping Ukraine with weapons would increase the stakes for Russian President Vladimir Putin at home.
5He added that "when mothers start seeing sons come home dead, when that price goes up, then that domestic support begins to shrink."
6Hodges said what Ukraine wants "is intelligence, counterfire capability and something that can stop a Russian tank."
7The White House still hasn't decided whether to send arms to Ukraine, and Hodges reiterated Washington wanted a diplomatic solution.
8Hodges also said U.S. plans to train three Ukrainian battalions had been put on hold to see if a cease-fire deal forged last month in the Belarusian capital Minsk will be fully implemented.
9General Martin Dempsey, chairman of the U.S. Joint Chiefs of Staff, also voiced support for arming Ukraine on March 3.
10Speaking before the Senate Armed Services Committee, Dempsey said Washington "should absolutely consider" providing Kyiv with arms through NATO.
11Dempsey said Putin's ultimate goal was to fracture NATO.
12Meanwhile, U.S. President Barack Obama and European leaders have agreed that a "strong reaction" would be necessary if the Minsk cease-fire agrement is violated.
13French President Francois Hollande's office said that in a March 3 video conference, the leaders underscored their support for the Minsk agreement.
14"They have agreed that a strong reaction from the international community would be necessary in case of a major breach of the process put in place in Minsk," it said in a statement.
15White House spokesman Josh Earnest said Obama used the call to condemn Russia for not following through on its agreements.
16"It was an opportunity ... for the president to condemn the continuing failure of Russia and the separatists it backs to abide by the commitments to which they agreed," Earnest said.
17The leaders of Britain, Germany, and Italy also participated in the conference, as did European Council President Donald Tusk of Poland.
18The French statement also said the parties wanted to bolster the resources of monitors from the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe (OSCE).
19European leaders on March 2 said they agreed that the OSCE needed a broader role as observers of the cease-fire agreement and of the removal of heavy weapons.
20Ukraine and Western governments have accused Russia of sending troops and weapons to support separatists in eastern Ukraine in violation of the Minsk agreement.
21Russia denies aiding the rebels.
22On March 3, Kyiv announced its highest casualty toll in several days, with three Ukrainian servicemen dead and nine wounded amid pro-Russian shelling.
23Later this week, Ukraine's parliament is expected to back Ukrainian President Petro Poroshenko's request for international peacekeepers to monitor the conflict.
