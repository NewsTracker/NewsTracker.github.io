What's holding up a deal on Greece's bailout

1A woman looks a front store as a worker paints the wall of a shop in the northern Greek port city of Thessaloniki, Wednesday, June 10, 2015.
2Greece has three weeks left to conclude a deal with its creditors before its bailout program expires at the end of the month.
3It is desperate for the disbursal of the final 7.2 billion euros in rescue loans, without which it cannot continue to repay its international debts.
4An elderly woman holds shopping bags as she leaves from a market area in the northern Greek port city of Thessaloniki, Wednesday, June 10, 2015.
5ATHENS, Greece (AP) - The ball is in Greece's court, its creditors say.
6But this isn't tennis - unless you can imagine a match between argumentative snails, coached by committee, without an umpire.
7After four and a half months of back and forth, Greece and its creditors remain far from a deal on what reforms the country must make to get more rescue loans.
8It needs the money to avoid defaulting on its debts and save itself from an even nastier mess, like falling out of the euro.
9The radical left-led government in Athens started off boldly in January, making pie-in-the-sky promises to Greeks to make life easy again by rolling back cuts and reforms.
10On the other hand, the creditors - fellow eurozone states and International Monetary Fund - are little inclined to budge from the policies they have pushed on Greece for five years: heavy cuts that have succeeded in taming government spending but otherwise mauled the economy and society.
11Creditors want cuts worth 1.8 billion euros ($2 billion) to pensions this year.
12They also want Greece to end state financing now for auxiliary pensions - a top-up to pensions that many Greeks receive.
13Greece refuses both measures.
14Creditors also wants to immediately make it more difficult to qualify for early retirement, whereas the government in Athens is offering a staggered increase in the required age and other measures to make early retirement less generous.
15There are currently three rates of tax on the sale of goods: 6.5, 13 and 23 percent.
16Creditors want two rates, 11 and 23 percent, which would automatically hike the cost of most food, medicine, electricity bills and - in a tourism-reliant economy - hotels and restaurants.
17Greece is proposing rates of 6.5, 12 and 23 percent, with key consumer staples, hotels and restaurants in the low and middle brackets.
18The Greek government wants to restore the minimum wage from around 580 euros ($654) monthly to 750 euros ($846) by the end of next year - it had initially promised to do that promptly after its election - and reintroduce collective wage negotiating.
19Creditors reject that.
20Creditors want primary budget surpluses - which exclude interest payments on debt - of 1, 2 and 3 percent of GDP for 2015, 2016 and 2017, respectively.
21The government is offering 0.75, 1.75 and 2 percent of GDP.
22Because the country is back in recession and GDP is shrinking, any surplus will require substantial belt-tightening.
23The elephant in the room, for creditors, seems to be Greece's bloated debt - a daunting 317 billion euros ($358 billion), or 176 percent of the country's annual economic output.
24Tsipras' government insists it won't sign an agreement unless it contains some provision on debt relief.
25Creditors promised last year to discuss the issue, but never did.
26None of the recent proposals leaked from the creditors mention debt relief.
