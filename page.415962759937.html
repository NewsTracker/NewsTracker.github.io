Kenya massacre victims were lined up and shot as they phoned parents

1Most of the 147 victims of the terrorist attack on a Kenyan university on Thursday died execution-style as they lined up waiting for their turn to be shot, a senior Kenyan government source has told The Telegraph, London.
2Some students were killed as they spoke to their parents on the telephone, having been ordered to call with messages from the four gunmen that their aim was to force Kenyan troops to leave Somalia, the source added.
3"This is the level of depravity that we are dealing with. It is something beyond the comprehension of anyone normal like you or I," the source, who spoke anonymously, said.
4"These are not people who can be reasoned with, only force can stop them."
5The gunmen, from the Somali Islamist terrorist group al-Shabaab, stormed Garissa University in north-east Kenya.
6They also told students that they were "here to make your Easter holidays better" and warned of further attacks to come, survivors disclosed.
7Maureen Manyengo, a 21-year-old Christian from western Kenya, who was training to be a teacher, described how she hid inside her wardrobe after seeing several friends killed.
8"I could hear the attackers telling my friends, 'Do not worry, we will kill you, but we will die too'," she said.
9She said the terrorists, clad in suicide vests, also told the cowering students: "We are not bad guys, we are just here to make your Easter holiday better."
10Reuben Nyaora, an aid worker who was among the first to enter the university after the terrorists' final clash with Kenyan special forces late on Thursday afternoon, described seeing women rise from among the bodies covered in blood but unscathed.
11"I have seen many things, but nothing like that," said Mr Nyaora.
12"There were bodies everywhere in execution lines, we saw people whose heads had been blown off, bullet wounds everywhere. It was a grisly mess."
13So far, 147 people have been confirmed dead and 79 injured in Kenya's worst terrorist attack in two decades, but officials have admitted that the number killed could rise as piles of bodies are recounted.
14Joseph Nkaissery, Kenya's interior minister, confirmed that the gunmen had been strapped with explosives and blew up "like bombs" as they were shot by a special forces squad.
15All four died.
16The Telegraph's government source said that when the squad reached the room where the attackers were holed up, they had just six hostages with them, whom they killed.
17They then detonated their vests as they died in a hail of bullets, the source added.
18At the gates of Garissa University, soldiers kept large crowds of sobbing relatives at bay as inside, the bodies of those killed were recounted.
19"I am so worried, I had a son who was among the students trapped inside the college, and since yesterday I have heard nothing," said Habel Mutinda, an elderly man, his face streaming with tears.
20"I tried to identify his body. I have to do that before the body goes bad in the heat. I have been camping overnight. It is really hard, it hurts."
21The gunmen targeted Christians over Muslims.
22One witness described how they headed directly to a lecture hall where Christian students had gathered for an early morning prayer session.
23Another told how Muslim classmates tried to dissuade the killers, but were ordered to go to the college's mosque where they would be safe from harm.
24Kenya's government, which has faced criticism for its failure to act on intelligence to combat threats, said it would not be "intimidated or humiliated" by what happened.
25"The government is determined to fight back the terrorists, and I am confident we shall win this war against our enemies," Mr Nkaissery said.
26Questions are now being asked about how the authorities reacted to intelligence that an attack was imminent, potentially on a university, and whether it has learnt lessons from its haphazard response to the Westgate shopping centre terrorist attack two years ago, in which 72 people died.
27Peter Aling'o, senior researcher with the Institute for Security Studies in Nairobi, said al-Shabaab was taking advantage of "gaps" in Kenya's intelligence-led security planning.
28"I think Kenya hasn't learnt anything at all in terms of how to respond to terror attacks," he said.
29"What we are seeing is a knee-jerk reaction that sends in security personnel in a manner that suggests they are not completely aware of what they are responding to."
30Some of those who escaped Thursday's massacre said posters had been put up around campus and the university's administrators warned about an imminent attack but they were "ignored" or dismissed as a prank.
31Students from a nearby teacher training college said they too had been warned that "strangers" suspected to be terrorists had been spotted in Garissa in the days leading up to the attack.
32As a result, their college was closed and they were sent home.
33The university massacre happened six days after Britain advised "against all but essential travel" to parts of Kenya, including Garissa.
34A day before the attack, President Uhuru Kenyatta dismissed the warning as well as one from Australia, saying: "Kenya is safe as any country in the world. The travel advisories being issued by our friends are not genuine."
