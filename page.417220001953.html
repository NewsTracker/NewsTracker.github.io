6It may be the most topical film at Cannes - but "Mediterranea" - a searing account of a desperate African migrant who travels by boat from Burkina Faso to Italy - is not just pulled from the headlines.
7It's been years in the making and is based on a true story.
8CANNES, France (AP) - The 68th Cannes Film Festival was going out in style this weekend, with Marion Cotillard drawing raves on both carpet and screen at the "Macbeth" premiere and the very cool entourage from the LA indie "Dope" continuing to wow the fest as anticipation grew for Sunday's Palme d'Or finale.
9Marion Cotillard plays a queen in Justin Kurzel's "Macbeth," but at its Cannes premiere she was also a bejeweled queen of the red carpet.
10In a blue-and-rose truncated minidress from Dior's haute couture spring-summer 2015 collection, Cotillard sparkled Saturday as she posed alongside co-star Michael Fassbender, thanks to the outfit's myriad embroidered pailettes and jewels.
11The Oscar-winner, who's known for her fashion-forward style, bucked the trend for classical va-va-voom and voluminous dresses that have been popular during this year's Cannes Film Festival.
12Although the look showed off the 39-year-old's enviable legs, she might have benefited from a scarf, as the overcast Cannes sky caused a rather chilly spell.
13Something wicked came the way of the Cannes Film Festival on Saturday when Justin Kurzel premiered his bloody, stylish adaptation of "Macbeth" with Michael Fassbender and Marion Cotillard.
14Cannes hailed the premiere of the Australian director's interpretation of the Shakespeare tragedy, the final film to screen in competition at the festival, which concludes Sunday.
15The film, among the most eagerly awaited at Cannes, is a grimly visceral adaptation of "Macbeth," shot partly in Scotland, striking in the bleak beauty of its earthy violence.
16To play the Scottish warrior, Fassbender gravitated to the idea that Macbeth suffered from post-traumatic stress disorder.
17He credited Kurzel for making the connection to contemporary warfare.
18"That changed everything for me," Fassbender told reporters Saturday.
19"We know from soldiers today coming back from Iraq or Afghanistan that describe post-traumatic stress disorder and the fact that they have these hallucinations. They can be walking down the street here, the Croisette, and the next thing, it's Basra."
20Cotillard drew the loudest raves for her Lady Macbeth.
21Hers is a tender version of the character, not the manipulative monster often depicted.
22A week and a half of French Riviera frenzy comes down to, as it always does, wild conjecture.
23The 68th Cannes Film Festival concludes Sunday with the awarding of its top honor, the prestigious Palme d'Or, as well as a handful of other distinctions.
24Few trophies in movies are more sought after than the Palme d'Or, but, unlike the Academy Awards, which whittles its field down to a few favorites over the course of months, Cannes winners are chosen clandestinely by a jury.
25Throughout the festival, the jury, led by Joel and Ethan Coen, has quietly slipped in and out of theaters.
26Their deliberations are private, but that doesn't stop the world's media from endlessly debating the possible preferences of the Coens and fellow jurors Guillermo del Toro, Jake Gyllenhaal, Sienna Miller, Sophie Marceau, Xavier Dolan, Rokia Traore and Rossy de Palma.
27All of the 19 films in competition could feasibly walk away with the Palme d'Or, but a handful of perceived favorites have emerged.
28A year ago, writer-director Rick Famuyiwa was struggling to find financing for his film after studios in Hollywood passed on it.
29Now, he's sitting in a restaurant at Cannes riding the sensation of "Dope," his comic Los Angeles coming-of-age tale that sparked a bidding war this year at the Sundance Film Festival.
30"It's pretty freaking awesome," Famuyiwa says, tipping back his white fedora.
31"Here we are less than a year later. It's been crazy from Sundance to here. I haven't quite gotten any perspective on it yet, but I'm enjoying the moment."
32"Dope" premiered Friday in the Cannes Film Festival's Directors' Fortnight section with most of its young cast enthusiastically in tow.
33While many indies that make it to Cannes arrive with a small retinue, "Dope" is here in full force - and some cast members even paid their own way to come.
34"Dope" is a buoyant, stereotype-busting high-school movie about a geek (Moore) in Inglewood, California, who's aiming to get into Harvard but is thrust into a rollicking adventure when drug dealers looking for a hiding place stuff his backpack full of Ecstasy.
35The close bond between the cast comes partly out of the unlikeliness of their journey.
36"Dope" was made last summer in a rapid 24-day shoot around Los Angeles.
37"We were making something we all believe in," says Famuyiwa.
38"Rams," a drama set among farmers and their sheep in a remote Icelandic valley, won the Cannes Film Festival's Un Certain Regard competition on Saturday.
39Jury president Isabella Rossellini said Grimur Hakonarson's film was being honored for "treating in a masterful, tragicomic way the undeniable bond that links all humans to animals."
40There were 19 films in the Un Certain Regard competition, which honors new directors and more offbeat films than those up for Cannes' main Palme d'Or prize.
41The second-place Jury Prize went to Croatian director Dalibor Matanic for "Zvizdan" ("The High Sun"), which explores love and ethnic hatred in the Balkans.
42Matanic dedicated the prize to his producer, his wife and his soon-to-be-born daughter.
43"I hope she will live in a better and far more tolerant world," he said.
44The jury bestowed the directing prize on Kiyoshi Kurosawa for "Journey to the Shore," and also gave awards to "Treasure," by Romania's Corneliu Porumboiu, "Nahid" by Iranian director Ida Panahandeh and "Masaan" by India's Neeraj Ghaywan.
45First time director Jonas Carpignano - who's half African-American, half-Italian - evoked the real-life experiences of his roommate and lead actor in the film, Koudous Seihon.
46Seihon witnessed shootings, race riots, violence and faced near-constant discrimination after he took the perilous trip by foot, car, bus and boat to southern Italy as a 20-year-old father seeking to support his family.
47Despite its small 1 million-euro ($1.1 million) budget, Carpignano's film, which was screened at the Critics Week, has already garnered great attention for its highly personal portrayal of Europe's great migrant crisis.
48"We're constantly bombarded and desensitized (about migrants). That's why I wanted to make a film about this, to give people something to latch on to," Carpignano said.
49"It doesn't help to constantly group immigrants as just migrants coming over. There has to be more of a personal angle."
50For much of its running time, Jacques Audiard's "Dheepan," which premiered at Cannes, is a gritty and naturalistic depiction of Tamil refugees trying to build a new life in France.
51"That was the origin of the origin," Audiard said.
52"At the beginning, it was a kind of vigilante movie."
53The French director has long mixed genre-movie conventions and tough real-world subjects into powerful and distinctive films.
54His international breakthrough, "A Prophet," reinvented the prison drama, while follow-up "Rust and Bone" was inspired by B-movie melodramas.
55"For me, genre is a Trojan Horse," Audiard said - a way of smuggling surprises past the audience's defenses.
56This year's Cannes festival features several films by directors working in second languages, including the English-language debuts of Italy's Paolo Sorrentino ("Youth"), Greece's Yorgos Lanthimos ("The Lobster") and Mexico's Michel Franco ("Chronic").
