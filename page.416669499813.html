7 Killed in Violence During Anti-Cartel Operation in Mexico

1At least seven people died as flames and gunfire erupted around the western Mexico state of Jalisco on Friday when a military operation targeting a violent drug cartel was launched at the start of a three-day holiday weekend.
2Suspected cartel members stopped buses and trucks to block key highways in the state capital of Guadalajara and other cities, snarling traffic on a day Mexicans took to the road in droves.
3Gunmen fired on a military helicopter, killing three soldiers and forcing it to make an emergency landing about 150 miles (250 kilometers) southwest of Guadalajara.
4Ten soldiers and two federal police officers were injured and three soldiers remained missing, a defense ministry statement said.
5The statement said the helicopter was participating in the anti-cartel offensive known as Operation Jalisco.
6Jalisco Gov. Aristoteles Sandoval said at an afternoon news conference that the violence around the state was a reaction to the operation, which he said was designed to "get to the bottom of and to be able to arrest all the leaders of this cartel, of this organization."
7Sandoval did not name the cartel, but authorities have been locked in an increasingly bloody battle with the Jalisco New Generation cartel.
8Last month, cartel gunmen killed 15 state police officers in an ambush that was the bloodiest single attack on Mexican authorities in recent memory.
9One week earlier, cartel gunmen attempted to assassinate the state security commissioner and on March 19 they killed five federal police officers.
10Authorities have said those attacks were revenge for state forces killing a cartel leader.
11On Friday, Sandoval said Operation Jalisco, which had been announced by federal authorities earlier in the day, had the goal of arresting all members of the cartel.
12Sandoval said there were a total of seven deaths Friday, but did not specify the circumstances or the victims.
13Later, state spokesman Gonzalo Sanchez said through his Twitter account that the dead included three soldiers, one state police officer, two suspected criminals and one civilian.
14In an interview with the newspaper El Universal, Sanchez said authorities believed the Jalisco New Generation cartel was responsible.
15The governor counted 29 road blockades around the state affecting 19 municipalities.
16He said 11 banks and five gas stations had been attacked and the state remained under a "code red" protocol.
17Fifteen people had been arrested and there were four armed confrontations, he said.
18Alejandro Hope, a former official with Mexico's intelligence service, said the violent response to the government offensive showed the cartel is cohesive and capable of reacting to attacks.
19He said the gang would likely tighten security to fend off the capture of its leader, who is believed to be Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes, nicknamed "El Mencho."
20Hope questioned the decision to go after the cartel's top leadership before first taking out mid-level leaders and weakening the gang's ability to react with coordinated violence.
21However, Jorge Chabat, another Mexican security expert, said he didn't think the cartel's response would have a big impact.
22"I don't believe that it is going to make the federal government leave them alone," he said.
23President Enrique Pena Nieto said Friday through his Twitter account that he lamented the soldiers' deaths in the course of their work in Jalisco.
