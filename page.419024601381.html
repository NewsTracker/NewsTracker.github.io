15Like a lot of communities in north St. Louis County, it had seen significant white flight and was now two-thirds African-American.
16My wife's grandmother lived in Ferguson until she died in 1991, so I spent some time there as a young man.
17But since joining the St. Louis office of The Associated Press in 1993, I had never been to Ferguson as a reporter.
18On Aug. 9, I returned home from a bike ride to learn that a young black man had been fatally shot by a white Ferguson police officer.
19By that humid Saturday evening, hundreds of people were congregating near the scene where Michael Brown Jr. was killed by Darren Wilson.
20The crowd was angry.
21Some witnesses said the 18-year-old had his hands up in surrender when he was shot.
22The next day, as Ferguson police prepared for a news conference to explain what happened, I was among a crowd of reporters who heard distant chanting.
23As I walked toward the noise, I could see in the distance hundreds of people, many holding signs.
24The chant soon became clear: "Hands up! Don't shoot!"
25That would become the rallying cry in the unrest that followed.
26It was also the first evidence that Ferguson would be a far bigger story than we initially imagined.
27Shootings by police are not uncommon, a sad reality of urban life.
28In April last year, about four months before Brown died, Dontre Hamilton, a mentally ill black man, was shot in a Milwaukee park by white police officer Christopher Manney.
29A few days before, John Crawford III, a black man with an air rifle in a suburban Bevercreek, Ohio, Walmart, was killed there by police.
30So what was different in Ferguson?
31Brown and Wilson had their fatal encounter in the middle of a street surrounded by apartment buildings.
32It was almost noon on a Saturday, and many people - residents, construction workers, visitors - were outside.
33Word quickly spread from witnesses who believed the shooting was unjustified, that Brown was trying to surrender.
34What we didn't know at the time was the depth of mistrust between black residents and the predominantly white Ferguson Police Department, a level of suspicion that no doubt fueled what happened next.
35On Sunday evening, thousands of people crowded the same street where Brown was killed for a vigil.
36The anger was evident, but the event was peaceful.
37Suddenly, a young woman came running: "They're rioting on West Florissant."
38I ran the three blocks to the busy four-lane street lined with retail businesses.
39My attention was drawn to a large group of people cheering and yelling obscenities in the direction of a QuikTrip convenience store.
40By the time I got there, it was on fire.
41People were running out, their arms full of stolen goods.
42Never before had the anger been as intense in Ferguson.
43Young men began hurling bricks through store windows, kicking in doors, throwing rocks and Molotov cocktails at police cars.
44The destruction that night led police to adopt a tougher stance.
45By Monday, hundreds of officers in riot gear, some in armored trucks, lined the streets.
46Now police were becoming more aggressive.
47Some aimed their threats and angry words at protesters and journalists.
48AP reporters, photographers and videographers from around the nation arrived, and the words and images we helped capture became part of the national debate about police interaction with black communities, the police response to protests and economic disparity between the races.
49It was often harrowing work.
50Our journalists faced threats from protesters and police.
51Gas masks and bulletproof vests arrived, but many of us on the front lines of the riots felt the sting of tear gas when we failed to deploy the masks quickly enough.
52The unrest lasted for months, worsened by a series of fatal police shootings in St. Louis.
53Most of the protests were nonviolent.
54Meanwhile, local authorities had released virtually no information about when the grand jury considering potential charges for Wilson would render a final decision.
55The announcement that Wilson would not be charged finally arrived on the evening of Nov. 24.
56The night produced striking visuals of buildings engulfed in flames and riot police massed under a "Season's Greetings" banner.
57The next morning, the AP team was back out on the streets of Ferguson as the National Guard rolled in and the community assessed the damage.
58Ferguson became the impetus for a national movement.
59Soon, other fatal police encounters with black suspects drew similar scrutiny.
60After Ferguson, old presumptions are gone and new questions asked.
61The events there intensified how the nation looks at law enforcement, the use of deadly force and the inflamed relations between blacks and American police.
