Thousands too injured to be admitted to Baltimore jail, records show

1FILE - In this Nov. 14, 2013 file photo, a corrections officer works at a security post at Baltimore City Detention Center in Baltimore.
2Maryland Gov. Martin O'Malley planned to unveil a new cellphone security system Friday, Feb. 7, 2014, at the Baltimore City Detention Center.
3The technology automatically blocks calls from all cellphones inside the building, except a small number of phones assigned to employees.
4It also allows any phone to call 911.
5BALTIMORE --Thousands of people have been brought to the Baltimore city jail in recent years with injuries too severe for them to be admitted, newly released records show.
6The records, obtained by The Baltimore Sun through a Maryland Public Information Act request, show that correctional officers at the Baltimore City Detention Center refused to admit nearly 2,600 detainees who were in police custody between June 2012 and April 2015.
7The records do not indicate how the people were injured or whether they suffered their injuries while in custody.
8However, they do suggest that police officers either ignored or did not notice the injuries.
9Suspects are constitutionally guaranteed health care before they are booked into jail.
10Baltimore police are under scrutiny for their treatment of detainees following the death of Freddie Gray last month.
11Gray died of a broken neck that prosecutors said he suffered while riding in a Baltimore police van, and six officers involved in Gray's arrest are facing criminal charges, including one charged with second-degree murder.
12Gray's death sparked rioting and widespread protests in the city and came amid national scrutiny of how police officers treat suspects, particularly black men.
13On Friday, the Justice Department announced that it is conducting a civil-rights investigation of Baltimore police.
14The records obtained by The Sun showed that 123 of the detainees who weren't admitted to jail had visible head injuries, the third-most common ailment cited by jail officials.
15Others had broken bones, facial trauma and high blood pressure.
16Police did not comment to The Sun, and department spokespeople did not immediately return messages left by The Associated Press on Sunday.
17In the wake of Gray's death, Americans' views on race relations in the U.S. have grown significantly more pessimistic, according to a CBS News/New York Times poll released last week.
18Sixty-one percent now say race relations are generally bad, up 23 points from earlier this year.
19It is the first time a majority has held this view since the 1990s.
