25(CNN)Gabriella Zanzanaini and Nicolas Petit have just completed everyone's dream trip -- they ate their way through 20 countries.
26Traveling by land for 306 days, from Brussels to Hong Kong, the adventurous and constantly hungry duo chronicled their delicious encounters on their popular travel blog, The Funnelogy Channel, which features enticing food porn and recipes given to them by the professional chefs/street vendors/proud grandmas they met on their journey.
27It all started in Brussels in April 2014, when Zanzanaini and Petit left their nonprofit sector jobs and never looked back.
28Heading toward Hong Kong, Zanzanaini's maternal hometown, the couple passed through countries such as Greece, Iran, Turkey, Tajikistan, Myanmar and Laos, and ate unbelievable meals of exotic foods that most of us have never even heard of.
29Their manifesto wasn't about marking off a bucket list of famous dishes, but finding the surprising, little known, often humble food that travelers discover only after being invited to the table of a loving home cook.
30We spoke to Zanzanaini and Petit about slow travel, their most memorable meals and how best to see Iran.
31CNN: Sounds like the classic contemporary bildungsroman: quit your hateful desk job, travel world to find yourself.
32What kick-started it?
33Zanzanaini: We didn't hate our jobs!
34We both really cared about our jobs so we gave six months' notice to find suitable replacements for us.
35A lot of people were asking why don't you just take a sabbatical, go for a year and then come back, but we knew we wanted to have the future completely open.
36If you knew you were coming back to the same life at the end of the journey then it's just like a big holiday.
37You're not going out to the open with no clue of where you'll end up.
38Petit: We didn't want an official end date to our trip because then we would be completely free.
39At the beginning of traveling, you are still thinking about what you were doing a few months ago.
40But once you let go of the time frame, everything becomes completely open, with no past, no future -- we can do whatever we want.
41Traveling for a year, it's all about the unexpected.
42Arriving in a strange place, you don't know what you're going to see, you don't know the pleasure you're going to get and then suddenly something opens up -- and there's always something opening up.
43CNN: How did the trip change you?
44Petit: We can never be bored again.
45It has opened a completely new creative way of looking at the world.
46[Zanzanaini] focused on the writing and I focused on the photography so we learned to look at the world through those media.
47We are mostly interested in people, more than landscapes or monuments, so you can never be bored, you sit there looking at the people passing by ... there's so much to talk about.
48It was very liberating to know that there is always something to do and it's not related to money or job or whatever, it's for you, you have a constant interest in the world you live in.
49Zanzanaini: You could dump us in a tiny town, it's not a picturesque village, it's not a fancy town, but we will look at it in detail.
50All these little things your mind finally has time to process.
51CNN: What was the hardest thing about the trip?
52Zanzanaini: At the beginning learning to trust was a really big thing.
53On this trip people were inviting us to dinner, people we don't know.
54In the beginning I felt so guilty.
55I was uncomfortable accepting all this free stuff or kind gestures.
56I felt that I owed them.
57Over time, people were constantly so nice, we became relaxed and trusting.
58One of the saddest things a guy said to me was in Eastern Turkey.
59There was a student who was watching the hotel at night and we started chatting to him and he said, you have to come to my mom's house for dinner, so at 11:30 p.m. we went to his house because his shift was over and when we were walking there he said, "Thank you so much for trusting me, most foreigners wouldn't come with me, they would be scared."
60That's so crazy, we should be thanking you and he was so happy to find people who were willing to accept his offer that showed me people are always so scared, we've built a fear inside ourselves.
61Petit: Maybe we were very lucky, but nothing bad happened to us and it's been 11 months now on the road.
62We were very happy we decided to trust people.
63CNN: So why the foodie theme for your trip?
64Zanzanaini: I am obsessed with food, but the key was the idea of meeting people, even if we don't speak the language, everybody eats, everybody cooks and most people are proud of their food, and it's something that opens so many doors.
65The second you show interest, especially in these regions where the politics and religion are sensitive issues, food was a safe choice.
66It was a good way of getting into more serious stuff through the very innocent breaking of bread.
67Petit: Every time you ask people if we can cook with them, people said yes.
68We never got a no.
69It's not like you come, you cook a recipe and you go.
70We recently went as tourists to the Philippines for a week.
71There was a town with a fish market and as soon as we asked about cooking we suddenly knew all the people in the fish market and vegetable market and everybody comes and checks what you're doing.
72You have this snowball effect with cooking that is completely a door opener.
