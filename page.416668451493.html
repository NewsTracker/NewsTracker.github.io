Wounded Islamic State leader Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi may have to give up control of jihadists

1Future in doubt: The leader of the Islamic State, Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, was reportedly paralysed by a US air strike in Iraq on March 18.
2Cairo: The leader of the Islamic State, Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, has been so badly wounded by a US air strike in Iraq that he may never resume control of the group he masterminded to power.
3According to reports, Mr Baghdadi has been incapacitated since March 18 due to spinal damage, suffered when an air strike hit a three-car convoy in which he was travelling through the al-Baaj district of Nineveh, close to the Syrian border.
4The enigmatic jihadist leader is being treated by two ideologically sympathetic doctors who travel to his hideout from the group's stronghold in the Iraqi city of Mosul.
5His location remains a closely guarded secret.
6Protesters carry defaced posters of Islamic State leader Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, left, and Iraqi Defence Minister Khaled al-Obeidi, right, as they chant slogans against the Islamic State group during a protest in Baghdad.
7Mr Baghdadi's wounds were said to be life-threatening initially, prompting urgent meetings among the leadership in order to name his successor.
8Operational control of IS has reportedly been passed to Abu Alaa al-Afri, a physics professor and longtime senior official, who had been appointed deputy leader when his predecessor was killed by another air strike late last year.
9Also known as Haji Iman, Mr Afri taught in the northwestern Iraqi city of Tal Afar, and has reportedly built solid respect among Islamic State's senior leadership.
10Mr Afri is thought to have acted as the link between Mr Baghdadi, his inner circle, and IS's network of emirs across their self-proclaimed caliphate, spanning large swathes of Iraq and Syria.
11Hisham al-Hashimi, senior adviser on Isis to the Iraqi government described Mr Afri as the "strongest man in [IS] after Baghdadi".
12"The injury of Baghdadi hasn't affected operations yet, but [his replacement] could see the beginning of disputes between IS's foreign fighters and its Iraqis," Mr Hishimi said.
13Mr Baghdadi has been a towering figure in the rise of IS, and its al-Qaeda affiliated predecessor.
14"Their big strategic moves have all been masterminded by Baghdadi, even if others have helped with the specifics," said Aymenn Jawad Al-Tamimi, a Fellow at the Middle East Forum.
15Mr Afri is understood to be more open to the prospect of reconciliation between Isil and Jabhat al-Nusra, a rival al-Qaeda affiliated group in Syria which has led rebel forces to impressive gains in recent months.
16The last known communique bearing Mr Baghdadi's name came last week, calling on jihadists to send reinforcements to the Iraqi provinces of Anbar and Salahedin.
