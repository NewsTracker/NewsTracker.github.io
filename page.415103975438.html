1Born and raised in Kenya, he cut a dashing and tough figure.
2What he was to achieve on the battlefield that day was exceptional.
3Initially, the attack against the Italians went well, and the Rifles managed to secure a bridgehead on the other side of the river.
4Leakey's role was to help bring mortar fire onto the Italian positions, but he soon ran out of ammunition.
5It was at that moment that the Italian tanks mounted a counter-attack, and it looked as if the bridgehead would be over-run.
6Armed only with a handful of grenades and a revolver, Leakey charged forward.
7He ran through heavy rifle and machine-gun fire, clambered onto a tank and wrenched open the hatch.
8A startled tank commander looked up and Leakey shot him dead.
9Without pausing, Leakey reached into the turret and killed the rest of the crew, apart from the driver, who he ordered to drive into some cover.
10Leakey's plan was to use the main gun of the tank, but he could not get it to work.
11He got out, shouting 'I'll get them on foot', and again ran through heavy gunfire until he found another Italian tank he could climb aboard.
12Once more, Leakey pulled open the hatch and shot dead a member of the crew.
13But his luck was not to hold.
14Another Italian tank spotted him and raked him with its machine-gun.
15It is likely he was dead before he hit the ground.
16Despite his death, Leakey's actions broke up the Italian attack.
17Though it was not immediately known by the British, one of the Italians Leakey had shot had been the commander of the tank unit, and he had therefore left the Italian counter-attack without a leader.
18As a result, the bridgehead was kept secure and the Allies won the battle.
19The Rifles took nearly 500 prisoners of war, and close to 100 Italians were killed.
20It had been an emphatic victory, but if it had not been for Nigel Leakey, it might have been a terrible defeat.
21For his actions that day, he was posthumously awarded Britain's highest award for gallantry - the Victoria Cross.
22His citation rightly states that he showed 'superb courage and magnificent fighting spirit'.
23Just over seven decades later, on August 22, 2013, a 25-year-old lance corporal in the Parachute Regiment in Afghanistan's Helmand Province discovers that he and his men are in a very bad spot.
24As part of a task force entrusted with disrupting insurgent safe havens, the paratroopers are working with the U.S. Marine Corps to destroy a Taliban stronghold.
25However, the operation goes wrong as soon as the force is landed by helicopter.
26Much of the team, including two machine-gunners and a mortar section, is pinned down by Taliban fire, and the Marine Corps captain is soon wounded.However, the lance corporal is not part of the group that is trapped, but realises he needs to act fast if he is to save his comrades.
27With 'complete disregard for his own safety', as the official citation notes, the young NCO rushes down a slope that is being sprayed with machine-gun fire.
28He reaches the captain, gives him first aid and - despite being the most junior commander in the area - arranges for his evacuation.
29Then, the lance corporal takes two machine-guns up to the top of the steep slope from where they can be used to return fire to the enemy.
30He is required to make two trips weighed down with heavy kit, and with enemy rounds hitting the frames of the machine-guns he is carrying.
31By what could be seen as a miracle, the lance corporal makes it and then returns fire.
32'This proved to be the turning point,' the official account states.
33'Inspired by (his) actions, and with a heavy weight of fire now at their disposal, the force began to fight back with renewed ferocity.' Thanks to his almost superhuman bravery, 11 insurgents are killed and four are wounded, and the captain is able to be evacuated safely.
34It comes as no surprise that the lance corporal is recommended for the VC.
35'Displaying gritty leadership well above that expected of his rank, (his) actions single-handedly regained the initiative and prevented considerable loss of life, allowing a wounded officer to be evacuated,' his superiors concluded.
36Finally, on Thursday, he was presented the award by General Sir Nick Carter, the head of the British Army, at a ceremony at Lancaster House in London.
37And the young man's name?
38Joshua Mark Leakey, a cousin of the Leakey who won his VC in Ethiopia 72 years before.
39It is impossible to overstate quite how extraordinary it is to have two members of the same family winning the VC.
40The courage of Nigel and Joshua Leakey alone, running through murderous machine-gun fire to save their comrades in distant lands, would be exceptional enough, but these two men are by no means the only members of their family who have shown amazing gallantry.Indeed, in a week dominated by vapid Oscar celebrations and puffed-up pop stars at the Brits, the Lucky Leakeys' story reminds us of values which are truly to be celebrated.
41Over the past century, the family have served this country with the utmost courage.
42In fact, it is no exaggeration to claim that the Leakeys - distant relatives of the prominent Kenyan family whose most famous member is the politician and conservationist Richard Leakey - can lay claim to being Britain's bravest family.
43When Nigel Leakey was killed in May 1941, the news came as a terrible blow to his younger brother, Arundell 'Rea' Leakey.
44At the time of his brother's death, the 25-year-old Rea was serving in North Africa as a captain in the 1st Tank Regiment.
45Rea had many fond memories of growing up with Nigel, whom he idolised.
46The two boys had been sent to a tough boarding school in Africa, where they had often got into trouble, usually for misdemeanours such as pinching apples.
47Despite being beaten with a whip made from rhino hide, the spirit of the brothers remained undimmed.
48'We were little heroes to the rest of the boys, because the weals were red and bleeding,' Rea later recalled.
49Like his brother, Rea was also to prove himself as a hero to the 'boys' in a far more deadly game.
50On January 21, 1941, Rea was commanding a light tank reconnaissance patrol near the airfield of Martuba in Libya.
51Like Nigel, Rea was doing his bit to kick the Italians out of Africa, in what was known as Operation Compass.
52As Rea and his unit advanced, he came under heavy fire from enemy guns and tanks.
53A lesser man would have turned back, but he pressed on, and managed to outwit the Italians and complete the patrol.
54For his actions that day, he was recommended for the Military Cross.
55The commendation stated that 'he has always shown dash and gallantry on patrols'.
56Of course, compared to his brother's VC, Rea's achievement seems modest, but as any serving soldier will tell you, winning an MC is an impressive feat.
57Besides, it wasn't as if that was the only medal that Rea was going to win.
58Just eight months later, in August 1941, Rea could be found in the fortress port of Tobruk, which the Allies were desperately trying to defend from the onslaught of Rommel, commander of Germany's North African campaign.
59Fed up with six months of having to sit tight and endure constant bombardment, Rea volunteered to be attached temporarily as a private to the 2/23 Australian Infantry Battalion.
60On the night of August 8-9, Rea accompanied two Australians to man an observation post three miles south of the Sidi Daud sector of Tobruk.
61It was a risky task, as the Germans knew the location of the post, and the unit was ordered to withdraw if they were attacked by a superior force.
62At 5.30pm on August 9, the Germans did, indeed, attack - with 26 men.
63But like his brother, and his cousin Joshua decades later, Rea was not a man to run away.
64Taking command of the unit, he waited for the Germans to get close, then ordered the two Australians to open fire at the last moment.
65Rea was manning a Bren gun, but to his horror, it jammed.
66Desperate, he ordered the two Australians to throw grenades while he tried to clear the gun.
67The enemy went to ground, but one valorous wounded German decided to mount a solo charge.
68Just in time, Rea managed to shoot the attacker right between the eyes with his revolver.
69'It was the best shot of my life,' he would recall.
70With the Bren gun cleared, he and his small unit were able to wipe out the enemy.
71In total, the three men killed 21 Germans and wounded a further five.
72Not one escaped.
73For such heroism, one might have expected Rea to be awarded a VC, but instead, he was 'merely' awarded another MC 'for his very fine personal courage'.
74In later years, the incident was portrayed in the war comic Victor, from which Rea took a lot of boyish pleasure.
75But there were more medals to be won.
76In October 1944, now an acting lieutenant-colonel at the immensely young age of 28, Rea was commanding the 7th Battalion of the Royal Tank Regiment (RTR) in the Dunkirk sector on Northern France.
77As well as leading his own men, Rea also worked closely with the Czech Armoured Brigade.
78The Czechs admired Rea to such an extent that they, too, awarded him their own Military Cross, which was approved by no less than Field Marshal Montgomery himself.
79The commendation cited Rea's 'personal courage and coolness', as well as his 'keen sense of co-operation' with the Czechs.
80Rea was to win one more medal - the Distinguished Service Order (DSO).
81This was awarded for his leadership of the 5th Battalion of the RTR towards the end of the war in Germany, during which he successfully led his men and tanks against strong opposition from the SS and Luftwaffe.
82'The leadership and disregard for personal safety of this young CO has been as noticeable as has his efficiency, drive and quick reactions,' the commendation read.
83After the war, Rea was to rise to major general, and even wrote a memoir called Leakey's Luck, despite his life being touched by further immense grief.
84One evening in October 1954, his 60-year-old father, Gray Leakey, and his mother and step-sister Diana were attacked by 30 native Mau Mau terrorists at their home 100 miles north of Nairobi.
85Having been accepted into the local Kikuyu tribe as a 'blood brother', Gray did not believe that the Mau Mau would harm him, and he refused to arm himself.
86Displaying typical Leakey bravery, Gray had once come across an armed band of Mau Mau in a forest, and he had simply turned his back on them and walked away.
87But that night in 1954, his luck would run out.
88Though Diana managed to hide in the attic, Mrs Leakey was too frail to follow her.
89The Mau Mau found her and slashed her to death on the lawn.
90Gray's body was found a few days later after a massive manhunt.
91He had been buried alive.
92Rea's son followed him into the military: today, Lieutenant General David Leakey is Black Rod, the Queen's representative in Parliament.
93And there is yet another story of Leakey bravery, which was to take place over the Mediterranean one night in November 1997.
94Returning from a bombing mission over Iraq, 41-year-old RAF wing commander Mark Leakey negotiated his Harrier jump jet onto the deck of the aircraft carrier Invincible.
95After 20 days on the go, he was tired, and made a mistake on his approach.
96Instead of touching down on the carrier, he landed on the water.
97Just before he lost consciousness, it is possible he thought of his wife Rosemary and two young boys.
98Leakey came round, but in his confused and groggy state, pulled not only his life jacket toggle, but also the handle of his ejector seat.
99What he had not realised was that his Harrier was upside down: he was fired straight down into the water.
100But the Leakeys are a lucky bunch.
101In 1982, when Mark Leakey was flying near the Falklands, his engine gave up and he had to eject over the South Atlantic.
102The Royal Navy once more found themselves fishing Leakey out of the sea.
103Incredibly, when he was given a medical check, it was revealed he had a brain tumour.
104Thanks to the crash, it was spotted and operated upon.
105In a sense, the crash saved his life, and meant Leakey could be around to watch his two boys grow up into fine young men.
