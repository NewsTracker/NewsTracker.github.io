News24.com | Turkey not headed toward dictatorship - Erdogan

Iraqi Kurds says Islamic State used chlorine gas against them

1"They say the presidential system would bring dictatorship. Is there dictatorship in the United States, Mexico or in Brazil?"
2Erdogan said in the western province of Canakkale.
3"Change is inevitable. The building of a new Turkey is inevitable. A new constitution is inevitable. A presidential system is, God willing, inevitable," Erdogan said.
4The co-founder of the ruling Islamic-rooted Justice and Development Party (AKP), who was elected president in August after more than a decade as prime minister, wants to cement his role as Turkey's number one with a US-style executive presidency.
5The August vote marked the first time the president - currently a largely ceremonial post - was directly elected.
6The AKP, which currently has 312 seats, is aiming for a significant boost in numbers following 7 June parliamentary elections.
7The opposition has frequently accused Erdogan of having dictatorial tendencies.
8But the president said on Saturday: "If people want [the executive presidency], it will happen."
1The Security Council of the semi-autonomous Kurdish region said in a statement to Reuters that the peshmerga had taken soil and clothing samples after an Islamic State car bombing attempt on Jan. 23.
2It said laboratory analysis showed "the samples contained levels of chlorine that suggested the substance was used in weaponized form."
3The Kurdish allegation could not be independently confirmed.
4Chlorine is a choking agent whose use as a chemical weapon dates back to World War One.
5It is banned under the 1997 Chemical Weapons Convention, which prohibits all use of toxic agents on the battlefield.
6Peter Sawczak, spokesman for the Dutch-based Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons, said: "We have not had a request from Iraq to investigate claims of use of chemical weapons in Iraq, and the OPCW cannot immediately verify the claims."
7Chlorine has been used "systematically" in the civil war in neighbouring Syria, an OPCW fact-finding mission found last year.
8The OPCW would have to get its own samples to confirm the use of chemical weapons in a member state.
9The Kurdish statement said the car bombing attempt happened on a highway between Mosul and the Syrian border.
10A Kurdish security source said that the peshmerga fired a rocket at the car carrying the bomb so there were no casualties, except for the suicide bomber.
11About a dozen peshmerga fighters experienced symptoms of nausea, vomiting, dizziness or weakness, the source said.
12The statement said the analysis was carried out in a European Union-certified laboratory after the soil and samples were sent by the Kurdish Regional Government to a "partner nation" in the U.S.-led coalition that is fighting Islamic State militants in Iraq and Syria.
13The source described the samples as "leftovers from the suicide bomber", but declined to identify the laboratory.
14The White House said in a statement it could not confirm the allegations but found them "deeply disturbing" and was monitoring the situation "very closely."
15A U.S. defense official said the use of chlorine as a weapon was a possible sign of "growing desperation due to the pressure being applied by coalition air power and Iraqi ground forces."
16Iraq's Kurds were the victims of the deadliest chemical attack of modern times when Saddam Hussein's air force bombed the town of Halabja in 1988, gassing at least 5,000 people to death.
17The U.S. Central Command said on Jan. 30 that an Islamic State chemical weapons expert had been killed in a coalition air strike six days earlier near Mosul - the day after the car bombing cited in Saturday's statement.
18The expert, Abu Malik, had been a chemical weapons engineer during the rule of Saddam Hussein and then affiliated himself with al Qaeda in Iraq in 2005, Central Command said at the time.
19When he joined Islamic State, it gave the insurgent force a chemical weapons capability, it added.
20Malcolm Dando, professor of international security at the University of Bradford, and an expert on biological and chemical warfare, said chlorine was an easy gas to obtain.
21"It's extensively used, you only need to google industrial uses of chlorine, it's extensively used industrially," he told Reuters.
22Western diplomats in The Hague, where the OPCW is based, have long feared IS fighters would get their hands on chemical weapons.
23It is not easy to make such weapons and IS tried to recruit experts when it took over Mosul last year, diplomatic sources told Reuters.
24They were not believed to have been successful.
25Kurdish forces, backed by U.S.-led air strikes, have taken a prominent role in fighting the Islamic State jihadists who last year declared a cross-border caliphate after seizing land in eastern Syria and northern Iraq.
26No international organization has documented the use of chemical weapons on Iraqi territory in the war with Islamic State.
27The Kurdish authorities said in their statement they had "long suspected that (IS) fighters have been using chemical agents" and cited video footage from recent battles around the city of Tikrit between the militants and Iraqi troops and allied Shi'ite militias where "plumes of orange smoke" were visible.
28Reuters was also e-mailed video footage and photos of what the Kurdish Security Council identified as images from the Jan. 23 attack.
29Among the photos were several canisters lying on the ground that the council says were found at the site and contained chlorine.
