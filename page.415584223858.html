In Netanyahu call, Obama says US reassessing peace approach

1FILE - In this March 17, 2015 file photo, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu greets supporters at the party's election headquarters in Tel Aviv, Israel.
2Netanyahu's Likud won 30 seats in the 120-seat parliament, and parties apparently willing to back him won another 37 for a possible majority.
3The reasons for that outcome go beyond the party's nationalist ideology and include Israel's sense of geographic vulnerability, its fragmented politics and a master campaigner in Netanyahu himself.
4WASHINGTON (AP) - Amid new tensions between two allies, President Barack Obama on Thursday told Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu that the U.S. is reassessing its approach to Israeli-Palestinian peace in light of Netanyahu's pre-election comments rejecting the establishment of a Palestinian state.
5In the telephone call, Obama extended congratulations to Netanyahu for his election victory two days ago.
6But a White House official said Obama also raised Netanyahu's critical comments about Israeli Arabs ahead of the election, which the White House has denounced as a "cynical" effort to mobilize voters.
7The official spoke on the condition of anonymity to describe the private call, because it touched on various delicate subjects between the two leaders.
8In a separate statement, the White House said Obama stressed the United States' close security cooperation with Israel, but also emphasized the U.S. commitment to the creation of a Palestinian state existing alongside Israel.
9On another divisive topic, the statement said Obama addressed negotiations with Iran over Tehran's nuclear program and said he was focused on a deal that would prevent Iran from acquiring a nuclear weapon.
10Netanyahu has been a vocal critic of Obama's diplomatic outreach to Iran.
11In an interview Thursday with MSNBC, Netanyahu appeared to walk back from his earlier remarks about a rejecting a two-state solution, saying he could support a demilitarized Palestinian state if conditions in the region change.
12But White House spokesman Josh Earnest said Netanyahu's earlier comment "raises questions about his commitment to a two-state solution."
13Earnest specifically mentioned that in the past the U.S. has repeatedly cited the goal of creating a Palestinian state when it has intervened on behalf of Israel in the United Nations.
14Earnest also criticized anti-Arab rhetoric used by Netanyahu's party in the lead-up to the election as a "cynical election day tactic (that) was a pretty transparent effort to marginalize Arab Israeli votes."
15Asked whether the subject came up in the phone call, the White House official said, "In his phone call with Prime Minister Netanyahu, the president made the same points in private that the administration has been making in public."
16Tensions between the White House and Netanyahu escalated as the Israeli elections drew nearer.
17The White House was especially annoyed when Netanyahu accepted an invitation from House Speaker John Boehner to address Congress earlier this month without consulting the administration.
18Earlier Thursday, Boehner mocked the Obama administration's chilly reaction to the Israeli prime minister's election victory.
19Asked by a reporter about the administration lukewarm response to Netanyahu's win, Boehner, R-Ohio, said, "Lukewarm?" and laughed heartily.
20White House chief of staff Denis McDonough will give a speech Monday to a major advocacy group that opposes Netanyahu.
21McDonough's address to the J Street group indicates that the administration will seek to strengthen voices that challenge Netanyahu.
22J Street is an Israel advocacy group that often criticizes the Israeli government - and especially Netanyahu.
23The group called Netanyahu's election victory a "deep disappointment" and accused him of winning by shredding U.S. bipartisanship on Israel and "preying on fear and racism at home."
