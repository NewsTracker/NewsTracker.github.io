2But the encirclement of Debaltseve and the intensification of the battle immediately after President Vladimir Putin signed the peace agreement in Minsk has many people convinced that it is a Russian-led operation.

4“Ok, I haven’t seen them, but I’ve seen their professionalism.
5You see how they work their mortars, it’s one ranging shot then straight away, one two three four five,” he said.
6The seeds of this battle were sown in August, when a two-pronged Ukrainian offensive drove wedges deep into rebel lines in an effort to surround the separatist stronghold of Donetsk.
7A massive Russian intervention halted and then reversed that advance, forcing Petro Poroshenko, Ukraine’s president, to sue for a peace deal signed in Minsk on September 5.
8The resulting ceasefire left Debaltseve at the tip of a Ukrainian salient jutting deep into separatist territory – and cutting the main road between the rebels’ most important strongholds of Donetsk and Luhansk.
9In January, the separatists launched a major offensive to retake the town, provoking panicked efforts to broker and end to the fighting.
10It was one of the most hotly contested points at the Minsk talks on Wednesday night, with Mr Poroshenko reportedly spending a great deal of time arguing with Mr Putin over whether or not the town was surrounded.
11For some, the battle is yet more confirmation of Russian deceit.
12“In Minsk, Putin said there was a pocket.
13There was no pocket.
14So he gave the order to create one,” said a civilian volunteer who delivers supplies to Ukrainian troops.
15But some say the situation has been much more difficult for much longer than Kiev officially wants to admit.
16On Friday, a prominent Ukrainian commander, Semen Semenchenko, criticised official military spokesmen for pretending that the road was still open, saying his men had been forced to withdraw from a key hamlet.
17And the wounded in Artemivsk hospital had come from positions outside the pocket.
18“No one gets out of Debaltseve,” said one soldier who declined to give his name.
19“You can carry on if you want to die,” a Ukrainian soldier at a checkpoint on the road from Artemivsk to Debaltseve warned reporters.
20Vyacheslav Abroskin, the Kiev-loyalist regional police chief, wrote on Facebook: “The rebels are destroying the town of Debaltseve.
21There are non-stop artillery bombardments of residential areas and buildings.
22Natalia Karabuta, head of Debaltseve’s health service, said shortly after fleeing the town that people were stranded by the bombardments.
23“The shelling doesn’t stop, the longest pause is for 30 minutes,” she said.
24“People are hiding from the mortar fire in residential areas in the basements of the hospitals.
25“The windows have been blown out but the building is still whole.
26The frost-covered road south-east of the Artemivsk, usually a bustling federal highway, is deserted.
27Now, only the occasional Ukrainian army vehicle, driving at high speed to escape shelling, disturbs what would in other circumstance be a tranquil winter scene.
28Exactly what is going on down the road is almost impossible to tell.
29With few, if any, Ukrainian troops making it in or out of Debaltseve, soldiers and civilians there are left only with rumours and the near-constant artillery fire to tell them what is still going on.
30For the doctors at the hospital, the proximity of the battle means overload.
31“All the other hospitals have been shelled or closed.
32We’re the first and the last, unfortunately,” said a surgeon in hospital scrubs as he filled in notes on the hospital wing.
33It must shoulder not only the work of all other civilian hospitals, but is also the first port of call for military casualties on this section of the front.
34Doctors declined to give figures for those casualties, but it is clear the hospital is overloaded.
35The beds are for the more serious casualties, while walking wounded take seats in the corridors.
36“Yesterday I took children to the morgue.
37What kind of ceasefire do you really expect?” said Sergei Supron, a volunteer with a civilian group that runs ambulances towards the front line to bring back wounded.
38Mr Supron, a Kiev advertising man in civilian life, was referring to a rocket strike in Artemivsk that killed three people, including a seven-year-old, on Friday.
39The attack, which peppered cluster bombs across gardens and driveways in a residential suburb, was another reminder that on this part of the front, the war is heating up, not cooling down.
40Yesterday, repair crews and police investigators carefully stepped around the bloodstains that marked where one of the victims died on Zagorodnaya Street.
41Every other home on the street of bungalows and vegetable gardens has been damaged.
42A bomblet that landed squarely in the driveway of number 41 Zagorodnaya Street peppered a green metal gate with shrapnel.
43“I was out when it happened,” said Tania Magda, the 58-year-old owner.
44“All we want is peace.
45But how many attempts to have a ceasefire have we had already?
46Alexander Zakharchenko, the head of the self-proclaimed Donetsk People’s Republic, signed a decree ordering a ceasefire on Friday afternoon.
47But he added in a press conference that the ceasefire would not apply to Debaltseve because he considered it to be inside separatist territory.
48And he added that any attempt by the Ukrainians to break out of or to relieve the pocket would be considered “a violation of the Minsk agreement”.
49That effectively presents the Ukrainians trapped in the pocket with an ultimatum: surrender or die.
