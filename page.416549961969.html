The Latest on Nepal Quake: Entire army involved in rescue

1A Nepalese elderly man prays next to a building damaged in Saturday's earthquake, not pictured, in Kathmandu, Nepal, Monday, April 27, 2015.
2A strong magnitude earthquake shook Nepal's capital and the densely populated Kathmandu valley on Saturday devastating the region and leaving tens of thousands shell-shocked and sleeping in streets.
3Jagdish Pokhrel, the clearly exhausted army spokesman, says nearly the entire 100,000-soldier army is involved in rescue operations.
4"90 percent of the army's out there working on search and rescue," he said.
5"We are focusing our efforts on that, on saving lives."
6Fears are growing that thousands of people may remain cut off in isolated, devastated mountain villages.
7Udav Prashad Timalsina, the top official for the Gorkha district where Saturday's quake was centered, says he is in desperate need of help.
8"Things are really bad in the district, especially in remote mountain villages. There are people who are not getting food and shelter. I have had reports of villages where 70 percent of the houses have been destroyed," he said when contacted by telephone.
9"We have been calling for help, but we haven't received enough from the central government."
10He says 223 people had been confirmed dead in the district but he presumed "the number would go up because there are thousands who are injured."
11Australian Foreign Minister Julie Bishop says Australia has dispatched a 9-person crisis response team that is scheduled to arrive in Kathmandu later Monday and "will assist in establishing the safety and welfare of Australians currently unaccounted for" after Saturday's earthquake.
12She says the team will also assist with humanitarian assessments to support Nepal's government and the international relief efforts.
13New Zealand is sending 37 urban search and rescue experts to Kathmandu.
14They are scheduled to leave Monday evening.
15Included are experts in rubble-pile rescues and technical rescues, as well as a structural engineer, a doctor, and paramedics.
16New Zealand is also contributing 1 million New Zealand dollars ($761,000) to the relief effort.
17Officials have made contact with over 200 New Zealanders in Nepal and are seeking contact with others.
18They say they have no reason to believe at this point that any New Zealanders died in the earthquake.
19On Monday morning, some pharmacies, groceries and shops selling basic provisions opened while bakeries began offering fresh bread.
20Long lines of people desperate to secure fuel formed outside gasoline stations.
21Fuel prices remained the same as they were before the quake.
22A sense of eerie calm and uneasiness hangs over the capital, Kathmandu, as the aftershocks keep coming for a third day after the massive quake on Saturday.
23People are still trying to absorb what's happening to them and their city.
24Part of their anxiety appears to be fueled by a near complete absence of any real information.
25Power lines are down and there is almost no Internet connectivity.
26Phone connections are spotty at best.
27Most people are camped outdoors so even where there is limited power back up there's no TV news to watch.
28This has made them anxious to buy newspapers every morning.
29Near Kathmandu's famed Dharahara Tower, reduced to an enormous pile of red brick dust, dozens of people were clambering around the debris clicking smiling selfies and photos of their friends posing.
30"This is earthquake tourism. This is not right," said 21-year-old business student Pawan Thapa who arrived from the suburbs to see how he could help.
31"They are more interested in clicking their selfies than understanding that it is a tragedy."
32Facebook has activated its "Safety Check" feature in response to the earthquake in Nepal.
33The feature, launched in October, allows users to tell friends and family they are safe if they are in the middle of a disaster area.
34Facebook engineers in Japan started development on the feature after the 2011 Japan earthquake and tsunami.
35The Kathmandu earthquake was one of the first natural disasters where it has been used.
36Google, which lost an employee in an avalanche as a result of the earthquake, has activated its own disaster tool called "Person Finder" http://google.org/personfinder/2015-nepal-earthquake/ and is updating satellite imagery to help with relief efforts.
37Google is also donating $1 million toward relief efforts, the company said.
38Japan says it will provide emergency relief goods such as tents and blankets worth 25 million yen ($210,000) to Nepal, through the Japan International Cooperation Agency following the massive earthquake on Saturday.
39In Kathmandu, those who came to help are frightened, too.
40Kathmandu district chief administrator Ek Narayan Aryal says tents and water are being handed at 10 locations in Nepal's capital, but that the many aftershocks were leaving everyone jittery.
41There's a bottleneck in the efforts to get relief to Nepal.
42The good news: Kathmandu's airport is open.
43The bad news: It's been a bumpy, frustration-filled experience for those arriving.
44The second bit of good news: Some aid vehicles have been able to travel overland from Indian to the stricken Nepalese city of Pokhara.
45Ben Pickering, Save the Children's humanitarian adviser in Britain, calls that a positive sign.
46He emphasizes this, though: "The airport opening is a small miracle."
47Nepal's Deputy Inspector General of Police Komal Singh Bam says at least 3,218 people have been confirmed killed in Saturday's earthquake.
48So far 18 people have also been confirmed dead in an avalanche that swept through the Mount Everest base camp in the wake of the earthquake.
49Another 61 people were killed in neighboring India.
50In a western part of Kathmandu, dozens of Nepali soldiers and Indian rescue workers pulled 16 bodies and one survivor from a collapsed green, three-story building that housed a church.
51Officials say a Christian meeting was being held on the second floor when the quake hit on Saturday.
52One Indian national disaster response force, who did not want to be named, said "I really doubt anybody is alive here, but we have to keep trying and look."
