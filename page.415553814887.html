Wash. state bill looks to cut college tuition up to $5,000

UN seeks to endorse military action against Boko Haram

1Last week, the Washington State Senate passed Senate Bill 5954 in a 37-12 vote, a law that would link college tuition rates to average salaries in the state.
2If passed, it would effectively cut tuition rates by up to $5,000 per year at public schools.
3"If you try and make your decision based on what's best for the students, this bill makes a lot of sense," says Sen. Andy Hill (R-Redmond), the Senate's budget writer.
4What doesn't make sense, says Sen. Jeanne Kohl-Welles (D-Seattle), is that no one knows how to pay for it yet.
5The average salary in Washington state is about $53,000.
6The bill links this figure to tuition rates, forbidding universities from charging any higher than a certain annual percentage.
7The larger research schools, University of Washington and Washington State University, may charge no higher than 14% of the average state salary, which caps their annual tuition at around $7,400.
8This cap significantly slashes the current tuition rates of $12,400 at UW and $11,400 at WSU.
9Hayley Hohman, the director of legislative affairs with the Associated Students of WSU, says the bill could make college more available to middle-class students.
10She includes herself, citing that she currently works two jobs while enrolled in school.
11"If tuition keeps increasing, or even stays constant at this point, it's not necessarily accessible for students across the state," Hohman says.
12Regional colleges, like Western Washington University, Central Washington University and Eastern Washington University would be allowed to charge up to 10% of the average salary.
13Community colleges could charge 6%.
14If passed, tuition reductions would start in fall 2015 and be implemented gradually over the following two years.
15"College tuition has more than doubled in the past 10 years," Hill says.
16"You should be able to graduate with little or no debt."
17Hill says this bill is a step in returning to the old model of higher education funding in Washington state, which was once a 50/50 split between state and university funding.
18Colleges have increasingly shouldered the burden over the last decade, a difference that ultimately falls to students and their families via rising tuition.
19The goal is to reduce tuition to the point where a student could realistically work his or her own way through college, Hill says.
20"We're trying to get back that 50/50 split," Hill says.
21However some senators, including Kohl-Welles, have expressed concern over how to make up the $350 million deficit this leaves Washington's universities.
22"I support the intention of the bill to reduce tuition, but unfortunately the bill does not provide a designated revenue stream," Kohl-Welles says.
23"I'm very concerned about what that would mean for funding for our social safety net."
24Kohl-Welles says cutting tuition so dramatically could ultimately hurt the quality of education, especially with the solution to the deficit still up in the air.
25Making college more affordable isn't helpful if the value of a degree suffers due to insufficient funding, she says.
26"What good does it do if students can't get into classes? If you have faculty and staff not wanting to stay?"
27Kohl-Welles says.
28The state budget has an additional $3 billion in the upcoming biennium, Hill says.
29Along with natural economic growth, he attributes this jump largely to the legalization of marijuana, which provides another tax revenue stream for the state's general funds.
30"It's really about prioritizing the budget," Hill says.
31The Ways and Means committee proposed adding amendment that would declare the bill null and void unless a source of funding is found, a proposal which was shot down on the senate floor, Kohl-Welles says.
32She expressed concerns over what that could mean for institutions of higher education, which would see their tuition drop regardless of who's making up the difference.
33Hohman is also concerned about the financial discrepancy, calling the bill an "unfunded mandate."
34However, she supports the legislation, citing the successful tuition freeze in 2013 that forbade Washington's schools from raising their price tags for two years.
35"A lot of our legislators understand that they cannot create a tuition freeze, but not pay for it," Hohman says.
36In the past, progressive reforms in for higher education in other states haven't always proved successful.
37Last year, Oregon chose not to pursue its "Pay it Forward" plan, which would reduce tuition to zero, but require graduates to pay a percentage of their income back to their college for several years.
38Oregon's higher education board's reasoning behind killing the plan came down to money - the board found that even a trial run of the plan on a small chunk of students could cost the state up to $20 million per year for more than 20 years.

4038 in average student debt, with graduates shouldering an average of $24,418 compared to the national average of $28,400.
41Hohman says that while concerns over financial backing aren't unfounded, students should write to their lawmakers rather than write the bill off entirely.
42"If students are looking to get involved, they should contact their legislators," Hohman says.
