Third Tunisia museum suspect 'on the run'

1"Definitely there were three," Essebsi told France media iTele television and Europe 1 radio.
2"Two were killed, but there is one who is now on the run," he said.
3"In any case, he will not get very far."
4Wednesday's attack on the National Bardo Museum in Tunis killed 20 foreign tourists and a policeman, and was the first assault claimed by the Islamic State group in the North African country.
5It came as Tunisia -- the birthplace of the Arab Spring that held its first free elections last year -- struggles with rising Islamist extremism.
6On Saturday, authorities released CCTV footage showing two black-clad gunmen with automatic weapons walking unimpeded though a large lobby in the Bardo, just after noon.
7The grainy footage then shows the gunmen passing an unidentified male.
8They point an automatic weapon at him briefly before letting him leave as they make their way up a staircase.
9After rampaging through the museum for several hours, the two gunmen were killed in an assault by security forces.
10Secretary of State for Security Rafik Chelly said Sunday on the website of French weekly Paris Match the third person in the video was "one of the two Vespa drivers that brought the killers", and that he wanted to leave because of the police response.
11"The drivers and other logistics people from the cell, a total of 15 people, are already behind bars," Chelly added.
12The interior ministry had earlier said more than 10 people were arrested.
13The interior ministry said on Saturday it had issued an arrest warrant for Maher Ben Moudli Kaidi, a Tunisian suspect described as a "dangerous terrorist element".
14According to Chelly, Kaidi coordinated the attack.
15Officials have admitted that guards tasked with protecting the museum and the nearby parliament were having coffee at the time of the assault.
16Essebsi acknowledged that more could have been done to prevent the attack, which raised fears for the lucrative tourism sector which represents eight percent of Tunisia's gross domestic product.
17Museum curator Moncef Ben Moussa told AFP on Sunday the Bardo would reopen on Tuesday and that all of its the artefacts were "intact".
18Essebsi admitted "there were failures" in the country's security mechanism, in an interview with Paris Match.
19"The police and intelligence were not systematic enough to ensure the safety of the museum," he said.
20But he also insisted his security forces were quick to respond after the attack and to prevent further deaths.
21"We were there 10 minutes later," he told iTele and Europe 1.
22Essebsi also vowed that an anti-terrorism law "will be voted rapidly".
23"Libya is one of our problems," he said.
24Officials said the Bardo gunmen had trained in neighbouring Libya, where IS has militant camps and is battling local militia for control of the country's oil wealth.
25Both Tunisia and Libya have seen an upsurge in Islamist extremism since separate uprisings that toppled veteran strongmen in 2011.
26But Tunisia has taken pride in forming a democratic government -- in marked contrast to Libya or Egypt, which has also been shaken by turmoil since its 2011 revolt.
27The IS group has threatened more attacks in Tunisia.
28The dead tourists were four Italians, three Japanese, three French, two Spaniards, a Colombian, an Australian-Colombian, a British woman, a Belgian woman, three Poles and a Russian.
29On Sunday, Essebsi laid a wreath at the museum for the victims, who included a Tunisian policeman.
30The attack in the heart of Tunis was unprecedented.
31But dozens of police and military personnel have been killed in attacks blamed on Islamist militants, who are battling the army in the Mount Chaambi region near the Algerian border.
32Tunisia's newspapers on Sunday demanded that the authorities do more to combat extremism.
33"What's essential now is to really take action," French-language daily Le Quotidien wrote, including more controls at mosques "under the influence of uncontrollable religious fanatics".
34Le Temps called for "urgent draconian measures" and withdrawing Tunisian citizenship from "terrorists who pledge allegiance to the enemy".
35Authorities say as many as 3,000 Tunisians have gone to Iraq, Syria and Libya to join IS and other jihadist groups, raising fears of returning battle-hardened militants plotting attacks.
36Essebsi said there are "sleeper cells" that must be identified.
