6His body fell face up on the sidewalk, said Soren Krebs, 22, an economics student who lives in the adjacent building, and it left a pool of blood that was hosed away Sunday by the fire department.
7"My first feeling was just panic," Krebs recalled, adding that he initially thought the gunfire was a battle between drug dealers.
8In Denmark, he said, "the first thing that comes to mind is not terrorism. This is not a problem we have had to think about much."
9After a January rampage in the Paris area killed 17 people, and police raids in Belgium a week later that authorities said thwarted a major terrorist operation, during the weekend Denmark became the latest European country plunged into what Prime Minister Helle Thorning-Schmidt described Sunday as "a fight for freedom against a dark ideology."
10Police technicians work next to the door to Krudttoenden in Oesterbro, Copenhagen, February 15, 2015.
11While the gunman's name and basic biographical details were unclear Sunday, he appears to have shared some traits with at least two of the militants responsible for the Paris violence, notably a criminal record and an abrupt transition from petty crime to Islamic militancy.
12The Danish news media identified him as Omar Abdel Hamid El-Hussein, but the Copenhagen police did not confirm his name.
13They identified him only as a 22-year-old, born and raised in Denmark, whom they knew for gang-related activity and for several criminal offenses linked to weapons violations and violence.
14A Copenhagen police statement issued in November 2013 asked for help in finding a suspect by the same name who was wanted at the time in connection with a stabbing on a commuter train.
15The police had noted that the suspect "should be considered dangerous."
16Police working at the scene where police shot and killed the alleged shooter in Copenhagen.
17Shooting took place early Sunday morning close to Noerrebro commuter station.
18Police have since charged two people with aiding the man suspected of the shootings.
19Two men who were detained on Sunday were charged the following day with helping through advice and deeds the perpetrator in relation to the shootings at Krudttonden and in Krystalgade, police said in a statement.
20During the weekend, Thorning-Schmidt warned the usually placid nation - whose 5.6 million citizens regularly rank in opinion surveys as among the world's happiest people - that "if a madman is willing to sacrifice his life, then we will never be able to guard ourselves 100 per cent."
21Danish police officers secure the area outside of a synagogue on Sunday.
22Heavily armed police officers were out in force across Copenhagen, the Danish capital, Sunday.
23While authorities said the gunman appeared to be acting alone, police officers raided a number of homes and other places, including an Internet cafe.
24In the Norrebro district, a search of the gunman's apartment uncovered an automatic weapon, the spokesman said.
25The attacker was carrying two guns - including the weapon apparently used to kill the director and Jewish security guard - when he was shot early Sunday.
26Awoken by a burst of gunfire shortly after 5am, Krebs said, he looked out of his ground-floor bedroom to witness a shootout "like in a movie" and then crawled next door to the room of a fellow student, Casper Dam, who had been out late drinking and was asleep.
27The two men took refuge in a bathroom away from the street.
28A street camera captures a man police suspect to be the gunman at a freedom of speech event in Copenhagen.
29Jens Madsen, the chief of Denmark's domestic security agency, said there was no indication the gunman had travelled to Syria or Iraq as a jihadi fighter or had any connection to the two French-born brothers who attacked the French satirical newspaper Charlie Hebdo on January 7 or a third Frenchman who, two days later, seized a Paris kosher supermarket and killed shoppers there.
30But Madsen, speaking to reporters at Copenhagen's Police Headquarters on Sunday, said it was possible that the city's attacks had been "inspired" by the Paris bloodshed.
31While most Danes responded with shock to the weekend shootings, the country's security services have been on alert against Islamic extremism since 2005, when the Danish newspaper Jyllands Posten published 12 cartoons of the Prophet Muhammad and, several months later, a Copenhagen mosque sent a mission to the Middle East to rally hostility against Denmark.
32Danish diplomatic missions were attacked, and Danish businesses boycotted across the Muslim world.
33In an editorial to be published Monday, Jyllands Posten said, "Unfortunately, it is difficult to claim surprise at the attacks in Copenhagen."
34Terrorism, it added, is "not a question of if but when."
35Kurt Westergaard, who drew a cartoon for the newspaper that showed Muhammad with a bomb in a black turban, narrowly escaped an assassination attempt in 2010, fleeing into a safe room at his home in the port city of Aarhus to escape a young Somali armed with an axe and a knife.
36In 2013, Lars Hedegaard, an outspoken critic of Islam and a defender of Lars Vilks, the Swedish cartoonist who appeared to have been targeted at the cafe Saturday, was shot at outside his Copenhagen home by a gunman disguised as a postal worker.
37The weekend violence, however, still represented the worst terrorism to hit Denmark since the 1980s, when left-wing extremists killed a police officer in the capital and still-unidentified extremists planted bombs near a Copenhagen synagogue and the offices of a US airline.
38In its response to the threat since the cartoon crisis, the authorities have combined extensive surveillance of suspected militants and of radical mosques with efforts to "rehabilitate," rather than punish, young Muslims who dabble in extremism but have not yet been implicated in criminal actions.
39While most European governments have sought to arrest or expel residents who have returned home after waging jihad in Syria and Iraq, for example, the city of Aarhus has set up a counselling program to help them reintegrate into society.
40Like the Paris gunmen, the 22-year-old responsible for the weekend's killings in Copenhagen was born in the country he sought to terrorise, into a Muslim immigrant family.
41He had a criminal record, and Danish TV2 television said that he had been released from prison just weeks earlier.
42His first attack took place Saturday afternoon when he sprayed bullets into the cafe where Vilks, who had caricatured the Prophet Muhammad, was speaking.
43That attack killed one man, identified by the Danish media as Finn Norgaard, 55, a director.
44Three police officers were wounded.
45Vilks, who was attending a meeting on freedom of speech, was not hurt.
46The gunman then fled by car, and the vehicle was later found abandoned.
47Video footage from surveillance cameras showed the suspect talking into a cell phone, apparently to order a taxi.
48He then took a cab to Mjolnerparken, an area of Norrebro, where surveillance cameras caught him entering a housing compound and leaving 20 minutes later.
49He then reappeared, according to the police, shortly before 1am Sunday at a synagogue in the centre of the city, opening fire on the police and security guards, one of whom was killed.
50Dan Rosenberg Asmussen, a leader of Denmark's Jewish community, said that the victim at the synagogue was a young Jewish man who was guarding a building adjacent to the synagogue.
51He said that about 80 people were inside the synagogue at the time celebrating a bat mitzvah and that the police had been asked to provide protection after the cafe shooting.
52Denmark's chief rabbi, Jair Melchior, identified the victim as Dan Uzan, 37, a long-time security guard.
53Soren Esperson, deputy chairman of the Danish People's Party, a right-wing populist party, said the attack "looks very much like a copycat action."
54"It has the same targets as in Paris: a cartoonist, Jews and the police," he said.
55A loud critic of immigration, Esperson's party has surged in recent years.
56Esperson derided pleas from leading mainstream politicians, including the prime minister, that Islam not be blamed for the violence.
57"Of course this has something to do with Islam just as the Spanish Inquisition, the Crusades and witch-burning had something to do with Christianity," he said.
58Christianity, he added, had "dealt with its fanatics" and Islam "must now do the same."
59Muslim organisations in Denmark condemned the attacks.
60The Islamic Religious Community, an umbrella organisation, denounced what it called a "wrong action" and also called on the Danish authorities to "show their solidarity with all, including Muslims, who will undoubtedly be the next victims in daily life."
61Meanwhile, the authorities and residents in the neighbourhood where the gunman lived are scrambling to learn how a petty criminal seemingly turned into a violent zealot.
62Mohammud Awil, who has lived in Mjolnerparken since emigrating from Somalia 26 years ago, blamed extremist self-declared preachers who "pick on young people who drink or use drugs because they are very weak."
63Awil, a bus driver, said he knew several families whose Danish-born children had gone to fight in Somalia or the Middle East.
64Thorning-Schmidt sought to calm tensions after the attacks, saying, "This is not a war between Islam and the West."
