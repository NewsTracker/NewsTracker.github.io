Nepal earthquake: Video emerges of moment avalanche hit Everest base camp

1A huge cloud of snow cascades down the mountain.
2Terrifying footage has emerged reportedly of the moment an earthquake-triggered avalanche hit Everest base camp, sending panicked climbers running for their lives.
3German climber Jost Kobusch, 22, posted the 2½-minute video on his YouTube channel a day after the earthquake, along with the description: "The ground was shaking from the earthquake and as soon as we saw people running we were running ourselves to save our lives."
4The avalanche moves towards the campsite.
5The video begins calmly enough by panning across the sprawling "city" of tents, from where mountaineers set off for the world's highest peak.
6A number of people can be heard laughing in the background, along with what sounds to be someone talking with an Australian accent.
7But seconds later their voices fill with urgency as the climbers realise a huge cloud of snow is cascading down from the mountain onto the campsite.
8The panicked climber taking the footage takes refuge beside a tent under a jacket, all the while keeping his camera rolling.
9He and another climber can be heard panting heavily and swearing in shock.
10A person attempts to get to their feet after the avalanche swept through.
11When the immediate danger appears to have passed, they start to head towards the kitchen tent to take shelter, only to realise the tent is no longer there.
12Is it unclear whether Mr Kobusch is the person taking the footage.
13In 2014, Mr Kobusch became the youngest person ever to free solo climb one of the Himalayas most iconic peaks, Ama Dablam (6812 metres).
14Photos posted on his social media accounts show him at Everest base camp in the days leading up to the avalanche.
15At least one Australian is reportedly among the more than 2500 people who were killed when the 7.9 magnitude quake rocked Nepal on Saturday.
16It is the worst such disaster to hit Nepal since 1934, when 8500 died.
17Thousands more were injured.
18A number of climbers were flown from Everest base camp to the relative safety of Kathmandu, although hospitals there were overflowing and hundreds of patients had to wait outside to be treated.
