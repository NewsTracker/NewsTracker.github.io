Greece proposes tax hikes, spending cuts, pension reforms

1The Greek government is pledging to stick to primary surplus targets " meaning that the government is earning more in taxes than it is spending " of 1 percent this year, 2 percent in 2016, 3 and 3.5 percent in 2017 and 2018 respectively.
2The proposals include a slew of tax hikes including a 23 percent value added tax on restaurants and catering, a reduced 13 percent tax on basic foodstuffs, energy hotels and water and a so-called "super reduced" rate of 6 percent on such things as pharmaceuticals, books and theater " perhaps appropriate for a country that pioneered drama. The new tax levels will kick into gear this October.
3Moreover, special tax breaks for the country's islands " popular tourist magnets " will be scrapped.
4Only the most remote islands will get to keep the coveted tax breaks.
5Military spending will be slashed by 100 million euros this year and double that in 2016.
6Corporate tax will increase from 26 to 28 percent and farmers will lose preferential tax treatment and fuel subsidies.
7The government will enact a clamp-down on tax dodgers.
8The country's huge shipping industry will also see a tonnage tax hike and the industry's preferential tax treatments will be phased out.
9A luxury tax will be extended to cover recreational vessels over five meters (16.4 feet) and the rate will jump from 10 to 13 percent.
10The government is looking at reforms that would bring permanent savings of - percent of gross domestic product in 2015 and 1 percent of GDP in 2016 and beyond.
11Measures aimed at achieving those numbers include discouraging early retirement and standardizing the retirement age to 67 by 2022 " except for those performing "arduous jobs" and mothers raising kids with a disability.
12Social pensions will be better targeted, while supplementary pension funds will be financed by employees' own contributions.
13Perks such as a solidarity fund will be gradually phased out and health contributions for pensioners will jump from 4 to 6 percent on average.
14More reforms will kick in to make the pension system more sustainable, including an overhaul of pension contributions for all self-employed.
15Authorities will also vet reforms to make sure that they're fair.
16Authorities will shape up public sector wages to ensure that they're on a downward trajectory by 2019 and that they fit "the skill, performance and responsibility" of staff.
17Perks such as paid leave and travel allowances will be streamlined to conform with EU norms.
18A plan to make it easier to reassign public sector workers to different posts wherever they're needed will also be introduced.
19A new strategic plan to fight corruption will be drawn up by the end of this month while new laws will make political party finances more transparent and protect financial crime investigations from political interference.
20The government will set up an autonomous tax revenue agency and enact reforms streamline tax collection, track down tax cheats and combat fuel smuggling.
