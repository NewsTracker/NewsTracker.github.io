Mexico struggles to clean seaweed surge from Cancun beaches

1Authorities on Mexico's Caribbean coast said Monday they are redoubling efforts to remove tons of sargassum seaweed that has been washing ashore in recent weeks.
2The state government of Quintana Roo, where the resort of Cancun is located, said there have been no reports of tourists cancelling visits because of the problem.
3Gov. Roberto Borge said the seaweed removal efforts will focus on much of the coast, from Holbox in the north down past Tulum to the south.
4Photos issued by the Cancun city government show piles of brown seaweed on some normally pristine white beaches.
5By last week, the city said it had raked or shoveled up 500 cubic meters of sargassum.
6Borge said the cause of the invasion is still unknown, though it could be due to high levels of nutrients in ocean water or changes in ocean temperatures, currents or wind patterns.
7Authorities have to be careful, because there are two groups of visitors they don't want to disturb with overly aggressive removal efforts: nesting sea turtles that return to the Caribbean beaches to lay their eggs, and tourists.
8Borge said the effort would take care not to cause erosion on the beaches, which has been a problem in Cancun in the past.
9The government announced the formation of a task force of naval and environmental authorities to study the problem.
10Authorities are also researching ways to use or dispose of the seaweed mounds.
11Sargassum is an algae that grows in the Sargasso Sea, a large body of warm water in the mid-Atlantic.
12Officials in the Caribbean island of Barbados have also struggled with seaweed washing ashore, and large piles of sargassum washed up on the shore in Galveston, Texas, in 2014 after drifting into the Gulf of Mexico.
