Modern tragedy: Quake-ravaged Nepal tower a site for selfies

1KATHMANDU, Nepal (AP) - Social media is a chronicle of life, and sometimes death.
2So it should be no surprise that a site of great human and cultural loss in Nepal's devastating earthquake is now barraged with the clicking of smartphones.
3Near Kathmandu's famed Dharahara Tower, a historic nine-story structure reduced to an enormous pile of red brick dust, dozens of people clambered around the debris clicking selfies and photos of their friends posing in front of the wreckage.
4The tower built by Nepal's royal rulers in the 1800s was one of the country's most treasured monuments, and was photographed far more than other buildings destroyed by Saturday's quake.
5It is unclear how many people were killed in the tower, but it was believed to have been filled with tourists.
6Pawan Thapa, a 21-year-old business student who arrived from the suburbs to try to help with the recovery effort, was dismayed by the scene.
7Some of those taking pictures were smiling for the camera.
8"This is earthquake tourism. This is not right," he said.
9"They are more interested in clicking their selfies than understanding that it is a tragedy."
10Most of those taking pictures, however, did not appear to be tourists but locals capturing the devastation of their community, and the loss of a landmark that had helped define it.
