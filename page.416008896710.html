Law graduate named as Kenya massacre killer as country mourns

1Hundreds packed Nairobi's Anglican cathedral where Archbishop Eliud Wabukala said Easter services were overshadowed by "great and terrible evil" as police patrolled outside.
2"These terrorists want to cause divisions in our society, but we shall tell them, 'You will never prevail,'" the archbishop said.
3Flags were at half mast and although President Uhuru Kenyatta has vowed to retaliate "in the severest way possible", there have also been calls for national unity.
4Kenyatta said people's "justified anger" should not lead to "the victimisation of anyone" -- a clear reference to Kenya's large Muslim and Somali minorities.
5Authorities meanwhile announced that they had identified one of the four dead Shebab gunmen as an ethnic Somali Kenyan national who was a A-grade pupil and law graduate -- highlighting Al-Qaeda-linked Shebab's ability to recruit within Kenya.
6Interior ministry spokesman Mwenda Njoka said it was "critical that parents whose children go missing or show tendencies of having been exposed to violent extremism report to authorities".
7The militants attacked the university in the northeastern town of Garissa at dawn, lining up non-Muslim students for execution in what Kenyatta described as a "barbaric medieval slaughter".
8The massacre, Kenya's deadliest attack since the 1998 bombing of the US embassy in Nairobi, claimed the lives of 142 students, three police officers and three soldiers.
9Top Muslim leader Hassan Ole Naado also offered his condolences.
10"Kenya is at war, and we must all stand together," said Naado, the deputy head of the Supreme Council of Kenya Muslims, saying the organisation was helping to raise money for the funerals of those killed and the medical costs of the scores of wounded.
11"We deeply feel the pain of the loss of young lives," he added in a statement, warning that the Shebab was aiming to "create religious conflict".
12Political and religious leaders have condemned the attacks.
13Pope Francis called the killings "senseless brutality", while the Cairo-based top Sunni Muslim body Al-Azhar has condemned the "terrorist act committed by Somalia's Shebab".
14On Saturday, Shebab warned of a "long, gruesome war" unless Kenya withdrew its troops from Somalia, and threatened "another bloodbath".
15Shebab fighters also carried out the Westgate shopping mall attack in Nairobi in September 2013, a four-day siege which left at least 67 dead.
16Hours after the Shebab's warning, police in Garissa paraded four corpses of the gunmen piled on top of each other face down in the back of a pick-up truck.
17Five men have also been arrested in connection with the attack, including three "coordinators" captured as they fled towards Somalia, and two others in the university.
18The two arrested on campus included a security guard and a Tanzanian found "hiding in the ceiling" and holding grenades, the interior ministry said.
19A $215,000 (200,000 euro) bounty has also been offered for alleged Shebab commander Mohamed Mohamud, a former Kenyan teacher said to be the mastermind behind the attack.
20Forensic investigators aided by foreign experts continued to scour the site where one student survivor emerging unharmed from a wardrobe Saturday where she had hidden for over two days.
21The remaining 600 traumatised student survivors from the now-closed college have since left Garissa, boarding buses for their home towns.
22Over 200 family members of those killed continue their agonising wait for the remains of their loved ones at the main mortuary in Nairobi.
23One of them was 50-year-old Abraham Koech, who last heard from his daughter when she called him on Thursdy saying, "Terrorists have come and I'm hiding under the bed."
24Koech said identification of corpses was difficult because the "bullets have deformed the heads" of the victims.
25There has been growing criticism in the media that critical intelligence warnings were missed, and that special forces units took seven hours to reach the university, some 365 kilometres (225 miles) from the capital.
26Foreign Minister Amina Mohamed defended the response, telling AFP that "fighting terrorism... is like being a goalkeeper. You have 100 saves, and nobody remembers them. They remember that one that went past you."
