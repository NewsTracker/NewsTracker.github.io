Small town mourns 18 young lives lost in Germanwings crash

1Haltern am See: The deaths of 16 teenage students and two young teachers in the Germanwings plane crash in the French Alps left the lakeside town of Haltern am See in a state of shock, with the German nation sharing in their mourning and grief.
2A tranquil and tidy town of 37,000 thatseemed to be a haven from the world's dangers, Haltern am See came to a halt after news that 14 girls, two boys and two teachers on a Spanish language exchange program were not coming home.
3Ulrich Wessel, principal of the Joseph-Koenig-Gymnasium high school, the day after 16 students and two teachers from the school were among passengers who were killed on Germanwings flight 4U9525.
4"On Tuesday last week we sent off 16 happy, young people with two happy, young teachers on a journey," said Ulrich Wessel, headmaster of the Joseph-Koenig-Gymnasium high school.
5"It was meant to be a journey full of joy, a school exchange that we've been doing for six years. It ended in tragedy," added Mr Wessel.
6"Our school will never be the same again".
7French investigators searched for clues as to why the German Airbus flying from Barcelona to Duesseldorf ploughed into an Alpine mountainside, killing all 150 on board.
8In front of the Joseph-Koenig-Gymnasium high school: 16 students and two teachers from the school were lost in the Germanwings crash.
9The 16 German students, all about 15 years old, had started learning Spanish at the start of the school year and were picked from 40 applicants to attend the popular language exchange programme.
10A group of Spanish students spent a week in Haltern in December.
11"Someone asked me how many we have at our school. Without thinking, I answered 1,283. There are actually 16 fewer now," said Mr Wessel, who was close to tears.
12"It's all so horrible that I can hardly find words".
13In all, 72 Germans were killed in the crash, the first major air passenger disaster on French soil since the 2000 Concorde accident just outside Paris.
14Spanish officials said 49 Spaniards were among the victims.
15Candles outside the Josef-Koenig-Gymnasium high school in Haltern am See.
16French President Francois Hollande was joined by Germany's Angela Merkel and Spain's Mariano Rajoy to pay their respects to the victims and to meet search teams at their base in the nearby village of Seyne-les-Alpes later on Wednesday.
17"It feels good that in a difficult hour like this that we're standing so closely together", Ms Merkel said.
18"Dear Francois, I'd like to say a heartfelt 'thank you' in the name of millions of Germans who appreciate this German-Franco friendship".
19There was a pall hanging over the closely knit town north of former mining centres of Dortmund and Gelsenkirchen.
20The despair was amplified by heavy low-lying clouds on a chilly spring day.
21Students at mass in Llinars del Valles, the town where German exchange students lost in the Germanwings crash studied Spanish.
22Crowds of students, some sobbing and many dressed in black, gathered in front of their high school to mourn.
23Some placed flowers on the steps next to the hundreds of candles in red glass left by mourners at the three-storey cement building.
24"It's a tragedy, a tragedy all over Haltern", said student Lisa Boening.
25"Haltern is so small. Friends came to me yesterday in tears and I was trying to just be there for them. It's hard."
26Local residents huddled among themselves, talking quietly and trying to avoid the journalists from across Germany and overseas who had descended on their once little-known town.
27A memorial in front of the Joseph-Koenig-Gymnasium secondary school in Haltern am See.
28"I haven't been able to sleep, not at all, because it really gets to me," said Haltern resident Macky Nowak.
29"I just cried".
30Former Germany soccer player Christoph Metzelder, who grew up here before playing for Borussia Dortmund and Real Madrid, said his home town would never be the same.
31"It's a national tragedy," he told Die Welt newspaper.
32"The students and the teachers from Haltern am See have become the symbol of this disaster. Haltern am See has lost its innocence. Before it was a fantastic little city. Now there's only sorrow."
33North Rhine-Westphalia's Education Minister Sylvia Loehrmann added: "This is a pain that no one, not even the greatest power on earth, can stop. We can only try to accompany."
34Schools across the state will hold a moment of silence for the victims on Thursday and flags across Germany were lowered to half mast.
35The Germany soccer team said they will wear black arm bands in their match against Australia to mourn the victims.
36The headmaster said the two teachers were also young - one got married last year in October and the other was engaged.
37"From one minute to the next, their life's plans were gone - they'll leave a big hole at our school," Wessel said.
38"It's a deep wound to lose 16 children and two teachers that will take long to heal and will leave deep scars."
39Across Germany, which takes pride in a safe and efficient transport system, the sorrow was exacerbated by the loss of so many of Haltern's bright youngsters bound for university.
40Tiny Schroers, a 65-year-old flying home to Duesseldorf after celebrating her grandson's birthday in Berlin, said it felt strange to travel by plane: "I can't help thinking about all those young people who died."
41The sorrow could also be felt at Duesseldorf airport, where people lit candles and placed flowers against a metal support and pinned up messages in German, English and Spanish.
42"My thoughts are with all the victims and relatives of this terrible air disaster," reads one message on black paper pinned to a pillar.
43"Keep strong, we're crying with you," read another.
