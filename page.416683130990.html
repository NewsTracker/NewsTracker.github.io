Damaged by deadly quake, fate of Nepal heritage unsure

1KATHMANDU, Nepal (AP) - The blue-rimmed eyes of Kathmandu's gold-spired Swayambhunath stupa stare silently from a hilltop overlooking this city in the Himalayan foothills.
2But since Nepal was shattered by a mammoth earthquake Saturday, those eyes have gazed upon a nation in mourning - and on a microcosm of destruction and despair inside the ancient temple itself.
3Here, monkeys scurry across the demolished ruins of a pair of precious bullet-shaped edifices built by King Pratap Malla in the 1600s.
4Saffron-robed monks haul golden relics and carpets out of a ruined monastery.
5The temple now has its own population of displaced - priests and vendors huddle under tents, sleeping in darkness waiting for authorities try to repair downed power lines.
6Swayambhunath, which dates back to the 5th century, is one of at least 68 cultural heritage sites in Nepal that were damaged by the tremor, according to Nipuna Shrestha of the U.N. Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization, citing preliminary figures from the Department of Archaeology.
7That's nearly 80 percent of historic landmarks in seven monument zones that have been declared as World Heritage Sites in the Kathmandu Valley alone.
8Few would compare the loss of Nepal's historic treasures to the massive human misery wrought by the magnitude-7.8 quake, which has claimed more than 6,600 lives, damaged more than have a million homes, and displaced nearly 3 million people.
9And yet, "it's hard to describe how painful this is," Shrestha said.
10"These are not just monuments, they are part of our daily life. It feels like losing part of your family."
11Shrestha said at least 18 other monuments are known to have been damaged elsewhere, but information has been incomplete because phone lines have been disrupted and roads cut off by avalanches.
12The sites most heavily affected were made of brick and wood.
13Terrifying footage posted on YouTube of the moment the quake hit one temple complex in Bhaktapur, just east of the capital, shows chunks falling from the top of a crumbling temple as it is enshrouded in a cloud of brown dust.
14Tourists can be heard screaming as some struggle to stand and others run from the collapsing buildings.
15Speaking earlier this week, Prime Minister Sushil Koirala said the government's first priority was relief and rescue for all those affected.
16But he also vowed to rebuild "all structures of historical, religious and archaeological significance."
17In an impoverished country that is already struggling to help the living, though, it is unclear when that will happen, or how.
18Among the landmarks destroyed in the capital was the iconic, nine-story Dharahara Tower, which was topped by a statue of Shiva - the god of destruction in Hinduism, the predominant religion among Nepal's 27.8 million people.
19The second biggest is Buddhism.
20Police say at least 60 bodies were recovered, most of whom were inside the tower's 213-step spiral staircase at the time.
21Built in 1832, Dharahara was partially destroyed during a 1934 quake, and like many sites that were toppled across the country, eventually rebuilt.
22The tower once offered panoramic views of the city.
23All that remains now is its base, the rest having smashed into the ground surrounding it.
24Amrit Sharma, an Indian national who grew up in Kathmandu and has spent most of his life here, said the tower was like "our Washington Monument, our Empire State building."
25"The damage to these sites is not just a huge loss for Nepal, it's a huge loss for humanity," Sharma said.
26"People come from all over the world to see these. But they're not just tourist attractions to us. If we lose them, we lose a sense of our past."
27Among the worst hit sites was Kathmandu's historic center, Basantapur Durbar Square.
28Built between the 12th and 18th centuries, it is a place where kings have been coronated and religious festivals are regularly held.
29The Archaeology Department website calls the complex "an open museum of Nepalese culture, art and architecture."
30Steps that once led to a pair of pagoda-style Hindu temples with multi-tiered roofs now lead to nowhere, resembling giant anthills.
31One of them, Kasthamandap, was built five centuries ago and is believed to have been constructed from the timber of a single tree.
32Elsewhere, a 17th-century statue of Garuda, a bird-like creature in Hinduism revered as the mount of god Vishnu, lies at the bottom of a tall stone pillar it once adorned.
33Other temples have been leveled completely.
34Although soldiers and police guard the sites, hundreds of residents freely clambered over the top of some of the ruins, most volunteering to help in a preliminary effort to clean up the square.
35Laxmi Rimal, 18, had slept on a wooden pallet under a blue tent in the square since the earthquake.
36"Our house was completely destroyed, it looks like that," she said, gesturing toward the crumbling white plaster edifice of Gaddi Durbar, which was built in 1908.
37At the Swayambhunath stupa, a Buddhist site also venerated by Hindus, and known to tourists as "the monkey temple" for its resident population of primates, police wave visitors away.
38The 365 stone steps leading up to it, each representing a day of the year, are blocked off with debris.
39Inside, shopkeepers and monks were shaking dirt from books, Buddha statues and relics they had dug out of the rubble of a monastery which had partially collapsed onto a shop.
40Supendra Buddhacharya, who sold tourist curios there, lamented what he predicted would a major drop in tourism nationwide if the sites are not rebuilt swiftly.
41"If we don't have heritage here, what will people come to Kathmandu to see?" he asked.
42Legend has it that a sage sowed a lotus seed in a beautiful lake that existed where the stupa now stands.
43The seed blossomed with thousand petals, and from that flower a dark-blue flame emanated, which came to be known as Swayambhu joti, or "self-originated flame," which gave the site its name.
44On Wednesday, Lama Zoba, a Buddhist monk, stood before the temple's white dome.
45The eyes painted on the gold spire above it symbolize the omniscient, all-seeing nature of Buddha.
46They peer across the valley onto stunning view of the city that belies the fact that bodies are still being dug out of the rubble below.
47"You can't compare this loss to the loss of human life. But you can't bring back the dead, they are gone," Zoba said.
48The temple, though, "will be rebuilt. And we will use it to pray for their next lives."
