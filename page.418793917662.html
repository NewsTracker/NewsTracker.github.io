Barack Obama, in Kenya, says discriminating against gays erodes freedom

1U.S. President Barack Obama lays a wreath at Memorial Park on the former site of the U.S. Embassy in Nairobi, where al-Qaeda militants bombed the compound in 1998, killing more than 200 people.
2U.S. President Barack Obama, commenting on gay rights in Kenya, said on Saturday his message across Africa was that the state should not discriminate against people based on sexual orientation.
3Obama told a joint news conference with Kenyan counterpart Uhuru Kenyatta that treating people differently eroded freedom and then "bad things happen."
4Kenyatta said Kenya and America shared many values but not on all issues, saying gay rights was not an issue for his people.
5Kenya, like many African states, outlaws homosexuality.
6Kenyan Deputy President William Ruto said in May that there was "no room" for gays in Kenya.
7Obama has hailed a July U.S. Supreme Court decision to allow same-sex marriage.
8Earlier in the day, Obama heralded Africa as a continent "on the move," as he opened a U.S.-sponsored business summit in the East African nation, where he has family ties.
9"Africa is one of the fastest growing regions of the world," Obama said.
10"People are being lifted out of poverty."
11Barack Obama, speaking at the Global Entrepreneurship Summit at the United Nations Compound in Nairobi, said 'Africa is on the move.
12Obama's visit to Kenya - the first by a sitting U.S. president - has been highly anticipated in a nation that views him as a local son.
13The president's late father was born in Kenya and many family members still live here, including his elderly step-grandmother.
14"This is personal for me," Obama said.
15"There's a reason why my name is Barack Hussein Obama."
16Much of the president's visit is focused on boosting business and security ties with Kenya, a growing economy grappling with the threat of terrorism, most notably from the Somalia-based al-Shabaab network.
17Nearly two dozen U.S. lawmakers and 200 American investors have joined Obama on his trip, which also includes a stop in Ethiopia.
18At the Global Entrepreneurship Summit on Saturday, Obama announced more than $1 billion in new commitments from the U.S. government, as well as American banks, foundations and philanthropists.
19Half of the money will go to support women and young people, who Obama says face bigger obstacles when trying to start businesses.
20"If half of your team is not playing, you've got a problem," Obama said, referring to women excluded from the formal economy.
21Barack Obama, left, meets Kenya's President Uhuru Kenyatta as he arrives for a visit at the State House in Kenya's capital, Nairobi.
22Obama hosted the inaugural entrepreneurship summit at the White House in 2010.
23This year's conference is the first to be held in sub-Saharan Africa.
24Kenyatta, who co-hosted the summit with Obama, lamented that the continent's security and other challenges, including the 2013 attack on an upscale Nairobi mall, had created a negative reputation.
25He said he hoped Obama's visit would help change the narrative about Kenya and Africa.
26"Africa is the world's newest and most promising frontier of limitless opportunity," Kenyatta said.
27"Gone are the days when the only lens to view our continent was one of despair and indignity."
28As the two leaders sat down for a formal meeting at Kenya's State House, Obama emphasized the need for timetables and concrete plans to make progress for the region.
29He said the U.S. wants to partner with Africa "not out of charity, but because we see opportunity."
30"What happens in Africa is going to affect the world," Obama said.
31While in Nairobi, Obama toured an innovation fair highlighting the work of vendors working with his Power Africa initiative, which aims to double sub-Saharan access to electricity.
32Obama also placed a wreath at the site of the 1998 bombing of the U.S. Embassy in Nairobi.
33The president bowed his head for a moment, then studied the names of the victims etched into a brick wall.
34Extremists simultaneously attacked the U.S. embassies in Nairobi and Dar es Salaam, Tanzania, on Aug. 7, 1998.
35The Kenya attack killed more than 200 Kenyans and 12 Americans at the embassy.
36Thousands were injured.
37Obama arrived in Kenya late Friday and spent the night reuniting with his father's family.
38Security was tight in the Kenyan capital, with some of the city's normally bustling streets closed to traffic and pedestrians during his visit.
39There was palpable excitement in Nairobi for Obama's long-awaited visit.
40U.S. and Kenyan flags lined the main road from the airport and billboards bearing Obama's picture dotted the city.
41Local newspapers marveled at the massive U.S. Secret Service contingent that accompanies Obama whenever he travels overseas.
