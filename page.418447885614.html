More Britons returning from Tunisia

1More UK holidaymakers are flying home from Tunisia after the government advised them to leave the country amid fears of another terror attack.
2Thomas Cook and Monarch Airlines expect to fly more than 2,000 people home over the weekend.
3Tunisia declared a state of emergency after 38 people - including 30 Britons - were killed by an Islamist gunman on a beach in Sousse on 26 June.
4The new travel advice drew criticism from British tourists and Tunisia.
5Tunisian Prime Minister Habib Essid said he "regretted" the UK government's decision but said "we understand".
6He said the country had done everything it could to protect foreigners, with 100,000 security personnel being deployed to protect the country and prevent new attacks, including 3,000 dedicated to protecting beaches, hotels and archaeological sites.
7Conservative MP Crispin Blunt, chairman of the Foreign Affairs Select Committee, which scrutinises the work of the Foreign Office, said the decision "raised questions".
8What next for Tunisia holidaymakers?
9Extra flights are being laid on across the weekend to evacuate thousands of British package holidaymakers and hundreds of independent travellers.
10The Foreign Office estimates there are now between 1,000 and 2,000 Britons left in Tunisia.
11On Friday the figure stood at 3,000-3,500.
12Thomas Cook and Monarch have cancelled all further bookings to Tunisia for the rest of the summer season.
13The BBC's Jim Muir, at Enfidha Airport, said there was anger among British tourists who felt the decision had been taken suddenly and was not justified.
14"They're angry at the British authorities for suddenly springing this surprise two weeks after the attack on Sousse - and they're angry that their tour operators are taking this advisory as an instruction and practically ordering them out," he said.
15Sara Southall, from Staffordshire, said the Foreign Office should have changed its travel advice sooner.
16"If they were still investigating, I think they should have stopped everyone from travelling until they knew it was safe rather than sending us out here and then sending us back," she said.
17"But obviously, if the decision's been made, I imagine there's a reason why, and I'd rather be safe."
18Myles Roberts said: "[I'm] very, very disappointed. When I left England on Wednesday there were no problems whatsoever... It's been made quite clear to us that if we don't leave on these flights we could be stuck here until October."
19Mr Blunt questioned why travel advice was not changed immediately after the attack.
20"What we don't know is the exact intelligence picture of what Philip Hammond (the foreign secretary) is seeing - but on the basis of what he's said it raises questions," he told BBC Radio 4's Today programme.
21Mr Blunt said he intended to question Mr Hammond about the decision when he appears before the committee on 21 July.
22Asked if he would be prepared to take his family on holiday to Tunisia, the former minister answered: "Yes."
23The UK has warned another terror attack in the country was "highly likely".
24Mr Hammond said it was "too big a risk" not to act but he hoped the guidance would be downgraded soon.
25Ireland, where three of the victims were from, has warned against "all non-essential travel" to Tunisia.
26Denmark followed suit but Germany and France, which accounts for the largest number of tourists in the country, has not.
27Former British ambassador to Libya Oliver Miles told the BBC he was "puzzled" at the decision to repatriate tourists.
28"There clearly is a threat but what puzzles me is that the threat is not confined to Tunisia and is not confined to the British," he said.
29The Sousse gunman has been identified as Seifeddine Rezgui, who authorities say had trained in Libya - but the Islamic State group claimed the attack.
