32Greece's prime minister was fighting to keep his government intact in the face of outrage over an austerity bill that parliament must pass Wednesday night if the country is to start negotiations on a new bailout and avoid financial collapse.
33The raft of consumer tax increases and pension reforms will condemn Greeks to years of more economic hardship and has fueled anger among the governing left-wing Syriza party.
34Civil servants protested with a 24-hour strike that disrupted public transport and shut down state-run services across the country.
35Clashes broke out at a demonstration attended by about 12,500 anti-austerity protesters rallying outside Parliament.
36Riot police fired tear gas to fight back groups of youths hurling Molotov cocktails from within the crowd.
37The clashes broke out just as lawmakers were starting to debate the bill.
38The bill is expected to pass with votes in favor by pro-European opposition parties.
39But large numbers of Syriza lawmakers are almost certain to vote against the package.
40Alternate Finance Minister Nadia Valavani resigned from her post, saying she could not vote in favor of the bill.
41In a letter she sent to Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras on Monday and released by the finance ministry Wednesday, Valavani said she believed "dominant circles in Germany" were intent on "the full humiliation of the government and the country."
42The economy ministry's secretary general, Manos Manousakis, also resigned over the agreement.
43A meeting between the parliament speaker and deputy speakers was being held Wednesday evening to decide on the exact timing of the parliamentary debate and vote.
44A similar vote last week was concluded after 3 a.m. after a debate that started at midnight.
45Tsipras agreed to a deal after a marathon 17-hour eurozone summit that ended Monday morning.
46It calls for Greece to pass new austerity measures his left-wing government had long battled against in return for the start of negotiations on a third bailout worth about 85 billion euros ($93 billion) in loans over three years.
47The government, a coalition between Syriza and the small right-wing Independent Greeks, holds 162 seats in Greece's 300-member parliament.
48More than 30 of Syriza's own lawmakers have publicly voiced objections.
49Tsipras has acknowledged the measures he agreed to go against his election pledges to repeal austerity, and described them in a Tuesday night television interview as "irrational."
50But he said he had no option if he was to prevent Greece's financial collapse.
51The International Monetary Fund, which was involved Greece's previous two bailouts and will also play a role in the third, has long argued the country's debt is too high and that any deal must include debt relief - something the Greek side has also insisted on.
52In a report released late Tuesday, the IMF said Greece's debt was now "highly unsustainable" and would reach "close to 200 percent of GDP in the next two years."
53On Wednesday, the European Union's executive Commission echoed that analysis, saying there are "serious concerns" about the sustainability of Greece's debt due to a worsening in the economy.
54Tsipras has faced strident dissent even from top ministers, with Energy Minister Panagiotis Lafazanis saying in a post on his ministry's website that the deal the prime minister reached was "unacceptable" and calling on him to withdraw it.
55The civil servants' strike disrupted public services.
56Pharmacies joined in with their own 24-hour strike to object to the austerity deal, which will allow some non-prescription drugs to be sold by supermarkets.
57"These laws will pass through parliament today, because they can't do otherwise," said Eleni Sari, 45, as she walked through central Athens.
58"Naturally, the people are furious, and they have not allowed them any choice. Unfortunately it's not in our hands anymore. That is, it's no longer in the people's hands. By necessity ... they will pass them in parliament, and by necessity we will bear their burden."
59Greeks continued to struggle with limits on cash withdrawals and transfers outside of the country.
60Banks were shut down June 29 and the finance ministry said they would remain closed through Thursday.
61With its banks dangerously low on liquidity and the state practically out of cash, Greece desperately needs funds.
62It faces a Monday deadline to repay 4.2 billion euros ($4.6 billion) to the European Central Bank, and is also in arrears on 2 billion euros to the IMF.
63Negotiations on the new bailout will take an estimated four weeks, leaving European finance ministers scrambling to find ways to get Athens some money sooner.
64The European Commission has proposed giving Greece 7 billion euros in loans from a special fund overseen by all 28 EU nations so it can meet its upcoming debts.
65The loan would be made pending the start of a full bailout program, but faces resistance from Britain, a non-euro member of the EU.
66Germany argued one way for Greece to meet its financing obligations was for it to issue IOUs for domestic needs.
67Associated Press writers Efty Katsareas in Athens and Raf Casert in Brussels contributed to this report.
