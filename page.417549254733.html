Google releases more details on self-driving car accidents

1NEW YORK (AP) - Google is disclosing more details about the 12 accidents involving its self-driving cars so far as part of a commitment to provide monthly updates about the safety and performance of the vehicles.
2The summary released Friday described all of the collisions as minor, saying no injuries were reported.
3As it has been doing for several weeks, Google said that the self-driving technology was not to blame for any of the accidents.
4In one case, however, an employee used the self-driving car to run an errand and rear-ended another car that was stopped in traffic.
5Google had previously disclosed that accident, which happened in August 2011.
6Google's breakdown of the accidents came just two days after company co-founder Sergey Brin told shareholders that the company had already disclosed most of the pertinent information about the crashes.
7Consumer Watchdog, a group that has been a longtime Google critic, has been pushing the Mountain View, California, company to release all of the accident reports filed with the California Department of Motor Vehicles and other law enforcement agencies.
8Dissatisfied with Google's accounting, Consumer Watchdog on Friday renewed its call for the company to release the official accident reports.
9The Associated Press has asked Google and the California Department of Motor Vehicles for the reports.
10Both have refused, citing privacy concerns.
11While the latest disclosures fell short of providing the official accident reports, they did give previously unreleased information on the locations and dates and circumstances of the 12 accidents.
12Google Inc. started testing the cars in 2009, and the first accident was in May 2010.
13The company says six of the accidents happened while the car was in autonomous driving mode.
14The other six happened while staffers were driving, including one incident where the car was hit by another driver who rolled through a stop sign.
15Google says the self-driving car automatically applied the brakes when it detected the other vehicle, and Google's driver took manual control once the brakes were applied.
16The Google vehicle sustained some damage.
17All but two of the accidents happened in Google's hometown of Mountain View, where the company plans to begin testing its latest self-driving car - a pod-like vehicle - this summer.
18While several of the accidents happened at low speeds or while the car was stopped, in one case a Google vehicle was driving 63 miles per hour on a highway in San Jose, California, when another vehicle veered into its side.
19Google's cars have been involved in four accidents so far this year, according to the company.
20It says the cars travel about 10,000 miles a week on public streets.
21The vehicles have driven about one million miles in autonomous mode and Google's drivers have been in control for 800,000 additional miles.
