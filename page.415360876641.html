6UNITED NATIONS (AP) - Confronting a growing political furor, Hillary Rodham Clinton conceded Tuesday it was a mistake to exclusively use private email as secretary of state, but she defiantly rejected accusations that she created a security risk or violated the law.
7She also defended getting rid of tens of thousands of personal emails.
8"I fully complied with every rule I was governed by," Clinton said in a 20-minute news conference that marked her first comments on the controversy.
9Clinton's acknowledgement that she deleted emails she described as personal in nature is likely to fuel further criticism from Republicans who see the matter as harmful to the former secretary of state's all-but-certain presidential campaign.
10Because Clinton's emails were run off a personal server that she does not intend to turn over to the government, there is no way to independently verify the content of the discarded emails.
11Clinton said she had exchanged about 60,000 emails in her four years as President Barack Obama's secretary of state.
12Half were work-related and none contained classified information, she said.
13The controversy has presented a major test for Clinton and upended her careful blueprint for the rollout of her campaign.
14The clear front-runner for the Democratic nomination, Clinton had planned to spend March touting her work on women's issues and giving a handful of paid speeches before announcing her candidacy in early April.
15Instead, she found herself standing before dozens of reporters at the United Nations addressing a matter that has revived questions of ethics and secrecy that have long trailed Clinton and her husband, former President Bill Clinton.
16Her appearance came after her previously scheduled speech on women's rights at the UN.
17Before Tuesday's news conference, Clinton's only comment on the email disclosures had been a late-night tweet last week saying she wanted the State Department to make her emails public.
18Her decision to weigh in further came as Democratic allies began publicly pushing her to do so.
19Asked whether the email disclosures would affect her presidential ambitions, Clinton said, "I trust the American people to make their decisions about political and public matters."
20The Republican Party's leading presidential prospects had no immediate response to Clinton's explanations.
21But in a signal that the issue was unlikely to fade soon, Republican National Committee Chairman Reince Priebus blasted Clinton's comments as "completely disingenuous."
22"No one but Hillary Clinton knows if she handed over every relevant email," Priebus said.
23Clinton's comments also appeared unlikely to quell efforts by GOP lawmakers to use the matter to bolster their investigation into the deaths of four Americans at a U.S. facility in Benghazi, Libya, in 2012.
24Following the news conference, Rep.
25Trey Gowdy, the South Carolina Republican leading the committee, said he planned to call her to testify at least twice.
26A central question for Clinton over the past week has been why she chose not to use government email if she wasn't trying to skirt federal rules that require officials' communications to be archived.
27She described her use of personal email as a matter of "convenience" and a way to avoid carrying two devices.
28She said that didn't appear to be an issue at the time, but that in hindsight, it would have been "smarter" to use a government account as well as her personal one.
29Last month, Clinton told an audience in Silicon Valley that she uses multiple electronic devices.
30"I have an iPad, a mini-iPad, an iPhone and a BlackBerry," Clinton said.
31The email controversy stems from reports last week that Clinton had exclusively used both a private email address and a private server while at the State Department.
32Clinton said repeatedly Tuesday that she had taken "unprecedented steps" to provide the State Department her work-related communications.
33She described the missing personal emails as ones she wrote about her daughter's wedding, her mother's funeral, her yoga routine and other similar matters.
34"No one wants their personal emails made public, and I think most people understand that and respect privacy," she said.
35Clinton left the Obama administration in early 2013, but didn't turn over her written communications for nearly two years and only in response to a State Department request.
36The email disclosures have raised questions about whether she fully complied with federal laws requiring government officials to preserve written communications involving official business.
37While Clinton insisted Tuesday that she was not violating any rules, she does appear to have gone against what the Obama White House has called "very specific guidance" that officials should use government email to conduct business.
38She said she emailed her State Department colleagues on their government accounts and therefore expected her communications would be archived.
39She provided no evidence to support her assertions that her email system experienced no security breaches.
40The department says it will take several months to review the material Clinton turned over last year.
41Spokeswoman Jen Psaki said Tuesday that once the review is complete, the emails will be posted online for the public to see.
42Passages revealing anything from trade secrets to sensitive national security information could be redacted, in keeping with Freedom of Information Act guidelines.

4As in every year, CAAM features numerous works from Bay Area filmmakers.

10Uncertain about which films to see?
11Here are five worth a look during the festival's run.
12"Love Arcadia": Lawrence Gan's kindhearted romance centers on two childhood friends who discover an unlikely attraction amid a real estate land deal that could shutter a family business.
13Although "Arcadia" meanders and the resolution comes out strained, it's sweet and features a charismatic performance from Anthony Ma as Jake, who works at his family's tea shop and helps a lovely workaholic tap into her inner child.
14Screenings: 7:10 p.m. March 14 at the Kabuki, San Francisco; 9:30 p.m. March 19 at New People Cinema, San Francisco; 1 p.m., March 22 at the New Parkway Theater, Oakland.
15"Nuoc 2030": Is it possible for a socially conscious sci-fi flick with low-fi special effects to be worthwhile?
16That would be a solid yes, as Nghiem-Minh Nguyen-Vo's moody, atmospheric genre mashup reveals.
17Set in a Vietnam that's been flooded by climate change, "Nuoc 2030" plays games with time as a wife tries to solve the murder of her fisherman husband.
18It's moody, sensual and surprising.
19Seek it out.
20Screenings: 7:15 p.m. March 13, Kabuki; 8 p.m. March 15, Berkeley Pacific Film Archive Theater; 9:30 p.m. March 17, Kabuki.
21"Hollow": Ham Tran's disturbing horror story taps into a parent's ultimate nightmare: the death of a child.
22The child comes back to life, and -- of course -- turns out to be not exactly of sound mind.
23Tran's film is a gripper, unsparing with its shocking twists as it barrels toward a climax with a serious theme.
24Screenings: 8:50 p.m. March 13, Pacific Film Archive; 9:15 p.m. March 14, Kabuki.
25"The Last Season": Berkeley resident Sara Dosa's documentary is a gentle, insightful look at a life-affirming relationship between two mushroom pickers working in Oregon: one a survivor of Cambodia's Khmer Rouge and the other a Vietnam veteran.
26Dosa immerses us into the lifestyles of these pickers, who are pursuing the matsutake mushroom, considered a delicacy in Japan.
27Screenings: 5 p.m. March 13, Kabuki; 5:10 p.m. March 18, Kabuki.
28"My Fair Wedding": You have to admire the moxie of Kim Jho Gwang-Soo and Dave Kim -- the first same-sex couple to marry in South Korea.
29They allow all their feelings to air in Jang Hee-Sun's documentary, which details the planning -- and the arguing -- that goes into their massive wedding.
30We also bear witness to the extensive media coverage, the outcry from opponents and the cultural significance of all this in South Korea.
31"Wedding" is compelling, reminiscent of watching larger-than-life characters on a reality TV show.
32Screenings: 9:40 p.m. March 15, Castro Theatre, San Francisco.
