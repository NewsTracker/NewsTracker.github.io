3Haddadi got into the business five years ago when she saw her artist friends struggling to make a living from their music.
4She realised there was a lack of managers and agents and seized the opportunity.
5"I believe that the biggest adventure you can take is to live the life of your dreams - and my dream was to inspire and to help others," she says.
6The music industry is not yet taken seriously as a career in Morocco, says Haddadi, who comes from a family of engineers and doctors.
7Despite their reservations, her family supported her and she went out and got her first client, DJ Van.
8She didn't tell him that she was just 18 and still at school - she pretended to be 21.
9The bluff worked, and Van asked her to produce his album.
10It was her first project.
11"Not a single, not a tour, not a booking - an album," says Haddadi proudly.
12"Now I don't prove myself any more. It's not ego, but I know I'm the best."
13"As a woman I get a lot of sexual harassment - not from my artists, but from other artists in the business, from partners in the business, even from fans," she says.
14The biggest challenge has been to be taken seriously in this "exclusive men's club".
15Most male musicians cannot accept that a woman might tell them what to do - their pride will not allow it, says Haddadi.
16When she approached a big Moroccan artist - she doesn't mention his name - he told her he could never employ her as a manager.
17"I cannot give my career, my life to a woman. I cannot do that," he said.
18The turning point came when she realised she had to think differently.
19"If you want people to stop judging you as a woman in this business, stop judging yourself as a woman. Judge yourself as a manager, not as a female manager. Judge yourself on how you can push your artists to the limits."
20Now that she has an established group of artists who trust her, the next hurdle is to build up the Moroccan music industry as a whole.
21"The biggest problem we have is distribution," says Haddadi.
22"You can make music, but where are you going to sell that product? I would love my artists to have their rights, their royalties" - and not just so that she can get her percentage.
23"To have a productive artist he needs to wake up every morning not worrying about how he's going to pay his rent, he just needs to wake up and think about his art."
24She has chosen not to accept an offer of work in the US, but to stay in Morocco.
25"I hope one day I can say I made the right choice," she says.
26"I have the chance to earn my living by living my dreams.
27"I think I have the best job in the world - if I can take just one song from one of my artists, and pour it in the ears of just one person, and they feel the positivity from the song, then I will leave my mark on this world."
28Haddidi says the situation for women of her generation has progressed.
29"Over the past 10 years Moroccan women have become more independent, they earn money, they party. I go nightclubbing with my friends, in a female group - it's not a problem."
30Moroccan women can travel alone, they can vote, run for election, can do business, be independent, says Haddidi - but these rules don't apply to all levels of society.
31Poorer women still live in a traditional society where "she gets a lot of sexual harassment on the street because she is wearing jeans", she says.
32"Now we are dealing with radical Islam - women are the devil. But I will say we are really lucky if you compare with other countries."
