Blatter stands down as FIFA president

1ZURICH (Reuters) - Sepp Blatter's shock resignation as FIFA president, hours before it emerged he was under investigation by U.S. authorities, prompted widespread calls for root-and-branch reforms at soccer's world governing body on Wednesday.
2The Swiss national, who has led FIFA for 17 years, is being investigated by U.S. prosecutors and the Federal Bureau of Investigation, a person who spoke on condition of anonymity told Reuters late on Tuesday.
3An FBI spokesman declined comment.
4News of Blatter's investigation was earlier reported by The New York Times and ABC News.
5Blatter has not been charged with any wrongdoing.
6FIFA did not respond to a request for comment on Blatter being under investigation.
7Blatter, 79, announced his decision to step down at a news conference in Zurich on Tuesday, six days after police raided a hotel in the city and arrested several FIFA officials - and just four days after he was re-elected to a fifth term as president.
8"FIFA needs profound restructuring," he said.
9"I decided to stand again to be elected because I was convinced it was the best option for football. Although the members of FIFA gave me a new mandate, this mandate does not seem to be supported by everyone in the world."
10He said an election to choose a new president would be held as soon as possible, though a FIFA official said it would probably not take place until at least December.
11Blatter will remain in his position until a successor is chosen.
12His daughter, Corinne Blatter-Andenmatten, told a Swiss newspaper her father's decision to stand down was not tied to recent corruption allegations.
13"His decision has nothing, absolutely nothing, to do with the allegations going around," she told daily Blick.
14European sports officials said Blatter's resignation, with FIFA mired in the worst crisis in its 111-year history, was an important step, but that the organisation needed deeper changes.
15"Beyond the people, structural reforms must be undertaken," said French Sports State Secretary Thierry Braillard.
16New Zealand Football (NZF) Chief Executive Andy Martin said soccer must now rebuild its tattered reputation.
17"This has lifted a cloud and taken away a lot of the concerns of stakeholders and their association with the sport," he told Reuters.
18"We now want a strong collaborative leader who can bring the football world together and can bring out the change that the game has been crying out for."
19The Asian Football Confederation (AFC), which has been a staunch ally of Blatter, said it was monitoring the situation and would discuss internally the "best way forward for both FIFA and world football".
20AFC member the Philippines Football Federation said it was surprised by Blatter's decision, but noted this "offers a big opportunity to continue and intensify the reforms that have been started. Indeed, deliberate focus on more governance reform and transparency is now called for."
21FIFA was stunned last week by the announcement of a U.S. investigation into alleged widespread financial wrongdoing stretching back more than two decades.
22Swiss authorities also launched their own criminal probe into the awarding of the 2018 and 2022 World Cups to Russia and Qatar, respectively.
23As Blatter signalled his exit, English Football Association chairman Greg Dyke raised the possibility that the controversial vote that awarded Qatar the 2022 tournament could be re-run.
24"If I was the Qatari organisers I wouldn't sleep very well tonight," he told British media.
25Qatar Football Association president Sheikh Hamad Bin Khalifa Bin Ahmed Al-Thani hit back, indicating the small Gulf state will not give up hosting soccer's showpiece event without a fight.
26"We would urge Mr Dyke to let the legal process take its course and concentrate on delivering his promise to build an England team capable of winning the 2022 World Cup in Qatar," he said.
27Qatar's stock index tumbled as much as 3 percent to a 6-week low in early Wednesday trade amid heightened investor fears that Qatar could be stripped of its hosting rights.
28Among the potential candidates to lead FIFA, European football federation chief Michel Platini, a French former international player, said Blatter had made "a difficult decision, a brave decision, and the right decision".
29Jordan's Prince Ali bin Al Hussein, who withdrew from last week's presidential election after winning 73 votes to Blatter's 133 in the first round, stopped short of confirming he would run again.
30Asked if there should be a fresh start at FIFA, he told Britain's Channel 4 News: "I'm willing to help."
31Several other candidates may emerge ahead of the election for a new president, including Domenico Scala, independent chairman of the audit and compliance committee of FIFA.
32Former Brazil international Zico, 62, did not rule out a bid, and Venezuelan President Nicolas Maduro suggested Argentine soccer legend Diego Maradona could be the next FIFA chief.
33Others in the frame to become only FIFA's ninth chief include Jerome Champagne, a former French diplomat and FIFA deputy secretary general, and German Wolfgang Niersbach, a former sports journalist and FIFA media chief.
