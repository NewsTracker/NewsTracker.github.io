GOP likely to feel the heat if court decision guts Obamacare

1WASHINGTON (AP) - A Supreme Court ruling due in a few weeks could wipe out health insurance for millions of people covered by President Barack Obama's health care law.
2But it's Republicans - not White House officials - who have been talking about damage control.
3A likely reason: Twenty-six of the 34 states that would be most affected by the ruling have Republican governors, and 22 of the 24 GOP Senate seats up in 2016 are in those states.
4Obama's law offers subsidized private insurance to people without access to it on the job.
5In the court case, opponents of the law argue that its literal wording allows the federal government to subsidize coverage only in states that set up their own health insurance markets.
6Most states have not done so, because of the intense partisanship over "Obamacare" and in some cases because of technical problems.
7Instead, they rely on the federal HealthCare.gov website.
8If the court invalidates the subsidies in those states, an estimated 8 million people could lose coverage.
9The results would be "ugly," said Sandy Praeger, a former Kansas insurance commissioner.
10"People who are reasonably healthy would just drop coverage," she said.
11"Only the unhealthy would keep buying health care. It would really exacerbate the problem of the cost of health insurance."
12Praeger, a Republican who retired this year, called it "a classic death spiral," using a term for market collapse.
13Oral arguments on March 4 revealed a divided court.
14Chief Justice John Roberts and Justice Anthony Kennedy seemingly are key to the outcome, which won't be known until late June.
15If the subsidies survive, the Affordable Care Act will look like settled law to all but its most passionate opponents.
16But if they are overturned, the shock could carry into next year's elections.
17Around the time when the court announces its decision, insurers will be working to finalize premiums and plans for the coming year.
18Contracts with the government for 2016 health law coverage have to be signed by early fall.
19If the subsidies are overturned, insurers would have to tear up their projections about markets in more than half the states.
20Populous states such as Texas, Florida, Ohio, Illinois, New Jersey, Georgia and Pennsylvania would be among those affected.
21State lawmakers could mitigate the impact by setting up their own insurance markets, or exchanges.
22But that can't be done overnight.
23States might try authorizing an exchange, and then contracting with the federal government to run it.
24But that sort of end run might prompt lawsuits from opponents of the law.
25During arguments, Justice Samuel Alito raised the possibility that the court might be able to delay the effective date of its decision.
26Even a delay through the end of this year wouldn't buy much time.
27Enrollment for 2016 health law plans is scheduled to start Nov. 1.
28The health law was designed as a balancing act.
29Insurers can't turn people away because of health problems, but most healthy people are required to contribute to the insurance pool, and the government subsidizes most of the premium for low- to middle-income households.
