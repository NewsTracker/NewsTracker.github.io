5A joint meeting of Congress, gathering senators and representatives together, is a ceremony typically bestowed on one or two friendly foreign leaders per year.
6It looks a lot like a presidential State of the Union address.
7The speaker embodies his or her nation; the audience of lawmakers represents all Americans.
8Unity and shared purpose are standard themes.
9So leaders speaking against the backdrop of the chamber's huge American flag tend to gloss over differences and steer clear of internal politics.
10These joint meetings have become a favored venue for foreign leaders and dignitaries, whose appearances usually get more notice back home than among Americans.
11That's another controversial element in Netanyahu's address, just two weeks before he's up for re-election.
12History offers scant precedent for Netanyahu's speech.
13The tradition of inviting foreign dignitaries dates to the Marquis de Lafayette, the French hero of the American Revolution, who gave separate speeches to the House and Senate in 1824.
14It took another 50 years for the two bodies to come together to hear a foreigner - King Kalakaua, of what was then the Kingdom of Hawaii.
15A few allies dominate the rostrum.
16With Netanyahu's return, it will tie Britain and France at eight speeches apiece.
17In all, 110 foreigners and a few Americans have addressed joint meetings.
18As Australian prime minister in 2011, Julia Gillard nailed the mutual-admiration genre.
19She recalled being a small girl amazed that Americans had landed on the moon.
20"On that great day I believed Americans could do anything," she said.
21"I believe that still."
22The closest comparison to Netanyahu's latest speech to Congress may be Netanyahu's last speech to Congress.
23The Israeli prime minister's 2011 visit went through diplomatic channels.
24But Netanyahu's appearance with Obama turned frosty.
25He rejected Obama's suggestion that peace negotiations with the Palestinians should start from Israel's boundaries before it seized territory in 1967.
26In his speech to Congress, Netanyahu repeated his rejection of "indefensible boundaries," prompting scathing reactions from Palestinian leaders.
27A foreigner invited to brace up the president still can stir controversy in Washington and at home.
28British Prime Minister Tony Blair spoke in defense of the Iraq War in July 2003, months after the invasion, when the failure to find weapons of mass destruction and the realization that fighting would not end quickly was souring public opinion in the U.S. and further inflaming war opposition in Britain.
29Congressional Democrats were starting to question President George W. Bush's rationale for going to war.
30Still, members from both parties cheered Blair, who assured them, "We will be with you in this fight for liberty."
31Nearly two decades earlier, another British leader, Margaret Thatcher, gave a rousing endorsement of President Ronald Reagan's Cold War strategy.
32She called her speech to Congress "one of the most moving occasions of my life."
33Netanyahu will become only the second leader to address a joint meeting three times.
34The other - British Prime Minister Churchill - gave one of the Capitol's most dramatic speeches in 1941.
35Churchill rushed across the Atlantic to make war plans with President Franklin Roosevelt when the United States entered World War II.
36Speaking under the newsreel cameras' bright lights, his voice carried live to anxious Americans by radio, Churchill voiced outrage at Japan's sneak attack on Hawaii's Pearl Harbor but openly rejoiced that the United States "has drawn the sword of freedom," bringing hope to its battered European allies.
37A few speakers bring Congress a transcendent vision.
38Mandela did it twice.

40In 1994, Mandela returned as South Africa's first black president.
41He urged Americans to look beyond their national borders and take the lead in creating a world of democracy, peace and prosperity.
