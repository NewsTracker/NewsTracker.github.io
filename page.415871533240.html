Apple CEO Tim Cook: New laws enshrine discrimination

1There's something very dangerous happening in states across the country.
2A wave of legislation, introduced in more than two dozen states, would allow people to discriminate against their neighbours.
3Some, such as the bill enacted in Indiana last week that drew a national outcry and one passed in Arkansas, say individuals can cite their personal religious beliefs to refuse service to a customer or resist a state non-discrimination law.
4Others are more transparent in their effort to discriminate.
5Legislation being considered in Texas would strip the salaries and pensions of clerks who issue marriage licences to same-sex couples - even if the Supreme Court strikes down Texas' marriage ban later this year.
6In total, there are nearly 100 bills designed to enshrine discrimination in state law.
7These bills rationalise injustice by pretending to defend something many of us hold dear.
8They go against the very principles our nation was founded on, and they have the potential to undo decades of progress toward greater equality.
9America's business community recognised a long time ago that discrimination, in all its forms, is bad for business.
10At Apple, we are in business to empower and enrich our customers' lives.
11We strive to do business in a way that is just and fair.
12That's why, on behalf of Apple, I'm standing up to oppose this new wave of legislation - wherever it emerges.
13I'm writing in the hopes that many more will join this movement.
14From North Carolina to Nevada, these bills under consideration truly will hurt jobs, growth and the economic vibrancy of parts of the country where a 21st-century economy was once welcomed with open arms.
15I have great reverence for religious freedom.
16As a child, I was baptised in a Baptist church, and faith has always been an important part of my life.
17I was never taught, nor do I believe, that religion should be used as an excuse to discriminate.
18I remember what it was like to grow up in the South in the 1960s and 1970s.
19Discrimination isn't something that's easy to oppose.
20It doesn't always stare you in the face.
21It moves in the shadows.
22And sometimes it shrouds itself within the very laws meant to protect us.
23Our message, to people around the country and around the world, is this: Apple is open.
24Open to everyone, regardless of where they come from, what they look like, how they worship or who they love.
25Regardless of what the law might allow in Indiana or Arkansas, we will never tolerate discrimination.
26Men and women have fought and died fighting to protect our country's founding principles of freedom and equality.
27We owe it to them, to each other and to our future to continue to fight with our words and our actions to make sure we protect those ideals.
28The days of segregation and discrimination marked by "Whites Only" signs on shop doors, water fountains and restrooms must remain deep in our past.
29We must never return to any semblance of that time.
30America must be a land of opportunity for everyone.
31This isn't a political issue.
32It isn't a religious issue.
33This is about how we treat each other as human beings.
34Opposing discrimination takes courage.
35With the lives and dignity of so many people at stake, it's time for all of us to be courageous.
