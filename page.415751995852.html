German co-pilot 'appears to have crashed plane deliberately'

1The Marseille State Prosecutor says recordings suggest the co-pilot took lone control of the plane, and is believed to have activated the descent button deliberately.
2The co-pilot of a Germanwings jet that went down in the French Alps, killing 150 people, appears to have crashed the plane deliberately, a Marseille prosecutor said.
3The German citizen, left in sole control of the Airbus A320 after the captain left the cockpit, refused to re-open the door and pressed a button that sent the jet into its fatal descent, Marseille prosecutor Brice Robin told a news conference carried on live television.
4The co-pilot was identified as 28-year-old Andreas Lubitz.
5"There is no reason to suspect a terrorist attack," he said.
6Asked whether he believed the crash that killed 150 people was the result of suicide, he said: "People who commit suicide usually do so alone....I don't call it a suicide."
7The prosecutor said the black-box cockpit recording recovered from the wreckage of the disaster suggested passengers did not realise what was happening until the last moment.
8Investigators have retrieved cockpit voice recordings from one of the 'black boxes' of the German jet that crashed in the Alps, killing everyone onboard, and the New York Times said the audio showed one of the pilots left the cockpit and could not get back in before the plane went down.
9"The guy outside is knocking lightly on the door and there is no answer," an unnamed investigator told the Times, citing the recordings.
10"And then he hits the door stronger and no answer. There is never an answer."
11"You can hear he is trying to smash the door down," the investigator added.
12The retrieval of the recordings came as French President Francois Hollande, Germany's Angela Merkel and Spain's Mariano Rajoy traveled to the crash site in a remote French Alpine region to pay tribute to the 150 victims, mostly Germany and Spanish.
13However, while Hollande promised that authorities would not rest until the causes of the crash were known, France's BEA air incident investigation bureau said it was still far too early to draw meaningful conclusions on why the plane, operated by the Germanwings budget arm of Lufthansa (LHAG.DE), went down.
14"We have just been able to extract a useable audio data file," BEA director Remi Jouty told a news conference at the agency's headquarters outside Paris.
15"We have not yet been able to study and to establish an exact timing for all the sounds and words heard on this file."
16Jouty expected the first basic analysis in "a matter of days" but warned this read-out could be subject to errors and that more work would be needed for a full interpretation.
17Although he said "words" had been heard on the tape, Jouty would not confirm whether that meant the Airbus A320's pilots were conscious and he gave no details of the recordings.
18The New York Times quoted a senior military official involved in the investigation as saying the cockpit audio showed "very smooth, very cool" conversation between the pilots in the early part of the flight.
19The audio then indicated one of the pilots left the cockpit.
20"We don't know yet the reason why one of the guys went out," the official added.
21"But what is sure is that at the very end of the flight, the other pilot is alone and does not open the door."
22A Lufthansa spokesman said the carrier was aware of the Times story, adding: "We have no information from the authorities that confirms this report and we are seeking more information. We will not take part in speculation on the causes of the crash. "
