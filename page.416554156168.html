Rescuers struggle to reach remote Nepal areas as toll rises

1KATHMANDU, Nepal (AP) - The death toll from Nepal's earthquake soared past 3,700 Monday, and how much higher it would rise depended largely on the condition of vulnerable mountain villages that rescue workers were still struggling to reach two days after the disaster.
2Reports received so far by the government and aid groups suggest that many communities perched on mountainsides are devastated or struggling to cope.
3Udav Prashad Timalsina, the top official for the Gorkha district, near the epicenter of Saturday's quake, said he was in desperate need of help.
4"There are people who are not getting food and shelter. I've had reports of villages where 70 percent of the houses have been destroyed," he said.
5Nepalese residents gather in an open space at the site of destruction caused after Saturday's earthquake in Bhaktapur, on the outskirts of Kathmandu, Nepal, Monday, April 27, 2015.
6A strong magnitude earthquake shook s capital and the densely populated Kathmandu valley on Saturday devastating the region and leaving tens of thousands shell-shocked and sleeping in streets.
7He said 223 people had been confirmed dead in the district but he presumed "the number would go up because there are thousands who are injured."
8Saturday's magnitude 7.8 earthquake spread horror from Kathmandu to small villages and to the slopes of Mount Everest, triggering an avalanche that buried part of the base camp packed with foreign climbers preparing to make their summit attempts.
9Timalsina said his district had not received enough help from the central government, but Jagdish Pokhrel, the clearly exhausted army spokesman, said nearly the entire 100,000-soldier army was involved in rescue operations.
10"We have 90 percent of the army out there working on search and rescue," he said.
11"We are focusing our efforts on that, on saving lives."
12Nepal police said in a statement Monday that the country's death toll had risen to 3,617 people.
13That does not include the 18 people killed in the avalanche, which were counted by the mountaineering association.
14Another 61 people were killed in neighboring India, and China reported 20 people dead in Tibet.
15Well over 1,000 of the victims were in Kathmandu, the capital, where an eerie calm prevailed Monday.
16Tens of thousands of families slept outdoors for a second night, fearful of aftershocks that have not ceased.
17Camped in parks, open squares and a golf course, they cuddled children or pets against chilly Himalayan nighttime temperatures.
18They woke to the sound of dogs yelping and jackhammers.
19As the dawn light crawled across toppled building sites, volunteers and rescue workers carefully shifted broken concrete slabs and crumbled bricks mixed together with humble household items: pots and pans; a purple notebook decorated with butterflies; a framed poster of a bodybuilder; so many shoes.
20"It's overwhelming. It's too much to think about," said 55-year-old Bijay Nakarmi, mourning his parents, whose bodies recovered from the rubble of what once was a three-story building.
21He had last seen them a few days earlier - on Nepal's Mothers' Day - for a cheerful family meal.
22"I have their bodies by the river. They are resting until relatives can come to the funeral," Nakarmi said as workers continued searching for another five people buried underneath the wreckage.
23Kathmandu district chief administrator Ek Narayan Aryal said tents and water were being handed out Monday at 10 locations in Kathmandu, but that aftershocks were leaving everyone jittery.
24The largest, on Sunday, was magnitude 6.7.
25"There have been nearly 100 earthquakes and aftershocks, which is making rescue work difficult. Even the rescuers are scared and running because of them," he said.
26"We don't feel safe at all. There have been so many aftershocks. It doesn't stop," said Rajendra Dhungana, 34, who spent Sunday with his niece's family for her cremation at the Pashuputi Nath Temple.
27Acrid, white smoke rose above the Hindu temple, Nepal's most revered.
28"I've watched hundreds of bodies burn," Dhungana said.
29The capital city is largely a collection of small, poorly constructed brick apartment buildings.
30The earthquake destroyed swaths of the oldest neighborhoods, but many were surprised by how few modern structures collapsed in the quake.
31On Monday morning, some pharmacies and shops for basic provisions opened while bakeries began offering fresh bread.
32With power lines down, spotty phone connections and almost no Internet connectivity, residents were particularly anxious to buy morning newspapers.
33Huge lines of people desperate to secure fuel lined up outside gasoline pumps; prices were the same as they were before the earthquake struck.
34As aid began pouring in from more than a dozen countries, aid workers warned that the situation could be far worse near the epicenter.
35The U.S. Geological Survey said the quake was centered near Lamjung, a district about 80 kilometers (50 miles) northwest of Kathmandu.
36While not far away, poor roads and steep mountains make Lamjung difficult to reach.
37Even before the quake, it could take six hours to drive from Kathmandu to parts of the area.
38Now, many of the few roads are believed to be cut off by small landslides.
39The earthquake was the worst to hit the South Asian nation in more than 80 years.
40It and was strong enough to be felt all across parts of India, Bangladesh, China's region of Tibet and Pakistan.
41Nepal's worst recorded earthquake in 1934 measured 8.0 and all but destroyed the cities of Kathmandu, Bhaktapur and Patan.
42The quake has put a huge strain on the resources of this impoverished country best known for Everest, the highest mountain in the world.
43The economy of Nepal, a nation of 27.8 million people, relies heavily on tourism, principally trekking and Himalayan mountain climbing.
