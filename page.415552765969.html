1Accomplices sought in Tunisia terror attack Armed terrorists stormed an iconic museum and killed 18 people Wednesday before security forces swept in, killed two gunmen, freed hostages and launched a manhunt for more perpetrators.
2TUNIS, Tunisia - A massive manhunt was underway for two or three accomplices after terrorists stormed a museum Wednesday, took hostages and killed 19 people, most of them tourists.
3Two gunmen were killed when authorities swept in and freed the hostages.
4The government blamed a war on terror, but no group claimed responsibility for the bold assault at the National Bardo Museum, a popular tourist attraction in the birthplace of the Arab Spring movement that led to democratic elections.
5"I want the people of Tunisia to understand firstly and lastly that we are in a war with terror, and these savage minority groups will not frighten us," newly elected President Beji Caid Essebsi said in an evening address to the nation.
6"The fight against them will continue until they are exterminated."
7Late Wednesday, parliament held an extraordinary session where Speaker Mohammed Ennaceur called for the creation of a special fund to combat terrorism and passage of an anti-terror law.
8Thousands of people gathered at a downtown landmark for an evening rally.
9Prime Minister Habib Essid said 17 foreign tourists were among those killed by the two Tunisian gunmen.
10Victims were from Japan, four from Italy, two from Columbia, two from Spain, and one each from Australia, Poland and France.
11The nationality of one victim was not released.
12At least 44 people were wounded.
13Japan's Prime Minister Shinzo Abbe said three of the dead and three of those injured were Japanese.
14The Tunisian prime minister earlier said five Japanese were among the dead.

18Some of the Italians at the museum were believed to have been passengers from the Costa Fascinosa, a cruise liner that had docked in Tunis.
19Ship owner Costa Crociere confirmed that some of its 3,161 passengers were visiting Tunis and that a Bardo tour was on the itinerary, but said it couldn't confirm how many were in the museum.
20The scene was chaotic with people being evacuated from nearby buildings, including parliament, which was discussing anti-terrorism legislation, Essid said.
21Tour guide Walid Ben Cheikh, 41, said he was accompanying an Italian group of cruise tourists and had just stepped off a bus when an armed young man in plain clothes started firing randomly with his automatic weapon.
22The man ran to the museum.
23"This is a tragedy, a catastrophe for Tunisia," he added.
24"What they have done is cowardly."
25Tunisia, the northernmost African nation, has struggled with militants since a revolution ousted President Zine El Abidine Ben Ali in 2011.
26Since then, there have been assassinations of liberal, secular politicians and attacks on tourist haunts.
27"Many are taking opportunities to undermine our homeland," Essid said.
28"We will act relentlessly, without mercy."

72Thousands of Tunisians have joined the Islamic State militants in Syria and Iraq and many have returned home, the government has said.
73The Bardo Museum, which dates to the 15th century, chronicles Tunisia's history and includes one of the world's largest collections of Roman mosaics.
74It is adjacent to the country's parliament in the capital of Tunis.
75Tunisia gained independence from France in 1956, and most of its 11 million people speak the language.
76Tunisia adopted a constitution in 2014 that guaranteed rights for women and mandated that the president's religion be Islam.
77"It is not by chance that today's terrorism affects a country that represents hope for the Arab world," French Foreign Minister Laurent Fabius said in a statement.
78"The hope for peace, the hope for stability, the hope for democracy. This hope must live."
79Paul Salem, vice president for policy and research at the Middle East Institute in Washington, said Tunisia has had a radicalization problem since the Arab uprising.
80The largest foreign group of radical fighters in Syria are from Tunisia, he said.
81That's partly because parts of Tunisia have become radicalized - and because of geography, Salem said.
82"Libya is next door. There are a lot of training camps for Tunisians to go to and then move on to Syria," he said.
83Even so, Tunisia has set an example for its neighbors that also saw revolutions.
84Syria, Libya and Yemen are disintegrating into lawlessness and terrorism, and Egyptians have seen their freedoms curtailed and terrorism surge under yet another military-backed regime.
85Tunisia has long attracted Europeans in large numbers because of its beaches, mountains, the Sahara and its antiquities.
86Before 2011, 12% of the working population worked in the sector, which contributed about 7% to GDP.
87But tourism declined sharply since then and has only partially recovered, according to the Ministry of Tourism.
88Meanwhile, residents here were in shock over the attack.
89"I feel sad about what happened to the victims, I feel sorry for this country," said Walid Ben Salah, 34, a lawyer in Tunis.
90"Tunisia deserves better than this! Who did this today has no relation with Islam."
