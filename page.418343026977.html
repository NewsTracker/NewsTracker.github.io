Greece races to restart talks with skeptical creditors

1Greece's Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras, center, leaves after a meeting with Greek political party leaders at the Presidential Palace as Minister of State Nikos Pappas, left, and Government spokesman Gabriel Sakellaridis, right, follow him in Athens, Monday, July 6, 2015.
2Greece and its membership in Europe's joint currency faced an uncertain future Monday, with the country under pressure to reach a bailout deal with creditors as soon as possible after Greeks resoundingly rejected the notion of more austerity in exchange for aid.
3BRUSSELS (AP) - With his country struggling to stave off financial collapse, Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras will try to convince European creditors to agree on a new bailout program when they meet Tuesday for an emergency eurozone summit.
4Tsipras is set to offer a new plan of economic measures to creditors that he hopes will restart negotiations on getting new loans for his country.
5The situation is urgent - without a deal, Greece's banks could go bust within days, the first step in the country's potential exit from the euro currency union.
6Greece's financial distress became more acute late Monday when the European Central Bank refused to increase assistance for Greek banks, which are not due to reopen until Thursday.
7A hastily arranged meeting of eurozone finance ministers, which will be Euclid Tsakalotos' first as Greek finance minister, is slated for the afternoon in Brussels.
8A full summit of leaders will then follow.
9European officials were cautious about the prospects for progress.
10Jean-Claude Juncker, the president of the European Union's executive Commission, indicated a comprehensive deal for Greece was unlikely to be reached Tuesday.
11"Were we to come up with a solution today, it would be an overly simplistic solution," he told European lawmakers ahead of the meetings.
12"What we're going to do today is talk to each other, understand each other, show tolerance to each other and restore order to the situation."
13The eurozone's top official, Jeroen Dijsselbloem, said that the pressure was on Greece to present an acceptable deal, since its position was increasingly dire financially.
14"The Greek government has a major interest in coming forward quickly with serious and credible proposals," said Dijsselbloem, who is the Dutch finance minister and chairs meetings with his eurozone counterparts.
15The upcoming talks follow Tsipras' bigger-than-expected win in Sunday's bailout referendum, when 61 percent of Greek voters rejected measures creditors had proposed last week in exchange for loans.
16In a sign he may be willing to compromise, Tsipras appointed a new finance minister to lead talks with creditors.
17Tsakalotos, a 55-year-old economist, has replaced Yanis Varoufakis, who constantly clashed with his peers.
18"I won't hide from you that I am very nervous and very anxious," Tsakalotos said after being sworn in Monday.
19"I am not taking over at the easiest moment in Greek history."
20Greek banks are running out of cash even after the government shut them last week and placed limits on how much depositors can withdraw or transfer.
21Normal commerce is now impossible in Greece.
22Small businesses, lacking use of credit cards or money from bank accounts, were left to rely on cash coming from diminishing purchases from customers.
23But Greeks are holding tightly onto what they have.
24And suppliers are demanding that businesses pay cash up front.
25Giorgos Kafkaris, a 77-year-old pensioner, was among Greeks standing in line to withdraw cash at an Athens ATM on Tuesday.
26The euro was down 0.7 percent ahead of Tuesday's meetings, though stock markets were relatively stable in Europe.
27Greece's stock market remains closed since last week amid the bank closures.
28Tsipras has previously indicated he's willing to raise taxes and cut some spending provided the country gets some debt relief.
29European officials are split on the issue of whether to grant Greece easier debt repayment terms - with lead eurozone lender Germany still reluctant.
