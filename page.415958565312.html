Shock, defiance in Kenya after Shebab massacre 148 at university

1The day-long siege of Garissa University in which 148 people, mostly students, were killed was Kenya's deadliest attack since the 1998 bombing of the US embassy in Nairobi, and the bloodiest ever by the Al-Qaeda-affiliated militants.
2The Kenyan government, however, vowed that it would not be "intimidated".
3Survivors recounted how the masked gunmen taunted students before kiling them, including forcing them to phone their parents to urge them to call for Kenyan troops to leave Somalia -- before shooting them anyway.
4As the gunmen prowled university rooms hunting down more people to kill, some students smeared blood from their dead friends over their bodies to pretend they too had been shot.
5"There were bodies everywhere in execution lines, we saw people whose heads had been blown off, bullet wounds everywhere, it was a grisly mess," said Reuben Nyaora, an aid worker who helped the wounded.
6Others appeared to have been killed by knives.
7The day-long seige ended with four gunmen being killed in a hail of heavy gunfire, and one suspect reportedly arrested.
8At least 79 people were also wounded in the attack on the campus, which lies near the border with Somalia.
9On Friday, a huge crowd of shocked and traumatised survivors and relatives of those killed or missing gathered at the university gate.
10"I am so worried, I had a son who was among the students trapped inside the college, and since yesterday I have heard nothing," said Habel Mutinda, an elderly man, his face streaming with tears.
11"I tried to identify his body among those killed... I have to do that before the body goes bad in the heat."
12Emergency workers collected the bodies left where they were killed, while Kenyan soldiers patrolled the campus.
13Visiting the scene of the carnage, Kenya's Interior Minister Joseph Nkaissery vowed his country would not give in to the Shebab, who despite losing territory inside Somalia have stepped up operations in Kenya.
14"Kenya's government will not be intimidated by the terrorists who have made killing innocent people a way to humiliate the government," he told reporters, promising that the government would "fight back".
15"We shall win this war against our enemies."
16Hurling grenades and firing automatic rifles, the gunmen stormed the university at dawn on Thursday as students were sleeping, shooting dead dozens before setting Muslims free and holding Christians and others hostage.
17Pope Francis on Friday condemned the massacre as an act of "senseless brutality."
18Just before darkness fell, Kenyan troops moved in on the dormitory where the gunmen were holed up, apparently determined to prevent a drawn-out siege like that seen in the Westgate shopping mall in September 2013.
19Maureen Manyengo, a 21-year-old education student from western Kenya, said she hid inside her wardrobe after seeing several friends killed.
20"I could hear the attackers telling my friends, 'Do not worry, we will kill you, but we will die too'," she recalled, saying the attackers spoke in Kenya's Swahili language, not Somali.
21"I could also hear them saying 'You will only be safe the day your president removes the soldiers from Somalia'."
22Gunmen also warned students it was "only the start" of such attacks.
23Several buses took traumatised students from the university -- now ordered to close permanently -- back to their home areas, as the bodies of those killed were flown to the capital.
24Scores of weeping family members gathered Friday at the main Nairobi mortuary to identify their relatives, where a line of the dead students were laid out.
25The university siege marks the worst attack on Kenyan soil since the 1998 bombing of the US embassy in Nairobi by Al-Qaeda, when 213 people were killed by a huge truck bomb.
26The Shebab also carried out the Westgate shopping mall massacre in Nairobi in September 2013 when four gunmen killed 67 people in a four-day siege.
27Shehab spokesman Sheikh Ali Mohamud Rage said the killings were in revenge for the presence of Kenyan troops in Somalia as part of the African Union's force supporting the internationally-backed government in Mogadishu.
28"Kenya is at war with Somalia," Rage said.
29A $215,000 (200,000 euro) bounty was offered for the capture of alleged Shebab commander Mohamed Mohamud, a former Kenyan teacher believed to now be in Somalia and said to be the mastermind behind the Garissa attack.
30Newspapers on Friday were critical that intelligence warnings had been missed.
31"The attack was preceded by a number of intelligence alarm bells," The Star newspaper said.
32But newspapers also called for national unity in the wake of the killings.
33"We must once again realise what the enemy wants to trigger," The Standard's editorial read.
34"They want an internal war in Kenya."
