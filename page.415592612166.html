A delayed emergency and challenges for reform: 5 key findings from AP's story on WHO and Ebola

1GENEVA - In the aftermath of the world's biggest outbreak of Ebola, the World Health Organization acknowledged it was too slow to act, blaming factors including a lack of real-time information and the unprecedented nature of the epidemic.
2But an investigation by The Associated Press has revealed the U.N. health agency knew from the start how unusual the outbreak was.
3WHO officials privately floated the idea of declaring an international health emergency in early June, more than a month before the agency maintains it got its first sign the outbreak merited one - in late July - and two months before the declaration was finally made on August 8, 2014.
4WHO blamed its slow response partly on a lack of real-time information and the surprising characteristics of the epidemic.
5In fact it had accurate field reports - including scientists asking for backup - and it identified the unprecedented features of the outbreak.
6The agency was also hobbled by a shortage of funds and a lack of clear leadership over its country and regional offices.
7Politics appear to have clouded WHO's willingness to declare an international emergency.
8Internal emails and documents suggest the U.N. health agency was afraid of provoking conflict with the Ebola-stricken countries and wary that a declaration could interfere with the economy and the Muslim pilgrimage to Mecca.
9An Ebola-infected WHO consultant in Sierra Leone violated WHO health protocols, creating a rift with Doctors Without Borders that was only resolved when WHO was thrown out of a shared hotel.
