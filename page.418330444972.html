It breaks my heart to see Greece grow so impoverished

1I was born in Athens 48 years ago, but left the country at the age of 17 to study and build my businesses abroad.
2Recently, on one of my trips back to the Greek capital, I got a rude shock.
3The contrast between the land I had left and the Greece of today is heart-wrenching.
4The fallout is clear to see.
5When the banking system collapsed in Cyprus, where both my parents were born, my philanthropic foundation set up a “Food from the Heart” initiative, handing out a free lunchtime snack to the needy in Nicosia and Limassol.
6The idea I would soon be contemplating a similar project for the vastly bigger and more prosperous population of Athens barely entered my head.
7Yet, by last month, we were handing out around 2,500 snacks every day in the Greek capital.
8A sandwich or croissant might not sound a lot, but for some people it’s vital.
9Soon our daily handouts may rise to more than 3,000.
10I am already working to open another two or three centres in Greece to do my bit to help.
11Greece needs our help now.
12I offer no panaceas – indeed, it would be wrong for someone who has spent most of his adult life outside the country to start lecturing Greece and her leaders on the way ahead.
13For the moment, however, the food queues remain.
14And those queues don’t just contain the unemployed or homeless.
15Today it is shop workers, clerks and even professionals who line up – all facing the grim prospect of being without work for the foreseeable future.
16For them the optimism of 1981 has gone.
17No one knows when or how it will return.
