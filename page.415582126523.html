1The cyclone made landfall between Cape Melville and Cape Flattery at about 4am (AEST).
2The very destructive core of the cyclone, which is packing wind gusts of up to 230km/h, is moving slightly further north than what was expected.
3Less powerful but still destructive winds, with gusts to 150 km/h, are currently affecting coastal and island areas between Cape Melville and Cooktown, and will extend to adjacent inland areas later on Friday morning.
4Gales with gusts up to 120km/h are being experienced 130km out from the centre of the cyclone in coastal areas between Coen and Cape Tribulation.
5These winds will extend to inland areas including Laura and Palmerville, and north to Lockhart River, later on Friday morning.
6The Cook Shire, which takes in Cooktown, just south of Cape Flattery, has tweeted that water services are cut but the power seems to be holding out in the town.
7Earlier Mayor Peter Scott said the town had done all it could to prepare, and that Ergon Energy had deployed generators to restore power if it went down.
8"We've got a lot of people on the ground that can support people now, or after the event," he told the ABC.
9A resident of Poison Creek, who lives west of Cooktown, said power had been cut there.
10"We're really copping it now. It's really blowing and raining really hard at the moment," the man, identified only as Peter, told the ABC.
11About 50 residents, including Rouel Saga and her two-year-old daughter, are riding out the savage storm in Cooktown's cyclone shelter.

17The Bureau of Meteorology has warned the sea is likely to rise steadily up to a level well above the normal tide, with damaging waves and flooding of some low-lying areas, which could also extend some way inland.
18Heavy rainfall that could cause flash flooding is also occurring in coastal and adjacent inland between Coen and Cape Tribulation, and will extend west across Cape York Peninsula with the passage of the cyclone.
19Queensland Fire and Emergency Services far north regional director Wayne Coutts says residents must remain indoors until they are advised by authorities that it's safe to go outside.
20"There are likely to be a number of hazards, including fallen trees, power lines, debris and even possible structural damage to infrastructure, and it is vital people do not explore collapsed buildings or go sightseeing," he said.
1According to a new study, though, strangers do notice the ears but don't make negative personality judgments based on them.
2It was very surprising to find no such bias, according to senior author Dr. Abel-Jan Tasman of the Cantonal Hospital in St. Gallen, Switzerland.
3"Looking closer, though, we realized that the 'bad cases' with the obviously most protruding ears, the longest visual dwell time on the ears and the largest difference in dwell times between uncorrected and corrected auricles all looked cute and smart in their own way," he told Reuters Health by email.
4Children develop a sharpened awareness of small differences between themselves and others, and bullying for different looks begins after the age of five or six, Tasman said.
5Parents' fear that protruding ears lead others to make conscious or unconscious assumptions about personality is often behind decisions to alter a child's ears surgically, Tasman and colleagues write in JAMA Facial Plastic Surgery.
6To see how much people really notice protruding ears and if seeing them triggers any biased assumptions about personality, the surgeons had 20 adult volunteers look at pictures of 20 children aged five to 19.
7All the children's parents had requested corrective plastic surgery for protruding ears.
8The researchers also digitally altered the same pictures to make the ears sit closer to the head, and showed the images to the volunteers.
9The observers rated the kids in all the images on 10 personality traits by scoring them on a scale of one to 10 representing pairs of opposite extremes, such as friendly-unfriendly, creative-uninspired and honest-dishonest.
10People did spend more time looking at ears that stick out compared to digitally altered ears.
11But they rated the kids equally assiduous, intelligent and likeable regardless of ear type.
12In fact, when people spent the most time focusing on protruding ears in the unaltered photographs, and those images scored higher on assiduousness, likeability and intelligence than the doctored images.
13Four of the five kids with the most protruding ears actually scored lower on the personality scale with corrected images.
14"In these, the protruding ears may have added to their cuteness," Tasman said.
15Deviation from socially accepted "norms" in appearance leads to penalties in perceived attractiveness, according to Dr. Lisa E. Ishii of Johns Hopkins School of Medicine in Baltimore, who was not involved in the study.
16In her own research, Ishii told Reuters Health by email, "we showed that a crooked nose, independent of other facial changes, caused an attractiveness penalty that was restored with straightening the nose."
17She was surprised that there seemed to be no such bias for kids with protruding ears, at least for personality traits, although the authors did not strictly assess attractiveness.
18"However, we have not performed experiments with photos of children, and the way adults perceive children with 'abnormalities' is probably different than how they perceive other adults with the same abnormalities," she said.
19"It is also probably different than how children view other children with same."
20Looking at pictures of faces on a monitor may not be the same as looking at a person in real life, the study authors note.
21This was a small pilot study, not large enough to draw general conclusions, said Ishii, who wrote an accompanying editorial in the journal.
22Cultural context is important as well, Tasman said.
23In western cultures 'dumbo ears' may look funny to some, but in some eastern cultures large ears are considered a sign of vitality and longevity, he said.
24In Switzerland, cosmetic surgery to correct protruding ears is one of the most common facial plastic surgeries for children, he said.
25"The procedure has a very high social acceptance in western Europe, to the point of most health insurances in Switzerland (and probably elsewhere) covering part of the costs, when they should not, strictly speaking, cover costs of purely esthetic procedures," Tasman said.
