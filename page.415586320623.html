Exclusive: Despite Hillary Clinton promise, charity did not disclose donors

Review: 'Insurgent' carries on 'Divergent' novels

1NEW YORK (Reuters) - In 2008, Hillary Clinton promised Barack Obama, the president-elect, there would be no mystery about who was giving money to her family's globe-circling charities.
2She made a pledge to publish all the donors on an annual basis to ease concerns that as secretary of state she could be vulnerable to accusations of foreign influence.
3At the outset, the Clinton Foundation did indeed publish what they said was a complete list of the names of more than 200,000 donors and has continued to update it.
4But in a breach of the pledge, the charity's flagship health program, which spends more than all of the other foundation initiatives put together, stopped making the annual disclosure in 2010, Reuters has found.
5In response to questions from Reuters, officials at the Clinton Health Access Initiative (CHAI) and the foundation confirmed no complete list of donors to the Clintons' charities has been published since 2010.
6CHAI was spun off as a separate legal entity that year, but the officials acknowledged it still remains subject to the same disclosure agreement as the foundation.
7The finding could renew scrutiny of Clinton's promises of transparency as she prepares to launch her widely expected bid for the White House in the coming weeks.
8Political opponents and transparency groups have criticized her in recent weeks for her decision first to use a private email address while she was secretary of state and then to delete thousands of emails she labeled private.
9CHAI, which is best known for helping to reduce the cost of drugs for people with HIV in the developing world, published a partial donor list for the first time only this year.
10CHAI should have published the names during 2010-2013, when Clinton was in office, CHAI spokeswoman Maura Daley acknowledged this week.
11"Not doing so was an oversight which we made up for this year," she told Reuters in an email when asked why it had not published any donor lists until a few weeks ago.
12A spokesman for Hillary Clinton declined to comment.
13Former President Bill Clinton, who also signed on to the agreement with the Obama administration, was traveling and could not be reached for comment, his spokesman said.
14The Reuters inquiries also raised questions about a second assurance Hillary Clinton made to the Obama administration: that the State Department would be able to review any new or increased contributions to CHAI by foreign governments while she served as the nation's top diplomat.
15The Clintons said the pledge was intended to defuse accusations that foreign governments might use such donations to earn favors.
16By the time Clinton left office in February 2013, the charity had received millions of dollars (Graphic: reut.rs/1Lvua8z) in new or increased payments from at least seven foreign governments.
17Five of the governments came on board during her tenure as secretary of state while two doubled or tripled their support in that time, according to data provided by CHAI spokeswoman Daley.
18The State Department said it was unable to cite any instances of its officials reviewing or approving new money from any foreign governments.
19Daley confirmed that none of the seven government donations had been submitted to the State Department for review.
20One instance was an admitted oversight, Daley said: CHAI should have told the State Department before accepting donations totaling $340,000 from Switzerland's Agency for Development and Cooperation in 2011 and 2012.
21However, it did not believe U.S. authorities needed to review the other six governments, including Britain and Australia, she said, citing various reasons.
22Hillary Clinton told the Senate during her confirmation hearing in January, 2009, that the disclosures she and her husband agreed to were "very unprecedented."
23At that time, she did not dispute the Senate Foreign Relations Committee's concerns that utter transparency was necessary to protect the integrity of the United States' diplomacy and foreign policy.
24BRITAIN, AUSTRALIA BOOSTED DONATIONS Since it was published in 2008, the foundation's online donor list has been updated annually, naming everyone from individuals giving a few dollars to governments awarding eight-figure grants, the foundation said.
25It has been pored over by interest groups, the Clintons' political opponents, and the media.
26The foundation list has not included those who donated just to CHAI since the initiative was spun off in 2010, foundation spokesman Craig Minassian said.
27Minassian said the foundation believed CHAI continued to be bound by the agreement with the Obama administration.
28He did not elaborate on why CHAI did not honor it and referred inquiries to Daley.
29In the donor list that CHAI published this year, for the first time since its spin-off from the Clinton Foundation, not all donors were identified.
30Many were grouped together as "Individual Donations", which cumulatively came to less than $1 million.
31Those donations were small and so "did not warrant posting," said Daley.
32In 2008, the Clintons agreed that existing government contributors that wanted to "materially increase" their commitments during Hillary Clinton's tenure would be reviewed by State.
33Australia almost doubled its support between 2009-2012, to $12.2 million, while the United Kingdom nearly tripled its support, to $11.2 million.
34CHAI did not report these increases to the State Department because the new money was for "expansions of existing programs," Daley said.
35Daley also provided a number of explanations for why other governments that appeared on a donor list provided to Reuters did not need to be reviewed by the State Department.
36Swaziland and Papua New Guinea, which gave small grants for AIDS programs, were not submitted for review because the money they gave originated from other sources, including existing donor Australia and the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, she said.
37In the case of Sweden, its International Development Cooperation Agency has given CHAI $7.2 million since 2012 to train health workers in Zambia, but nothing in at least the previous three years.
38This did not need State review because Sweden had given to the foundation prior to 2009, Daley said.
39Rwanda, which CHAI listed as a donor, gave the charity $200,000 in 2012.
40CHAI considered this a fee for medical work it did in the country, not a grant or donation, and so did not tell the State Department about it, Daley said.
41Money from all of these governments amounted to about 1 percent of CHAI's total budget, she said.
42The White House declined to answer questions about whether the Obama administration was aware of CHAI not disclosing its donors or submitting new donations from foreign governments.
43White House spokeswoman Jennifer Friedman noted, however, that the agreement the Clintons entered into "went above and beyond standard ethics requirements."

5At this point, the adaptations of Veronica Roth's adolescent "Divergent" novels and Suzanne Collins' "Hunger Games" are near-duplicates in all but the smallest details.
6Oppressive governments forced the heroines of each series to enter a homogeneous social cult.
7(Tris hardly fits as she's a "Divergent," a rare mixed breed with equal amounts of intelligence, bravery, compassion, honesty and sacrifice.)
8Each is a defiant girl who has broken free from her bloc, joining a fearsome rebellion against the ruling class.
9Both fight a bit through arguments and a whole lot with science fiction weaponry.
10Each pursues romance with someone facing difficulties like their own.
11At least Tris (played by the eternally watchable Shailene Woodley) gets hunkier eye candy and hotter kisses.
12When the fighting pauses, she and warlike love interest Four (Theo James) cross into more interesting territory.

16To many teenage viewers, their kissing and cuddling will top the series' repeated views of its characters jumping aboard speeding trains.

19In this chapter, totalitarian leader Jeanine Matthews (Kate Winslet, meaner than the Nazi war criminal she played in "The Reader") aims to force Tris to open an impenetrable trunk concealing information about their society's future.
20In her second outing as Tris, Woodley proves she can work her way through plausible fight scenes yet maintain control of the familiar character.
21At 23, she passes perfectly for a 17- or 18-year-old, not just with her porcelain features, but through a sense of emotional sincerity most futuristic kid-oriented genre films lack.
22Whether she is hiding peacefully alongside Amish-style vegetarians at the beginning, feeling heartbreaking personal loss, or falling to earth from rocket-scarred Chicago skyscrapers, she stays sharply focused.
23Director Robert Schwentke delivers all the story's loud, heavy, glass-smashing fight scenes with the force of a gut-slammed buzz saw.
24Woodley's plentiful action scenes grow until near the climax she leaps through giant windows and across the skyline like Spider-Man's big sister.
25Despite such ludicrous moments, she helps give the film a fine artistic sheen.
26She's not the only skilled actress in the cast.
27Naomi Watts steps in as a dangerous frenemy, but Woodley is hard to steal a scene from.
28In long, handsomely framed close-ups Schwentke recognizes her ability to raise the bar in every shot, translating random dialogue into tidbits of poetry.
29Whether she's furious for revenge or pensively melancholy about her late family's suffering, you feel for her, too.
30Woodley understands how to be heroic and tough at the same time.
