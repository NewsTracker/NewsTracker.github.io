10Vaughn plays Dan, a St. Louis sales exec who quits his job toiling for a bullying boss named Chuck (Sienna Miller, gamely overdoing a brassy American accent) and starts his own company with two fellow workplace outcasts: retirement-age Timothy (Tom Wilkinson) and sweet-natured but slow-witted Mike (Dave Franco, you-know-who's little brother).
11The unlikely trio travels first to Maine, then to Europe in an effort to beat out Chuck for a lucrative deal with a firm fronted by Jim Spinch (James Marsden, smarming it up).
12Needless to say chaos ensues.
13But Unfinished Business never works up enough momentum to get us into the anarchic spirit of things.
14The movie unfolds, choppily, as a series of half-hearted set pieces written and directed with little flair or commitment and no connective tissue between them; some of those sequences scarcely run long enough to register, as if the studio couldn't decide whether or not they were worth keeping in the final cut.
15Like The Wedding Ringer, another mediocre male-bonding flick released during the early-2015 dump months, Unfinished Business goes through the motions, offering up ostensibly outrageous sights and situations - a hotel maid who's actually a sex worker; Mike's quest to master a certain coital position; a professional negotiation hashed out amid the glory holes of a gay club - either so derivative or so listlessly staged as to barely warrant a raised eyebrow.
16When it's not indulging in lowbrow sex humor (not a bad thing in itself, mind you), the script, courtesy of Steven Conrad (The Pursuit of Happyness), tosses around some very lame jokes - sometimes repeatedly.
17Much is made, for instance, of the fact that a main character's last name is Pancake; are you laughing yet?
18Even one of the movie's more gently amusing bits, Mike's serial mispronunciation of words like "exploit" and "imperative," is run into the ground.
19Vaughn's work here might be best described as functional - he does a very slight variation on the same persona he's been playing for years: the brash guy with a heart of gold.
20As appealing and assured a comic performer as he is, the actor hasn't stretched or challenged himself in a long time; Unfinished Business makes one hope, more urgently than ever, that he has something else up his sleeve on the next season of True Detective.
21Meanwhile, Wilkinson's role consists essentially of uttering words like "pussy," "titty" and "cock," as if the prospect of an actor of a certain age - and a Brit, at that!
22- cursing is all the comedy anyone needs.
23If the movie has a bright spot, it's Franco.
24Speaking in stoner-surfer cadences, his face regularly expanding into an infectiously goofy grin, the actor is the one person onscreen who seems determined to cobble together what little he's given into a distinctive character.
25As a director, Scott is workmanlike though uninspired, displaying little visual imagination and even less sense of risk.
26Undemanding audiences may be satisfied, but Unfinished Business is the cinematic equivalent of subpar fast food (think Carl's Jr. or Jack in the Box): It's cheap, easy and maybe even tasty for a second or two, but leaves you feeling queasy and undernourished.
27In other words, take your business elsewhere.
