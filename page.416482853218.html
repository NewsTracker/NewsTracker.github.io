Greece bailout deal elusive, 'significant' more work needed

1A man watches the city of Athens at Filopalous hill, on Friday, April 24, 2015.
2Time is running out for Greece to reach a deal with bailout creditors and prevent the country going bankrupt, the eurozone's top official said Friday as he headed into discussions with his colleagues from the 19-country single currency zone.
3RIGA, Latvia (AP) - Greece remains at loggerheads with its European creditors on an economic reform program required to unlock money the country will need to avoid a possible default in coming months and even a ruinous exit from the euro.
4At a meeting Friday in the Latvian capital of Riga, Greece's finance minister came under fire from his peers in the 19-country eurozone for failing to come up with a comprehensive list of economic reforms after weeks of slow progress.
5For some normally restrained finance ministers, it was time to express discontent at what they perceive to be the delaying tactics on the part of the new left-wing Greek government, which came to power just under three months ago.
6"It was a very critical discussion," said Jeroen Dijsselbloem, the eurozone's top official, after the meeting.
7Others spoke of being "tired" and "annoyed" with the way the talks are going.
8Just two months ago, Greece secured an agreement from the eurozone to get the remaining money in its bailout fund - 7.2 billion euros ($7.7 billion) - but only if it came up with a mutually agreed set of reforms.
9An end-of-April deadline was considered achievable and markets breathed a sigh of relief that the threat of bankruptcy and a Greek exit from the euro had been averted.
10But with days to go until that self-imposed deadline, Athens hasn't even presented a full list as it haggles with representatives from the European Commission, the European Central Bank and the International Monetary Fund.
11European officials are now talking of a new deadline of the end of June, when the European part of Greece's bailout program officially ends.
12How the country can continue to pay wages and salaries and meet its debt commitments, notably to the IMF, in that period remains sketchy.
13The decision this week by the Greek government to scrape together spare cash from municipalities and state enterprises like hospitals and the national gallery is likely to buy it some time.
14Dijsselbloem conceded that there had been a step-change in Athens' appetite for discussion over the past few days but that "significantly more progress" was required from Athens.
15He spoke of "wide differences" between the sides, without specifying where the problems lay.
16He also ruled out that the creditors might consider a half-way deal that could give Greece part of the pending rescue loans.
17Greece's Varoufakis sought to portray the discussions in a more positive light.
18"We look at the last few weeks and what we see is convergence," he said, noting progress on issues such as privatization, reforming the tax system, the judiciary, the bureaucracy and product markets.
19A deal will happen, he added, "and will happen quickly as it's the only option we have."
20Varoufakis said the main sticking points related to pensions and the level of the budget surplus Athens has to post after debt and interest payments are stripped out - a higher level means less money would effectively mean the government has less money spending on its priorities.
21"This is a European family that needs to work out its difference in a collegiate manner," said Varoufakis.
22All sides agree that the clock is ticking - a notion expressed repeatedly by officials at the meeting here.
23The next possible date for a deal could be May 11, when eurozone finance ministers will meet next and just one day before Greece owes a big payment to the IMF.
24Greece has relied on 240 billion euros-worth of bailout funds since May 2010 after it was effectively locked out of international bond markets when investors thought a default was on the cards.
