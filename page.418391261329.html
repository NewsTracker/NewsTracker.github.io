Greece rushing to finalize reforms and remain in euro

1Pensioners wait outside the main gate of the national bank of Greece to withdraw a maximum of 120 euros ($134) in central Athens, Thursday, July 9, 2015.
2With a deadline just hours away to come up with a detailed economic reform plan, Greece requested a new three-year rescue from its European partners Wednesday as signs grew its economy was sliding toward free-fall without an urgently needed bailout.
3A police officer stands as pensioners wait outside the main gate of the national bank of Greece to withdraw a maximum of 120 euros ($134) in central Athens, Thursday, July 9, 2015.
4Pensioners line up outside the main gate of the national bank of Greece to withdraw a maximum of 120 euros ($134) in central Athens, Thursday, July 9, 2015.
5Greece's government was racing Thursday to finalize a plan of reforms for its third bailout, hoping this time the proposal will meet with approval from its European partners and stave off a potentially catastrophic exit from Europe's joint currency, the euro, within days.
6Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras was meeting with finance ministry officials a day after the government requested a new three-year aid program from Europe's bailout fund and promised to immediately enact reforms, including to taxes and pensions, in return.
7Details of Greece's reforms are to be submitted Thursday, giving time for creditors to review them ahead of a summit of the European Union's 28 members set for Sunday.
8Thursday is "a decisive day" for Europe, EU Economics Commissioner Pierre Moscovici said on France-Inter radio, adding he was hopeful a new Greek bailout deal was possible, in exchange for "concrete, complete" reform proposals.
9"I have the sense that the dialogue is established, or restored, and that there is a way out," Moscovici said.
10Failure to reach a deal could be the first step toward Greece leaving the shared euro currency.
11The last-minute negotiations come amid bank closures in Greece, where capital controls have been imposed that restrict Greeks to cash withdrawals of 60 euros ($67) per day.
12The closures have been extended through Monday.
13Pensioners without bank cards have been particularly hard hit as they have struggled to access their accounts.
14Certain bank branches opened last week to allow them to withdraw a weekly allowance of 120 euros each.
15The government announced Wednesday that this was being renewed so they could withdraw the same sum.
16Hundreds of elderly Greeks lined up outside banks Thursday morning.
17If Tsipras does not get a deal, Greece faces an almost inevitable collapse of the banking system, which would be the first step for the country to fall out of the euro.
18"I believe he will have to get an agreement. We will pay dearly for it, but at least we'll get an agreement," said mechanic Pantelis Niarchos, walking down the street in central Athens.
19After months of fruitless negotiations with Tsipras' government, elected in January on promises to repeal bailout austerity, the skeptical eurozone creditor states have said they want to see a detailed, cost-accounted plan of the reforms.
20But Greece's major creditors disagree on key aspects of how to deal with the struggling but defiant EU member.
21International Monetary Fund chief Christine Lagarde reiterated Wednesday that Greece's massive debt would need restructuring, something that Germany - Greece's largest European lender - has resisted.
22Speaking in Washington, Lagarde said Greece needed to continue cost-cutting reforms, but added: "The other leg is debt restructuring, which we believe is needed ... for debt sustainability."
23"It well may be that the numbers may have to be revisited, but our analysis has not changed," she said of the need for granting Greece better repayment terms.
24U.S. Treasury Secretary Jack Lew added pressure on the European lenders, arguing debt relief was needed for a deal - and describing a Greek euro-exit as a "geopolitical mistake.
