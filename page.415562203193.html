Op-Ed Contributor: Netanyahu?s Win Is Good for Palestine

Hard-right shift delivers upset election win for Netanyahu

1Then he decided to engage in a bit of fear-mongering against Palestinian citizens of Israel in hopes of driving his supporters to the polls.
2"The right-wing government is in danger," Mr. Netanyahu announced on Election Day.
3"Arab voters are heading to the polling stations in droves."
4But Mr. Netanyahu's victory is actually the best plausible outcome for those seeking to end Israel's occupation.
5Indeed, I, as a Palestinian, breathed a sigh of relief when it became clear that his Likud Party had won the largest number of seats in the Knesset.
6This might seem counterintuitive, but the political dynamics in Israel and internationally mean that another term with Mr. Netanyahu at the helm could actually hasten the end of Israel's apartheid policies.
7The biggest losers in this election were those who made the argument that change could come from within Israel.
8It can't and it won't.
9Israelis have grown very comfortable with the status quo.
10In a country that oversees a military occupation that affects millions of people, the biggest scandals aren't about settlements, civilian deaths or hate crimes but rather mundane things like the price of cottage cheese and whether the prime minister's wife embezzled bottle refunds.
11For Israelis, there's currently little cost to maintaining the occupation and re-electing leaders like Mr. Netanyahu.
12Raising the price of occupation is therefore the only hope of changing Israeli decision making.
13Economic sanctions against South Africa in the 1980s increased its international isolation and put pressure on the apartheid regime to negotiate.
14Once Israelis are forced to decide between perpetual occupation and being accepted in the international community, they may choose a more moderate leader who dismantles settlements and pursues peace, or they may choose to annex rather than relinquish land - provoking a confrontation with America and Europe.
15Either way, change will have to come from the outside.
16The boycott, divestment and sanctions campaign (B.D.S.)
17has thrived while Mr. Netanyahu has led Israel.
18He has become the internationally recognized face of Israeli intransigence, settlement building and brazen disregard for Palestinian human rights.
19But while Mr. Netanyahu has become synonymous with the occupation, he is in many ways a product of it.
20There are also entrenched political and economic interests that benefit from maintaining the status quo.
21By monopolizing West Bank land and natural resources, Israel reaps the benefits of occupation with few costs.
22Settlements are a major state investment, and add both a geographic and political obstacle to peace since settlers play a key role in shaping Israeli politics and their interests cannot be ignored.
23Mr. Netanyahu's style has certainly heightened tensions and harmed relations with Israel's allies.
24He has clashed with President Obama and thumbed his nose at the Democratic Party by helping to make Israel a partisan political issue in America.
25His most recent speech before a joint session of Congress, which 60 members of Congress boycotted, was merely the latest incident.
26Replacing Mr. Netanyahu with his challenger, Isaac Herzog, would have slowed down the B.D.S. movement and halted pressure on Israel by creating the perception of change.
27A new prime minister would have kick-started a new "peace process" based on previous failed models that would inevitably fail again because of a lack of real pressure on Israel to change its deplorable behavior.
28The re-election of Mr. Netanyahu provides clarity.
29Two years ago Secretary of State John Kerry declared that the maximum time left for a two-state solution was two years.
30Mr. Netanyahu officially declared it dead this week in order to drive right-wing voters to the polls.
31The two-state solution, which has seen more funerals than a reverend, exists today only as a talking point for self-interested, craven politicians to hide behind - not as a realistic basis for peace.
32The old land-for-peace model must now be replaced with a rights-for-peace model.
33Palestinians must demand the right to live on their land, but also free movement, equal treatment under the law, due process, voting rights and freedom from discrimination.
34Mr. Netanyahu's re-election has convincingly proved that trusting Israeli voters with the fate of Palestinian rights is disastrous and immoral.
35His government will oppose any constructive change, placing Israel on a collision course with the rest of the world.
36And this collision has never been more necessary.
37The election results will further galvanize the movement seeking to isolate Israel internationally.
38B.D.S. campaigns will grow, and more countries will move toward imposing sanctions to change Israeli behavior.
39In the past few years, a major Dutch pension fund divested large sums from Israeli banks active in the West Bank, and hundreds of millions of dollars have been divested from companies, like G4S and SodaStream, that operate in occupied territory.
40There won't be real change on the ground or at the polls without further pressure on Israel.
41And now, that pressure will increase.
42For this, we have Mr. Netanyahu to thank.
1In the final days of campaigning, Netanyahu abandoned a commitment to negotiate a Palestinian state - the basis of more than two decades of Middle East peacemaking - and promised to go on building settlements on occupied land.
2Such policies defy the core vision of a solution to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict embraced by President Barack Obama and his Republican and Democratic predecessors.
3With nearly all votes counted, Netanyahu's Likud had won 29 or 30 seats in the 120-member Knesset, comfortably defeating the center-left Zionist Union opposition on 24 seats.
4A united list of Israeli Arab parties came third.
5The result was a dramatic and unexpected victory for Netanyahu - the last opinion polls four days before the vote had shown Likud trailing the Zionist Union by four seats.
6Although Netanyahu must still put together a coalition, his victory all but guarantees that Israel's president will give him the first opportunity to form a government, putting him course to become the longest-serving leader in the country's history.
7"I am moved by the responsibility Israel has given me and I appreciate the decision by Israel's citizens to elect me and my friends, against all odds and in the face of powerful forces," Netanyahu said during a traditional thanksgiving visit to Judaism's holy Western Wall in Jerusalem.
8But the promises he made to ultranationalist voters in the final days of the campaign could have wide consequences, including deepening rifts with the United States and Europe and potentially emboldening Palestinians to take unilateral steps toward statehood in the absence of any prospect of talks.
9The White House scolded Netanyahu for abandoning his commitment to negotiate for a Palestinian state and for what it called "divisive" campaign rhetoric toward Israel's minority Arab voters.
10Washington signaled that its deep disagreements with Netanyahu will persist on issues ranging from Middle East peacemaking to Iran nuclear diplomacy.
11Saeb Erekat, chief Palestinian negotiator in peace talks that collapsed last year, lamented "the success of a campaign based on settlements, racism, apartheid and the denial of the fundamental rights of the Palestinian people".
12Likud said Netanyahu intended to form a new government within weeks, with negotiations already under way with the far-right pro-settler Jewish Home party led by Naftali Bennett, the centrist Kulanu party and ultra-Orthodox groups.
13The critical party to get on side will be Kulanu, led by former Likud member and communications minister Moshe Kahlon, who won 10 seats, making him a kingmaker given his ability to side with either Netanyahu or the center-left opposition.
14"The citizens of Israel expect us to quickly put together a leadership that will work for them regarding security, economy and society as we committed to do - and we will do so," Netanyahu said.
15Netanyahu's deputies were already holding preliminary talks with potential coalition partners, Israeli media said.
16President Reuven Rivlin said he would launch consultations with party leaders on Sunday to try and form a new government as soon as possible.
17Isaac Herzog, leader of the Zionist Union, conceded defeat and congratulated Netanyahu.
18Herzog said he would not seek to join a Netanyahu-led government.
19The Tel Aviv stock market was largely unmoved by Netanyahu's victory, with the benchmark Tel Aviv 100 index up marginally at mid-day.
20"The market's indifference to the results apparently stems from its belief that the coalition that will be formed will be more stable than its predecessor," said Idan Azoulay, chief investment officer at the Epsilon brokerage.
21While Likud is the largest party, the process of forming a coalition could be challenging, with potential political partners certain to raise a variety of demands.
22With the backing of right-wing and religious factions, and with Kahlon also on board, Netanyahu could end up with 67 parliamentary seats, a commanding majority.
23If Netanyahu follows through on his pledges it would put him on a collision course with the Obama administration and the European Union, which has been weighing steps including trade measures to sanction Israel for its settlements policy.
24The White House was already angry with him for addressing the U.S. Congress at the invitation of Republican lawmakers in a bid to scupper U.S. nuclear talks with Iran.
25Obama was not quick to congratulate Netanyahu on his victory, and the White House said such a call would be made in the coming days.
26But White House spokesman Josh Earnest was critical of some of Netanyahu's comments in the final days of his campaign.
27Earnest told reporters the administration would evaluate its approach on the Middle East peace process following Netanyahu's statement that there would be no Palestinian state on his watch.
28During much of the campaign, Netanyahu had focused on security issues and the threat from Iran's nuclear program, a message that appeared to gain little traction with voters.
29The Zionist Union emphasized socio-economic issues, including the high cost of living in Israel, and its message had seemed to be generating much more momentum.
30But Netanyahu's move to the right, playing up fears of the spread of Islamist groups, promising no concessions to the Palestinians and raising alarm about growing support for Arab-Israeli parties, looks to have spurred his base into action.
31For the Palestinians, the results are a deep concern, raising the prospect of more settlement expansion on land they want for their own state in the occupied West Bank and East Jerusalem, as well as in Gaza.
32Netanyahu's victory also raises questions about what happens on Iran, with Obama determined to pursue negotiations toward an agreement on Tehran's nuclear program and the Israeli leader determined to foil what he has termed a "very bad deal".
33The Palestinians may quickly create problems for Netanyahu as they join the International Criminal Court on April 1 and intend to pursue war crimes charges against Israel over its occupation of the West Bank and last year's war in Gaza.
