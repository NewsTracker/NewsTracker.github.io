Yahoo hopes to cash in on daily fantasy sports contests

1Exterior view of Yahoo headquarters on Wednesday, Nov. 5, 2014, in Sunnyvale, Calif.
2Yahoo on Wednesday launched daily fantasy sports games, allowing its tens of millions of fantasy sports fans the chance to win cash in daily contests.
3Unlike season-long fantasy leagues, users participate in the daily contests by paying an entry fee (up to $500) and choosing a team from a list of players competing in that day's games.
4Users can also create their own contests and invite friends.
5Yahoo generally takes a 10 percent cut from each entry fee.
6ESPN and Yahoo have long competed for fans playing season-long leagues, where friends pick favorite players and compete against each other for months during baseball or football season.
7In the daily game space, Yahoo will now square off against companies like FanDuel and DraftKings.
8Kenneth Fuchs, a Yahoo vice president, said the opportunity is huge.
9He pointed to research that showed the market for contest entry fees in daily fantasy sports games are expected to reach $18 billion by 2020.
10"The rise of daily is early, but real," Fuchs said at a San Francisco press conference.
11Fuchs says what sets Yahoo's games apart from competitors is the site's in-depth content, including stories and stats on players for a fantasy team.
12Yahoo has a loyal following among fantasy sports players; currently its users spend a whopping 500 million hours a year playing fantasy sports on its site.
13Fuchs declined to say whether the daily fantasy sports games would have a meaningful impact on revenue, stating that Yahoo does not break out those figures.
14But analysts say they believe the games could boost the Sunnyvale company's coffers.
15"It's just a question of how much of that money they are going to be able to capture," said Rob Enderle, with advisory services firm Enderle Group.
16Yahoo has been under a lot of pressure from investors to increase its revenue and profit.
17The company, once a leader in desktop search ads, has struggled in its transition into the mobile space.
18CEO Marissa Mayer has heavily invested in making the company mobile first, by increasing staffing in that area and acquiring startups.
19One of those was Bignoggins Productions, a fantasy sports company that had only one employee but specialized in creating a range of mobile apps for fantasy sports.
20"They really can't afford any failures now," Enderle said.
21"They need to build success on success. They have this to showcase progress."
22Yahoo's daily fantasy sports games are available in 45 states and available on iOS, desktop and mobile Web.
