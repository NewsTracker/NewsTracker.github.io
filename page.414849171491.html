Chelsea condemn fans' racist behaviour on Paris Metro

U.S. to aid African allies in fight against Boko Haram

1The video was filmed before Chelsea's Champions League match against Paris St Germain at the Parc des Princes stadium in Paris on Tuesday evening, according to reports in British media.
2The fans chanted 'Chelsea, Chelsea, Chelsea' moments before the incident and then sang 'we are racist, we're racist, and that's the way we like it'.
3Another passenger, a black woman, then stepped off the train.
4The amateur footage, posted on the website of the Guardian newspaper, shows a black man trying to squeeze onto a busy train at the Richelieu-Drouot metro stop only to be pushed back repeatedly by a group of men.
5The men can be heard chanting "we're racist, we're racist, and that's the way we like it".
6"Such behaviour is abhorrent and has no place in football or society," the club said in a statement.
7"We will support any criminal action against those involved, and should evidence point to involvement of Chelsea season-ticket holders or members of the club, will take the strongest possible action against them, including banning orders."
8The identities of the men in the video, including the victim, have not yet been established, prosecutor's office spokesperson Agnes Thibault-Lecuivre told The AP.
1West African military commanders have long complained that cross-border operations against Islamist groups, from al Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM) in Mali to Boko Haram in Nigeria, have been obstructed by lack of compatible communications equipment, making it hard to swap information and coordinate.
2Major General James Linder said that, as part of the annual U.S.-sponsored "Flintlock" counter-terrorism exercises this year in Chad, the United States would introduce technology allowing African partners to communicate between cellphones, radios and computers.
3The RIOS system would allow soldiers in the field to transmit photos from a remote location in the Sahel immediately to a central command room and can also precisely pin-point the coordinates of personnel, a U.S. military official said.
4Boko Haram killed an estimated 10,000 people last year in its campaign to carve an Islamist emirate from northern Nigeria.
5Amid growing international alarm, the four nations of the Lake Chad region - Chad, Niger, Cameroon and Nigeria - plus neighbouring Benin are preparing a joint task-force of 8,700 men to take on the Sunni jihadist group.
6Chad's military, which played a leading role in a French-led campaign that ousted Islamist groups from northern Mali in 2013, has already led attacks against Boko Haram positions in Nigeria's border regions.
7"The Lake Chad nations are battling Boko Haram and we have a vested interest in that group of nations' collective success ... What Boko Harm is doing is a murderous rampage, about brutality intolerance and subjugation," Linder said in an interview late on Monday.
8"Our national leadership has been very clear that more was going to be done ... There is an ongoing discussion on how will we provide additional tools, techniques, and material to partner nations."
9At the Flintlock exercises, the U.S. military will also be introducing a "cloud-based" technology to allow African allies to quickly share intelligence across borders, such as mapping information on the location of potential targets, Linder said.
10The U.S. assistance in improving communications between regional armies was welcomed by Brigadier General Zakaria Ngobongue, the Chadian director of the Flintlock exercises.
11"We need support in terms of intelligence and logistics, above all in communication because the equipment we have comes from different countries, and their support will make our task much easier," he told Reuters.
12"The Americans have aerial capabilities that we do not have."
13The ninth edition of Flintlock, grouping 1,300 soldiers from 28 African and Western nations, will emphasise the importance of troops fostering strong relations with local communities to gain intelligence on insurgent groups.
14Linder said that African armies were well placed to gather this kind of information, but the United States could share other kinds of intelligence to boost the success of operations against Boko Haram.
15Washington's long-term goal was to enable African nations to be sufficiently trained and equipped to face their own security challenges, Linder said.
16"By 2050, one-third of the global population will be on the African continent," he said.
17"The global economy and the global community need stable countries in Africa and that can only happen through African nation states themselves."
