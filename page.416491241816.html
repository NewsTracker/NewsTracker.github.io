Baltimore mayor praises marchers for peaceful protests over man's death

1BALTIMORE, April 24 (Reuters) - The mayor of Baltimore praised the citizens who organized five days of peaceful protests over the death of a black man in police custody and vowed after meeting with clergy leaders on Friday to find answers in the case.
2The largely black city has seen daily marches and demonstrations since Sunday, when 25-year-old Freddie Gray died of a neck injury sustained when police arrested him a week earlier.
3The incident was the latest in the string of deaths of black men at the hands of police, which have prompted waves of protest across the United States since the deaths last year of Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri, and Eric Garner in New York.
4Unlike in Ferguson, where weeks of peaceful protests were punctuated by several nights of rioting, arson and looting, the protests in Baltimore have seen only a handful of arrests and no major violence.
5This city of 620,000 people will face a major demonstration on Saturday, with police estimating more than 1,000 protesters will gather for a march from the site of Gray's arrest to city hall.
6"I want to commend the members of our community for their commitment for peaceful and respectful protests during this process," Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake said after meeting with local clergy.
7"Our commitment is clear. They demand answers."
8Six Baltimore officers have been suspended with pay over the incident.
9Unlike in Ferguson, where police quickly turned to paramilitary tactics, donning riot helmets and vests and rolling out armored vehicles to confront crowds of protesters, Baltimore police have taken a less confrontational approach, remaining in patrol uniforms and giving protesters a wide berth.
10That low-key response has not been entirely hands-off, though.
11When a skirmish developed between a handful of officers and demonstrators in west Baltimore on Thursday night, scores of police quickly descended on the scene, cordoning off the area and arresting two people.
12Police have also maintained barricades preventing protesters from approaching the domed city hall, and the station house where Gray was taken has been fenced off by barriers.
13Ken Jones, a police spokesman, said Baltimore police had taken lessons from the Ferguson unrest.
14"We don't want to come off heavy-handed, yet," Jones said.
15"I don't think anyone is going to cause a riot."
16Sharon Black, a veteran protest organizer with the leftist People's Power Assembly in Baltimore, said she did not regard the police's posture as low-key, calling the barricades around city hall and the police station heavy-handed.
17But Scott Bolden, a Washington lawyer and former New York prosecutor with expertise in cases involving police, said U.S. police generally had seen the value of officers remaining non-confrontational in the face of protests.
18Police forces across the United States, he said, have "learned how not to handle these issues, based on what we saw in Ferguson."
19Police said Gray fled when officers approached, and they found a switchblade knife in his pocket.
20He was put in a police van to be taken to a station.
21When Gray was taken out, he was unresponsive and taken to a hospital.
22Police have said Gray was not properly buckled into a seat belt inside the van.
23Police have said they will conclude their investigation by May 1 and turn results over to state prosecutors, followed by an independent review.
24The U.S. Department of Justice is conducting a separate probe.
25A wake for Gray is scheduled for Sunday, with his funeral on Monday.
