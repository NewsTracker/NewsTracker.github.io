'Amy' director on Winehouse family reaction: 'Did they expect a One Direction movie?' [Video]

1'Amy' director on Winehouse family reaction: 'Did they expect a One Direction movie?'
2Asif Kapadia had no agenda when he set out to make Amy.
3He just wanted to tell Amy Winehouse's story.
4Kapadia, who grew up in North London, was approached by Universal Music UK honcho David Joseph to make the movie; Joseph is the label executive who announced a few days ago that Amy Winehouse's unreleased demos have been destroyed to prevent a flurry of posthumous subpar releases.
5Joseph described his actions as a "moral thing," possibly the first time that phrase has ever been used in a recording industry story.
6But we digress.
7"Warts 'n' all" is the Amy Winehouse movie Joseph wanted Kapadia to make; the filmmaker had carte blanche from the label and then slowly won the cooperation of everyone he wanted to talk to for Amy.
8The film has had extraordinary success already, although some people (such as Mitch Winehouse) were less enthusiastic when they saw the finished product.
9"What they expected, I don't know," says Kapadia, who visited Toronto to promote Amy.
10"Maybe they thought they were going to get The One Direction movie."
11The award-winning Kapadia came to filmmaking through a route more circuitous than most.
12He is the youngest in a family of five children, "And my parents, by the time they got to me, they just let me do whatever I liked, which was great."
13Nobody else in his family was involved in the creative world, says Kapadia, "And I got into it accidentally."
14Kapadia was studying graphic design when he got a job as a gopher on a movie.
15It was thrilling for him.
16"I thought, 'This is great! So exciting!'" He laughs.
17"I'm carrying boxes in and out of a truck in the rain. And then this bus goes somewhere else, and I go with them -- it's like travelling."
18He elaborates.
19"I come from an ordinary, working class background and my family never went anywhere. And I love travelling. I love to go places and meet people. So film was kind of like my way of escape from a pretty rough part of London, at the time. I was studying and working for other people. Every holiday I'd work for free on films."
20It wasn't even a job, he says.
21"I just enjoyed it. I did the gopher thing, then I was a sound recordist, and I was a camera assistant, and I was being paid to work while I was also studying."
22Kapadia essentially learned filmmaking while pursuing his love of travel.
23"I come from making movies, rather than watching movies," he says.
24His graduate film for the Royal College of Art, a short called the Sheep Thief, is tied to his personal history, says Kapadia.
25He made the film in India.
26"The Sheep Thief was made with street kids," he says, "non-professionals in India. It was really tough, the toughest film I've made probably, and I learned a lot" I got to learn about my heritage while I was creating art.
27That film was really my step into the industry.
28It got me an agent, won an award at Cannes, won quite a few awards around the world," he understates.
29"The designer on that film is now my wife, and we have kids, so it was really a life-changing experience."
30Adds Kapadia, "And everyone said about The Sheep Thief, 'It's not going to work.' And I think there was always something in me, even previous to that, that every film has to be a challenge to myself, pushing myself creatively to the limit. With every movie it's been a bit like that."
31His first feature, The Warrior won a slew of awards; his documentary, Senna, about Brazilian racer Ayrton Senna, changed the face of documentary filmmaking and won Kapadia two BAFTAs, among many other honours.
32Kapadia describes Amy as his "musical," noting that the film is a bit like a Bollywood movie -- because the songs are the narrative, and the songs came first.
33"What we have to do is write the story around the songs. For the first time, the lyrics fit a bigger picture," he says.
34He adds, "I liked her live performances much better than the records. I liked the simplicity of just her and a guitar."
