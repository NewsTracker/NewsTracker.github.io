Who is Greece's new finance minister Euclid Tsakalotos?

1Photo: Newly appointed Greek finance minister Euclid Tsakalotos says he is anxious about the task before him.
2Euclid Tsakalotos, the mild-tempered professor who has been appointed as Greece's new finance minister, is a clear change in style from his combative predecessor Yanis Varoufakis.
3Mr Tsakalotos, 55, studied at prestigious private London school St Paul's and at Oxford University, speaks Greek with a British accent and rarely appears in public, let alone wearing the torso-hugging T-shirts Mr Varoufakis favours.
4But if European officials expect Athens' new finance chief, who has already been a key negotiator in drawn-out meetings between the Greek government and creditors, to take a softer approach in the substance of new talks, they can think again.
5As the brainchild of Syriza's economic thinking, Mr Tsakalotos is likely to redouble efforts to put one of the most contentious issues in the five months of financial aid negotiations between Greece and its creditors - debt relief - back on the table.
6In a news conference after being sworn in, Mr Tsakalotos said he was anxious about the task before him.
7"I cannot hide from you that I am quite nervous. I am not taking on this job at the easiest point in Greek history," he said.
8But the minister, who sat beside his predecessor, said he was keen to restart talks with European partners, in order to act on a decision taken by Greeks in a Sunday referendum to reject previous terms offered by creditors in exchange for aid.
9"We want to continue discussions, to take this mandate given to us by the Greek people [to strive] for something better ... for all these people who have been suffering so much."
10Mr Tsakalotos, who co-authored a book with Greek central bank governor Yannis Stournaras, has been dubbed in leftist jargon a "Revolutionary Europeanist" - an economist who supports European Union integration, but not its capitalist principles.
11Like Mr Varoufakis, Mr Tsakalotos has often decried Europe for big democratic deficiencies and argued that ill-guided fiscal austerity imposed by the core of the eurozone has unnecessarily impoverished Greece and other countries on the periphery.
12"European Monetary Union has created a split between core and periphery, and relations between the two are hierarchical and discriminatory," he wrote in a paper on the website of the Alliance for Workers' Liberty, a British socialist group.
13Born in Rotterdam in the Netherlands, Mr Tsakalotos has a notable international academic curriculum.
14He studied politics, philosophy and economics as an undergraduate at Oxford, where he later completed his PhD in economics in 1989.
15He taught at the University of Kent before returning to Athens as a professor at Athens University of Economics.
16There, nearly a decade ago, he led his students in a months-long protest against a proposed reform of Greece's education system.
17When students occupied classrooms and marched through the streets, Mr Tsakalotos was on the frontlines, recalls Thanos Tsouknidas a 30-year-old accountant who knew him from the time.
18"He was there, involved in the struggle. We were fighting together," Mr Tsouknidas said.
19"He is a pure left-wing individual and professor. His door was always open to all students, to discuss university issues or political issues, or for a normal chat."
20The active role of Mr Tsakalotos, who joined the teachers' union, brought him popularity.
21Classes, including a course in the history of economic theory, were packed, recalls Vassilis Alevromitis, 25, a former student who is now unemployed.
22Some students would giggle at the professor's British accent.
23Mr Tsakalotos dabbled in politics in the teachers' union, drawing close to Syriza, which was growing in popularity on the back of Greece's economic crisis.
24Mr Tsakalotos has pushed Mr Tsipras to ally with other European left-wing groups, including Podemos in Spain and Ireland's Sinn Fein.
25In March, the economist visited Dublin and addressed Sinn Fein political delegates, alongside leader Gerry Adams.
26Mr Tsakalotos has said debt relief for Greece could happen in many forms.
27In an interview with Reuters, the economist suggested Athens be offered debt relief in the form of having the European bailout fund - known as the European Stability Mechanism - take over Greek bonds held by the ECB as one option that would not increase debt for Greece or its partners.
28"There are lots of technical solutions on how that could be done," he said.
29"If there is goodwill, I can think of 10-15 solutions right now of how that could be done. If there's no political goodwill then for any solution I can think of a drawback."
30People who know Mr Tsakalotos say he has strong negotiating qualities, which will come in handy as Athens heads back into talks with creditors.
31"He is the ideal person to help the Greek government break the current deadlock and reach an agreement with international lenders. He is modest, calm, and a very good listener," Panicos Demetriades, a former ECB Governing Council member for Cyprus who knew Mr Tsakalotos in Britain, said.
