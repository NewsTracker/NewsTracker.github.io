Prince debuts song about Baltimore at 'Rally 4 Peace'

1Prince debuts song about Baltimore at 'Rally 4 Peace' - but is the music too light and bubbly?
2Pop-star Prince has debuted a song that he wrote for the city of Baltimore in the wake of Freddie Gray's death.
3Called Baltimore, its lyrics are serious in tone, but the music, in contrast, is bubbly pop-rock.
4The track was released on Saturday and performed live for the first time before thousands of fans on Sunday at a 'Rally 4 Peace' concert at the Royal Farms Arena.
5The crowd were dressed in gray in tribute to a man whose death in police custody prompted riots and drew international attention.
6'For those who have lost loved ones, we're here for you tonight,' Prince told the crowd.
7'Nobody got in nobody's way, so I guess you could say it was a good day - at least a little better than the day in Baltimore,' Prince sung.
8The track also makes reference to last year's Michael Brown shooting.
9Longtime fan Luther Washington, stationed at Ft Meade, shows off his Prince tattoo while waiting for the doors to open for Prince's Baltimore concert Sunday, May 10, 2015.
10It continues: 'Does anybody hear us pray?
11For Michael Brown or Freddie Gray.
12Notes released with the track on SoundCloud explained that it was recorded at the singer's Paisley Park Studios in Minnesota and that the artist played all the instruments himself.
13Prince's set also included his most famous hits.
14Prince, sprightly with elegant dance moves at nearly 57 years old, told the crowd to seek 'peace' as much as 'piece' - a slice of the economic output of their community.
15'I want to stay tonight in a hotel that is owned by one of you,' he told the crowd of thousands.
16At one point, Prince invited onstage Baltimore State's Attorney Marilyn Mosby, who has charged six officers in Gray's death.
17The activism marks a revival of sorts for Prince, who, since his superstardom in the 1980s, has made headlines not only for his music but for his idiosyncrasies such as changing his name to an unpronounceable symbol amid a dispute with his record label.
18Even the tickets for the show - on sale a whole three days in advance - showed a different approach for Prince who in recent years has grown fond of announcing concerts at the last minute.
19But even if he has preferred the seclusion of his Paisley Park estate in Minnesota, Prince has never, from the start of his career, played it safe.
20In an allusion to his early risk-taking, Prince performed the title track of Controversy, his sexually and politically forthright 1981 album, with the opening lines: 'I can't believe all the things the people say / Am I black or white?
21Prince said that proceeds from the concert would go to charity, and authorities readily accommodated the show, with the city's light rail extending its hours.
22The only hint of trouble came not with police but the concert's security, who zealously enforced Prince's ban on photos.
