1- Long after Dan Jones's last flight over Vietnam ended in wreckage and blood, his combat tour was never quite complete.
2In 1968, Mr. Jones, a Marine first lieutenant, was co-piloting a helicopter carrying supplies to troops in battle when an American howitzer accidentally shot the aircraft down.
3Three Marines were killed.
4Lieutenant Jones, the only survivor, was notified while convalescing that the deceased crew members, victims of an embarrassing mishap, would not be awarded Purple Heart medals, which recognize troops wounded or killed in action.
5He was instructed, he said, not to inquire further.
6Forty-seven years on, Mr. Jones's determined journey - from silenced victim of a fratricidal mistake to a veteran vindicated by his insistence that his dead crew be honored - is reaching its end.
7Last year, the Marine Corps reversed previous decisions and approved Purple Hearts for all the Marines who had been aboard the aircraft.
8A spokesman said the family of the one Marine who had not yet been formally recognized, a door gunner who was ejected from the crippled helicopter and fell to his death, would receive the medal as soon as his surviving family members set a date.
9"The Purple Heart is absolutely an appropriate recognition of their courageous service and sacrifice to our nation," the spokesman, Maj.
10Rob Dolan, said of the crew members.
11"This is closing a tragic story from the Vietnam era."
12The corps's about-face points to the continuing evolution in how the Pentagon determines who qualifies for one of the military's most emotionally resonant awards.
13Eligibility questions are often contentious, and rules have repeatedly changed.
14During the recent wars in Afghanistan and Iraq, for example, eligibility was expanded to include victims of a trauma that often does not leave visible wounds: brain injuries caused by explosive blasts.
15And in an interview last month, Brad Carson, the under secretary for the Army, said the services were considering Purple Hearts for troops wounded while clearing improvised bombs made from old chemical weapons.
16This could result in honors for veterans of the Iraq war who were previously denied them.
17That change, however, will cover a small number of veterans compared with the thousands presumably struck by American gunfire or ordnance in Southeast Asia, said Fred L. Borch, a retired Army colonel who is a historian of American medals and decorations and the author of "For Military Merit: Recipients of the Purple Heart."
18For troops wounded by their colleagues in Vietnam, recognition has been difficult to obtain.
19Mr. Jones, now 70 and living in Arizona, twice asked the Marine Corps to issue the medals, including in 1981 with the help of Senator Barry Goldwater.
20He was told that victims of so-called friendly fire were excluded from the honor.
21The policy effectively prevented ceremonies that would have acknowledged grave battlefield errors.
22It also compelled Mr. Jones to abandon his efforts.
23"I dropped it," he said, "thinking there was pretty much no hope."
24A change to the law in 1993 granted eligibility to those who had been wounded by American ordnance intended to cause an enemy harm.
25The law was amended, Mr. Borch said, after Congress became concerned that some victims of friendly fire in the 1991 Persian Gulf war had been awarded Purple Hearts but others had not.
26There had also been disagreement over the denial of the medal to an Army officer wounded in Panama in 1989.
27"The genesis of the law was, 'We want uniformity,' " Mr. Borch said.
28Few Vietnam-era cases have been opened, veterans said, in part because the change was not widely publicized but also because the Pentagon has not actively searched for all of the victims.
29The complicated work of marshaling evidence has largely been left to veterans and their families or friends - many of whom, including Mr. Jones, were not aware the rules had changed.
30He learned of his crew's eligibility only after he and other veterans began pooling records and recollections in 2013.
31The chronicle they assembled was one that many combat veterans would recognize; it told of a routine leg of a mission that instantly turned bad.
32Early on June 11, 1968, the squadron of CH-46 helicopters in which Lieutenant Jones served had flown a ground force to a landing zone northwest of Danang.
33That afternoon, those Marines were in heavy fighting.
34Lieutenant Jones's aircraft was returning to provide supplies and take out the wounded and dead.
35The aircraft had just picked up a cargo net loaded with what looked like water cans, he said, and was gaining altitude.
36First Lt. Glenn J. Zamorski, Lieutenant Jones's friend since flight school, was in command.
37Sgt. Raymond W. Templeton was the crew chief.
38Cpl. Conrad Lerman tended a .50-caliber machine gun at the left-side door.
39He had just re-enlisted, and was due home in July to see his wife and infant son, his widow, Faye Kelley, said by telephone.
40With Lieutenant Zamorski on the controls, Lieutenant Jones sat quietly in the left seat, enjoying a lull before reaching a dangerous landing zone.
41"I relaxed and was sitting much as you would in an easy chair at home, with my arms resting on the edges of my armored seat and my feet flat on the cockpit floor," he wrote in a statement submitted to the Marine Corps.
42As the helicopter climbed through 1,200 feet, he said, he heard an explosion.
43The aircraft nosed over, out of control.
44"The tail lifted up and to the right, continuing way past the 90-degree point," he wrote.
45"I would describe it as having the motion of a football tumbling end over end."
46The helicopter was falling.
47Lieutenant Jones jumped on the controls, but the rapid descent could not be checked.
48He heard no human sound as they plummeted, he said, save a scream behind him roughly halfway down.
49This, Mr. Jones said, was probably Corporal Lerman, who he later learned left the aircraft at about 500 feet, most likely after being ejected.
50In the 10- or 12-second descent, Mr. Jones said, he first assumed the aircraft had suffered a mechanical failure, then accepted that he was about to die.
51As the ground seemed to rush to meet him, he said, he had a vision of two Marine officers in dress uniforms walking up his sidewalk to notify his wife that he was gone.
52"The only emotion I felt," he wrote, "was anger."
53The last sight he recalled was an emerald tuft of grass on white sand as the aircraft slammed to earth.
54His seat, with him attached, was thrown clear at impact.
55He ended up face down and unconscious near the wreckage.
56A Navy corpsman at a nearby firebase sprinted to the site, flipped him over, cleared his airway and saved his life.
57(The corpsman, Lloyd E. Colvin, had crossed a minefield; the Navy later awarded him the Bronze Star.)
