17MEXICO CITY (AP) - Protesters burned ballot boxes in several restive states of southern Mexico on Sunday in an attempt to disrupt elections seen as a litmus test for President Enrique Pena Nieto's government, while officials said the vote was proceeding satisfactorily despite "isolated incidents."
18Thousands of soldiers and federal police were guarding polling stations where violence and calls for boycotts threatened to mar elections for 500 seats in the lower house of Congress, nine of 31 governorships and hundreds of mayors and local officials.
19Midterm elections usually draw light turnout, but attention was unusually high this time as a loose coalition of radical teachers' unions and activists vowed to block the vote.
20They attacked the offices of political parties in Chiapas and Guerrero states and burned ballots in Oaxaca ahead of the vote.
21The teachers' demands include huge wage hikes, an end to teacher testing and the safe return of 42 missing students from a radical teachers' college.
22Those students disappeared in September, and prosecutors say they were killed and incinerated by a drug gang.
23One student's remains were identified by DNA testing.
24Protesters burned at least seven ballot boxes and election materials in Tixtla, the Guerrero state town where the teachers' college is located.
25"We want the children to be found first, and then there can be elections," said Martina de la Cruz, the mother of one of the missing students.
26Soon after, there was an exchange of rock-throwing between protesters and hundreds of people who said they intended to defend their right to vote.
27There were no immediate reports of injuries.
28Ballot boxes were also destroyed in the southern states of Chiapas and Oaxaca.
29In Oaxaca's capital, masked protesters emptied a vehicle of ballots, boxes and voting tables and burned the material in the main square.
30Municipal policeman Onesimo Rojas said masked protesters torched ballot boxes in at least eight places in the city.
31Voters trickled to a polling place relocated from a Oaxaca city school to a white tent on a muddy road because the schools are under the control of radical teachers' groups that frequently stage public protests.
32"They think they own all the public spaces," said chemist Luz Maria Velazquez, 54.
33"It is a violation of our rights, because one group can't decide for everybody. ... They can block streets and hold protests whenever they want, and we're the ones affected."
34About 50 military vehicles were stationed on a highway outside the city.
35In the northern city of Monterrey, two political parties reported that armed men were intimidating voters in three towns near the border with Texas.
36Interior Secretary Miguel Angel Osorio Chong called it the "most watched-over" vote in Mexican history and said there were only "minimal" problems so far.
37"That is good for all of us, to be able to carry out a calm process in which citizens may determine their next leaders and for that to be something normal," Osorio Chong said.
38Mexico's National Electoral Institute reported that nearly 100 percent of polling places were able to open.
39Most of the nine governors' races were too close to call, and in at least one race - for the governorship of the northern border state of Nuevo Leon - an independent candidate was a top contender, all of which is novel for Mexico.
40"There is an enormous amount of competition, and that is good news," said Luis Carlos Ugalde, formerly the country's top electoral official.
41The vote comes amid widespread discontent with politicians in Mexico, where a series of corruption scandals, a lackluster economy and human rights concerns related to the missing students and suspected army massacres have tarnished Pena Nieto's image and fed anti-government protests.
42Among the most volatile states is Guerrero, where at least 10 people died in a clash between community self-policing groups Saturday, but it did not appear to be linked to the election.
43Mexico needs "a complete change in the economic question, in jobs, in security," said Juan Altamirano, a 52-year-old resident of Mexico City, who said he supports two leftist opposition parties.
44"Things are going badly. We have been moving backward."
45Some cast blank ballots as a protest against a political class they consider corrupt and ineffective.
46"What bothers me the most is that they think we're stupid," said Josue Mendoza, a 40-year-old graphic designer in the capital.
47He said he considered arguments that throwing away votes does not lead to change, but ultimately concluded that "there is no real option" on the ballot.
48On a national level, Pena's Nieto's Institutional Revolutionary Party is seeking to preserve its commanding position in Congress, despite the president's diminished popularity.
49Violence ahead of the elections has already claimed the lives of three candidates, one would-be candidate and at least a dozen campaign workers or activists.
50Candidates have been assassinated in the past, but the widespread threat to block elections is a new phenomenon.
51Ugalde saw another threat in the possibility of postelection legal challenges, in part because new electoral laws highly regulate campaign funding, advertising and spending, and make violations a potential cause for overturning results.
52"These may be the elections with the most post-electoral conflicts in Mexico's history," Ugalde said.
