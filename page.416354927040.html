ISIS releases video appearing to show killing of Christians

1The 29-minute online video purports to show militants holding two groups of captives.
2It says one group is held by an IS affiliate in eastern Libya known as Barka Province and the other by an affiliate in the south calling itself the Fazzan Province.
3A masked fighter brandishing a pistol delivers a long statement, saying Christians must convert to Islam or pay a special tax prescribed by the Quran.
4The video then switches between footage of the captives in the south being shot dead and the captives in the east being beheaded on a beach.
5It was not immediately clear who the captives were or when they were captured.
6It was also not clear how many captives were killed.
7The video bore the official logo of the IS media arm Al-Furqan and resembled previous videos released by the extremist group, including one in February in which IS militants in Libya beheaded 21 captured Egyptian Christians on a beach.
8The Islamic State group has been able to gain a foothold amid the chaos in Libya, where two governments backed by rival alliances of militias are battling each other as well as extremist groups.
9The Islamic State group is also advancing in Iraq, where the extremists captured three villages near the city of Ramadi in the western Anbar province and were locked in heavy clashes with Iraqi troops.
10More than 90,000 people have fled the Islamic State group's advance in Anbar, a United Nations humanitarian agency said Sunday.
11The Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs said in a statement that civilians are fleeing Ramadi as well as the three nearby villages captured by the IS group a few days ago.
12It said humanitarian agencies have moved quickly to provide assistance, including food, water and shelter.
13"Our top priority is delivering life-saving assistance to people who are fleeing -- food, water and shelter are highest on the list of priorities," said Lise Grande, the U.N. humanitarian coordinator for Iraq.
14Grande expressed concern over the safety of the displaced people, who are mainly heading to Baghdad and the IS-held city of Fallujah.
15"Seeing people carrying what little they can and rushing for safety is heart-breaking," she added.
16Iraqi officials in Anbar have described Ramadi as a ghost town, with empty streets and closed shops.
17Iraqi troops backed by Shiite militias and U.S.-led airstrikes managed to dislodge the IS group from the northern city of Tikrit earlier this month.
18But the troops have struggled against the militants in Anbar, which saw some of the heaviest fighting of the eight-year U.S. military intervention that ended in 2011.
