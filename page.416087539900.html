Greek PM gets support, not money from Putin

1MOSCOW, April 8 (Reuters) - Russian President Vladimir Putin offered Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras moral support and long-term cooperation but no financial aid on Wednesday, leaving Athens to fend for itself in resolving urgent debt problems with Western creditors.
2The leftist-led Greek government, at loggerheads with its euro zone and International Monetary Fund creditors, risks running out of money within weeks unless it can reach a new cash-for-reform deal.
3"The Greek side has not addressed us with any requests for aid," Putin told a joint news conference after Kremlin talks.
4"We discussed cooperation in various sectors of the economy, including the possibility of developing major energy projects."
5Tsipras added: "Greece is not a beggar going around to countries asking them to solve its economic problem, an economic crisis that doesn't only concern Greece but is a European crisis."
6Athens managed to sell 1.138 billion euros (1.24 billion) in six-month Treasury bills on Wednesday to keep itself afloat.
7The next test comes on Thursday when the government is due to repay the IMF 450 million euros, leaving it scraping state agencies' cash reserves to pay April public sector salaries and pensions.
8Greece must roll over another 1 billion euros in short-term debt on April 15.
9On a maiden visit to Moscow that caused unease among some EU partners, Tsipras mostly heeded warnings from Brussels and Berlin not to break ranks with European Union economic sanctions imposed on Russia over its role in destabilising Ukraine.
10He told the news conference that Greek exports had been hurt by Russian counter-measures he called understandable, and said he had made clear to his EU peers that he disagreed with the rationale for "economic warfare".
11Responding indirectly to a warning from European Parliament President Martin Schulz, Tsipras said: "Greece is a sovereign country with an unquestionable right to implement a multi-dimensional foreign policy and exploit its geopolitical role."
12Putin said he understood that Greece, which has historical and cultural ties to Russia, had been forced to go along with the policy and disavowed any attempt to use Athens' debt woes to drive a wedge among EU nations.
13"I want to assure you that we do not aim to use any internal European Union situations to improve ties with the European bloc as a whole. We want to work with the whole of united Europe," the Russian leader said.
14In a goodwill gesture, Russia's Economics Ministry said it had prepared a package of proposals to ease a retaliatory ban on Western food products for a meeting of Greek and Russian officials on Thursday.
15Putin said Moscow could provide credits for joint projects with Greece in the future, possibly including the planned Turkish Stream pipeline to carry Russian gas to Europe via Turkey, in which Tsipras voiced interest.
16Putin also said Russia would also be interested in taking part in privatisation tenders if Greece held any, touching a raw nerve since Tsipras' government is split over whether or not to continue with the sale of state assets which its predecessors began as part of the country's bailout programme.
17However, such long-term prospects offered no relief from Greece's immediate financial stress.
18In Brussels, senior euro zone finance officials met for an update on slow-moving negotiations between Athens and its creditors on a comprehensive package of reforms they require to release urgently needed remaining bailout funds.
19Greece submitted a 26-page reform list last week but negotiators say it was vague and optimistic on revenue numbers and omitted key issues of pensions and labour markets, which are the most sensitive for Tsipras' leftist Syriza party.
20A euro zone official said no breakthrough was expected at the meeting, and the crunch was likely to come at a meeting of the currency area's finance ministers in Riga on April 24.
21Before then, many of the ministers will have a chance to meet on the sidelines of the IMF's spring meetings in Washington on April 17-19.
22Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis met IMF chief Christine Lagarde and U.S. Treasury Undersecretary Nathan Sheets in Washington to seek support for Greece's position.
23The Greek finance ministry said Lagarde had pledged the "utmost flexibility" in assessing Greek reform commitments, and the United States had offered to act as an "honest broker".
24However, a U.S. Treasury statement said: "Undersecretary Sheets urged Greece to fully commit to a process of technical negotiations with its international partners in order to finalise a comprehensive set of reforms that merits timely financial support from Greece's creditors."
