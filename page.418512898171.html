Greek PM Alexis Tsipras faces party revolt over eurozone bailout deal

1Alexis Tsipras (centre) and Finance Minister Euclid Tsakalotos (centre left) leave bailout talks in Brussels.
2Athens: Greece's left-wing Prime Minister, Alexis Tsipras, faces a showdown with rebels in his own party on Tuesday, furious at his capitulation to German demands for one of the most sweeping austerity packages ever demanded of a eurozone government.
3Just hours after a deal that meant Greece surrendered much of its sovereignty to outside supervision in return for agreeing to talks on an 86 billion ($127 billion) bailout, doubts were emerging about whether Mr Tsipras would be able to hold his government together.
4Independent Greeks leader Panos Kammenos has indicated he will not tear up election pledges regarding his party's role as junior coalition partner.
5The terms imposed by international lenders led by Germany in all-night talks at an emergency summit required Mr Tsipras to abandon promises of ending austerity.
6Instead, he must pass legislation to cut pensions, increase value-added tax, clamp down on collective bargaining agreements and introduce quasi-automatic spending constraints.
7In addition, he must set 50 billion of public sector assets aside to be sold off under the supervision of foreign lenders and get the whole package through parliament by Wednesday.
8Mr Tsipras, who was elected five months ago to end five years of suffocating austerity, said he had "fought a tough battle" and "averted the plan for financial strangulation".
9A customer buys clothing using the informal barter currency TEM at an indoor market in Volos, Greece.
10However, to get the accord through parliament by Wednesday's deadline, he will have to rely on votes from pro-European opposition parties, raising big questions over the future of his government and raising the prospect of snap elections.
11Left-wing rebels in the ruling Syriza party, and his junior coalition partner, the right-wing Independent Greeks party, indicated they would not tear up election pledges that brought them to power in January.
12"We cannot agree to that," Independent Greeks leader Panos Kammenos told reporters after meeting Mr Tsipras.
13"In a parliamentary democracy, there are rules and we uphold them."
14A meeting of the Syriza parliamentary group on Tuesday could result in the sacking of Energy Minister Panagiotis Lafazanis and Deputy Labour Minister Dimitris Stratoulis over their opposition to the bailout.
15There might also be a battle over parliament speaker Zoe Konstantopoulou, an uncompromising left winger who also defied Mr Tsipras over the bailout and could create serious procedural obstacles for the package.
16If the summit on Greece's third bailout had failed, Athens would have been staring into an economic abyss with its banks on the brink of collapse and the prospect of having to print a parallel currency and exit the euro.
17Instead, it has won conditional agreement to receive a possible 86 billion over three years, provided its European partners are satisfied that the conditions are met.
18"The agreement was laborious, but it has been concluded. There is no Grexit," European Commission president Jean-Claude Juncker told a news conference after 17 hours of bargaining.
19He dismissed suggestions Mr Tsipras had been humiliated even though the summit statement insisted repeatedly that Greece subject much of its public policy to prior agreement by bailout monitors.
20"In this compromise, there are no winners and no losers," Mr Juncker said.
21"I don't think the Greek people have been humiliated, nor that the other Europeans have lost face. It is a typical European arrangement."
22Eurozone finance ministers told officials to prepare options for bridge financing arrangements during talks on a bailout.
23A decision was expected by Wednesday.
24Athens must meet a tight timetable for enacting unpopular reforms of value-added tax, pensions, budget cuts, bankruptcy rules and an EU banking law that could be used to make big depositors take losses.
25German Chancellor Angela Merkel said she could recommend "with full confidence" that the Bundestag authorise the opening of loan negotiations once the Greek parliament approved the programme and passed the first laws.
26The Bundestag is due to vote on Greece on Friday.
27Ms Merkel's allies defended the deal.
28Her chief of staff, Peter Altmaier, said Europe had won and Germany "was part of the solution - from the beginning until the end."
29However in Greece, relief was mixed with anger at Germany.
30"Listen, it is some sort of victory but it is a pyrrhic victory because the measures are very strict," said Marianna, 73, who didn't give her surname.
31Asked whether the tough conditions imposed on Greece were similar to the 1919 Treaty of Versailles that forced crushing reparations on a defeated Germany after World War I, Ms Merkel said: "I won't take part in historical comparisons, especially when I didn't make them myself."
32The deterioration of the Greek economy since Mr Tsipras won office in January, and particularly in the last two weeks, had led to a much higher financing need, she said.
33One senior EU official put the cost to Greece of the last two weeks of turmoil at 25 billion to 30 billion.
34A eurozone diplomat said it might be closer to 50 billion.
35Malta's Prime Minister, Joseph Muscat, said Greece had been "humiliated", mostly as a result of its refusal to take an offer made two weeks ago.
36He said the talks had been brutal.
37Mr Tsipras accepted a compromise on German-led demands for the sequestration of Greek state assets worth 50 billion, including recapitalised banks, in a trust fund beyond government reach, to be sold off primarily to pay down debt.
38In a gesture to Greece, some 12.5 billion of the proceeds would go to investment in Greece, Ms Merkel said.
39The Greek leader had to drop his opposition to a full role for the International Monetary Fund in the next bailout, which Ms Merkel had insisted on to win parliamentary backing in Berlin.
40In a sign of how hard it may be for Mr Tsipras to convince his own Syriza party to accept the deal, Labour Minister Panos Skourletis said the terms were not viable and would lead to new elections this year.
41Six sweeping measures including spending cuts, tax hikes and pension reforms had to be enacted by Wednesday night and the entire package endorsed by parliament before talks could start, the leaders decided.
42In almost the only concession after imposing its tough terms, Germany dropped a proposal to make Greece take a "time-out" from the eurozone that many said resembled a forced ejection if it failed to meet the conditions.
43Mr Tsipras was subjected to a 17-hour browbeating by leaders furious that he had spurned their previous bailout offer on more favourable terms in June and had held a referendum to reject it.
44Only France and Italy worked to try to soften the terms being imposed on Greece.
45Some diplomats questioned whether it was feasible to rush the package through the Greek parliament in three days.
46Even if this week's rescue succeeds, some also question whether Greece will stay the course on a three-year programme.
47Eurozone finance ministers were tasked with finding sources of immediate bridge funding for Greece to prevent it defaulting on a key payment to the European Central Bank next Monday.
48Greece needs 7 billion by July 20, when it must make a bond redemption to the ECB, and 12 billion by mid-August when another ECB payment falls due.
49The country missed its second debt payment to the International Monetary Fund in two weeks, despite reaching an agreement with official creditors.
50Athens was supposed to remit about 456 million to the crisis lender, but it had not been expected to make the payment after missing a 1.5 billion debt payment on June 30.
51IMF spokesman Gerry Rice said Greece's arrears to the fund totalled about 2 billion.
52After the first default, the IMF froze Greece's access to its resources, including the fund's ongoing financing program for the country.
