2Now, the number of visitors is shrinking due to the economic crisis in those countries and people like her in this former Soviet republic are feeling the pain.
3"There were some signs of life in January, when suddenly some tourists from Russia, Ukraine and Poland appeared, but since then it's been a complete catastrophe," said Matiashvili.
4Some days she makes five lari from tourists, sometimes only one.
5Georgia, a mountainous nation in the South Caucasus, relies heavily on wine exports and on tourists attracted to the mountains and beaches that for decades made the country a favored holiday destination for the Soviet elites.
6While tourists might ordinarily be enticed as Georgia's currency sinks, making travel cheaper, many of those who visit Georgia come from countries facing their own economic problems.
7Georgia's currency, the lari, has lost more than 20 percent of its value against the dollar since August - but Ukraine's hryvnia currency has lost about 60 percent against the dollar and the Russian ruble roughly 50 percent.
8Due to the economic tensions, tens of thousands are expected at an opposition-led protest Saturday in the capital, Tbilisi.
9There are fears the march could turn violent, due to the bitter political divide between the government team formed by billionaire Bidzina Ivanishvili's party and supporters of former President Mikhail Saakashvili, whose party has been in opposition since losing the 2012 election.
10The government, led by Ivanishvili ally Irakli Garibashvili, insists the crisis has been talked up by political opponents and sensationalist media and is not the result of policy failings.
11"I think that really an artificial hysteria has been created, especially by the media," Prime Minister Garibashvili said last month in a notably pugnacious response.
12"We have to calm, normalize and stabilize the situation. I want to say that we're doing everything to correct the situation as quickly as possible."
13While it has been 24 years since Georgia voted for independence from the Soviet Union, the country retains close business links to Russia and Ukraine.
14As the war in eastern Ukraine, international sanctions against Russia and the sinking price of oil price worldwide have ravaged the economies of those two countries, Georgia has become collateral damage.
15While the falling lari has made many imports more expensive, it has fallen by less than the Russian ruble over the last year, making exports to that key market more difficult.
16One affected industry is Georgian wine, long renowned in neighboring countries, and which has started to attract the attention of European and American connoisseurs in recent years.
17Despite producers branching out, Russia and Ukraine remain the top two wine export markets.
18In January and February this year, exports to Russia were down 85 percent and those to Ukraine fell by two-thirds.
19Paata Sheshelidze, director of the Institute of Economic Freedom, said the economic difficulties have been made worse by government policies, including regulations and taxes that he said have restricted development of the private sector.
20The weakening of Georgia's currency, he said, was largely due to an increase in the money supply to cover budget expenses.
21The currency turbulence has also had a devastating effect on those Georgians who took out foreign-currency loans in search of lower interest rates.
22Igor Khuchua mortgaged his apartment to fund a bakery, but now, at 66, he faces being made homeless.
23Despite getting help from the bank to restructure his debt, Khuchua sees no way out.
24"I've basically lost my apartment. I took out a loan on it to open my business, but the lari's fall has had a negative effect," he said.
25"I can't blame anyone. I took the risk myself, but the situation's objectively worsened with the fall of the national currency."
