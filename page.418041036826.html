In Tech: Microsoft Office comes to Android, Foursquare brings back mayors

1This product image provided by Microsoft shows the new PowerPoint app for Android devices.
2Android versions of Word, Excel and PowerPoint are now available in the Google store.
3Android phone owners can now use an updated version of Microsoft's Office tools for writing, spreadsheets and slideshows.
4The new Word, Excel and PowerPoint apps are designed to provide more functions on small touchscreens.
5Microsoft released them for Android phones this week in another move to make some of its most popular software tools available to people who don't use Microsoft's Windows operating system.
6The company had released redesigned versions for Android tablets in January and for iPhones and iPads last year.
7But Android phone users had only a bare-bones version, except through a limited "preview" of the expanded offering.
8(Of course, Android device owners have also been able to use competing tools from Google and other app-makers.)
9There are still no comparable apps for phones and tablets running Microsoft's own Windows system, though that will change once Windows 10 gets released next month.
10As Android phones have had until now, Windows phones get only the bare-bones Office.
11Windows tablet owners have to use a version more suited for laptops and desktops with real keyboards.
12The mobile apps are free, although Microsoft is offering more features to users with an Office 365 subscription, which starts at $70 a year.
13Microsoft says some phone makers including Samsung, Sony and LG will ship Android phones with Office already installed.
14The new apps will work on older phones running the KitKat version of Android from 2013 and Lollipop from last year, but nothing older than that.
15For a certain subset of people, life started to have meaning again this week, when the startup Foursquare announced that its location-sharing app Swarm is bringing back mayorships.
16Come again?
17Foursquare, you see, was an app that allowed users to "check in" to bars, restaurants and other places.
18You became the "mayor" of a place if you checked in more than anyone else in a given period.
19There were fierce competitions for mayorships.
20While Foursquare's user base stayed small, many users were fiercely loyal.
21In 2014, with user growth stalling and competitors like Yelp eclipsing it, Foursquare split itself into two apps.
22Foursquare became the place for people to discover businesses and venues around them, while the social features migrated to a new app called Swarm.
23At Swarm, you can check in to places and share this information with your Swarm buddies.
24The Swarm app also let users compete for mayorships, but only among their friends.
25This, Foursquare said, "just wasn't as much fun."
26So in a blog post, Foursquare said it was "upping the ante and letting you compete for mayorships against everyone."
27-- Facebook is now letting people sign up for its Messenger app even if they don't have a Facebook account.
28It's another way the world's biggest online social network is spreading its tentacles over your phone.
29This option is available in the U.S., Canada, Peru and Venezuela.
30-- Instagram is creeping on Twitter and Periscope territory.
31The photo-sharing app announced this week that it wants to let users "see the world as it happens."
32It's revamping its "Explore" page to help users discover events while they happen, from concerts to sports events to sunsets on the Santa Cruz boardwalk.
33It's also made it easier to search for people, places and tags -- at the same time, if you'd like.
