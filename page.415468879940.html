Vanuatu struggles to account for cyclone damage, deaths

1Packing winds of 270 kilometers (168 miles) per hour, Cyclone Pam tore through Vanuatu early Saturday, leaving a trail of destruction.
2In this March 14, 2015, photo provided by World Vision, debris is scattered along the coast in Port Vila Vanuatu, after Cyclone Pam ripped through the tiny South Pacific archipelago.
3Vanuatu's President Baldwin Lonsdale, left, shakes hands with Japanese Prime Minister Shinzo Abe during their talk in Sendai, northeastern Japan, Saturday, March 14, 2015, on the sidelines of the U.N. World Conference on Disaster Risk Reduction.
4WELLINGTON, New Zealand (AP) - Officials struggled Sunday to determine the scale of the devastation wrought by a monstrous cyclone that tore through the tiny South Pacific archipelago of Vanuatu, with death counts varying in the single digits but expected to rise once communications are restored with outlying islands.
5Cyclone Pam tore through Vanuatu early Saturday, packing winds of 270 kilometers (168 miles) per hour, and leaving a trail of destruction and unconfirmed reports of dozens of deaths.
6Two people were confirmed dead in the capital, Port Vila, with another 20 injured there, said Paolo Malatu, coordinator for the National Disaster Management Office.
7Earlier, Chloe Morrison, a World Vision emergency communications officer, said Vanuatu's disaster response office told her agency that at least eight people died.
8She had also heard reports of entire villages being destroyed in more remote areas.
9The confusion over the death toll is due largely to a near-total communications blackout across the country.
10With power lines and phone circuits down, officials in the capital had no way of knowing what the scope of the damage was on the outer islands, where the storm scored a direct hit.
11"We haven't been able to communicate outside Port Vila," Malatu said.
12"At this point, the damage is severe and we haven't had figures of how many houses destroyed. ... It's really bad, it's really bad."
13Officials are planning to head to the outer islands Monday in helicopters, small planes and military aircraft to get a better sense of the destruction, Malatu said.
14Telephone networks are notoriously spotty in South Pacific island nations such as Vanuatu, particularly in the aftermath of storms.
15It often takes days before networks can be restored, making it incredibly difficult for officials to quickly analyze the breadth of devastation following natural disasters.
16Vanuatu's government has declared a nationwide state of emergency, and Australia and New Zealand have sent in relief supplies.
17Port Vila's airport was damaged by the storm and closed for commercial flights, but the first delivery of supplies arrived Sunday from the Red Cross, Malatu said.
18"People are really upset and it's really hard, just because for the last couple of years, we haven't received a really big cyclone like this one," said Isso Nihmei, Vanuatu coordinator for the environmental and crisis response group 350.
19"Most people right now, they are really homeless."
20He came upon one of the storm's victims on Saturday while surveying the damage along the coastline with other relief workers.
21The group spotted a man lying on the ground, not breathing, and rushed him to the hospital.
22By the time they arrived, however, he was dead, Nihmei said.
23Structural damage across Port Vila was extensive, Nihmei said, with the majority of homes severely damaged or destroyed.
24Some residents began cleaning up what was left of their wrecked houses and checking on family members.
25Relief workers, meanwhile, were trying to get victims to temporary shelters as fast as possible, Nihmei said.
26Hannington Alatoa, head of the Vanuatu Red Cross Society, said flyovers by New Zealand and Australian relief teams showed much of the country had been "flattened."
27At least half of the population, or about 130,000 people, has been affected, Alatoa said in Sendai, Japan, where he and other Vanuatu officials were attending a U.N. conference on disaster risk reduction.
28He had no accurate information on the numbers left homeless.
29"No trees, no foliage, no iron structures standing on the western part of Tanna," Altoa said, referring to one of Vanuatu's southern islands.
30"Trees blocked the roads. ... People are in great need of water."
31Residents awoke to much calmer weather Sunday after many hunkered down in emergency shelters for a second straight night.
32Many people who have ventured out from 23 emergency shelters around Port Vila have found their homes damaged or blown away altogether, Morrison of World Vision said.
33Teetering trees and downed power lines have made parts of the capital hazardous.
34She said communications have been so problematic that her aid group hasn't yet been able to account for many of its own 76 staff members on the islands.
35For anybody who wasn't in a secure shelter during the cyclone "it would have been a very, very tough time for them," she said.
36Vanuatu has a population of 267,000 people spread over 65 islands.
37About 47,000 people live in the capital.
38UNICEF estimated that 54,000 children were among those affected by the cyclone.
39The small island nation, located about a quarter of the way from Australia to Hawaii, has repeatedly warned it is already suffering devastating effects from climate change with the island's coastal areas being washed away, forcing resettlement to higher ground and smaller yields on traditional crops.
40Scientists say it's impossible to attribute single weather events like Cyclone Pam to climate change.
41The cyclone has already caused damage to other Pacific islands, including Kiribati and the Solomon Islands.
42Authorities in New Zealand are preparing for Cyclone Pam, which was forecast to pass north of the country on Sunday and Monday.
