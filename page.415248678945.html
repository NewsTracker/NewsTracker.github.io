Email Issue Dogs Hillary Clinton

Hong Kong chief urges residents to go easy on mainland hordes

1Then-Secretary of State Hillary Clinton checks her Blackberry from a desk inside a C-17 military plane in 2011.
2In the latest political furor swirling around Hillary Clinton, Republicans are piling on the Democratic presidential front-runner because she kept many potentially sensitive emails off limits to outside scrutiny during her four years as secretary of state.
3Fellow Democrats are trying to deflect the controversy from Clinton by attacking GOP presidential candidates.
4But some Democrats are concerned that the fuss is reviving bad old memories of a major Clinton vulnerability - a penchant for secrecy that extends back to her eight years as first lady during her husband Bill Clinton's presidency, and that sometimes makes it appear she has something to hide.
5David Axelrod, former chief political strategist for President Barack Obama, told MSNBC, "However this turns out, this problem is being exacerbated by the lack of answers from the Clinton campaign or the nascent campaign. And it would be good to get out there and answer these questions: Why did she use a separate email? How did she secure that email? Was there another email through which she communicated with people? By not answering these questions, they're allowing the story to fester in ways that are unhelpful."
6The flashpoint is that Clinton exclusively used a private email system and server to conduct official business, bypassing congressional oversight and the administration's record-keeping preferences and policies.
7Raj Shah, research director for the Republican National Committee, noted that RNC Counsel John Phillippe's wrote a letter Thursday to the State Department Inspector General "calling for an investigation into Clinton's private email address and 'home-brewed' server," and that "the RNC is continuing to raise serious questions regarding Clinton's decision to undermine federal guidelines intended to ensure proper government archiving and public access to records."
8On Wednesday night, Clinton tweeted, "I want the public to see my email. I asked State to release them. They said they will review them for release as soon as possible."
9But opposition Republicans say Clinton only gave the State Department the emails she and her staff had reviewed, not all of them.
10Some conservatives suggested that perhaps Clinton's private emails weren't properly safeguarded and may have been open to scrutiny by U.S. enemies and hackers.
11The Democratic National Committee jumped into the fray to deflect attention from Clinton, the overwhelming front-runner for the Democratic presidential nomination in 2016.
12The DNC's response Thursday was to attack possible Republican candidates for president.
13"This week we have observed many Republicans become outraged over questions about email," DNC Press Secretary Holly Shulman told reporters in an email.
14"It is surprising at the very least just how silent these same Republicans have been about members of their own party who have used their own private servers, conducted office business on private email accounts, have stonewalled open record requests, have routinely deleted official emails, and more."
15Among the examples cited was former Florida Gov. Jeb Bush, a possible Republican presidential candidate in 2016, who, Shulman said, "did not turn over emails from his private account (hosted on a server he owns) to the state that he deemed 'not relevant to the public record.' And though he cites 'the spirit of transparency' as the reason he released some of the emails, the truth is he was required by law to do so."
16The GOP-controlled House Select Committee on Benghazi has issued subpoenas for all Clinton's communications regarding Libya, where four Americans died in a terrorist attack while Clinton was secretary of state.
17Some GOP legislators say she didn't do enough to protect the Americans.
18"The Benghazi Select Committee needs to see all of these emails, because the American people deserve all of the facts," House Speaker John Boehner said in a news release.
1HONG KONG/BEIJING (Reuters) - Hong Kong must consider the rights of mainland Chinese as well as the economic impact of any changes to its visitor policy, Chief Executive Leung Chun-ying said on Friday, amid mounting anger in the former British colony against hordes of mainland shoppers.
2Hong Kong people fume that mainland tourists crowd them out of malls and buy up all available supplies of daily necessities such as diapers and milk powder.
3Police have used pepper spray to break up confrontations as locals have cursed mainlanders, shouting at them to go back to China.
4Leung said last week that Hong Kong may need to restrict the number of mainland Chinese coming into the city and promised to raise the issue while he was in Beijing during China's annual parliamentary meeting.
5He appeared to soften that stance in Beijing on Friday.
6"We have to balance mainland residents' need to travel to Hong Kong and their rights under the existing policy with the livelihood of Hong Kong people," he said on the sidelines of a meeting of the National People's Congress.
7"Also, any adjustments in the policy cannot result in a significant drop in tourist numbers and hence affect the overall economy of Hong Kong and employment."
8Over 40 million mainland tourists visited Hong Kong last year, vastly outnumbering the city's population of 7.2 million.
9At the same time, retail sales have slipped to their lowest levels since the SARS scare in 2003 and mainland visitors during this year's Lunar New Year holiday fell for the first time in 20 years.
10Anger toward mainland Chinese in Hong Kong is longstanding but has come to a head as frustrations mount over Beijing's unwillingness to negotiate on political reforms.
11A special administrative region of China, Hong Kong was returned to Chinese Communist Party rule in 1997 under a "one country, two systems" formula that gives it separate laws and an independent judiciary but leaves Beijing with ultimate authority.
12Student-led pro-democracy protests shut down parts of the city for two and a half months last year as activists called for open nominations in the city's next chief executive election in 2017.
13Leung reiterated on Friday that there was no room for negotiation.
14Beijing will allow a vote, but only between pre-screened candidates.
15"There is no question of the National People's Congress withdrawing the decision that they made on Aug. 31. There is no question of restarting the process," Leung said.
16"Everyone at home has to be realistic about this."
