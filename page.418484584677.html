Nintendo's Satoru Iwata dies at 55; under him, Wii created hordes of new gamers

1Satoru Iwata held the reins of Nintendo during the launch of two of its most successful gaming consoles: the handheld Nintendo DS and Nintendo Wii home system.
2Nintendo President Satoru Iwata, who had led the company since 2002 and presided over the launch of the monumentally successful Wii video game console, died Saturday.
3He was 55.
4Nintendo announced Iwata's death with a brief statement Sunday, saying he had died due to a bile duct growth.
5Known to be in ill health, Iwata had skipped North America's largest video game trade show, the Electronic Entertainment Expo, for the last two years.

10Iwata was named director of Nintendo's operations in June 2000.
11He assumed the role of president of the Kyoto, Japan-based company in May 2002, after the retirement of Hiroshi Yamauchi, a descendant of Nintendo's founding family.
12Yamauchi ran Nintendo for 52 years and died in 2013.
13Iwata held the reins of the company during the launch of two of its most successful gaming consoles, the handheld Nintendo DS and the Nintendo Wii home system.
14The Wii was released in 2006 and has sold more than 101 million units worldwide.
15Born Dec. 6, 1959, in Sapporo, Japan, Iwata was the son of a politician who was a municipal mayor.
16He became interested in video game programming as a teenager, fashioning simple games on his calculator.
17While studying computer science at the Tokyo Institute of Technology, he took a part-time job as a programmer at HAL Laboratories, focusing on development of games such as the Kirby series.
18After his graduation in 1982, he worked full-time on game development for HAL and did contract work for Nintendo.
19When HAL Laboratories verged toward bankruptcy in 1992, Iwata was made the company's president, and he helped restore it to fiscal soundness.
20He left in 2000 to become head of corporate planning at Nintendo.
21Shortly before Iwata became Nintendo president in 2002, the company launched what became one of its bigger flops: the GameCube console, a successor to the Nintendo 64.
22The GameCube failed to outsell Sony's PlayStation 2 and Microsoft's Xbox, compelling Nintendo to rethink its direction.
23Enter the Wii.
24The Wii was instrumental in ushering in the modern era of casual gaming, as it was heavily based on motion controls and family-centric entertainment.
25The system, as well as Iwata's corporate mandate, was drastically different from those of Nintendo's competitors in the home video game console business.
26Rather than focusing on technological achievements or becoming an all-in-one home media center, the Wii broadened the audience by enabling just about anyone to easily pick up and play a game.
27Games such as "Wii Sports," which lets people play virtual tennis with the wave of an arm, captivated the public.
28In an opinion piece Iwata wrote for The Times in 2006, he argued that the game industry was becoming too closed-off to new consumers.
29He wrote that the industry should worry less about graphical enhancements and high-tech wizardry and instead look for ways to enhance "the emotional ways people interact with our games."
30"Like Hollywood, which in the past has focused too heavily on special effects, we need to find other ways to improve," Iwata wrote.
31"The challenge for our industry is to also find ways that improve the experience by means other than what is seen. That shouldn't be too hard, because we operate interactively, rather than within the restraints of passive storytelling."
32In 2011, poor sales led Nintendo to its first operating loss ever.
33Shaken, Iwata cut his own salary by 50%.
34The follow-up to the Wii, the Wii U, hasn't been nearly as successful as its predecessor -- it has sold just 9.5 million units since its introduction in 2012.
35But Nintendo has a follow-up in the works, a project it is referring to as "NX."
36Iwata had said more information would be coming on the product in 2016.
37Iwata spoke in recent months of further broadening Nintendo's scope via a pledge "to redefine entertainment as something that improves people's quality of life," as he wrote in a message to shareholders last year.
38He said this would be separate from the company's video game business and would focus first on a health-orientated theme.
39"What Nintendo will try to achieve in the next 10 years is a platform business that improves people's [quality of life] in enjoyable ways," he wrote.
