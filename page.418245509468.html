Dutch seek UN tribunal over downing of MH17

1The Dutch government is lobbying its political allies for the establishment of a UN tribunal to prosecute suspects in the downing of a Malaysia Airlines plane last year.
2Flight MH17 came down over territory held by pro-Russian separatists in eastern Ukraine a year ago.
3Dutch Prime Minister Mark Rutte said a UN tribunal would give "the best guarantee of cooperation from all countries" in seeking justice for the victims' families.
4A total of 298 people, most of whom were Dutch passengers, died in the incident.
5Malaysia told the UN Security Council yesterday that it plans to push for a UN-backed tribunal, even as Russia described the move as premature, diplomats said.
6"No route is 100% perfect, but this is the route that is by far the most preferred," Mr Rutte said after a weekly meeting with his cabinet.
7"We also have plan Bs if this approach doesn't work, both national and international, but this is the route that would be the best."
8Mr Rutte's comments confirmed a report by Reuters last month that the Netherlands was seeking to establish such a tribunal.
9He declined to comment on where the court might be located or how important it would be for Russia, which has a veto on the UN Security Council, to cooperate.
10Russia denies involvement in the conflict in eastern Ukraine, where pro-Russian rebels are fighting Ukrainian government forces.
11Moscow has suggested MH17 was downed by a Ukrainian fighter jet and denied a widely supported theory that the rebels shot it out of the sky with a Russian-supplied BUK missile.
12The Netherlands is leading the criminal investigation, which is also assisted by Belgium, Australia and Ukraine.
13A final report on the cause of the crash is due to be released in October by the Dutch Safety Board.
