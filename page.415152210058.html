Forbes billionaire rankings has some fresh and familiar faces

Director of 'Verite' and 'Hand to God' discusses his methods

1Forbes said Monday that Bill Gates's net worth rose to $79.2 billion in 2015 from $76 billion last year.
2That put him at the top of the magazine's list of the world's billionaires for the second consecutive year.
3The co-founder of Microsoft Corp. has topped the list for 16 of the last 21 years.
4In second place is Mexican telecommunications mogul Carlos Slim Helu, with a net worth of $77.1 billion.
5He had topped the list in 2013.
6Next is investor Warren Buffett, who moved up one slot this year with a net worth of $72.7 billion.
7In fourth place was Amancio Ortega, the Spanish co-founder of clothing retail chain Zara, with a net worth of $64.5 billion.
8Rounding out the top five was Larry Ellison, founder of technology company Oracle Corp., with $54.3 billion.
9Forbes said there were 1,826 billionaires on its list this year, up from 1,645 in 2014.
10Added together, they were worth a combined $7.05 trillion, up from $6.4 trillion last year.
11Most of the those on the list were men.
12But there were 197 women, up from 172 a year ago.
13The highest-ranking woman was Christy Walton, the widow of John Walton, a son of the founder of Wal-Mart Stores Inc. She has a net worth of $41.7 billion, according to Forbes.
14The world's youngest billionaire was 24-year-old Evan Spiegel, the CEO and co-founder of mobile messaging company Snapchat, with a net worth of $1.5 billion.
15Snapchat's other co-founder, 25-year-old Bobby Murphy, had the same net worth as Spiegel.
16Other tech billionaire newcomers were two co-founders of taxi-ordering app Uber and one of its executives.
17Three co-founders of Airbnb, the vacation-home rental website, also made the list.
18Basketball legend Michael Jordan joined the list for the first time this year, thanks to his ownership in basketball team Charlotte Hornets and payouts form his Nike brand.
19Jordan had a net worth of $1 billion, the magazine said.
20According to Forbes, Ukrainian President Petro Poroshenko and fashion designer Michael Kors were among 138 people from last year's list who dropped out of the rankings this year.
21This is the the 29th year that Forbes has released its billionaires list.
22The magazine said it calculated each person's wealth based on stock prices and exchange rates on Feb. 13, 2015.
1Von Stuelpnagel, the artistic director of the downtown Studio 42 company, is directing two works: "Verite," a thriller starring Anna Camp currently at Lincoln Center, and "Hand to God," a satire starring a sock puppet that's coming to Broadway this month.
2NEW YORK (AP) - Not many theater directors have the range to do a play with a foul-mouthed hand puppet and then one with a whiff of film noir.
3Moritz Von Stuelpnagel can do both - in the same season.
4Von Stuelpnagel, the artistic director of the downtown Studio 42 company, is directing two works that seem to have little in common: "Verite," a thriller starring Anna Camp currently at Lincoln Center, and "Hand to God," a gothic comedy starring a sock puppet that's coming to Broadway this month.
5He originally planned for a career as a graphic designer, but soon fell in love with the collaborative nature of theater.
6Von Stuelpnagel got a master's from Rutgers University, was the associate director for Broadway's Tony-nominated "Cry-Baby" and has strong ties with The Ensemble Studio Theatre.
7The Associated Press asked him about his process, his attitude and why he thinks the theater is thriving.
8AP: What do you see as the role of a director is?
9Von Stuelpnagel: A director becomes, over the course of the process, a stand-in for the audience.
10I have to listen and respond in the way that I imagine the audience will respond.
11And it is a particular skill, I think, to always be watching with fresh eyes and yet also be watching from the actor's perspective - do we feel like the actions and motivations of the characters feel truthful?
12AP: How do you handle actors?
13Von Stuelpnagel: I like my actors to feel very empowered as much as possible.
14I think that they respond especially well when they feel very clear about what they're doing.
15In the same way that I think a coach works with a team, the entire team needs to be clear about what their role is and then they need to be empowered in the moment to just be able to go and do it.
16I look for places where they feel unsure and I try to bolster them with clarity or an exciting challenge.
17AP: Are you a director who likes to go big - fire, helicopters, explosions?
18Von Stuelpnagel: I don't like to paint with colors that feel broader than the thing requires.
19There are plenty of directors who love to make these paint-outside-the-line choices but I actually feel I like to be succinct and honor the play as simply as possible.
20It's funny because the plays I'm doing aren't simple and yet I try to do them with as much simplicity as I can and yet have them seem satisfying.
21AP: People worry about the state of theater.
22Do you?
23Von Stuelpnagel: I don't know that I believe that it's dying.
24It's not in terms of the volume of work that we're putting into the world, in terms of the number of new plays, in terms of the financial statistics.
25I think everyone's always struggling, so it FEELS like the theater is dying and we feel underappreciated.
26But actually I feel like the theater is thriving.
27AP: You've gone this season from noir to puppetry, drama and comedy all rolled up into one.
28That's quite a range.
29Von Stuelpnagel: I'm always looking to balance the darkness with comedy and the lightness within drama.
30For example, both 'Hand to God' and 'Verite' are pretty dark, ultimately.
31My hope is that by investing in the characters, it actually becomes funnier.
32I think that, in life, everyone is on their personal battle.
33Obviously, some people have it worse.
34But everyone is out there suffering and struggling and wrestling with some degree with self-loathing.
35To be able to acknowledge that honestly - that, to me, lets us laugh at it, too.
36AP: For you, what makes a good night out?
37Von Stuelpnagel: I believe that your evening at the theater should unfold with a number of surprises throughout the evening and, when it works best, those surprises are by virtue of the virtuosity of the players - performers, designers, all of it.
38They should surprise you not with haphazard randomness, but with something that is a pleasing revelation.
