Romania turns hacking crisis into advantage, helping Ukraine

2Romania, the eastern European country, known more for economic disarray than technological prowess, has become one of the leading nations in Europe in the fight against hacking.
3The reason: the country's own battle against Internet renegades and a legacy of computing excellence stemming from Communist dictator Nicolae Ceausescu's regime.
4Ukraine is turning to an unlikely partner in its struggle to defend itself against Russian cyber warfare: Romania.
5The eastern European country known more for economic disarray than technological prowess has become one of the leading nations in Europe in the fight against hacking.
6Both historic twists have ironically turned Romanian cyber sleuths into some of Europe's best.
7So much so that NATO tapped Bucharest to defend Ukraine from Russian digital espionage by sending experts to monitor Kiev government institutes and train Ukrainian IT specialists.
8Ukraine says Russia's Federal Security Service is coordinating attacks on government offices as part of a proxy war against Ukraine's government, amid real fighting between Ukrainian forces and pro-Russian rebels in the east.
9Ukrainian security operatives say that, with Romania's help, they have foiled attempts to spread malicious software intended to disable the government's computer network or steal intelligence.
10At NATO's summit last year in Wales, President Barack Obama and other leaders decided to create five "trust funds" - or narrowly focused programs funded by NATO member counties that are meant to help Ukraine reform and modernize its defense capabilities.
11- including one for cyber defense to help Ukraine's military modernize.
12Romania, a member of NATO's cyber coalition exercises, volunteered to lead the Ukraine Cyber Defense Trust Fund - and tapped the state-owned Rasirom company.
13The alliance notes the Rasirom, the cyber-security provider for Romania's top state institutions, has "a rich experience in cyber defense."
14"We are trying to be a regional leader," Romanian Prime Minister Victor Ponta said in e meeting with foreign media outlets.
15"That is why Romania ... has been appointed by NATO to be in charge of (Ukraine's) cyber security and preventing cyber warfare."
16At the opening of a regional conference on cyber security, U.S deputy Secretary for Commerce Bruce Andrews on Monday praised Romanian company Bitdefender which created "Bitdefender Box." one of the first security products made specifically for home networks.
17It protects devices by scanning network traffic to detect and block potential security threats and noted that Romanian was the second language spoken at Microsoft in offices around the world, after English.
18Ukraine itself had no say on which country NATO tapped to provide its cyber security.
19But since 2013 it has cooperated with Romania on fighting hacking, phishing, child porn and DDoS attacks, which attempt to disable a website by swamping it with traffic.
20Europol is also tapping into Romania's cyber savvy.
21The European police agency's chief, Ron Wainright, said 20 percent of Europol's cyber defense experts are Romanian police officers.
22"The expertise that Romanian specialists bring in their daily fight against this threat is extraordinary," he said on a recent visit to Bucharest.
23If Romania is a leader in cyber policing today, it can perhaps thank a string of high-profile cases of Romanian hackers attacking the United States in 2011.
24That year, Romanian cyber criminals stole $1 billion from victims in the U.S., and an unemployed Romanian taxi driver calling himself "Guccifer" hacked the emails of former U.S. Secretary of State Colin Powell and former President George W. Bush.
25Marcel Lehel Lazar accessed and published private Bush family photos, including selfies of Bush who appeared to be shaving in the shower and soaking his feet in a bathtub.
26The security breaches so alarmed Washington that the FBI launched a program to train 600 Romanian law enforcement officers on fighting cybercrime.
27Last month, Romanian police broke up an alleged cybercrime ring and arrested 25 Romanians.
28The Directorate for Investigating Organized Crime and Terrorism said that between February and December 2013, a group of 52 Romanians and foreign citizens carried out more than 34,000 fraudulent cash withdrawals in 24 countries, netting more than euros 15 million ($17 million).
29The gang targeted banks in Puerto Rico and Oman.
30Romanian police said the group broke IT firewalls to obtain the details of corporate clients which were used to clone credit cards.
31Romania's tradition of computer skills dates back to the communist era when Ceausescu decided to make engineering and math education priorities at top universities.
32Microsoft, Google and Oracle all have businesses in Romania and there are 93,000 employed in the industry with 6,500 graduating in IT every year, according to Anis, the Romanian software industry association.
33Romanians also have computer success abroad.

35One industry expert said Romania's success relies on teaching IT from a young age.
36"Faculties are turning out very good specialists who are highly sought after by big companies," said Rasirom general manager Aurelian Tolescu.
