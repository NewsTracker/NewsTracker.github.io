Greece makes new proposals to end bailout impasse

1Greece's Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras leads a meeting with Palestinian foreign minister Riyad Al-Maliki at Maximos Mansion in Athens, on Monday, June 8, 2015.
2German Chancellor Angela Merkel said at the Group of Seven summit in Germany on Monday that Greece needs to agree to take steps to straighten out its finances and economy, as it negotiates with other eurozone countries and the International Monetary Fund for more bailout loan money.
3ATHENS, Greece (AP) - Greece has made new proposals aimed to end a long standoff with international bailout creditors and unlock vitally needed rescue funds, officials in Athens and Brussels said Tuesday.
4An official in the radical left-led government said the suggestions were submitted Monday to the European Union's economic and monetary affairs chief, Pierre Moscovici, in Brussels.
5He said the documents included alternative ideas on budget measures required for creditors to approve the bailout payment, and proposals on "a workable plan" to render Greece's crushing debt load viable.
6No further details were made public.
7The submission follows last week's impasse, when bailout creditors responded to a 47-page Greek proposal with a brief counter-offer that Athens rejected as containing "absurd" measures that would hurt the economy and worsen the lot of Greeks already reeling from five years of deep cuts and soaring unemployment.
8The two sides mainly disagree on creditors' demands for Athens to raise the sales tax on food, medicine and power bills, abandon plans to improve labor protection laws and further cut pensions.
9These would be extremely hard for the new government to implement - less than five months after its election on a combative anti-austerity platform.
10A deal must be struck before the end of June, when Greece's bailout program ends - and the pending 7.2 billion euro payment ($8 billion) will no longer be available.
11Without the cash, Greece will be unable to pay its creditors, and would default on its loans.
12While it still should be able to pay pensions and salaries, and keep the essential functions of state running through tax revenues, a default would likely kill its banks.
13The banks depend on emergency funding from the European Central Bank, but in the case of a government default, the state-backed guarantees they give the ECB as collateral for the emergency funding would become worthless overnight.
14The import-reliant country could then have to put limits on money transfers and withdrawals and see shortages of basic goods.
15It could even have to drop out of the eurozone as it adopts a devalued version of its old drachma currency to get liquid currency back into the banks and economy.
16The price of vital imported goods would rocket under the new, weaker currency.
17A spokesman for the European Commission - which together with the ECB and the International Monetary Fund, is overseeing the rescue program - said Moscovici met a Greek government delegation Monday "to exchange views on the situation."
18"We continue to be in discussion with the Greek authorities," Margaritis Schinas told a briefing in Brussels.
19"Diverse proposals are being circulated including new suggestions which were received earlier this morning ... The three institutions are currently assessing these suggestions with diligence and care."
20Schinas said that "if the conditions are there, there is always the possibility" that Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras could hold a new meeting Wednesday with Commission head Jean-Claude Juncker, in the context of an EU and Latin America summit in Brussels.
21At the summit, Tsipras is also expected to meet German Chancellor Angela Merkel and French President Francois Hollande - key drivers in any bailout deal with whom he has been in close contact for weeks.
22Greece has depended on international rescue loans since 2010, when its creditworthiness plunged and it was unable to finance itself by borrowing from financial markets.
23In exchange for the loans, it implemented deeply resented tax hikes and income cuts that managed to quickly tame extravagant budget deficits but backfired by shrinking the economy by a quarter and sending unemployment to a high of 28 percent.
24Failure to agree with creditors on new required reforms and cuts has left the country without rescue loans since last summer - during which time Greece has managed to pay both creditors and its key domestic obligations through tax revenue.
