7The Department of Justice released its report Wednesday on the Ferguson Police Department.
8The Justice Department on Wednesday cleared a white former Ferguson, Missouri, police officer in the fatal shooting of an unarmed black 18-year-old, but also issued a scathing report calling for sweeping changes in city law enforcement practices it called discriminatory and unconstitutional.
9The dual reports marked the culmination of months-long federal investigations into a shooting that sparked protests and a national dialogue on race and law enforcement as the tenure of Attorney General Eric Holder, the first black person to hold that office, draws to a close.
10In pairing the announcements, the Obama administration sought to offset community disappointment over the conclusion that the shooting of Michael Brown was legally justified with a message of hope for Ferguson's majority-black citizens.
11Officials announced 26 recommendations, including training officers in how to de-escalate confrontations and banning the use of ticketing and arrest quotas, for the police force and municipal court.
12Holder called the federal report a "searing" portrait of a police department that he said functions as a collection agency for the city, with officers prioritizing revenue from fines over public safety and trouncing the constitutional rights of minorities.
13"It is not difficult to imagine how a single tragic incident set off the city of Ferguson like a powder keg," Holder said.
14Mayor Knowles said the city had cooperated with the Justice Department and has made some changes, including a diversity training program for city employees.
15But the Rev.
16Al Sharpton, the chief eulogist at Brown's funeral, countered that Knowles' remarks - during a six-minute news conference where the mayor took no questions - were "mostly evasive, insignificant, and showed a total failure to address the need for a change in leadership at the police department."
17The decision not to prosecute Darren Wilson, the white officer who was cleared in November by a state grand jury and has since resigned, had been expected.
18To win a federal civil rights case, officials would have needed to prove that Wilson willfully deprived Brown of his rights by using unreasonable force.
19Instead, the report found no evidence to disprove Wilson's testimony that he feared for his safety during the Aug. 9 confrontation.
20Nor were there reliable witness accounts to establish that Brown had his hands up in surrender when he was shot, Justice Department lawyers said.
21One of Wilson's attorneys, Neil Bruntrager, said his client was satisfied with the outcome.
22Brown family lawyer Benjamin Crump said the family was not surprised but disappointed.
23Brown's uncle, Charles Ewing, said he believed Wilson was "getting away with it."
24While nights of protests over Wilson's actions drew hundreds of protesters in recent months in Ferguson, only about 30 braved sub-freezing weather to gather Wednesday night outside the suburb's police station, at times blocking traffic.
25By 10:30 p.m., most of the protesters had dispersed.
26While the federal government declined to prosecute Wilson, it found the shooting occurred in an environment of systematic mistreatment of blacks, in which officials circulated racist emails without punishment and black residents were disproportionately stopped and searched without cause, fined for petty offenses and subjected to excessive force.
27The report found its lack of racial diversity - only four of 54 commissioned officers are black - undermined community trust.
28It also said the city relied heavily on fines to raise revenue and issued arrest warrants for minor infractions including jaywalking and late fees.
29The confrontation that led to Brown's death began when Wilson directed him and a friend to move from the street to the sidewalk.
30The Justice Department identified multiple examples of what it called a discriminatory criminal justice system.
31Among them: One black woman spent six days in jail because of a parking violation.
32A lawful protest was broken up with a police warning of "everybody here's going to jail."
33And a black man sitting in a car with tinted windows was accused without cause of being a pedophile by an officer who pointed a gun at his head.
34Between 2012 and 2014, black drivers were more than twice as likely as others to be searched during routine traffic stops, but 26 percent less likely to be carrying contraband.
35The report also included seven racially tinged emails, including some from city officials who remain employed, that did not result in punishment.
36The writer of one 2008 email stated that President Barack Obama wouldn't be in office for long because "what black man holds a steady job for four years."
37Knowles, the mayor, said one employee was fired and two others are on leave over the emails.
38The report's recommendations, if accepted by city officials, could lead to an overhaul of basic police and court practices.
39Those include improving officer supervision, better recruiting, hiring and promotion, new mechanisms for responding to misconduct complaints and a new system to reduce fine amounts.
40Federal officials on Wednesday described Ferguson city leaders as cooperative and seemingly open to change, saying there were already some signs of improvement.
41The city, for example, has eliminated bond requirements for many municipal offenses and has extended a program that allows individuals to have warrants recalled and assigned a new court date.
42In the last five years, the Justice Department has investigated roughly 20 police departments over alleged civil rights violations.
43Some have led to the appointment of independent monitors and have been resolved with negotiated agreements in which the police department commits to major changes in its practices.
44Federal officials say they hope to avoid a court fight with Ferguson to force change.
45"It is time for Ferguson's leaders to take immediate, wholesale and structural corrective action," Holder said.
46THE BASIC FINDING: The police and municipal courts in Ferguson have been treating blacks unfairly, violating the law and the U.S. Constitution.
47Police use force too often.
48HOW THE INVESTIGATION WAS DONE: Representatives of the Justice Department interviewed city and court officials, police officers and residents.
49Police records were reviewed, along with court and police data.
50Justice Department representatives rode along with police officers and observed court sessions.
51" From 2012 to 2014, 93 percent of all arrests and 85 percent of all vehicle stops involved black people, who constitute two-thirds of the population.
52" Blacks were more than twice as likely as whites to be searched during vehicle stops, yet contraband was found 26 percent less often on them than on white drivers.
53" Almost 90 percent of documented force used by officers was used against blacks.
54" Police make many arrests for talking back to officers, recording police activities and engaging in lawful protest.
55" Blacks are 68 percent less likely than others to win dismissal of their cases.
56" Blacks represent 92 percent of cases in which an arrest warrant was issued in 2013.
57" The court often imposes large bail amounts that do not seem justified by public safety concerns.
58" City officials often fix tickets for themselves and their friends.
59" As a result of a single illegal parking violation in 2007, a black woman spent six days in jail, paid $550 in fines and still owed $541 as of December.
60" In 2013, police on their way to arrest someone at an apartment building instead arrested without justification a black man parked outside, handcuffed him and kept him in a patrol car while they ran his record. Faced with a complaint later, a police sergeant described the detention as "minimal" and said the car was air-conditioned.
61" In 2012, an officer stopped to question a 32-year-old black man sitting in his car with windows that may have been more deeply tinted than allowed under the city code. The officer went on without cause to call the man a pedophile, order him out of his car for a pat-down and ask to search the car. When the man refused, the officer reportedly pointed a gun at his head and arrested him.
62" Police broke up a lawful protest on the six-month anniversary of Michael Brown's death by shouting, "Everybody here's going to jail." Two people were arrested simply for recording the police action. Four others were arrested. It appears police were upset about insults written in chalk on the department parking lot and on a police vehicle.
