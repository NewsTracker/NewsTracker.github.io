14WASHINGTON (AP) - The Justice Department on Wednesday cleared a white former Ferguson, Missouri, police officer in the fatal shooting of an unarmed black 18-year-old, but also issued a scathing report calling for sweeping changes in city law enforcement practices it called discriminatory and unconstitutional.
15The dual reports marked the culmination of months-long federal investigations into a shooting that sparked protests and a national dialogue on race and law enforcement as the tenure of Attorney General Eric Holder, the first black person to hold that office, draws to a close.
16In pairing the announcements, the Obama administration sought to offset community disappointment over the conclusion that the shooting of Michael Brown was legally justified with a message of hope for Ferguson's majority-black citizens.
17Officials announced 26 recommendations, including training officers in how to de-escalate confrontations and banning the use of ticketing and arrest quotas, for the police force and municipal court.
18Holder called the federal report a "searing" portrait of a police department that he said functions as a collection agency for the city, with officers prioritizing revenue from fines over public safety and trouncing the constitutional rights of minorities.
19"It is not difficult to imagine how a single tragic incident set off the city of Ferguson like a powder keg," Holder said.
20Ferguson Mayor James Knowles III said the city had cooperated with the Justice Department and has made some changes, including a diversity training program for city employees.
21But the Rev.
22Al Sharpton, the chief eulogist at Brown's funeral, countered that Knowles' remarks - during a six-minute news conference where the mayor took no questions - were "mostly evasive, insignificant, and showed a total failure to address the need for a change in leadership at the police department."
23The decision not to prosecute Darren Wilson, the white officer who was cleared in November by a state grand jury and has since resigned, had been expected.
24To win a federal civil rights case, officials would have needed to prove that Wilson willfully deprived Brown of his rights by using unreasonable force.
25Instead, the report found no evidence to disprove Wilson's testimony that he feared for his safety during the Aug. 9 confrontation.
26Nor were there reliable witness accounts to establish that Brown had his hands up in surrender when he was shot, Justice Department lawyers said.
27One of Wilson's attorneys, Neil Bruntrager, said his client was satisfied with the outcome.
28Brown family lawyer Benjamin Crump said the family was not surprised but disappointed.
29Brown's uncle, Charles Ewing, said he believed Wilson was "getting away with it."
30"I really was hoping they would have come up with better findings because this whole thing just does not add up," Ewing said.
31While nights of protests over Wilson's actions drew hundreds of protesters in recent months in Ferguson, only about 30 braved sub-freezing weather to gather Wednesday night outside the suburb's police station, at times blocking traffic.
32By 10:30 p.m., most of the protesters had dispersed.
33While the federal government declined to prosecute Wilson, it found the shooting occurred in an environment of systematic mistreatment of blacks, in which officials circulated racist emails without punishment and black residents were disproportionately stopped and searched without cause, fined for petty offenses and subjected to excessive force.
34The report found its lack of racial diversity - only four of 54 commissioned officers are black - undermined community trust.
35It also said the city relied heavily on fines to raise revenue and issued arrest warrants for minor infractions including jaywalking and late fees.
36The confrontation that led to Brown's death began when Wilson directed him and a friend to move from the street to the sidewalk.
37The Justice Department identified multiple examples of what it called a discriminatory criminal justice system.
38Among them: One black woman spent six days in jail because of a parking violation.
39A lawful protest was broken up with a police warning of "everybody here's going to jail."
40And a black man sitting in a car with tinted windows was accused without cause of being a pedophile by an officer who pointed a gun at his head.
41Between 2012 and 2014, black drivers were more than twice as likely as others to be searched during routine traffic stops, but 26 percent less likely to be carrying contraband.
42The report also included seven racially tinged emails, including some from city officials who remain employed, that did not result in punishment.
43The writer of one 2008 email stated that President Barack Obama wouldn't be in office for long because "what black man holds a steady job for four years."
44Knowles, the mayor, said one employee was fired and two others are on leave over the emails.
45The report's recommendations, if accepted by city officials, could lead to an overhaul of basic police and court practices.
46Those include improving officer supervision, better recruiting, hiring and promotion, new mechanisms for responding to misconduct complaints and a new system to reduce fine amounts.
47Federal officials on Wednesday described Ferguson city leaders as cooperative and seemingly open to change, saying there were already some signs of improvement.
48The city, for example, has eliminated bond requirements for many municipal offenses and has extended a program that allows individuals to have warrants recalled and assigned a new court date.
49In the last five years, the Justice Department has investigated roughly 20 police departments over alleged civil rights violations.
50Some have led to the appointment of independent monitors and have been resolved with negotiated agreements in which the police department commits to major changes in its practices.
51Federal officials say they hope to avoid a court fight with Ferguson to force change.
52"It is time for Ferguson's leaders to take immediate, wholesale and structural corrective action," Holder said.
53Associated Press writers Jim Suhr and Jim Salter in St. Louis and Alan Scher Zagier in Ferguson contributed to this report.
