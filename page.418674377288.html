Greece escapes default to the ECB after emergency cash from the UK-funded EFSM

1Greece has narrowly escaped defaulting to the European Central Bank after it received an emergency bridge loan of 7.16 billion from the European Union.
2The funding came from the European Financial Stability Mechanism, a fund that non-eurozone European countries contribute to.
3UK Chancellor George Osborne had spoken out against use of the EFSM, but backed down on the assurance that the money would be guaranteed.
4Part of the money from the loan will be kept aside in an escrow fund as a guarantee.
5A European Commission spokeswoman confirmed that Athens has been sent the emergency loan that will allow it to repay 4.2 billion bond, plus 600m in interest to the ECB and escape from default that could have had dire consequences.
6A default would have put further assistance from the ECB in jeopardy - and it is financial aid from the ECB that is currently keeping the Greek banks functioning.
7Greek banks reopened on Monday after being closed for three weeks.
8A cap on withdrawals was switched from 60 a day to 420 a week - but Greeks still cannot transfer money out of the country or cash cheques.
9The bridge loan will also be used to pay 2 billion to the International Monetary Fund.
10Greece defaulted on an IMF payment of 1.55 billion on June 29, the same day that it called a referendum asking the Greek people if they wanted to accept the austerity demands of its creditors.
11Gerry Rice, a spokesman at the IMF confirmed that Greece was no longer in arrears to the organisation.
12"I can confirm that Greece today repaid the totality of its arrears to the IMF, equivalent to about 2 billion," Rice said.
