Baltimore police union defends 6 cops charged in Freddie Gray death

1Marilyn Mosby, Baltimore state's attorney, speaks during a media availability, Friday, May 1, 2015 in Baltimore.
2Mosby announced criminal charges against all six officers suspended after Freddie Gray suffered a fatal spinal injury in police custody.
3Saying "no one is above the law," Baltimore's top prosecutor announced charges Friday against six officers involved in the arrest of a black man whose neck was broken in police custody, a decision that comes amid outrage around the country over police brutality against African-Americans.
4State's Attorney Marilyn Mosby declared that Freddie Gray's death was a homicide, his arrest was illegal, and his treatment in custody amounted to murder and manslaughter.
5She said even though Gray requested medical help several times, officers repeatedly missed opportunities to get it for him.
6"The findings of our comprehensive, thorough and independent investigation, coupled with the medical examiner's determination that Mr. Gray's death was a homicide," Mosby said, "has led us to believe that we have probable cause to file criminal charges."
7In this Feb, 6, 2015 photo, State's Attorney Marilyn Mosby speaks at a news conference, in Baltimore.
8Onlookers cheered and expressed amazement over Mosby's announcement, which few expected so quickly.
9The city, which has been on edge and seen looting and a riot after Gray's death on April 19, was still under a nighttime curfew and National Guard troops and police were out in full force.
10Whoops, cheers and shouts of "Justice!" erupted on the steps of the War Memorial Building, and in the streets of Baltimore, which has faced nearly two weeks of growing anger over Gray's death.
11Mosby announced the charges one day after receiving the results of the internal police investigation and the autopsy report.
12As she spoke, the city braced for huge protests Friday and Saturday.
13"Mr. Gray suffered a severe and critical neck injury as a result of being handcuffed, shackled by his feet and unrestrained inside of the BPD wagon," she said.
14The stiffest charge -- second-degree "depraved heart" murder -- was filed against driver of the police van.
15The other five were charged with crimes including manslaughter, assault, false imprisonment and misconduct in office.
16Fraternal Order of Police local president Gene Ryan told Mosby in a letter before the charges were announced Friday that none of the six suspended officers were responsible for Gray's death.
17President Barack Obama said it was "absolutely vital that the truth comes out."
18He said he doesn't comment on the legal process, "but I can tell you that justice needs to be served."
19"Those individuals who are charged obviously are also entitled to due process and rule of law," he said.
20Mosby said Gray was illegally arrested and assaulted.
21He was handcuffed and then hoisted into the metal compartment of a police van without the seatbelt that all officers are told they must put on for safety of both detainees and officers.
22The van's driver failed to restrain Gray five different times, she said.
23The officers later failed to get medical help even though Gray requested it repeatedly, she said.
24At some point along the way, he suffered a mysterious spinal injury and died a week later.
25Mosby said what police described as an illegal switchblade -- Officer Garrett E. Miller swore in a court record under penalty of perjury that he found such a knife clipped inside Gray's pants pocket -- was actually a legal knife, and provided no justification for Gray's arrest.
26She said Gray was assaulted by Miller, Officer William G. Porter, Officer Edward M. Nero, Lt. Brian W. Rice and Sgt. Alicia D. White.
27Each faces up to 10 years if convicted of second-degree assault.
28The van driver, Officer Caesar R. Goodson Jr., faces up to 30 years on the murder charge, and 10 years each for involuntary manslaughter, assault and "manslaughter by vehicle."
29All of the officers also face a charge of misconduct in office.
30At least five were in custody, Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake said at an early afternoon news conference.
31She promised to change the police department's culture.
32"To those of you who wish to engage in brutality, misconduct, racism and corruptions, let me be clear. There is no place in the Baltimore City Police Department for you," she said.
33Mosby said she comes from five generations of police officers, that she respects and honors how police serve the people, and that this case should in no way damage the relationship between police and prosecutors in Baltimore.
34She swiftly rejected a request from the Baltimore police officers union asking her to appoint a special independent prosecutor because of her ties to attorney Billy Murphy, who is representing Gray's family.
35Murphy was among Mosby's biggest campaign contributors last year, donating the maximum individual amount allowed, $4,000, in June.
36Murphy also served on Mosby's transition team after the election.
37Before Gov. Larry Hogan visited a fire station Friday, a man leaning out of a passing truck window pumped both arms in the air and yelled, "Justice! Justice! Justice!"
38When Hogan arrived, he said he was focusing on keeping Baltimore safe.
39"I want to continue to ask for calm and peace," said Hogan, who on Monday declared a state of emergency and called in 2,000 National Guardsmen.
40At the corner of Pennsylvania and North avenues, where the worst of the rioting took place on Monday, drivers honked their horns.
41When buses stopped in front of the subway station, people spilled out cheering as the doors opened.
42There was no large gathering at the intersection immediately after the announcement, though: Nearly 100 police in riot gear were deployed, and for the moment, they had nothing to do.
43Ciara Ford, of Baltimore, expressed surprise at the decision to prosecute.
44They both hoped the officers would be convicted.
45And both believed that the protests in the city made a difference in ensuring that authorities took the case seriously.
46"If we had kept quiet, I don't think they would have prosecuted," Ford said.
47Community activist Ted Sutton surveyed the joyous scene with amazement.
48"You don't see people chanting. What you see is people celebrating," Sutton said.
49The charges, and Mosby's detailed explanation of what happened, are a first step toward transparency, he said.
50"She took the time to critique the evidence," Sutton said, noting that the officers faced different charges specific to their actual alleged misconduct.
51"To have each person charged with what they actually did . to have it come out this quick -- this is something else."
52Councilwoman Helen Holton said the decision to bring charges was a "defining moment" and noted other injustices against blacks, from Emmett Till, a teenager killed after flirting with a white woman in Mississippi in 1955, to Rodney King, who was beaten by Los Angeles police in 1991 to Walter Scott, who was fatally shot running away from an officer in South Carolina earlier this year.
53"My God, how many more black men must die before we say enough? Hopefully, Freddie Gray will be the last. If not, it is my fervent prayer that Baltimore city will lead the nation to say, 'We will take down those who violate the rights of citizens, any citizens,'" she said.
54"I'm psyched today! I'm like, 'Whoo!' My feet are not even touching the ground."
