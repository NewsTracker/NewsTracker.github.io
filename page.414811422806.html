Review: New 'Three Sisters' adaptation lands at Hayward's Douglas Morrisson Theatre

Liberia reopens schools as Ebola wanes

1Berkeley Repertory Theatre produced Sarah Ruhl's new version in 2011, and David Mamet, Michael Frayn and Lanford Wilson have all had a go at translating it.
2Now Hayward's Douglas Morrisson Theatre gives us the regional premiere of an adaptation by the provocative playwright Tracy Letts ("Killer Joe," "Bug" and the Pulitzer Prize-winning "August: Osage County").
3Commissioned by Portland's Artists Repertory Theatre, where it premiered in 2009, this version played in Letts' artistic home, Chicago's Steppenwolf Theatre Company, in 2012.
4Billed as a "lean and mean" version, Letts' adaptation is still 2 hours and 45 minutes long, but he modernizes the language, cussing freely and doing away with confusing nicknames and patronymics.
5Chekhov's play, which premiered in 1901, focuses on the Prosorov sisters, who live off in the boonies of Russia and keep talking about how much they want to go back to Moscow while doing nothing about it.
6The only real life in town is the garrison, whose troops have nothing better to do than to hang out at the sisters' house, drinking and philosophizing into the wee hours of the night.
7Born to the idle class, the characters rhapsodize a lot about the importance of work only to become exhausted and miserable when they actually try it.
8Mostly everyone sits around and watches their lives slip away.
9For all the refreshingly blunt dialogue in this translation, DMT artistic director Susan E. Evans' staging is slow moving.
10It's not just because not much happens at any given time, but because it's played very low-key and slightly mechanically, at least at first.
11An awkward first act is redeemed somewhat by the second, in which people who hadn't shown much sign of life till then have a little more to do.
12The titular sisters anchor the large cast nicely -- Daria Hepps as the fretful eldest, Olga; Kate Dunlop Tomatis as the bored and gloomy married sister, Masha; and especially Miriam Ani as the youngest, Irina, who still has some hope left to be crushed.
13Lindsey Marie Schmeltzer is pleasingly appalling as Natasha, the high-strung and controlling new wife of the sisters' listless brother, Andrey (a vague and distant Craig Dickerson).
14Gene Mocsy is amusingly clueless as Fyodor, Masha's dull but doting teacher husband, who's seemingly oblivious to his wife's romance with Vershinin (a blandly pleasant Brian Levi), the new military commander who's always waxing philosophical about the future and whether or not happiness is possible.
15Also among the ubiquitous soldiers are two unsuitable suitors for Irina, the nice but unstimulating Baron Tusenbach (a genial Paul Stout) and the obnoxious, mean-spirited Solyony (a sneering Tim Holt Jones).
16John Baldwin slumps wearily as a nihilistic old army doctor who confesses to have forgotten even the basics of medicine.
17John Hutchinson has some funny moments as an elderly and near-deaf messenger for the county council, and Marlene Walker is charmingly befuddled as the feeble old nanny.
18Nick Louie and Steve Allhoff are cheerful presences as two other soldiers who are always around but never become relevant, like guys you always see at parties but don't actually know their names.
19There are a few odd anachronisms in the design, such as Masha wearing what appears to be a black leather jacket with puffy 19th-century shoulder -- conspicuous among the other period costumes by Daisy Neske-Dickerson -- or three large fluorescent overhead lights being curiously prominent in Allen Willner's lighting design.
20One particularly clever touch is in Michael Locher's set of the elegant family manor; the tall green walls, smudged and streaked with age, close in tighter and tighter as the play goes on.
21It gives a strong sense of the sisters' world growing smaller, capturing that claustrophobic feeling of shrinking possibilities as effectively as anything actually happening between those walls.
1Monrovia: Thousands of Liberian children in pristine uniforms flocked back to school for the first time in six months, after a after a closure designed to stem the spread of the worst Ebola outbreak in history.
2Dozens of schools reopened on Monday, in a sign the outbreak is ebbing in Liberia, once its epicentre.
3The epidemic has killed more than 9,000 people there and in Sierra Leone and Guinea.
4The closures were yet another setback for a country whose health system and economy, based on diamonds, coffee and cocoa, were devastated by the virus.
5Students stand in line at Don Bosco High School, in the Monrovia.
6At the peak, last summer, Ebola patients were collapsing outside overflowing hospitals.
7Now there are only a handful of new cases a week.
8In the sunny courtyard of a Catholic school in the Congo Town district of the capital Monrovia, hundreds of students gathered to hear the principal's welcome.
9Medics took children's temperatures and told them not to stand too close together.
10"I feel very great being in school in the first day, most especially after a long period of time waiting for this day," said teenager Faith Sayeh.
11However, there were some empty desks in the classrooms, as some parents kept their children at home while other schools remained closed.
12"Only one of my seven children I have registered," said Lindsay Seakor, who lost her job last August due to the epidemic.
13She says she cannot afford to pay for books and uniforms.
14Some schools opened on Monday but had to send students home by mid-morning because teachers failed to turn up.
15George Wuo, a regional director of Liberia's Ministry of Education, said all schools in the country must open by March 2 or face fines.
16Neighbouring Guinea reopened most of its schools in January, but some parents have withdrawn children amid rumours schools were infected with the virus.
17Sierra Leone hopes to open schools by the end of next month.
18About 30 of its schools have been converted into treatment centres and will have to be emptied and decontaminated first.
19Liberia has more than 1 million enrolled students, some of whom have been keeping up with their lessons by radio.
20"Authorities in the three countries are looking at catchup sessions and cancelling some school vacations. It will be challenging," said Sayo Aoki, education specialist in Ebola Emergencies at the UN Children's Fund.
