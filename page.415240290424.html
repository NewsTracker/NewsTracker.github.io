Fires kill 900 in winter's deep freeze

A Clinton candidacy is weakened by string of revelations

1DENVER - Nearly 900 Americans have been killed in house fires during this winter's deep freeze, federal officials and the Red Cross say in urging Americans to safeguard against the danger as cold weather continues.
2At least 899 people have been killed in house fires since Dec. 1, federal officials said.
3That appears to be down from the 990 deaths in the same period the previous winter although statistics are still being compiled.
4"When temperatures go down, we see home fire numbers rise and deaths increase," said Anne Marie Borrego, a spokeswoman for the American Red Cross.
5Requests to help people following house fires have jumped from about 5,000 a month this fall to 8,000 in January with more than $1 million in additional aid given out compared to the summer, Red Cross officials said.
6House fires kill about 2,500 Americans annually.
7Cooking is the most common cause of fire, and smoking materials are the most common cause of fatal fires, according to the National Fire Protection Association.
8But more than a third of all house fire deaths happen in December, January and February, the association said.
9That's in part because people trying to stay warm overload electrical circuits, run improperly installed heaters and crank up their wood stoves.
10The cold also makes it hard to fight blazes because firefighters also are battling bitterly cold temperatures, deep snow and icy conditions, including slippery sidewalks and frozen hydrants.
11"It has been a deadly year," said Thomas Olshanski, a spokesman for the U.S. Fire Administration.
12"Michigan is seeing it. Wisconsin is seeing it. Everybody's seeing it. It extends even down into Texas and the South because the cold has been so far reaching."
13Detroit had several major fires this winter.
14On Feb. 17, a house fire in the suburb of Hamtramck killed a family of four, including two young boys.
15Six days later, a fire displaced 15 families from a 30-unit apartment building on the city's east side.
16In Massachusetts, an elderly woman and her son died Feb. 15 in a Waltham fire whipped by icy winds that made it hard to erect ladders, witnesses and firefighters said.
17On March 1, a fire destroyed a large home in Boston's Jamaica Plain neighborhood.
18And in the Chicago area, a man died March 4 after he jumped from the third floor of a condo building to escape a raging fire that took about 100 firefighters nearly 4 hours to control.
19This weekend - at 2 a.m. Sunday - begins the spring forward into Daylight Saving Time.
20The Red Cross reminds people to change the batteries in their smoke alarms as they move their clocks ahead.
21Keep all potential sources of fuel such as paper, clothing, bedding, curtains or rugs at least 3 feet away from space heaters, stoves or fireplaces.
22Don't leave portable heaters and fireplaces unattended.
23Turn off space heaters and make sure any embers in the fireplace are extinguished before going to bed or leaving home.
24Place space heaters on a level, hard and nonflammable surface (such as ceramic tile floor), not on rugs or carpets or near bedding or drapes.
25Keep children and pets away from space heaters.
26When buying a space heater, look for models that shut off automatically if the heater falls over.
27Never use a cooking stove or oven to heat your home.
28Use a glass or metal fire screen to keep your fire in your fireplace.
29Make sure it's large enough to catch sparks and rolling logs.
30Have wood and coal stoves, fireplaces, chimneys and furnaces professionally inspected and cleaned once a year.

2Washington: Hillary Clinton is suddenly a weaker presidential candidate.
3While the former secretary of state remains a strong favourite to win the Democratic presidential nomination, she's now more vulnerable to a party challenger - and, perhaps more ominously, more likely to give an already-alienated electorate new reasons to drop out of the political process.
4The furore over foreign money and secret emails may be urging Ms Clinton to engage politically more quickly than she planned.
5She issued her first personal statement near midnight on Wednesday night to address the latest concern - that she'd used a private email account to conduct United States State Department business.
6She's been added to the list of speakers alongside her husband and daughter at a Saturday meeting of the Clinton Global Initiative.
7And she may be moving the formal launch of her campaign forward.
8Ms Clinton strove to defuse the latest controversy with a late-night tweet that tried, likely without success, to turn the tide of the email stories and make it appear she was the one demanding transparency.
9"I want the public to see my email," she said, and added that she'd asked the State Department to release it.
