Christian students at Kenya's Garissa University foretold massacre

1Kitengela, Kenya: As Stanley Muli hid in a wardrobe from attacking Somali militants last week, he wondered why the army was taking so long to arrive.
2For two hours after the attack at 5:30am Thursday, no one came to the rescue.
3Muli, listening to al-Shabab fighters searching rooms and killing terrified Garissa University College students, thought bitterly about how quickly the army had arrived in November to brutally put down a student protest over the lack of university security.
4"I was just praying to God that the (army) would come," the 22-year-old said Sunday.
5"I was just thinking how come they have taken so long, because the barracks are near."
6In a town long known for violent extremist attacks, the campus of mainly Christian students was an obvious target in a predominantly Muslim area within striking distance of Somalia, 90 miles away.
7Students said they felt unsafe and exposed-put in harm's way by the government itself.
8"We were fearing that if these people (a-Shaban) came, they could kill many, many Christians," said Muli, who was shot in the thigh but survived in his hiding place.
9He said the government "failed to protect us. We are angry, because we lost some of our best friends. ... They took no care."
10Garissa University College was inaugurated in 2011.
11It was the first university in northeastern Kenya, but its first full-year intake was in 2013.
12Students said almost no one wanted to be there because of Garissa's security problem, but they were declined spots on the mother campus, Moi University in Eldoret.
13Most wanted to transfer, but found it impossible.
14"It's like we were being experimented on. When this university was being put in that place, I don't think it was the right place," said Gideon Nyabwengi, 19, who escaped death by crouching behind the low, half-built wall of a washroom.
15"When we went to that university, we thought what kind of university is this? The lack of security was a major thing. When you got your letter of admission to Garissa some people were saying it wasn't safe to go. This thing was being predicted," he said.
16Some of his friends told him he should get a gun if he was going to study at Garissa.
17Others said they would pray for him.
18When the attack arrived, it was pitiless.
19Hiding, Nyabwengi heard his best friend beg for his life, pretending to be a Muslim.
20When the friend was unable to recite a Muslim prayer, he was shot to death, Nyabwengi said.
21"I heard them shouting, 'We've come to kill and be killed.' They would go and bring some students out of the dormitories and shoot them. (The students) were told to lie down, then I'd hear gunshots, gunshots and crying."
22In the three hours before he escaped, al-Shabab continued killing unimpeded.
23Students said Kenya's army eventually surrounded the campus but didn't overcome al-Shabab or end the killings.
24A crack police squad, the General Service Unit, didn't arrive until about 4:30pm, 11 hours after the attack, according to Kenyan media reports.
25The police ended the siege 30 minutes later.
26Furious students and their parents question why the Garissa campus was left so poorly guarded, especially after recent, widely circulated intelligence warnings of an impending attack on a university.
27Students from a neighbouring teachers college in Garissa were sent home Tuesday, two days before the attack, because of the uncertain security situation.
28In January 2013 and again last September, Garissa residents protested over insecurity for non-indigenous residents in the town, after many killings, including shootings of hotel patrons.
29In November, students angry at the lack of security at Garissa University College held a strike and demonstration.
30They demanded extra police guards and a fence.
31There were requests the campus be relocated.
32Although the fence was built, survivors of Thursday's attack said authorities did not take the university's security problem seriously enough.
33Ironically, the high metal fence made it harder for some to escape.
34When Nyabwengi tried to vault the fence, he fell back down at the first attempt and was shot in the arm.
35On the second effort, he managed to get away.
36But he said that female students had a harder time scaling the fence.
37Nyabwengi said he and many of his friends tried unsuccessfully to get a transfer.
38Instead, students were told at orientation that security came down to the individual, with warnings that girls should avoid wearing miniskirts.
39Nyabwengi said locals on occasion threw stones at female students in the town, apparently because they didn't like the way they were dressed.
40Muli said that at his orientation last year, students were warned that local people had tried to strip a female student for wearing a miniskirt.
41When al-Shabab claimed responsibility Thursday for the attack, it said the university was on Muslim land and was there to promote "missionary activities and to spread deviant ideology".
42Students said very few of the students on the campus were Muslims and even fewer were members of the local population.
43There had been local protests that few Garissa people had gotten jobs on the campus.
44There was also tension over the presence of a mosque on the campus, used by the local community to pray.
45Students protested that those using the mosque weren't subject to security checks.
46Seeing it as an added source of insecurity, they called for it to be moved off campus.
47When the gunmen arrived, their first target was an early morning Christian prayer meeting.
48Of 29 students there, just seven survived.
49"We were praying," said Duncan Obwamu, 25.
50"We were in a circle in the room holding hands."
51First, the barrel of a gun appeared at the door.
52Then it fired, striking a young woman leading prayers.
53An al-Shabab gunman stepped into the room and continued firing.
54"He didn't say anything, but you could see from the look on his face he was very happy," he said.
55Obwamu, hit in the arm and shoulder, covered in the blood of other students, lay still as the gunman kicked the bodies to be sure they were dead.
56"I heard him laugh as he communicated with the others outside. He was very happy about what he'd done."
57Muli's father, Joseph Mwavu, 50, a mason, said he was unhappy when his son got an admission letter to Garissa University College, "because the whole world knows that anywhere near the Somali border is dangerous."
58He said if help had arrived earlier on the day of the attack, many students could have been saved.
59"I'm very bitter because (the government) had all the information. They knew what was happening but they took so many hours to respond," Mwavu said.
