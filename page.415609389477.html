Brazil's attorney general wants Congress to approve anticorruption measures

Retreating Boko Haram leaves mass of throat-slit corpses near Nigerian town

1Rodrigo Janot made the proposal just two days after President Dilma Rousseff announced similar measures in the wake of last weekend's massive rallies calling for her impeachment and protesting rampant corruption in Brazil.
2Both Janot and Rousseff call for the criminalization of slush funds used to finance election campaigns, the seizure of assets of people found guilty of corruption, and the requirement that government officials have no record of crimes.
3The attorney general's proposals also would classify corruption as a heinous crime punishable by up to 15 years in prison.
4They would make illicit enrichment a crime, with penalties determined by the amount of money involved.
5"Corruption steals food, medicine and the education of Brazilians. He who steals millions, kills millions," federal prosecutor Delton Dallagnol said at the event where the proposals were announced.
6"Like homicide, corruption kills, and leaving Brazil without health or education is a heinous crime," said Dallagnol who is coordinating the investigation into the corruption-kickback scandal engulfing Brazil's state-owned oil company, Petrobras.
7Federal prosecutors say at least $800 million in bribes were paid by construction and engineering firms to politically appointed former executives at the oil company in exchange for winning inflated contracts.
8Investigators say some of the money was funneled back to the campaign coffers of the Workers' Party and its allies.
9Dozens of lawmakers and some former executive branch officials, including two former chiefs of staff to Rousseff, are under investigation.
10The president, who served as chairwoman of Petrobras' board during several years when the graft occurred, is not implicated.
1Damasak, Nigeria: Soldiers from Niger and Chad who liberated the Nigerian town of Damasak from Boko Haram militants have discovered the bodies of at least 70 people scattered under a bridge, a witness said.
2In what appeared to be an execution site for the Islamist group, the bodies were strewn beneath the concrete bridge on one of the main roads leading out of the town.
3The bodies were partially mummified by the dry desert air, suggesting that the killings had taken place some time ago.
4Weapons captured from Boko Haram by Chadian and Nigerien soldiers are seen in the recently retaken town of Damasak.
5Boko Haram has killed thousands of people in a six-year insurgency aimed at establishing an Islamic caliphate in northeast Nigeria.
6Damasak was seized by the Islamist group in November but recaptured by troops from Niger and Chad on Saturday as part of a multinational effort to wipe out the militants.
7Chadian soldiers, who said the bodies were discovered on Thursday, spoke of at least 100 corpses in the area around the dry river bed.
8A witness was able to count at least 70.
9A trail of blackened blood was visible along the side of the bridge facing the bodies, suggesting they had been thrown off the side after being killed.
10Among the dead was the imam of the town.
11A signpost painted by Boko Haram is seen in the recently retaken town of Damasak, Nigeria, near where the massacre of its residents occurred.
12All but around 50 of the town's residents had fled by the time Damasak was recaptured.
13Those who remained were mostly too old or too sick to leave.
14The witness said a strong smell of decomposition in many parts of town suggested there could be more bodies concealed there.
15Chad's military spokesman Colonel Azem Bermandoa said the Chadians had asked Nigeria's military to occupy the town, which lies close to the border with Niger, and would remain there until Nigerian troops arrived.
16The regional offensive launched this year with Chad, Niger and Cameroon comes as Nigeria, Africa's most populous country and biggest economy, prepares to hold presidential elections on March 28.
17Nigerian troops cross the Kaffin-Hausa bridge, which earlier had been destroyed by terrorists and reconstructed by military engineers at Damasak in north-eastern Nigeria Borno State.
18At the start of this year, Boko Haram controlled around 20 local government areas, a territory the size of Belgium.
19With the help of its foreign allies, Nigeria's army said on Tuesday it had pushed the rebels out of all but three districts.
20On Thursday, however, two security sources said that Boko Haram had killed at least 10 people in the town of Gamburu, on the border with Cameroon, demonstrating it can still attack civilians despite being forced into retreat.
21President Goodluck Jonathan has been criticised for not doing enough to tackle the insurgency.
22His challenger Muhammadu Buhari has campaigned on a reputation for toughness gained when he was military ruler of Nigeria in the 1980s.
