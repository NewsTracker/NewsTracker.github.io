Baltimore on edge: National Guardsmen take up positions

1Baltimore was a city on edge Tuesday as hundreds of National Guardsmen patrolled the streets against unrest for the first time since 1968, hoping to prevent another outbreak of rioting.
2Maryland's governor said 2,000 Guardsmen and 1,000 law officers would be in place overnight to try to head off a repeat of the racially charged violence that erupted Monday in some of the city's poorest neighborhoods and sent a shudder through all of Baltimore.
3"This combined force will not tolerate violence or looting," Gov. Larry Hogan warned.
4In a measure of how tense things were, the city was under a 10 p.m.-to-5 a.m. emergency curfew.
5All public schools were closed.
6And the Baltimore Orioles canceled Tuesday night's game at Camden Yards and - in what may be a first in baseball's 145-year history - announced that Wednesday's game will be closed to the public.
7The streets were largely calm all day and into the evening, with only a few scattered arrests.
8The real test was expected after dark, when the curfew was set to take effect.
9The looting, arson and rock- and bottle-throwing by mostly black rioters broke out just hours after the funeral of Freddie Gray, a 25-year-old black man who suffered a fatal spinal cord injury while in police custody.
10It was the worst such violence in the U.S. since the unrest that erupted last year over the death of Michael Brown, the unarmed black 18-year-old shot by a white police officer in Ferguson, Missouri.
11Political leaders and residents called the violence a tragedy for the city and lamented the damage done by the rioters to their own neighborhoods.
12"The same community they say they care about, they're destroying. You can't have it both ways," Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake said.
13But the rioting also brought out a sense of civic pride and responsibility in many Baltimore residents, with hundreds of volunteers turning out to sweep the streets of glass and other debris with brooms and trash bags donated by hardware stores.
14Blanca Tapahuasco brought her three sons, ages 2 to 8, from another part of the city to help clean up the brick-and-pavement courtyard outside a looted CVS pharmacy in the hard-hit neighborhood where Gray was arrested.
15"We're helping the neighborhood build back up," she said.
16"This is an encouragement to them to know the rest of the city is not just looking on and wondering what to do."
17CVS store manager Haywood McMorris said the destruction didn't make sense: "We work here, man. This is where we stand, and this is where people actually make a living."
18All day, police fielded rumors of would-be rioters gathering at various places in and around Baltimore.
19As evening fell, police with riot shields lined up shoulder to shoulder across West North Avenue and kept close watch on a chanting crowd of about 1,000 people at the corner where some of the worst violence took place the night before.
20The gathering resembled a street festival, with musicians playing in the intersection.
21Separately, several hundred demonstrators marched through the streets toward downtown Baltimore as the curfew drew near.
22The crisis marks the first time the National Guard has been called out to deal with unrest in Baltimore since 1968, when some of the same neighborhoods that rose up this week burned for days after the assassination of the Rev.
23Martin Luther King Jr. At least six people died then, and some neighborhoods still bear the scars.
24Jascy Jones of Baltimore said the sight of National Guardsmen on the street gave her a "very eerie feeling."
25"It brought a tear to my eye. Seeing it doesn't feel like the city that I love," she said.
26"I am glad they're here, but it's hard to watch."
27At the White House, President Barack Obama called the deaths of several black men around the country at the hands of police "a slow-rolling crisis."
28But he added that there was "no excuse" for the violence in Baltimore, and said the rioters should be treated as criminals.
29The rioting started in West Baltimore on Monday afternoon and by midnight had spread to East Baltimore and neighborhoods close to downtown and near the baseball stadium.
30At least 20 officers were hurt, one person was critically injured in a fire, more than 200 adults and 34 juveniles were arrested, and nearly 150 cars were burned, police said.
31The governor had no immediate estimate of the damage.
32With the city bracing for more trouble, several colleges closed early Tuesday, including Loyola University Maryland, Johns Hopkins University and Towson University.
33The violence set off soul-searching among community leaders and others, with some suggesting the uprising was about more than race or the police department - it was about high unemployment, high crime, poor housing, broken-down schools and lack of opportunity in Baltimore's inner-city neighborhoods.
34The city of 622,000 is 63 percent black.
35The mayor, state's attorney, police chief and City Council president are black, as is 48 percent of the police force.
36"You look around and see unemployment. Filling out job applications and being turned down because of where you live and your demographic. It's so much bigger than the police department," said Robert Stokes, 36, holding a broom and a dustpan on a corner where some of the looting and vandalism took place.
37In the aftermath of the riots, state and local authorities found themselves facing questions about whether their initial response had been adequate.
38Batts, the police commissioner, said police did not move in faster because those involved in the early stages were just "kids" - teenagers who had just been let out of school.
39"Do you want people using force on 14- 15- and 16-year-old kids that are out there?" he asked.
40"They're old enough to know better. But they're still kids. And so we had to take that into account while we were out there."
41Rawlings-Blake said officials initially thought they had the unrest under control.
42Gray was arrested April 12 after running away at the sight of police, authorities said.
43He was held down, handcuffed and loaded into a police van.
44Leg cuffs were put on him when he became irate inside.
45He died a week later.
46Authorities said they are still investigating how and when he suffered the spinal injury - during the arrest or while he was in the van, where authorities say he was riding without being belted in, a violation of department policy.
47Six officers have been suspended with pay in the meantime.
