Review: New Apple Photos app makes fixing, cropping easy

1The app makes organizing and editing photos easy by bringing many professional-level tools to a free consumer package.
2This screen shot shows picture adjustment tools, at right, within Apple's new Photos app for Mac computers.
3This screen shot shows the "moments view" within Apple's new Photos app for Mac computers.
4NEW YORK (AP) - If you're like most people, those hundreds of photos you took on vacation are still on your camera or phone.
5You shared a handful on Facebook or Instagram, and tell yourself that you'll sift through the others - one day.
6Procrastinate no more.
7Apple's new Photos app for Mac computers, available Wednesday as a free software update, makes it easy to organize and edit your pictures.
8The app, which replaces iPhoto, bundles professional-level tools such as granular color correction into one free consumer package.
9Like other free apps such as Google's Picasa, Photos is good for auto-enhancing, cropping and other basic touches such as lightening underexposed shots.
10But it goes further by also including some of the advanced fine-tuning you'd find in a tool like Adobe Lightroom, which costs $149.
11If you already use Photos on your iPhone or iPad, you'll see many similarities.
12Images are organized automatically, partly using location information embedded in the pictures.
13You can also view photos on a map.
14The Mac's app goes further in using face-detection technology to group photos by the people in them.
15Click on any photo to begin editing.
16The Enhance button alone will improve many shots.
17The Adjust tool enhances lighting, color and other attributes separately.
18Each attribute has an auto button along with a slider you can adjust.
19Click an arrow to unveil the advanced controls.
20I like to adjust something called white balance to compensate for, say, the yellowish glow of indoor lighting.
21Cameras do this automatically, but not always correctly.
22In pictures taken on a recent trip, a friend's baby looked too blue, and a waterfall looked too yellow.
23Photos fixed those quickly, just by hitting "auto."
24Lightroom usually requires more steps to correct similar issues.
25Photos has a lot of cropping options, though my favorite is the auto button.
26It straightens photos based on the horizon, among other features.
27My only complaint is it takes a few extra steps to make sure the cropped image retains the original's dimensions.
28I hope a future update will let me set that as the default.
29With a new iCloud Photo Library online-storage service, all your mobile photos will sync to the Mac app, along with your iPhoto albums.
30You can import additional photos, including those in cameras' proprietary RAW formats, which many pros prefer using.
31All images are stored online in high resolution, whether they were taken on an iPhone or imported from another camera.
32Your entire library is then accessible on all your devices, and any edits you make will sync.
33By storing full-resolution images online, Photos can free up space on your Mac or mobile device by substituting a lower-quality version.
34You can still get the original whenever you need it, but it's not taking up room if you don't.
35Photos figures all that out for you and takes into account how much space you have.
36Any photo you delete disappears from all your devices simultaneously, but don't fear, you have about a month to retrieve it from the cloud.
37Check the Mac's App Store for version 10.10.3 of the Mac system.
38Turn on iCloud Photo Library on your Mac and mobile devices when you see the prompts.
39You may need to buy more iCloud storage through Apple, as the 5 free gigabytes only translates to roughly 3,000 iPhone photos, not to mention video or larger files from stand-alone cameras.
