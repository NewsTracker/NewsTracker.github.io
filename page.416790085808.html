Germanwings co-pilot tested descents hours before crash

1PARIS (AP) - A co-pilot who purposely crashed a Germanwings A320 plane into the French Alps appeared to have rehearsed sending the jet into a deadly descent on another flight just two hours earlier, investigators said Wednesday.
2Authorities are still puzzling over why Andreas Lubitz, who had suffered from suicidal tendencies and depression in the past, locked the captain out of the cockpit on March 24 and sent Flight 9525 from Barcelona to Duesseldorf straight into a mountain, killing all 150 people on board.
3The revelation about the earlier flight that day from Duesseldorf to Barcelona appeared to support the theory that the Germanwings crash was not only deliberate but premeditated.
4It came in a 30-page interim report from the French accident investigation agency BEA.
5FILE - In this March 26, 2015 file photo, rescue workers work on debris of the Germanwings jet at the crash site near Seyne-les-Alpes, France.
6The co-pilot of Germanwings Flight 4525 tried a controlled descent on the previous flight that morning to Barcelona before the plane crashed into a mountainside in March on its way back to Germany, French air accident investigators said in a new report released Wednesday May, 6, 2015.
7The development also raised questions about all flights where Lubitz was in the cockpit but BEA told The Associated Press that, due to practical considerations, it would not investigate those flights.
8Lubitz seemed to be toying with the airplane's settings on the flight into Barcelona, programming it for a sharp descent multiple times in a 4 1/2-minute period while the pilot was out of the cockpit before resetting the controls, the report said.
9Unlike the later flight, he did not lock the pilot out of the cockpit.
10On the flight to Barcelona, the plane's "selected altitude" changed repeatedly and several times was set as low as 100 feet (30 meters) above sea level.
11The report says Lubitz also put the engines on idle, which gives the plane the ability to quickly descend.
12On the return flight to Germany, Lubitz also set a 100-foot altitude before the plane crashed into the Alps.
13Aviation experts say it would be highly unusual for a pilot to repeatedly set a plane for such a low altitude for no apparent reason.
14But the report said Lubitz did so while air traffic controllers were asking him to bring the airplane down gradually from 35,000 feet to 21,000 feet for its descent to Barcelona.
15A BEA chart showed the plane didn't actually descend sharply while Lubitz was repeatedly adjusting the settings, so the passengers and crew might not have noticed any change.
16"The captain didn't realize at all, because the co-pilot's tests during the outgoing flight took place during a normal, preprogrammed descent and it never had an impact on the plane's trajectory," said Remi Jouty, the director of BEA.
17Aviation experts said the BEA findings were clearly unusual.
18Lubitz changed the setting in the "altitude select" window, although the airplane didn't move in response to the inputs, said aviation safety expert John Cox, president of Safety Operating Systems.
19"It's clear that it's a very unusual act," Cox said.
20"I've never seen it done, and it is the same methodology he used to fly the airplane into the ground. Was he practicing? I think that certainly is a possibility."

23Even if Germanwings had done so, it probably would have taken a while to notice a problem - or such odd readings might have dismissed such readings as an anomaly, he said.
24Many airlines have flight data monitoring programs to look for safety trends.
25"They don't analyze every recorder every day for every minute it recorded," Goglia said.
26"The lag time is about 30 days to analyze it," he said.
27A BEA official said information from flight recorders is often erased, and is only systematically drawn upon in cases of air incidents or disasters.
28"We're not going to verify whether he had tried to select 100 feet (altitudes) in other flights," BEA spokeswoman Martine del Bono told the AP.
29With the Barcelona-bound Germanwings flight data, "we have here the previous proof."
30Germanwings "welcomed any new information that can help clarify what happened," but said it would not comment on the ongoing BEA investigation.
31It would also not comment on whether it was reviewing other flights involving Lubitz.
32Lufthansa, the parent company of Germanwings, had no immediate comment.
33The BEA report didn't analyze why Lubitz repeatedly tried to descend the plane going into Barcelona.
34But the agency said it was continuing to look at "systemic failings that may have led to this accident or similar events."
35The BEA investigators said they are focusing on "the current balance between medical confidentiality and flight safety" - a reference to whether German rules on medical patient privacy may have prevented Lufthansa from being fully aware of Lubitz' mental health issues.
36The agency is also looking at "compromises" made on security following the Sept.

38Since the Germanwings crash, several airlines have imposed rules requiring two people in the cockpit at all times.
39It remains unclear why Lubitz would have wanted to crash the plane.
40German authorities say he researched suicide methods and cockpit door security online in the week before the crash.
