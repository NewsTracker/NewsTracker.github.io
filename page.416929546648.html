Kerry to explore Putin's flexibility on Ukraine, Syria

1U.S. Secretary of State John Kerry waves as he boards his airplane before departing for Russia and Turkey from Andrews Air Force Base in Maryland May 11, 2015.
2SOCHI, Russia U.S. Secretary of State John Kerry hopes to explore Russia's willingness to curb its involvement in Ukraine and its support for Syria's president at talks on Tuesday with President Vladimir Putin.
3Kerry flew to the Black Sea resort of Sochi for the highest-level U.S. visit to Russia in two years, to discuss issues including the Iran nuclear talks, Yemen and Libya.
4With relations between Russia and the United States at their lowest level since the Cold War, the trip appeared designed as much to maintain contact as anything else.
5"It's important for us to keep these lines of communication open. It's important to try to talk to the senior decision-maker," said a senior U.S. State Department official who briefed reporters traveling with Kerry.
6"We have a lot of business that we could do together if there is interest," said the official, who spoke on condition of anonymity.
7Putin's spokesman, Dmitry Peskov, welcomed the meeting as a positive step.
8"Through dialogue, it is possible to find ways for a normalization, closer coordination in dealing with international problems," he Peskov reporters.
9But he added: "Russia was never the initiator of this cooling of relations."
10Kerry is also expected to meet Russian Foreign Minsiter Sergei Lavrov while in Sochi.
11Relations between Washington and Moscow have sunk since Russia annexed the Crimea peninsula in March of last year and backed pro-Russian rebels in eastern Ukraine.
12Moscow accuses Washington of orchestrating last year's overthrow of a Ukrainian president who was backed by Russia.
13The United States has accused Russia of failing to withdraw heavy equipment such as air defense systems, tanks and artillery from eastern Ukraine in violation of a peace plan agreed in February and known as Minsk 2.
14Russia denies Western and Ukrainian accusations that it is arming the pro-Russian separatists battling the government and supporting them with its own military forces.
15More than 6,100 people have been killed since April 2014 in the Ukraine crisis.
16The United States and European Union imposed economic sanctions on Russia after it annexed Crimea and have intensified them since.
17The U.S. official dangled the possibility of easing them if Russia complied with the Minsk plan, which calls for withdrawing heavy weaponry and respecting Ukraine's border.
18Washington and Moscow are also at odds over the civil war in Syria, where Russia has backed Syrian President Bashar al-Assad while the United States wants a political transition to end his family's four-decade rule.
19While there have been no outward signs of a Russian reversal on Ukraine or Syria, U.S. officials hope recent defeats to Assad's forces may change Moscow's stance.
20The senior U.S. official also said it was important to meet Putin to discuss the Iran nuclear talks, which aim to reach an agreement by June 30 under which Tehran would curb its atomic program in exchange for the easing of economic sanctions.
