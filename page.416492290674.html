North America's largest powwow draws thousands to New Mexico

1ALBUQUERQUE, N.M. (AP) - Droves of Native American and other indigenous performers and spectators have converged in New Mexico for the 32nd annual Gathering of Nations, North America's largest powwow.
2More than 3,000 dancers, drummers and singers representing some 700 tribes from the United States and Canada typically attend the three-day festival in Albuquerque.
3The competitions also draw tens of thousands of spectators from around the world.
4This year's celebration started Thursday.
5Dancers file into the arena in a large procession during grand entries Friday and Saturday.
6They don traditional costumes with colorful beads, feathers, fringed leather and bells.
7Their pounding feet keep time with beating drums as they funnel into the bottom of the University of New Mexico arena, better known as The Pit.
8The participants join the procession in a specific order and then twist toward the arena's center.
9The formation gets tighter with each pass until the floor is packed.
10Grand entries can last close to an hour.
11Twenty-one women are competing this year for the crown of Miss Indian World.
12The contest is one of the largest and most prestigious cultural pageants for young Native women.
13They're judged on public speaking, dance, traditional presentations and more.
14Current Miss Indian World Taylor Thomas will pass on the crown to this year's winner Saturday.
15The winner will spend the next year traveling internationally, serving as a role model and cultural ambassador.
16This year's powwow was preceded by a special gathering to kick off a White House initiative called Generation-Indigenous, or Gen-I.
17About 300 Native youth from across the country met Thursday to discuss individual community projects aimed at tackling some of the challenges faced by American Indian and Alaska Native teens, from high suicide rates to low graduation rates.
18Like any good gathering, the powwow has music, vendors and fry bread.
19At the Indian Traders Market, more than 800 artisans and crafters show off their wares.
20On Stage 49 near the arena, Native American musicians perform a range of genres, including country, reggae, rap and rhythm and blues.
21Organizers expect anywhere from 80,000 to 100,000 visitors, providing a boost to Albuquerque's hospitality industry.
22According to a recent study by the Albuquerque Hispano Chamber of Commerce, the Gathering of Nations has had an average annual economic impact of about $21 million over the past five years.
