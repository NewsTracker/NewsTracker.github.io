The Latest on Nepal: Banks, ATMs reopen in Kathmandu

1Nepalese people block traffic and shout slogans against the government as they protest the slow pace of aid delivery for the earthquake affected in Kathmandu, Nepal, Wednesday, April 29, 2015.
2The protesters numbering about 200 faced off with police and there were minor scuffles but no arrests were made.
3In another sign of life inching back to normal, banks in Kathmandu opened for few hours Wednesday and stuffed their ATMs with cash.
4At the Standard Chartered Bank in the city, people are lining up.
5"I needed money in cash so I can take my family out of Kathmandu. I want to be out of here for at least a few days," said Suraj Shrestha.
6He wasn't sure if ATMs outside the city were dispensing cash and wanted to carry "as much as possible."
7About 200 people have blocked traffic in Kathmandu to protest the slow pace of aid delivery.
8The protesters faced off with police and there were minor scuffles but no arrests were made.
9One protester says they haven't received any relief.
10"We are hungry, we haven't had anything to drink. We haven't been able to sleep. I have a 7-year-old child who is sleeping in the open. It's getting cold and people are getting pneumonia," he said.
11He accused the government of not doing enough.
12The death toll has climbed to 5,093 and more than 8 million people have been affected.
13Police in Nepal say the death toll from Saturday's earthquake has topped 5,000.
14The Kathmandu police say 4,989 have died and another 10,260 people have been injured in Nepal.
15The quake that was centered just outside Kathmandu also triggered an avalanche that killed at least 18 people at the Everest base camp.
16Another 61 were killed in neighboring India and Bangladesh, and China's official Xinhua News Agency reported 25 dead in Tibet.
17Police have arrested dozens of people on suspicion of looting abandoned homes as well as causing panic by spreading rumors of another big quake.
18Police official Bigyan Raj Sharma says 27 have been detained for stealing from homes whose owners moved out following Saturday's quake and powerful aftershocks.
19The aftershocks have waned but people are still anxious, many preferring to stay out in the open.
20Sharma says another four people were arrested for spreading false rumors of an impending quake through social media and text messages.
21The first aid shipments have reached Dhading district, close to the epicenter of the devastating earthquake in Nepal.
22U.N. food agency emergency officer Geoff Pinnock says the distribution in remote quake-hit villages will start Wednesday, but cautions it would take time.
23In Gorkha, the neighboring district to the west, five cargo trucks filled with rice, cooking oil and sugar stood on a grassy field in Majuwa village waiting for a helicopter from Kathmandu to take the supplies to the hardest-hit areas of that district.
24The World Food Program also expects the delivery of high-energy biscuits, which will be send out to areas without enough water for cooking.
25A man pulled from the rubble of a collapsed building in Kathmandu more than three days after the deadly Nepal earthquake says he drank his own urine to survive.
26Rishi Khanal tells The Associated Press that he had given up all hope of rescue as his lips cracked and his nails turned white.
27There were dead bodies around him and a terrible smell.
28But he kept banging on the rubble all around him and eventually this brought a French rescue team that extracted him after an operation lasting many hours.
29He's now being treated for leg injuries at a hospital in the capital the day after French rescuers found him and brought him out from a collapsed hotel.
30He had been buried for 82 hours.
31While many villages across Nepal are still waiting for rescue and relief teams, life in the capital, Kathmandu, is slowly returning to normal.
