Refugee situation on Kos eased as police work around the clock

1Greek police registration officers working around the clock greatly have reduced the numbers of Syrian refugees stuck in miserable conditions on the holiday island of Kos.
2Hundreds arrive on the island daily in packed boats from Turkey.
3A rudimentary processing centre set up in a stadium on Kos was running much more smoothly after police issued temporary travel papers to at least 1,000 men, women and children who had been penned inside for up to a day without adequate shelter, food or drinking water.
4But large numbers of refugees remained camped on a tree-lined coastal promenade outside the stadium waiting to register.
5About 7,000 Middle Eastern migrants, mainly Syrians, reached the eastern Aegean Sea island last month - twice as many as in June - and up to 5,000 are thought to be stuck there waiting for the documents that will let them travel on toward wealthier European countries.
6Kos mayor Giorgos Kyritsis said: "The registration process is going very well, it's very important to say that (police) were working until 4 in the morning yesterday and started again very early today."
7"About 700-1,000 people are being registered every day, and I believe that most of the people here will have been registered by the weekend, and the situation will no longer be so intense."
8An official for the Doctors without Borders medical charity, which is helping refugees on Kos, noted the improvement but stressed that the refugees' situation remains far from desirable.
9"There is still no care being provided for the refugees," Vangelis Orfanoudakis said.
10"Simply, the administrative process is more humane. But these people are getting no support, and no information on what they need to do."
11Greece is Europe's main gateway for refugees and economic migrants, after the alternative route from Libya to Italy became too dangerous.
12More than 130,000 people have reached the Greek islands this year - a 750% increase on 2014.
13The influx has overwhelmed authorities on tourism-reliant Kos.
14Refugees were first left to sleep rough in parks, archaeological sites and pavements, and then - after complaints from local residents - pushed to the old stadium that lacks elementary facilities.
15A handful of police clerks struggled to control and register the crowd, twice using fire extinguishers to stop jostling.
16Police flew in reinforcements on Wednesday, which accelerated the process, allowing some 1,500 people to leave on Athens-bound ferries.
17At least as many more are expected to leave soon, starting Thursday evening, while the government is sending in a large ferry ship on Friday to replace the stadium as a detention and registration centre.
18Mr Kyritsis said the ferry is "the best solution available right now".
19"(It) will be needed to shelter these people in a decent place, and to house those who are yet to come, because this wave (of immigration) will not stop until the end of the crisis in the countries these people are coming from," he said.
20More than 200 people, mainly Syrian Kurds from the war-devastated town of Kobani, arrived early on Thursday, in at least six rubber boats.
