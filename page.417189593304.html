Hillary Clinton has shifted left, but so have Americans

1Democratic presidential candidate Hillary Rodham Clinton speaks to child care workers during a visit to Chicago on May 20.
2Clinton has emphasized liberal positions in her campaign so far.
3Amid discussion of Hillary Rodham Clinton's move to the left in her presidential campaign, a new Gallup poll provides an important piece of context: The nation has shifted left, as well.
4In recent weeks, Clinton has called for reforming the nation's criminal justice system to reduce the number of people in prison.
5The policies she backed tacitly repudiated some of the tough-on-crime moves of her husband's administration which helped boost incarceration rates.
6Hillary Rodham Clinton staked an early claim for Latino support Tuesday by calling for a pathway to citizenship for millions of immigrants living in the country illegally, elevating the debate on an issue likely to play a vital role in the 2016 presidential race.
7She has advocated an immigration policy that would go further than President Obama to shield some illegal immigrants from deportation.
8Earlier, like many other Democratic political figures, she embraced same-sex marriage rights that she had not previously supported.
9Politically, those moves -- and others on economic policy that are likely to come this summer -- have the benefit of fending off challenges in the Democratic primaries from politicians to Clinton's left, including Sen. Bernie Sanders of Vermont, who announced his candidacy earlier this month, and former Maryland Gov. Martin O'Malley, who is expected to join the fray in a couple of weeks.
10But as Clinton advisors say -- and as Gallup's figures show -- something else is happening as well.
11Compared with 2008, when Clinton last sought the presidency, the country, and the Democratic Party, in particular, have become more liberal on social issues.
12On economic matters, the country is less conservative and more moderate.
13In 2008, slightly more than 1 in 3 Americans described their views on social issues as conservative or very conservative in Gallup's surveys, while just over 1 in 4 called their views liberal or very liberal.
14Since then, the number of self-described liberals on social policy has gone up, and the number of conservatives has declined.
15For the first time since Gallup began asking the question, in 1999, the two groups are at parity, with 31% on either side.
16The move is particularly striking among Democrats.
17In 2008, about 4 in 10 Democrats called themselves liberal on social issues, now 53% do, compared with 31% who say they are social moderates and 14% who say they are conservative.
18As the number of Democrats calling themselves liberals has risen, the number of Republicans who call themselves social conservatives has dropped, Gallup found.
19From a high of 67% in 2009, the share has now dropped to 53%, the lowest since 1999.
20About 1 in 3 Republicans now call themselves moderate on social issues, up from 1 in 4 in 2009.
21A similar, but gentler, shift has taken place on economic issues.
22The share of Americans calling themselves conservative on economics, which rose sharply to 51% during the first few years of President Obama's tenure, has now declined for six years and stands at 39%, again the lowest since 1999.
23The share of Americans calling themselves liberal on economic issues has fluctuated slightly, staying just under 1 in 5.
24The Gallup survey, conducted May 6-10, polled 1,024 American adults.
25The results have a margin of error of plus or minus 4 percentage points.
