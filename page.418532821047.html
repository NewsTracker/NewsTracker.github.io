14James Holmes was legally sane when he entered a packed movie theater armed with an assault rifle, a shotgun and a pistol, intent on killing as many people as he could, a prosecutor told jurors Tuesday in closing arguments at the gunman's trial.
15"That guy was sane beyond a reasonable doubt, and he needs to be held accountable for what he did," District Attorney George Brauchler said.
16But defense lawyer Daniel King countered that Holmes was controlled by his schizophrenia.
17"The mental illness caused this to happen. Only the mental illness caused this, and nothing else," King said.
18Brauchler and King made their final appeals to jurors Tuesday before handing over the case.
19Deliberations are scheduled to begin Wednesday morning.
20Holmes slipped into the packed theater on July 20, 2012 - almost three years ago - and opened fire.
21Twelve people died and 70 were injured.
22Defense attorneys are asking for a verdict of not guilty by reason of insanity, which would send Holmes to the state mental hospital for an indefinite commitment.
23Prosecutors say Holmes should be convicted of murder and executed.
24Brauchler again stressed the heavy toll on unsuspecting victims who had gone to see the midnight premier of a Batman movie, "The Dark Knight Rises."
25"They came in hoping to see the story of a hero dressed in black, someone who would fight insurmountable odds for justice," Brauchler said.
26"Instead, a different figure appeared by the screen. ... He came there with one thing in his heart and his mind, and that was mass murder."
27Many of the victims and family members in the courtroom wept as Brauchler showed photos of the dead and wounded and recounted their stories.
28Josh Nowlan, who was shot in the leg and walks with a cane, pressed his hands into his eyes and shook.
29Jurors showed no emotion but craned their heads toward the gallery when Brauchler said one badly wounded victim, Caleb Medley, was seated there.
30King urged the jurors to set aside the deeply emotional impact of the massacre and decide based on the wording of the statute.
31He repeatedly told them the courtroom was "the fortress of the law."
32"Here in the fortress of the law, there is no room for hatred or revenge or retaliation," he said.
33Holmes, now 27, does not dispute that he was the lone gunman who attacked the theater but pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity.
34His lawyers say schizophrenia so warped his mind he could not tell right from wrong, and that he was in the grip of a psychotic breakdown.
35"When he stepped into that theater, the evidence is clear that he could not control his thoughts, that he could not control his actions, and that he could not control his perceptions," King said.
36King pleaded with jurors to believe that Holmes' mental illness was the sole cause of the attack.
37On the courtroom video screens, King showed multiple images of Holmes as he appeared in his first court appearance, wide-eyed with shocking orange hair.
38"I would ask you to do good, be strong, and do the right thing," King said.
39"He's not guilty by reason of insanity."
40Brauchler told jurors the evidence shows Holmes knew what he was doing was illegal and wrong and that he cannot be considered insane under Colorado law.
41The prosecutor methodically reviewed Holmes' elaborate preparations, the horrific attack and finally his decision to surrender when he saw police closing in outside the theater.
42Brauchler frequently pointed at Holmes, who sat impassively at the defense table, often looking at one of the three video screens in the courtroom.
43"He knows it's wrong," Brauchler said at one point.
44"Wrong for him, wrong for society."
45Both sides are trying to help jurors make sense of thousands of pieces of evidence and more than 250 witnesses who testified in the 11-week trial.
46With that information, it will be up to the jury to decide whether prosecutors met their burden of proving Holmes was legally sane.
47Two state-appointed forensic psychiatrists who evaluated Holmes determined he was legally sane, despite severe mental illness.
48One of the doctors, William Reid, showed jurors nearly 22 hours of sometimes chilling videotaped interviews in which Holmes haltingly describes taking aim at fleeing moviegoers and longing to kill others to increase his own self-worth.
49The defense called its own psychiatrists who testified Holmes was insane.
50Holmes spent months amassing an arsenal of weapons and body armor.
51He scrawled detailed plans for the massacre in a spiral notebook, weighing which auditoriums in the Aurora theater complex would allow for maximum carnage.
52Holmes also rigged his 800-square-foot apartment into a potentially lethal booby trap that he hoped would divert police and paramedics from the theater as he set about his attack.
