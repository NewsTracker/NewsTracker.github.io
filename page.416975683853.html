Nepal Villagers Demand Help After 2nd Quake

1Thousands of villagers jammed the streets of this small Himalayan town Thursday, demanding government help after Nepal suffered its second major quake in less than three weeks.
2And while there have been occasional food handouts here, there was nowhere near enough supplies for all the people who kept arriving.
3"We came here with such hopes and such difficulty, but now we're just waiting and waiting," said Navraj Nama, 25, who came to Charikot with his brother and elderly uncle after the second earthquake hit Nepal on Tuesday.
4He said 90 percent of their home village, Danda Khorka, had been damaged in the first quake, and about 50 of those buildings had collapsed when the second one hit.
5The past three weeks have been misery for Nepal.
6On April 25, a magnitude-7.8 earthquake killed more than 8,150 people, injured tens of thousands more and left hundreds of thousands homeless.
7Then, just as the country was beginning to rebuild, a magnitude-7.3 earthquake battered it again, killing at least 91 people and injuring more than 2,300.
8A search also continued Thursday for a U.S. Marine Corps helicopter carrying six Marines and two Nepalese soldiers.
9It went missing Tuesday while delivering aid in the country's northeast, U.S. officials said.
10Normally a placid town of perhaps 10,000 people, Charikot is the administrative center of the worst-hit district in the Tuesday earthquake, making it the obvious place for residents of the many small surrounding villagers to go for help.
11By Thursday morning, thousands of additional people were living there, nearly all of them too afraid to sleep indoors.
12The Nepal Army had set up a small aid distribution center, but supplies were limited and the center was rarely open.
13So people simply waited at the locked gates, shaking the fence angrily when their frustration got the better of them.
14Nama's village, like much of the quake-hit Nepal, was in desperate need of shelter, and the young farmer came to Charikot hoping to get tents or tarpaulins to carry back with him.
15But none were available.
16There was also a shortage of tarps and tents in the Nepalese capital and elsewhere, with some people even using cardboard boxes as temporary shelters.
17"We have nowhere to go. This is our home for now. We had just moved back into our rented rooms and again the earthquakes are back," Raj Kumar, a carpenter who was sharing a small tent with two other families, said Thursday in Kathmandu.
18The most recent quake hit hardest in deeply rural parts of the Himalayan foothills, hammering many villages reached only by hiking trails and causing road-blocking landslides.
19"Damaged houses were further damaged or destroyed. Houses and schools building spared before were affected ... roads were damaged," Jamie McGoldrick, a top U.N. official in Nepal, said Wednesday.
20Among 14 quake-hit districts, some are barely accessible, and a large part of the affected population could not be reached easily because of damaged roads.
21"Some are even difficult to reach by helicopter. We are facing monumental challenges here to support the government in these districts," McGoldrick said.
22Meanwhile, the U.S. and Nepal militaries had been searching the hills for the missing Marine helicopter.
