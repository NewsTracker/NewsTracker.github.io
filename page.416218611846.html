Wiped from the face of the earth: Horrifying satellite images show city in Nigeria reduced to rubble by Boko Haram as it emerges militants have abducted 2,000 women in just 12 months

1Bama, the second largest city in Borno state, was left decimated after thousands fled or were gunned down in the street by Boko Haram fighters as they overran the troops stationed there in September 2014.
2The militants, who recently swore allegiance to ISIS, slaughtered civilians, burned women and the elderly alive inside buildings, and forced people to convert to their strict vision of Islam.
3In March, Nigerian troops regained control of the city.
4Knowing they were going to be defeated, the Islamist fighters fled, burning as they went, leaving almost three quarters of the once thriving city in ruins.
5The images have been released as part of a new report by Amnesty International, which reveals that at least 2,000 women and girls have been abducted in a reign of terror by Boko Haram in Africa, with many being forced into sexual slavery.
6Targeted: Boko Haram militants attacked the city of Bama, home to around 300,000 people, in September last year, driving the military away and taking control of the city.
7Nothing left: When the Nigerian military returned six months later, the Islamists fled, burning and killing as they went.
8Survivors say women and the elderly were burned in their homes.
9The militants destroyed 5,900 buildings, including schools, markets, the city's hospital, and a local palace.
10According to villagers who escaped the slaughter, the fighters torched homes with people still inside them and burned the local prison with inmates still locked in their cells.
11The report lays bare the savagery with which Islamist fighters attacked, the failure of the Nigerian army to protect its civilians, and the dire conditions people were forced to endure under Boko Haram rule.
12It chronicles the brutality of life under Boko Haram, where children carry out stonings, there are public floggings and rapes, children are sold into marriage and slavery, and executions are commonplace.
13In one case study, a survivor said that some men were lined up to have their throats cut with a blunt knife.
14The vast majority of the women and girls who were seized were taken at gunpoint in and around Boko Haram's heartland of north-eastern Nigeria, the report says.
15Some were as young as 10.
16The shocking 90-page report, entitled 'Our job is to shoot, slaughter and kill', was published to mark the first anniversary of the kidnap of 276 schoolgirls from the Nigerian village of Chibok, a crime that outraged the world.
17Despite an international military operation backed by Britain and the United States, and a huge '#BringBackOurGirls' campaign supported by world leaders and celebrities, many of the schoolgirls are still missing - although the BBC reported that about 50 were seen alive in late March.
18Since the start of 2014, there have been at least 300 raids and attacks carried out by Boko Haram against civilians.
19In the first three months of this year alone, 1,500 are believed to have been killed by the group which regularly carries out raids in to neighbouring countries.
20The report, which is based on nearly 200 witness accounts, including 28 with abducted women and girls who escaped captivity, includes an interview with a 15-year-old boy who witnessed 10 stonings.
21'They will gather all the children and ask them to stone,' he said.
22'I participated in the They will dig a hole, bury all the body and stone the head.
23Boko Haram take the women and girls they abduct directly to camps in remote communities or to makeshift camps where they indoctrinate them with their version of Islam in preparation for marriage.
24One 19-year-old called Aisha told how she was abducted from a friend's wedding in September last year along with her sister, the bride and the bride's sister.
25Boko Haram took them to a camp where approximately 100 abducted girls were held, forcing the bride and the bride's sister to marry their fighters.
26There Aisha and the other women and girls were taught how to fight.
27She said: 'They used to train girls how to shoot guns.
28I was among the girls trained to shoot.
29I was also trained how to use bombs and how to attack a village.
30She said that during the three months that she was held captive, she was raped repeatedly, sometimes by groups of up to six fighters.
31She also saw more than 50 people killed by Boko Haram, including her sister.
32'Some of them refused to convert,' she said.
33'Some refused to learn how to kill others.
34They were buried in a mass grave in the bush.
35'They'll just pack the dead bodies and dump them in a big hole, but not deep enough.
36Trapped: Survivors say Boko Haram sent them letters a week before the attack warning them to flee, but soldiers guarding the city forced them to stay, saying: 'See our numbers.
37The attack on Bama, once home to nearly 300,000 people, began on 1 September last year.
38A week before the assault, militants sent letters to civilians warning them of their intentions.
39However, when residents tried to flee, the 350 soldiers guarding the city stopped them.
40'Before the attack on Bama we passed on information to the military.
41'But the military stoppped anyone with luggage and said "go back home".
42They said, "We are here to guard you. If you leave we will have no one to guard. See our numbers. Nothing will happen to Bama".
43Two days later, Boko Haram attacked.
44Despite the early warnings of an attack, the militants took the soldiers by surprise, capturing their barracks, taking their guns, heavy weapons and ammunition, and then turning their attention to the civilians.
45Witness accounts of exactly what happened during the attack are unclear, but in typical Boko Haram raids, fighter are divided into groups.
46One goes house to house, collecting valuables and burning buildings; a second loots the local shops; a third is tasked with killing residents - usually local officials, students and doctors, as well as men of fighting age - and another abducts residents and prevents them from fleeing.
47Witnesses said that afterwards, decapitated bodies littered the streets, while some of the corpses had their stomachs cut open.
48Amnesty International was able to confirm 50 civilians had been killed, but it is feared that man more died of disease, wounds, or after fleeing into the bush, where fighters hunted them down and executed them.
49Desperate: After taking Bama, Boko Haram militants rounded up men of fighting age, and killed or conscripted them.
50Women are often raped, or forced to marry fighters.
51Danger: Those who fled risked being killed if they were captured.
52One survivor said: 'As we were travelling through the bush, Boko Haram stopped us.
53Me, my son and my younger daughter were travelling together.
54'They wanted to kill my son, so I begged them "don't kill him, don't kill him, don't kill him".
55I said my son didn't go to school and didn't attend any government work.
56I said we supported them and said "Allahu Akbar".
57'They let us go.
58They killed five men that were also travelling with us and took four others into the bush.
59Boko Haram bound their hands with straw.
60They were all middle aged men.
61Those who remained were kept prisoners in their own homes, banned by the fighters from leaving or contacting people in other towns or villages.
62Women were barred from leaving their homes without permission, and as most of the men had been killed or conscripted into Boko Haram, this left them completely dependent on their children or the fighters for food and water.
63In keeping with their strict religious code, the only permitted trades were between producer and buyer all 'middle men', such as market traders, were abolished.
64One woman said she saw a friend given 30 lashes for selling some of her child's clothes to buy food.
65Other punishments included floggings for missing prayers, prison sentences, beatings and executions for fleeing, and executions for adultery.
66After Bama was captured in September last year, it took the Nigerian military six months to launch an offensive to retake it, with support from Cameroon, Chad and Niger, driving Boko Haram out of most of their territory across Borno state.
67Between 14 and 21 of March this year, fighters began burning the city to the ground.
68Dozens of civilians were killed, some burned alive inside their houses, while thousands more were told to leave.
69Amnesty International UK Director Kate Allen said: 'Yet again, we can see the trail of destruction and ruin caused by Boko Haram attacks.
70These satellite images show that at least 5,900 buildings- about 70 per cent of the town and including the town's hospital - have either been damaged or destroyed.
71Amnesty International's secretary general Salil Shetty added: 'The evidence presented in this shocking report underlines the scale and depravity of Boko Haram's methods.
72'Men and women, boys and girls, Christians and Muslims, have been killed, abducted and brutalised by Boko Haram during a reign of terror which has affected millions.
73The report also highlights growing tensions between Christians and Muslims.
74Many Christians interviewed by Amnesty believe that Muslims have informed Boko Haram of their whereabouts or failed to share information about impending attacks and this has left a legacy of distrust between communities that previously lived harmoniously side-by-side.
75Whilst Boko Haram has destroyed churches and killed Christians who refuse to convert to Islam, they have also targeted moderate Muslims.
