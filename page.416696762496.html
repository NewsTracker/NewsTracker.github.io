The Latest on Nepal: Quake death toll climbs past 7,000

1Officials in Nepal say the death toll from last weekend's massive earthquake has climbed past 7,000.
2National police officer Babu Kanji Giri said Sunday that the death toll had reached 7,040 as more bodies are found in the debris.
3The Home Ministry said that 14,123 people were injured in the April 25 quake.
4Of them, 6,512 are being treated in hospitals.
5- Binaj Gurubacharya, Kathmandu, Nepal.
6Elderly Nepalese visit a temple in Bhaktapur, Nepal, Saturday, May 2, 2015.
7Nepal's Home Ministry official Laxmi Dhakal says hopes of finding any more survivors are fading away as more than a week has passed since the massive earthquake.
8He says: "Unless they were caught in an air pocket, there is not much possibility."
9Two survivors - a 15-year-old boy and a woman in her 20s - were rescued from the rubble on Thursday.
10Dozens of Nepalese have gathered in central Kathmandu's Maitighar Mandala Park to recite a prayer and light candles for those who perished in the earthquake.
11A woman cried, a boy said a prayer and others placed candles on the ground to form a map of Nepal, wishing that victims can be united to overcome their grief.
12Nila Shrestha, a 31-year-old Kathmandu resident, appealed to the world to continue sending aid to Nepal.
13Nepal police say the death toll from last week's magnitude-7.8 earthquake has climbed to 6,841, as more reports come in from remote areas and rescue workers dig out more bodies from under the rubble.
14More than 14,000 people have been reported injured.
15In Kathmandu, hundreds of volunteers have turned out to help those who have been left homeless by the magnitude-7.8 quake a week ago.
16They have collected clothes, medicines, packaged food and money and are distributing it at the Bhrikutimandap, an exhibition ground in the city.
17Doctors, engineers, business executives and office workers folded donated clothes and packed medicines to be sent across neighborhoods.
18"We are gathering whatever we can. Our priority is providing medicines and clean drinking water and warn people about the possibility of spread of disease," said a visibly exhausted Srijana Jyoti, a businesswoman.
19Jyoti said they have been shipping out water storage tanks to those in need.
20"Water is the essential and we are trying to get them access to clean drinking water," she said, adding they are printing banners in the local language telling what precautions to take to avoid getting sick.
21UNICEF says the health and well-being of children affected by the earthquake are hanging in the balance - many are homeless, in deep shock and with no access to basic care.
22With about 40 percent of children stunted in Nepal, UNICEF is raising alarm about the effect of the earthquake on their nutrition.
23It says that at least 15,000 children with severe acute malnutrition require therapeutic feeding.
24There is also an urgent need for children in the 12 most affected districts to get back to their normal routine by setting up child-friendly spaces, opening schools and providing access to basic services, such as health and water.
25A week after a massive earthquake shook Nepal, angry villagers in some parts of central Sindhupalchok district say they're still waiting for aid to reach them.
26In Pauwathok, three trucks sped by without stopping Saturday.
27One of the trucks had police with automatic weapons, escorting the two others, which were covered by tarps.
28"What about us?" screamed the villagers.
29But the trucks sped off without halting.
30Of the 85 homes in Pauwathok, all but a handful were destroyed in the magnitude-78 quake that struck April 25.
31"Nobody has come here to help us. No government, no police, no aid," said 71-year-old Badri Giri.
32The villagers said they were barely eating once a day, surviving with whatever they could forage from the debris of their collapsed homes.
33"We have no homes, no place to live anymore. We are sleeping outside," Giri said.
