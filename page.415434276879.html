Russell Brand cancels SXSW appearance at the last minute

Obama tours VA hospital that was site of long waits linked to deaths

1The SXSW Film Festival begins Friday night with the premiere of "BRAND: A Second Coming," a documentary about the actor that details his transformation from a junkie to a worldwide celebrity to an outspoken activist.
2But Brand, who says he's spent seven years working on this project with just as many directors, decided one day before the premiere that he would not be attending.
3"It is just difficult for Russell because it's a very personal film and he is vulnerable in it," director Ondi Timoner tells the Daily News.
4"Reliving his life on screen is just not a pleasant experience for someone who has lived such a tumultuous life, and is now in a very different place, and so actively engaged in the present tense."
5Brand, 39, made the announcement that he would not be attending the premiere on his personal blog.
6"You'd think a narcissist would like nothing more than talking about themselves and their 'rags to riches,' 'hard luck' story," Brand wrote on his blog Thursday, "but actually, it felt like, to me, my life was hard enough the first time round and going through it again was painful and sad."
7In the doc's many different developments over the years, Brand had complete creative control and was even the director at one point.
8But, he explains in his blog, as he became "disillusioned and disconnected from fame," he lost interest in the project, eventually handing the reins over to Timoner.
9Timoner won the Sundance Grand Jury Prize in 2004 and 2009 for her documentaries "Dig!" and "We Live in Public," respectively.
10Brand says that, having seen the final version of "BRAND: A Second Coming," he found it "oddly intrusive and melancholy."
11This film apparently proved too personal for the British rabblerouser, however Timoner thinks fans will feel differently.
12The News reached out to Brand for comment, but did not hear back by deadline.
1Almost a year after ordering a massive shake-up of the Veterans Affairs Department, President Obama gave one troubled outpost a once-over Friday to see whether his massive staff housecleaning led to improvements.
2In a trip to the VA hospital in Phoenix, where a whistle-blower exposed the existence of waits so long that dozens of veterans may have died awaiting treatment, Obama declared that his new VA leadership was "chipping away at those problems."
3"We've brought in a new team that has been tackling these issues to make sure that wait times for scheduling, access to providers, is greatly improved," the president told reporters.
4"But what we know is there is still more work to do.
5"Trust," he said, "is one of those things that you lose real quick, and then it takes some time to build."
6Aides to the president say the Phoenix center has shown progress since the revelations last April, as has the rest of the agency's division charged with providing healthcare to American veterans.
7Promising accountability, new VA Secretary Bob McDonald fired or disciplined hundreds of employees and hired a net increase of more than 8,000 doctors, nurses and other healthcare workers, according to administration figures.
8But change hasn't come quickly or thoroughly enough for some veterans advocates who hope Obama will see the job as only half done during his review.
9The conservative Concerned Veterans for America warned the president publicly against the perils of a "whitewashed tour" by hospital administrators who were part of the problem.
10A whistle-blower who helped bring attention to the problems said she had seen signs of improvement but also expressed hope that Obama would get an unvarnished view, not just a meeting with senior executives.
11"The person that he should be meeting with is the person that works the night shift on the psychiatric floor or who works the busiest shifts in the ER or the housekeeper that is struggling to clean all these really important places," said Katherine Mitchell, a physician who testified before Congress last summer that she suffered retaliation routinely for reporting health and safety concerns at the troubled facility.
12"If they want to find out what's going on in the hospital, they need to speak with the people on the front lines," Mitchell said in an interview before Obama's tour.
13While at the center, Obama met with executives of the healthcare system and members of Congress as well as with two nurses, a mental health practitioner, a scheduler and a labor representative, according to the White House.
14The overhaul at the VA came after a CNN report said that at least 40 veterans died waiting for appointments at the Phoenix VA Health Care System, many of them after being placed on a secret waiting list designed to hide their months-long delays in getting healthcare.
15Other reviews showed long patient wait times at hospitals and clinics across the country with data manipulated to downplay the delays.
16The White House responded by dismissing the VA secretary and bringing in McDonald, and pushing a reform law that included a new program to help veterans get appointments with private doctors when needed.
17According to a report last week from the Veterans of Foreign Wars, 8.6 million veterans had signed up for the new program, Veterans Choice, in its first three months.
18Of that number, 26,662 had requested healthcare services from non-VA facilities, the overwhelming majority of whom received appointments.
19But fewer than 1 in 5 veterans who qualified to get care outside the VA were offered that option, according to a survey conducted by the VFW.
20Local VA staff members were not sufficiently familiar with the program, the VFW found, but acknowledged that such problems were to be expected with the rollout of an "ambitious" program.
21As part of Obama's visit, the VA announced a new advisory committee of experts to make more recommendations for change.
22Sen. John McCain (R-Ariz.)
23was dismissive of the committee, calling it a "high-profile but empty gesture."
24During a round-table discussion with the president Friday, McCain said, he questioned Obama about his administration's "foot-dragging" on fully funding and implementing the reform law.
25Rep.
26Jeff Miller (R-Fla.)
27, chairman of the House Committee on Veterans' Affairs, said he's still worried about a lack of accountability for those who caused the problems, especially in Phoenix.
28Noting that no one had been fired at the Phoenix hospital, Miller said a "dearth of accountability also exists at VA facilities across the nation, as evidenced by the fact that VA has not fired a single senior executive for wait time manipulation."
29"It's becoming quite evident that this administration is either unwilling or unable to take accountability at VA seriously," said Miller.
30Mitchell, the whistle-blower, said hiring and access to care had improved significantly.
31But she said she still had concerns that potential informants fear retaliation if they report problems.
32"I get calls every week describing problems and asking for help," said Mitchell, who still works for the VA as the special care medicine coordinator for the VA Southwest Health Care Network.
33"At the Phoenix VA, even just in the last three weeks, I've had serious patient care issues that were brought to me. ... People are just afraid to speak up."
34Though some high-profile officials have been disciplined, Mitchell said the retaliation employees fear most is at lower levels.
35"What I don't understand is why there is so few people being held accountable," she said.
36"They really, really need to emphasize holding supervisors accountable for retaliation because that essentially drags the employee and prevents them from speaking out."
37Obama said he saw room for improvement but thought it was possible.
38"Just the fact that there have been a few bad apples, mistakes that have been made, systems that aren't designed to get the job done," he said, "I don't want that to detract from the outstanding work being done."
