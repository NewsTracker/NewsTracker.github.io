Netanyahu's Likud surges to stunning Israeli election win

1Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's right-wing Likud Party scored a resounding victory in Israel's election, final results showed Wednesday, a stunning turnaround after a tight race that had put his lengthy rule in jeopardy.
2Netanyahu surged ahead after a last-minute lurch to the right in which he opposed Palestinian statehood and vowed continued settlement construction, setting the stage for fresh confrontations with the White House just weeks after criticizing U.S. talks with Iran in a divisive address to America's Congress.
3With nearly all votes counted, Likud appeared to have earned 30 out of parliament's 120 seats and was in a position to build with relative ease a coalition government with its nationalist, religious and ultra-Orthodox Jewish allies.
4The election was widely seen as a referendum on Netanyahu, who has governed for the past six years.
5Recent opinion polls indicated he was in trouble, giving chief rival Isaac Herzog's centre-left Zionist Union a slight lead.
6Exit polls Tuesday showed the two sides deadlocked but once the actual results came pouring in early Wednesday, the Zionist Union dropped to just 24 seats.
7Given the final results, it is all but assured that Israel's largely ceremonial President Reuven Rivlin will task Netanyahu with forming a new government.
8Netanyahu says he hopes to do so quickly, within two to three weeks.
9"Against all odds, we achieved a great victory for the Likud," Netanyahu told supporters at his election night headquarters, declaring victory even before final results were known.
10"I am proud of the people of Israel, who in the moment of truth knew how to distinguish between what is important and what is peripheral, and to insist on what is important."
11Turnout in the election was about 72 per cent, more than the last election in 2013.
12Netanyahu focused his campaign primarily on security issues, while his opponents instead pledged to address the high cost of living and housing crisis while accusing him of being out of touch.
13Netanyahu will likely look to battle that image now by adding to his government Moshe Kahlon, whose upstart Kulanu party captured 10 seats with a campaign focused almost entirely on bread-and-butter economic issues.
14Kahlon is expected to be the next finance minister.
15A union of four largely Arab-backed factions became Israel's third largest party - with 14 seats - and gave Israel's Arab minority significant leverage in parliament for the first time.
16Ten parties in all made it into parliament.
17Herzog conceded defeat, saying he called Netanyahu and offered him congratulations.
18He signalled that he would not join forces with Netanyahu and would rather head to the opposition.
19"I think that at this moment what Israel needs most of all is another voice, a voice that offers an alternative and a voice that tells it the truth," he said outside his Tel Aviv home.
20"It must be clear that for the citizens of Israel, the challenges remain the same, the problems are the same. Nothing has changed."
21Netanyahu's return to power for a fourth term likely spells trouble for Mideast peace efforts and could further escalate tensions with the United States.
22Netanyahu, who already has a testy relationship with U.S. President Barack Obama, staked out a series of hard-line positions in the final days of the race that will put him on a collision course with much of the international community.
23Polls had Zionist Union party leader Isaac Herzog slightly ahead of Netanyahu in the lead up to the election.
24In a dramatic policy reversal, he said he now opposes the creation of a Palestinian state - a key policy goal of the White House and the international community.
25He also promised to expand construction in Jewish areas of east Jerusalem, the section of the city claimed by the Palestinians as their capital, where violence has increased in recent months.
26Canadian Prime Minister Stephen Harper gave no hint of any concern in a tweet congratulating Netanyahu.
27"Israel has no greater friend than Canada," the tweet said.
28The Palestinians, fed up after years of deadlock with Netanyahu, are now likely to press ahead with their attempts to bring war crimes charges against Israel in the International Criminal Court.
29"Now, more than ever, the international community must act," Palestinian official Saeb Erekat said.
30The world overwhelmingly supports the establishment of a Palestinian state in the West Bank, east Jerusalem and the Gaza Strip, areas captured by Israel in 1967, and opposes settlement construction.
31With the race close, Netanyahu reneged on his previous stated support for a Palestinian state in an attempt to shore up his hawkish base.
32But peace talks last collapsed nearly a year ago, and it's unclear whether the next government will pursue any drastic policy changes.
33Netanyahu also infuriated the White House earlier this month when he delivered a speech to the U.S. Congress criticizing an emerging nuclear deal with Iran.
34The speech was arranged with Republican leaders and not co-ordinated with the White House ahead of time in a rare breach of diplomatic protocol.
35In Washington, White House spokesman Josh Earnest said Obama was confident strong U.S.-Israeli ties would endure far beyond the election, regardless of the victor.
36Throughout the campaign, Netanyahu portrayed himself as the only politician capable of confronting Israel's numerous security challenges.

2Its revised constitution will now read that marriage is 'a unique commitment between two people, traditionally a man and a woman,' as opposed to just a man and woman previously.
3'Finally, the church in its constitutional documents fully recognizes that the love of gays and lesbian couples is worth celebrating in the faith community,' Rev.
4Brian D. Ellison, executive director of the Covenant Network of Presbyterians, which advocates gay inclusion in the church, told the New York Times.
5The new language, which was approved by the church's General Assembly last year, had to be ratified by more than half its regional bodies.
6The decision is likely to drive a further wedge between Presbyterian USA members, who have taken opposite sides on the issue.
7The church has in fact been losing some of its members as it adopts a more liberal stance on social and religious issues.
8That being said, the fact that some many members who were against same-sex marriage have moved on to other churches made the passage of this new definition far easier for the church.
9At last year's Presbyterian General Assembly, the church also gave its pastors a green light to marry gay and lesbian couples in states where such marriages are allowed.
10'These decisions have been welcomed by some and are a disappointment to others,' Gradye Parsons, stated clerk of the General Assembly, said at the time.
11The Presbyterians' position stands in sharp contrast to that of the Roman Catholic church, the biggest single denomination among Americans, and fundamentalist Evangelicals who strongly oppose gay marriage.
12In 2011, the Presbyterian Church USA voted to approve the ordination of openly gay and lesbian clergy.
