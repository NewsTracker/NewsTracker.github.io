Microsoft cutting 7,800 jobs as phone sales flag

1FILE - This July 3, 2014, file photo, shows the Microsoft Corp. logo outside the Microsoft Visitor Center in Redmond, Wash.
2Microsoft announced, Wednesday, July 8, 2015, it will cut 7,800 jobs and take a $7.6 billion impairment charge as it attempts to revive its flagging phone hardware business.
3SAN FRANCISCO -- Microsoft is cutting 7,800 jobs and writing off $7.6 billion in connection with its purchase of Nokia's phone business, as the giant software maker tries to narrow its focus and pull back from a series of ill-fated forays onto rival tech companies' turf.
4The cuts announced Wednesday come on top of 18,000 jobs that Microsoft trimmed last year, just months after the company paid $7.3 billion for Nokia in the hope of expanding its footprint in the smartphone hardware business where Apple and Samsung are market leaders.
5Three years ago, Microsoft wrote off another big sum, $6.2 billion, on its purchase of digital advertising firm aQuantive.
6Microsoft bought aQuantive for $6.3 billion in a bid to increase its role in the online ad sector that was dominated by the likes of Google and Yahoo.
7Both the Nokia and aQuantive deals were engineered by former CEO Steve Ballmer, who sought to compete against younger, faster-growing tech companies by expanding beyond Microsoft's original business of making software for desktop computers.
8But Microsoft's new boss, Satya Nadella, has been pulling back from phone hardware and digital advertising after seeing little success in those ventures.
9Last month, he announced a deal to hand over most of Microsoft's remaining display advertising business to AOL.
10Wall Street seems to prefer Nadella's strategy of focusing on software and Internet services.
11Analysts have said the Nokia business was a drag on Microsoft's profits.
12The company's stock slipped 2 cents to $44.28 in afternoon trading Wednesday.
13"We believe these cuts are positive for Microsoft as they help streamline the business by getting rid of non-core components," said Mark Moerdler, an investment analyst with Bernstein Research, in a note to investors.
14"The new CEO has got to right the ship," agreed David Mitchell Smith, an industry analyst with the Gartner research firm.
15"They were heading in some directions that needed course correction, and he's doing it."
16That doesn't mean Microsoft is out of the woods.
17The company has struggled to adapt as consumers have increasingly turned away from personal computers, in favor of smartphones and tablets that run software made by Apple and Google.
18Analysts say Nadella is making a big bet that people will use new versions of Microsoft's software on a wide variety of computing devices -- including tablets, gaming consoles and even holographic headsets.
19In recent months, Microsoft has released new versions of its popular Office software for Apple and Android devices, while preparing to launch a major overhaul of its flagship Windows operating system at the end of this month.
20"Our reinvention will be centered on creating mobility of experiences across the entire device family including phones," Nadella wrote in an email to employees that sought to explain the latest cuts.
21When added to last year's layoffs, Microsoft will have trimmed roughly the number of employees it added when it acquired the Nokia business.
22Microsoft said it had 118,000 workers at the end of March.
23While the company did not provide specifics, Nadella said the latest cuts will primarily affect the Nokia phone business, which has several thousand workers in Finland.
24The operation has struggled for profitability and seen little growth in its share of the global phone market.
25Along with writing down the value of that business, Microsoft said it will incur $750 million to $850 million in restructuring costs related to the cuts.
26Wednesday's announcement was not entirely a surprise.
27Nadella has warned in recent months that the company would have to make "some tough choices in areas where things are not working."
28Microsoft isn't getting out of the hardware business altogether.
29Nadella said the company will still make some phones, along with its Surface tablets, Xbox gaming consoles and its new HoloLens augmented reality headsets.
