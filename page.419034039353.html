5Jurors on Monday moved one step closer toward sentencing James Holmes to death for his Colorado movie theater attack, taking less than three hours to reject arguments that the former neuroscience student's mental illness means he should not die.
6The decision clears the way for one last attempt from both sides to sway the jury, with gripping testimony from victims about their suffering and more appeals for mercy for the man convicted of murdering 12 people and trying to kill 70 more during the 2012 assault at a Batman movie.
7Holmes, his reactions dulled by anti-psychotic drugs, stood as ordered and appeared emotionless as Judge Carlos Samour, Jr. read the decisions.
8Robert and Arlene Homes held hands, their fingers interlaced, and directed their eyes at the floor.
9With each unanimous "yes," it became ever more clear that jurors believe their son's crimes outweighed their testimony.
10She began to cry, and her husband held out a box of tissues.
11More tears flowed in the gallery.
12Rena Medek began silently sobbing when the judge read the name of her 23-year-old daughter Micayla.
13Ian Sullivan, the father of Holmes' youngest victim, 6-year-old Veronica Moser-Sullivan, closed his eyes when her name was read.
14Veronica's grandfather, Robert Sullivan, glared at Holmes and nodded his head softly.
15"We are one step closer," Joshua Nowlan said outside court, adding that he's "very happy with the results."
16Wounded by the gunfire, Nowlan used the cane he now needs to support his legs as a prop during the trial, to show how Holmes sprayed bullets from his assault rifle.
17The jury was told to return Tuesday morning for the final phase.
18Then, the nine women and three men will finally decide whether the 27-year-old Holmes should receive a lethal injection, or spend life in prison without parole.
19Sandy Phillips, whose daughter Jessica Ghawi was killed, said prosecutors advised her that she would be testifying on Tuesday.
20"I'm a little overwhelmed, but at the same time my job is to share Jessie with the jury, and I will do that to the best of my ability," she said outside the courthouse.
21The same jury swiftly rejected Holmes' insanity defense, deciding that he was capable of telling right from wrong when he carried out the theater attack in the Denver suburb of Aurora on July 20, 2012.
22Their quick decision on Monday raised expectations that they will choose a death sentence after what prosecutors estimate will be two or three more days of testimony from survivors.
23But legal experts said there's no way to predict that final decision.
24Monday's preliminary verdict was highly technical.
25They found simply that Holmes' mental problems and the portrait his attorneys painted of a kinder, gentler younger man did not outweigh the horrors of his calculated attack on defenseless moviegoers.
26This next stage can be more challenging for each juror, and to choose capital punishment, they must be unanimous, Denver defense attorney Dan Recht said.
27"They're making the ultimate decision of life or death, quite literally," Recht said.
28"All they need is one holdout ... We are far from over on this."
29The defense had argued that mental illness reduced his "moral culpability," and that his personal history made him worthy of mercy.
30They said it was schizophrenia, not free will, that drove him to murder.
31They called his former teachers, friends, sister and parents, who said "Jimmy" had been a friendly child who withdrew socially as he grew older.
32Robert and Arlene Holmes testified that they never suspected their son was mentally ill.
33But Robert Holmes acknowledged that they rarely communicated in the months before the theater attack, and that in his family, emotions just weren't talked about, even though his own father and sister had been hospitalized with mental illness.
