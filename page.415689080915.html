Netanyahu apologizes for remarks about Arabs during Israel election

1The move appeared to be an attempt to heal rifts and mute criticism at home and in the United States.
2Netanyahu drew accusations of racism in Israel, especially from its Arab minority, and a White House rebuke when, just a few hours before polling stations were to close across the country, he warned that Arab citizens were voting "in droves."
3Netanyahu, who's Likud Party won re-election in the vote, met with members of the Arab community at the prime minister's residence in Jerusalem on Monday and apologized.
4He said he knows his "comments last week offended some Israeli citizens and offended members of the Israeli-Arab community."
5"This was never my intent. I apologize for this," Netanyahu said.
6"I view myself as the prime minister of each and every citizen of Israel, without any prejudice based on religion, ethnicity or gender."
7"I view all Israeli citizens as partners in the building of a prosperous and safe state of Israel, for all Israelis," he also said.
8A recently established alliance of four small, mostly Arab parties called the Joint List made unprecedented gains in the March 17 election, earning enough votes to make it the third-largest party in Israel's parliament.
9Arab citizens make up 20 percent of Israel's population.
10Equality is guaranteed in Israel's laws but many Arabs have long complained of discrimination, mainly in the job and housing market.
11Ayman Odeh, the head of the Joint List, told channel 2 TV that Netanyahu's apology was not accepted.
12"This is not a real apology," Odeh said.
13"He incited against citizens who were exercising their basic right to vote for Knesset."
14Odeh also accused Netanyahu of "zigzagging" by saying one thing one day and a different another.
15In the final days of the campaign, Netanyahu angered the U.S. by taking a tough stance toward the Palestinians and by saying a Palestinian state will not be established on his watch in the current climate of regional chaos and violence.
16Resolving the conflict between Israel and the Palestinians in a two state solution is a key U.S. foreign policy priority.
17The tough talk was part of a last-ditch attempt by Netanyahu to spur his more hard-line supporters to the polls after it appeared he was losing voters to a more hawkish party.
18The election was widely seen as a referendum on Netanyahu, who has governed the country for the past six years, and CBS News correspondent Barry Petersen reported it drew an enormous turnout of almost 72 percent.
19In Washington, State Department spokeswoman Marie Harf told reporters Monday that she had not seen the Netanyahu apology but that the Israeli prime minister is hard to read because "he said diametrically opposing things in the matter of a week."
20"When you say things, words matter. And if you say something different two days later, which do we believe," she said.
21"What we're looking for now are actions and policies."
22Netanyahu defended his election-day remarks in the days after the vote.
23He told NBC last Thursday that he remains committed to Palestinian statehood - if conditions in the region improve - and to the two-state vision first spelled out in a landmark 2009 speech at Israel's Bar Ilan University.
24"I haven't changed my policy," he said.
25"I never retracted my speech."
26He told NBC that his government has spent billions in Arab towns to upgrade infrastructure, schools and narrow gaps.
27Earlier Monday, Netanyahu secured a majority of backers in the new parliament and will later be tasked with forming the next government.
28Israel's ceremonial president, Reuven Rivlin, has been meeting with the parties in parliament to hear their recommendations before appointing who will form the next coalition government.
29Kulanu, a new centrist party gave its nod to Netanyahu on Monday, giving him 61 backers out of the 120 in parliament.
30Netanyahu appears poised to set up a coalition with hawkish, centrist and religious parties.
