1In a new blog post, Facebook explained its updated Community Standards, which offers clarification on what users can and can't post on their timelines.
2Facebook also clarified its approach to government requests and released its Global Government Requests Report.
3The new Community Standards page is longer than its previous version.
4This update is split up into four sections: safety, respectful behavior, privacy and security, and intellectual property.
5One of the most notable changes is on nudity.
6"We remove photographs of people displaying genitals or focusing in on fully exposed buttocks. We also restrict some images of female breasts if they include the nipple," said the social network in the Nudity portion of the Community Standards page.
7There are two exceptions to the nudity clause of the Community Standards.
8Facebook users will be able to post pictures of women breastfeeding and portraits and sculptures that depict nudity.
9Prior to this update the rules on nudity were vague.
10Facebook users would be confused as to why their content was banned and other offensive material was left online.
11Pornography or other graphic sex content will still be removed from users' timelines.
12Facebook will also ban written content that describes sexual acts in detail.
13Facebook also revamped its hate speech, bullying and harassment guidelines.
14Users will no longer be able to post anything that promotes violence in a distasteful manner.
15"We remove graphic images when they are shared for sadistic pleasure or to celebrate or glorify violence," states the Violence and Graphic Content section.
16Hate speech and other material that directly attacks race, ethnicity, nationality, disabilities, religion or sexual orientation will also be removed.
17Satire, humor and social commentary relating to these topics will still be allowed, though.
18Aside from all the Community Standards updates, Facebook also provided new details on its Global Government Requests Report.
19Facebook said that the number in government requests for data and content restrictions have increased over the second half of 2014.
20"The amount of content restricted for violating local law increased by 11% over the previous half, to 9,707 pieces of content restricted, up from 8,774. We saw a rise in content restriction requests from countries like Turkey and Russia, and declines in places like Pakistan," Facebook said.
21Twitter's transparency report, which was published in February, also revealed that countries like Turkey had the most data requests.
22Facebook concluded its blog post by saying that it'll continue to scrutinize government requests that have deficiencies and work with governments on surveillance policy.
23"We will also continue to push governments around the world to reform their surveillance practices in a way that maintains the safety and security of their people while ensuring their rights and freedoms are protected," Facebook said.
