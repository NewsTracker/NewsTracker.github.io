Technology shares lead a slump in US stocks; Apple sinks

1FILE - This July 15, 2013, file photo, shows the New York Stock Exchange.
2A cautious outlook from Apple coupled with indifferent earnings from U.S. technology firms weighed on global stock markets Wednesday, July 22, 2015, ahead of another key vote in the Greek Parliament.
3Weak showings from Apple, Microsoft and other companies pulled stocks lower in afternoon trading Wednesday, pointing the market toward its second decline in as many days.
4Technology stocks were among the biggest decliners.
5A report indicating U.S. home sales surged last month helped lift homebuilder stocks.
6KEEPING SCORE: The Dow Jones industrial average fell 93 points, or 0.5 percent, to 17,825 as of 2:10 p.m. Eastern Time.
7The Standard & Poor's 500 index slipped six points, or 0.3 percent, to 2,112.
8The Nasdaq composite lost 36 points, or 0.7 percent, to 5,171.
9THE QUOTE: As the biggest publicly traded company in the world, Apple has a big impact on the market.
10However, its latest results don't necessarily speak to the overall health of the economy or corporate America, said Erik Davidson, chief investment officer at Wells Fargo Private Bank.
11"Do iPhone sales tell us a lot about the broader economy and the markets or does it tell us more about Apple? It probably tells us more about Apple," Davidson said.
12"In general, you have a U.S. economy that is recovering. Earnings are going fairly well."
13SECTOR VIEW: Five of the 10 sectors in the S&P 500 declined, led by technology stocks.
14The sector was down 1.6 percent.
15Utilities stocks rose the most, 0.5 percent.
16APPLE WORRIES: Apple slumped 4.5 percent a day after the company gave a cautious outlook for the current quarter and didn't provide much detail on how its new smartwatch was doing.
17The stock fell $5.92 to 124.84.
18BAD CALL: Microsoft reported a hefty quarterly loss late Tuesday reflecting an expense of $8.4 billion related to its purchase of the Nokia phone business over a year ago.
19The stock was down $1.84, or 3.9 percent, to $45.44.
20EXCEEDING EXPECTATIONS: Whirlpool jumped 7.3 percent on news that the appliance maker's second-quarter earnings beat Wall Street's expectations.
21The stock rose $12.18 to $178.39.
22APPETIZING RESULTS: Chipotle Mexican Grill climbed 8.1 percent a day after the Mexican-food chain served up earnings that beat forecasts by financial analysts.
23The stock added $54.70 to $727.77.
24HOME SALES SURGE: The National Association of Realtors said that sales of previously occupied homes climbed 3.2 percent in June to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 5.49 million.
25That's the highest pace in more than eight years and indicates that demand has eclipsed the amount of homes available for sale.
26Homebuilder shares got a bump from the report, with New Home notching the biggest gain.
27The stock rose 86 cents, or 5.1 percent, to $17.68.
28OVERSEAS MARKETS: In Europe, Germany's DAX was down 0.7 percent, while the CAC-40 in France fell 0.5 percent.
29The FTSE 100 index of leading British shares was down 1.5 percent.
30In Asia, stocks closed mostly lower.
31Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 1.2 percent and South Korea's Kospi lost 0.9 percent.
32Hong Kong's Hang Seng slipped 1 percent.
33ANOTHER GREEK VOTE: Though fears of a Greek exit from the euro have faded somewhat following last week's decision by Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras to back further austerity measures in return for a third bailout of the country, some hurdles remain to be cleared.
34On Wednesday, Greek lawmakers have to back further economic reforms demanded by international creditors in return for the new bailout.
35ENERGY: Benchmark U.S. crude dropped 97 cents to $49.90 a barrel in New York.
36Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils used by many U.S. refineries, fell 54 cents at $56.50 a barrel in London.
37METALS: Precious and industrial metals futures fell.
38Gold lost $12 to $1,091.50 an ounce.
39Silver gave up six cents to $14.71 an ounce and copper declined five cents to $2.43 an ounce.
40BONDS: U.S. government bond prices rose.
41The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 2.32 percent from 2.33 percent.
