Apple invests in China solar project, US forest conservation

1The initiatives come as the tech giant this year met a self-imposed goal of powering all its U.S. operations with renewable energy to reduce carbon emissions - initiatives that have won high marks from environmental groups like Greenpeace.
2On Thursday, Apple announced a new focus on using paper from trees harvested under environmentally sound conditions.
3It's also promising to use more renewable power overseas, where Apple relies heavily on contract manufacturers - and where a top executive acknowledged the company can do more.
4"It's important to us to tackle climate change everywhere we are," Lisa Jackson, Apple's vice president for environmental initiatives, told The Associated Press.
5"When you talk about China, you're talking about manufacturing partners. We're looking to bring the same innovation there. This is the start."
6The new solar project in China has a capacity of 40 megawatts, which is smaller than some projects Apple has announced in the United States.
7By comparison, Apple is spending $850 million for rights to nearly half the output of a 280-megawatt solar facility planned for construction south of Apple's Cupertino, California, headquarters.
8That project will produce enough energy to power all of Apple's California offices, a computer center and 52 retail stores.
9Still, the Chinese project will produce more than the amount of energy consumed by Apple's 19 corporate offices and 21 retail stores in China and Hong Kong, Jackson said.
10She added that Apple uses renewable energy for 87 percent of the power at its facilities worldwide.
11That figure, however, doesn't include substantial power consumption by contract manufacturers.
12With the new project in China, Apple is looking to improve its own operations first.
13"Before we go somewhere else and start asking and eventually requiring clean energy, you want to make sure you show folks how to do it," said Jackson, who was U.S. Environmental Protection Agency administrator during President Barack Obama's first term.
14Jackson declined to say how much Apple is investing in the plant, which is being built in partnership with U.S. energy company SunPower and four Chinese firms.
15Although China is known for heavy reliance on coal, its government has set aggressive goals for solar, wind and hydroelectric power.
16Meanwhile, Apple pledged an unspecified amount of money for a Virginia-based nonprofit, the Conservation Fund, to purchase two large tracts of timberland on the East Coast.
17The Conservation Fund will resell the land to commercial interests under legally binding terms that require future owners to preserve the forest and follow environmentally sound principles for cutting and replanting trees.
18Larry Selzer, the group's chief executive, said that will protect the forest while keeping it in the hands of private owners who pay taxes and create jobs.
19Selzer said he'll use proceeds from reselling the land to buy and protect additional tracts.
20Apple won't necessarily buy paper made from trees on that land, but Jackson said the investment will increase the supply of sustainable wood fiber.
21She said the two tracts would produce about half the non-recycled wood fiber used in Apple's product packaging last year.
22That would put Apple halfway toward its goal of obtaining all its non-recycled paper products from sustainable timber.
23Apple wouldn't say how much paper it uses, but it says two-thirds of its paper packaging comes from recycled material.
24In the last three months of 2014, Apple sold more than 100 million iPhones and other gadgets, most in cardboard boxes.
