Cyclone-ravaged Vanuatu struggles to determine scale of damage, deaths as aid starts flowing

1Winds from the extremely powerful cyclone that blew through the Pacific's Vanuatu archipelago are beginning to subside, revealing widespread destruction.
2WELLINGTON, New Zealand - Vanuatu's president said the cyclone that hammered the tiny South Pacific archipelago was a "monster" that has destroyed or damaged 90 percent of the buildings in the capital Port Vila and has forced the nation to start anew.
3Looking weary and red-eyed, Baldwin Lonsdale said in an interview Monday with The Associated Press that the latest information he has is that six people are confirmed dead and 30 injured from Cyclone Pam.
4Lonsdale was interviewed in Sendai, northeastern Japan, where he had been attending a disaster conference when the cyclone struck.
5He appealed for international aid for the place he calls "paradise."
6"This is a very devastating cyclone in Vanuatu. I term it as a monster, a monster," he said.
7"It's a setback for the government and for the people of Vanuatu. After all the development that has taken place, all this development has been wiped out."
8The president said because of the break in communications, even he could not reach his family.
9"We do not know if our families are safe or not. As the leader of the nation, my whole heart is for the people, the nation."
10Lonsdale and other top Vanuatu government officials were preparing to return home later Monday from Sendai.
11Officials in Vanuatu had still not made contact with outlying islands and were struggling to determine the scale of devastation from the cyclone, which tore through early Saturday, packing winds of 270 kilometers (168 miles) per hour.
12Paolo Malatu, coordinator for the National Disaster Management Office, said officials had dispatched every plane and helicopter they could to fly over the hard-hit outer islands.
13"The damage to homes and infrastructure is severe," Malatu said.
14"The priority at the moment is to get people water, food and shelter."
15He said bridges were down outside the capital, making travel by vehicle impossible even around the main island of Efate.
16New Zealand radio journalist Frances Cook and her husband traveled to Vanuatu for their honeymoon two days before the cyclone struck.
17She said they knew a storm was coming but figured from the forecasts it wouldn't be too bad.
18Instead of zip-lining through the jungle and looking at active volcanoes as planned, she has instead been reporting back home on the devastation.
19She said a lot of stranded tourists are desperate to leave and some are afraid for their security.
20"It's a glorious place and the people are so lovely," she said.
21"It's quite upsetting to see this happen."
22The airport in Port Vila has reopened, allowing some aid and relief flights to reach the country.
23Lonsdale said a wide range of items were needed, from tarpaulins and water containers to medical supplies and construction tools.
24Georgina Roberts, New Zealand's High Commissioner to Vanuatu, said two defense force planes would arrive Monday to retrieve about 100 stranded New Zealand tourists.
25Some commercial flights have resumed as well, although services are limited.
26Vanuatu has a population of 267,000 people spread over 65 islands.
27About 47,000 people live in the capital.
28Hannington Alatoa, head of the Vanuatu Red Cross Society, said flyovers by New Zealand and Australian relief teams showed much of the country had been "flattened."
29At least half of the population, or about 130,000 people, has been affected, Alatoa said in Sendai.
