Border Force Counter-Terrorism Unit ramps up efforts to detect potential jihadists trying to leave Australia

Three British teens stopped en route to Islamic State as battle for Tikrit stalls

1The alarming figures from the recently established Border Force Counter-Terrorism Unit have added further fuel to the debate over the merits of cancelling passports, preventing travel and keeping jihadists in Australia, where they are "ticking time-bombs".
2Unit teams at eight airports conducted 75,906 "real-time assessments" between August and February, a spokeswoman for Immigration Minister Peter Dutton said.
3The assessments are not random and involve specialist officers pulling suspicious travellers aside and asking them a series of questions to determine their risk to national security.
4The spokeswoman would not say what proportion of the 75,906 suspects were subjected to further action or proven to be travelling to join terrorist groups.
5"Since their deployment Counter-Terrorism Unit teams have successfully intercepted a number of people of national security concern," she said.
6Initial results from August suggest a sizeable number of travellers are being caught with extremist material on their phones, placed under surveillance or found to have "inconsistencies" with their luggage and visas, yet it still forms a tiny portion of the thousands being stopped by counter-terrorism officers.
7Many more have been pulled off planes, had luggage searched and been forced to re-book flights.
8Muslim community leaders say most are legitimate travellers who are unfairly profiled and targeted.
9The Grand Mufti of Australia, Dr Ibrahim Abu Mohammad, lashed out at the "extremely unprofessional" and "seemingly random yet profiled manner" of the assessments following the detention of a highly regarded senior imam in September.
10Sheikh Shady Alsuleiman, who was travelling with a group of pilgrims to perform the Hajj, was detained for more than two hours in Sydney and missed his flight.
11In its first three weeks, Border Force Counter-Terrorism Unit teams in Sydney and Melbourne reportedly intercepted at least 11 terror suspects, seized three devices containing extremist material, denied one Malaysian man entry to Australia and pulled dozens of people off flights and placed them under surveillance.
12Two men were reportedly stopped in August with tens of thousands of dollars in undeclared currency and extremist propaganda material.
13Fairfax Media is aware of one person who has been charged with attempting to fly to a conflict zone yet several more have been sent home without charge, including two brothers, aged 16 and 17, intercepted at Sydney Airport earlier in March on their way to join Islamic State.
14Controversial preacher Mohammed Junaid Thorne said he was the only traveller pulled aside at the boarding gate for a flight from Sydney to Perth on March 9.
15He missed his flight but was later released.
16A Facebook post about his experience prompted several Muslims to say they had been pulled aside every time they travelled.
17Joseph Wakim, founder of the Australian Arabic Council, said innocent Muslims were purposely not speaking Arabic at airports to avoid being embarrassed, interrogated or inconvenienced by the crackdown.
18"I can't prove that [racial profiling] occurs but I know of many people, and I believe them, that expect to be picked at 'random' without fail because of their appearance."
19However, he said community leaders had been asking governments for years to stop Australians from leaving to take up arms.
20The Border Force Counter-Terrorism Unit was recently rolled out to eight airports, costing $50 million over four years and employing 80 specialist officers with enhanced powers to intercept inbound and outbound passengers of national security interest.
21Muslim community leader Jamal Rifi has repeatedly warned that thwarting the travel of extremists and keeping them in Australia with no passport is a recipe for disaster because they become "ticking time-bombs".
22Australia has narrowly averted several terror plots planned by extremists who wanted to travel overseas but had their passports cancelled.
23However, terrorism expert Nick O'Brien, from Charles Sturt University, said it was not realistic to allow jihadists to simply leave and not come back, especially because citizens cannot be rendered stateless.
24"It's far better to stop these people and try to find out why they're going over, and use it to stop others rather than just washing our hands of the responsibility," Mr O'Brien said.
1Ankara: Three British teenagers who had been planning to join Islamic State militants in Syria have been detained in the Turkish city of Istanbul, Turkish and British officials said on Sunday.
2The three, who have not been named, were detained on Friday, Turkish sources told Reuters, after a tip-off from British authorities that two of them were travelling to Turkey via Spain.
3Iraqi Shi'ite militants rest near the front line with Islamic State in Tikrit.
4Their detention comes after three British schoolgirls entered Turkey last month and are thought to have joined Islamic State in Syria.
5Police in London said they had been made aware that two 17-year-old boys had gone missing and were thought to be travelling to Syria.
6Further inquiries revealed they had travelled with a 19-year-old male, police said.
7"Officers alerted the Turkish authorities who were able to intercept all three males, preventing travel to Syria. They remain in detention in Turkey. The families have been kept informed of developments," the police statement said.
8Arrangements were being made with British authorities to deport all three back to the UK, a senior Turkish official, who spoke on condition of anonymity, said.
9Security services estimate some 600 Britons have gone to Syria or Iraq to join militant groups, including the man known as 'Jihadi John', who has appeared in several Islamic State beheading videos.
10The British government plans to ban airlines from taking Britons to join Islamic State.
11Hundreds of other Europeans are also fighting for the militants, with a 13-year-old boy last week reported as having become the youngest foreigner to die for Islamic State.
12The involvement of foreign fighters has raised fears about the possibility of attacks at home if they return trained and further radicalised.
13Turkey meanwhile has faced criticism for not better controlling its south-eastern borders, and has accused European countries of failing to prevent would-be jihadists from travelling in the first place.
14Also on Sunday, Islamic State fighters traded sniper fire and mortar rounds with Iraqi troops and allied Shi'ite militia forces in the city of Tikrit amid reports the militants had used chlorine as a chemical weapon elsewhere in the country earlier this year.
15A military official returned from the front in Tikrit said no major advances were made by either side nearly two weeks into an operation to win back the city Islamic State fighters seized last June.
16Iraqi Kurdish authorities said on Saturday they had proof the radical Islamist militants occupying large parts of the country's north and west used chlorine against Kurdish peshmerga fighters in January in a car bombing attempt west of the city of Mosul.
17The Baghdad government official reached by Reuters on Sunday declined to comment.
18But the mayor of a town on the northern edge of Tikrit told Reuters that storage containers filled with chlorine were found by troops and mainly Shi'ite militiamen when they entered al-Alam last week, the day before they fought their way into Tikrit.
19"We found a number of storage units containing chlorine that we think were seized by Daesh from water purification stations in different parts of Tikrit," Laith al-Jubouri said, using the Arabic acronym for the group.
20He said security forces had sealed off the area where the containers were found and alerted Baghdad authorities.
21On Tuesday, when Iraqi forces and allied militias pushed Islamic State fighters out of the town, a Reuters photographer was present when Iraqi police instructed journalists to stand back and hold their breath as they detonated a roadside bomb they suspected contained chlorine.
22When they detonated the bomb, a yellowish plume burst into the air, and as bystanders coughed, officials shouted "be careful, it's chlorine!", the Reuters photographer said.
23The military campaign to retake Tikrit has been stalled since Friday, when security officials said Iraqi forces and their militia allies would wait for reinforcements before moving forward.
24Two days later, back-up had not yet arrived, and officials continued to stress the challenges they faced in flushing out militants in street-by-street battles and defusing bombs and booby traps they laid while retreating from parts of the city.
25"There were no new pushes today from either side, only scattered skirmishes in the northern and southern parts of the city," Colonel Mohammed Abid al-Jubouri told Reuters by phone after visiting the southern edge of Tikrit on Sunday.
26Jubouri said the process of "organising troops and waiting for reinforcements" was ongoing and did not say when a fresh effort to seize the central districts of the city still held by Islamic State would begin.
27The militants still hold about half the city, which lies about 160km north of Baghdad.
28The effort to retake Saddam Hussein's home city from the militants who overran it last year has been the biggest offensive yet against the group that has declared an Islamic caliphate on territory it controls in Iraq and neighbouring Syria.
29Though Sunni residents of areas near Tikrit recently freed from the militants' grip continued to return home on Sunday in trucks carrying white flags, fears remain that sectarian tensions will again rise to the fore after the battle for Tikrit.
30"The growing weight of the Shi'ite militias and Iran further weaken the Iraqi state's credibility and structures as well as the standing of local elites" in Sunni areas north of Baghdad like Tikrit, said Maria Fantappie, Iraq analyst at the International Crisis Group.
