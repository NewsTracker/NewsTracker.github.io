IMF quits Greek talks; EU tells Tsipras: stop gambling

1Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras listens to European Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker (R) during a meeting at the EU Council in Brussels, Belgium, June 11, 2015.
2WASHINGTON/BRUSSELS (Reuters) - The International Monetary Fund dramatically raised the stakes in Greece's stalled debt talks on Thursday, announcing that its delegation had left negotiations in Brussels and flown home because of major differences with Athens.
3The surprise IMF move came as the European Union told Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras to stop gambling with his cash-strapped country's future and take the crucial decisions needed to avert a devastating default.
4A Greek source told Reuters that the entire Greek delegation that had been negotiating a cash-for-reform deal had also left for home on Thursday, citing continuing disagreements.
5"There are major differences between us in most key areas," IMF spokesman Gerry Rice said in Washington.
6"There has been no progress in narrowing these differences recently and thus we are well away from an agreement."
7Greece needs a deal to unlock aid before the end of the month when it is otherwise set to default on a 1.6 billion euro ($1.8 billion) repayment to the Washington-based IMF.
8That could trigger capital controls and possibly push Greece out of the euro zone, with unpredictable consequences for financial markets and the European economy.
9Rice said the sticking points remained pensions, taxes and financing.
10The IMF technical team had returned to the United States but remained "fully engaged" with Athens.
11European stocks fell after the IMF comments.
12Athens stood by its assertions of recent days that all is not lost.
13"The Greek delegation, as agreed, is ready to intensify deliberations in order to conclude a deal soon, even in the coming days," spokesman Gabriel Sakellaridis said.
14GAME OVER?
15One official close to the negotiations also cautioned against excessive pessimism, saying the IMF team's departure represented a break in the talks rather than a breaking off.
16IMF experts sit in the "Brussels Group" of technical officials which has been on the sidelines in recent days as the politicians bargained.
17This suggested there was little point in their staying in Brussels, although they appeared ready to return at short notice if need be.
18However, European Council President Donald Tusk delivered an unprecedentedly forthright message to Greece's radical anti-austerity government after four months of bitter negotiations.
19"There is no more time for gambling. The day is coming, I'm afraid, that someone says that the game is over," he told a news conference after chairing an EU-Latin America summit that was dominated by intense talks with Tsipras on the sidelines.
20"It is very obvious that we need decisions, not negotiations," Tusk said, adding that Athens needed to be "more realistic".
21Tsipras held two hours of talks with European Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker, but neither side reported any breakthrough.
22"Come in the torture room," Juncker told Tsipras at the start of their meeting.
23EU officials later described the talks as a "last attempt" to reach a debt deal.
24Asked about concerns for the process raised by the departure of IMF and Greek negotiators, an EU diplomat said: "If the process was working properly the president would not have had to have a meeting with Tsipras today."
25Tsipras told reporters he had worked on bridging the remaining differences on fiscal and financing issues.
26"We are working to assure an agreement which will ensure that Greece will recover with social cohesion and viable public debts," he said.
27Tusk's admonition reinforced warnings by German Bundesbank President Jens Weidmann and EU Economics Commissioner Pierre Moscovici that time was running out to avert a Greek state bankruptcy and possible exit from the euro zone.
28Late-night talks between Tsipras and the leaders of Germany and France produced no breakthrough, although all sides said they had moved closer on the procedure leading to an agreement.
29"At the end of the talks there was absolute unanimity that Greece will work intensively and full steam ahead ... in the coming days to solve all remaining issues," German Chancellor Angela Merkel said.
30To clinch a deal, EU officials said Tsipras's government needed to offer alternative savings and tax measures to replace proposed pension cuts and tax rises he rejected as antisocial, to deliver a modest fiscal surplus before interest payments.
31People familiar with the talks said the two sides have come closer to agreeing a primary surplus target but are still wide apart on how to achieve it, with EU and IMF experts doubting that measures touted by Greece can do the job.
32More dire warnings rained down on the Greek leader as he contemplated what concessions to make to clinch a deal without alienating his left-wing supporters who elected him in January on promises to put an end to years of austerity.
33"There is a strong determination to help Greece," Weidmann said in a speech in London.
34"But time is running out, and the risk of insolvency is increasing by the day."
35The main losers of a Greek default and euro exit would be Greece and the Greek people, he said.
36The Greek leader faces pressure both from left-wing hardliners and from voters, most of whom say they want to remain in the euro zone and want him to make concessions for a deal.
37Underlining the resistance Tsipras faces if he gives too much ground, hundreds of supporters of the Communist-affiliated trade union PAME flooded on to an Athens square in a peaceful anti-bailout protest on Thursday evening.
38Earlier they replaced the EU flag with PAME's on the roof of the finance ministry.
39They also draped a banner across five storys of the ministry showing two previous prime ministers who each negotiated a deal with the lenders.
40Next to them was a portrait of Tsipras above the slogan "Bailout Number Three".
