UN warns against complacency as Ebola fight enters new phase

Sieren's China: Great firewall little more than a transparent fig leaf

1U.N. experts said Monday that the spread of the disease in Guinea, Liberia and Sierra Leone has dropped to almost 10 percent of what it was in September.
2But U.N. Special Envoy on Ebola David Nabarro said the fight has reached a "second phase, getting to zero, and it really is the hardest part."
3Ebola has infected nearly 24,000 people and killed more than 9,600, according to World Health Organization statistics.
4Nabarro said eradicating the disease requires treating all those who are ill and tracking everyone they come in contact with.
5"It's a really difficult, painstaking task," he said in Brussels, on the eve of a major international conference on Ebola expected to draw around 600 delegates, including the leaders of Guinea, Liberia and Sierra Leone.
6According to U.N. figures, the number of new cases has dropped from some 800-900 per week at the height of the outbreak to around 100 per week.
7"We are reaching the most difficult phase, where there is a fatigue among the governments among the population, certainly among the donors, and we are not yet there," he said.
8The outbreak is focused in an area of West Africa about the size of France, with a population of about 20 million people, and where infrastructure is limited.
9Ebola has cost the three countries an estimated 12 percent of their gross domestic product, shifted resources causing other health care problems, and hurt business as people became scared to leave their homes or go to markets.
1It's no secret that Internet censorship is rampant in China.
2Reports abound of blacklisted keywords, as well as blocked websites and search engines.
3According to a recent report from the European Union Chamber of Commerce in China (EUCCC), in mid-February, 86 percent of its members said that Internet censorship has had a bad effect on business (compared to 71 percent in mid-2014).
4The EUCCC says that a number of companies are therefore reducing investment in technology and research in China.
5It described the development as massively detrimental to the country, one that sends a negative message and stops companies from bringing qualified staff to China.
6But this is only one side of the story.
7Today, only small businesses continue to insist that Internet censorship hampers their research and development.
8Larger companies have long since been operating dedicated lines which allow them to circumvent the Great Firewall.
9Even the German school in Beijing has one.
10Normal users also know how to access whatever information they're after by using VPNs (virtual private networks) that can be activated via smartphones or on a PC.
11It takes about 30 seconds to get connected - easy enough but time-consuming, with slow download speeds.
12But it nonetheless allows users to open banned websites and find out, for example, what Taiwanese academics and Uighur freedom fighters are saying, or even to access blocked content on the BBC, the New York Times or DW.
13However, VPN-blocking is also a common problem.
14Astrill, one of the most popular VPN providers in China, found a number of its channels blocked by the authorities in early January.
15These days, iPhone and iPad owners can no longer automatically use Astrill's VPN options but have to access the service through a complicated process.
16Even so, 90 million of China's 650 million Internet users continue to use VPN services - in other words, anyone who is interested in information from abroad.
17They continue to access international websites, even if doing so takes them longer than it needs to.
18And, on average, it doesn't take them as long as it takes Internet users in India.
19According to the "State of the Internet" quarterly report by content delivery network Akamai, India has the slowest Internet speed in Asia, ranking 118th in the world - behind the Philippines and Vietnam - with 2.0 megabytes per second (mbps), while China ranks 79th with 3.8 mbps.
20Meanwhile, smartphone Internet connection speed in China is three times faster than in India.
21There might be places in India where Internet speed is better than the fastest VPN in China, but the rule of thumb is that even with the Great Firewall, it's easier to access international websites in China than in India, a country where there's no Internet censorship.
22Although in India, unlike in China, domestic content isn't censored either.
23In terms of international connectivity and free, fast access, the biggest digital divides in Asia are therefore not so much between democratic India and dictatorial China but between China, India, Indonesia and the Philippines on the one side and on the other, South Korea, Japan and Hong Kong, which boast Internet speeds between 25 and 15 mbps - the world's fastest.
24Europe's fastest is in Switzerland (14.5 mbps); in Germany, it's below 10.
25But China is the emerging economy that's catching up the quickest, aiming to reach Internet speeds of over 4 mbps this year, and as fast as 20 mbps in the cities.
