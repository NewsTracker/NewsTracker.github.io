Oldest known stone tools found in Kenya

1Sonia Harmand and Jason Lewis with stone tools found in the West Turkana area of Kenya.
2New York: By taking a wrong turn in a dry riverbed in Kenya, scientists discovered a trove of stone tools far older than any ever found before.
3At 3.3 million years old, they push back the record of stone tools by about 700,000 years.
4One of the ancient stone tools found in Kenya.
5More significantly, they are half-a-million years older than any known trace of our own branch of the evolutionary tree.
6Scientists have long thought that sharp-edged stone tools were made only by members of our branch, whose members are designated "Homo", like our own species, Homo sapiens.
7That idea has been questioned, and the new finding is a big boost to the argument that tool-making may have begun with smaller-brained forerunners instead.
8Archaeologist Sonia Harmand examines a stone tool she and a colleague found in Kenya.
9The discovery was reported by Sonia Harmand and Jason Lewis of Stony Brook University in New York and co-authors of a paper released on Wednesday by the journal Nature.
10The Nature paper describes 149 stones and stone flakes found west of Lake Turkana in a remote area of northern Kenya.
11Most objects are "cores", which are stones that have been struck to break off sharp-edged flakes.
12Other stones appear to have been used as hammers or anvils.
13Africa is where our own species first appeared, and it has long been a hotbed for finding fossils of our forerunners.
14The Kenyan site was discovered one day in July 2011, when Harmand, Lewis and a crew set out to survey one area and by accident ended up in another one.
15There were gullies and hillsides that seemed promising, so they looked around, Lewis recalled.
16Just before tea time, a team member spotted a stone tool on the ground.
17More quickly appeared.
18Excavations followed.
19As stone tools go, the artifacts are remarkably big.
20On average, the cores stretch about 15 centimetres long and wide and weigh nearly three kilograms, for example, while the flakes are up to 20 centimetres long.
21Generally, ancient human relatives are thought to have used stone tools for hammering, such as for cracking nuts, and for their sharp edges, useful for butchering and skinning animal carcasses as well as cutting up tough plant material.
22Experts said they were stumped about the purpose of the Kenyan tools.
23Harmand said she thinks the overall purpose was to make sharp-edged flakes for cutting, but exactly how they were used is not known.
24One candidate would be some Homo species not yet known to science, he said.
25Other possibilities come from outside the Homo branch, such as Australopithecus afarensis, best known for the skeleton nicknamed Lucy.
26Still another candidate is a creature called Kenyanthropus platyops, known from remains found not far from the site of the stone tools.
