Blatter says he will forgive but won't forget UEFA rebellion

1FIFA president Sepp Blatter after his election as President greeted by UEFA President Michel Platini, right, at the Hallenstadion in Zurich, Switzerland, Friday, May 29, 2015.
2Blatter has been re-elected as FIFA president for a fifth term, chosen to lead world soccer despite separate U.S. and Swiss criminal investigations into corruption.
3The 209 FIFA member federations gave the 79-year-old Blatter another four-year term on Friday after Prince Ali bin al-Hussein of Jordan conceded defeat after losing 133-73 in the first round.
4Emboldened by his fresh mandate to lead world soccer, Sepp Blatter made it clear there may be consequences for European leaders who tried to oust him from FIFA.
5"I forgive but I don't forget," Blatter said Saturday at FIFA headquarters.
6The relationship between FIFA and UEFA will be one of the key issues for Blatter as he begins his fifth term as president of the governing body.
7UEFA President Michel Platini, who helped Blatter first gain the presidency in 1998, led opposition against his former ally.
8Blatter didn't extend much of an olive branch in his first news conference since Friday's election, but did reiterate that he is "also the president of those associations that are against me."
9He also challenged UEFA to come back into the fold.
10"(UEFA) have to be an example also and not only to say, 'FIFA, what is FIFA?' They shall help and come in and take responsibility," Blatter said.
11Repairing the relationship may not be so simple though.
12Following this week's arrests of senior FIFA official as part of a U.S. investigation into soccer corruption, Platini appealed directly to Blatter to stand down.
13That request was unequivocally rejected and Platini now has to work with Blatter for four more years.
14However, the former France great said change is still crucial if FIFA "is to regain its credibility."
15Platini and other European officials have suggested UEFA should look into splitting with FIFA completely, although the consequences of such a drastic move would be so far-reaching that it seems highly implausible.
16It would bar some of soccer's biggest countries from the World Cup, and would cut off the European club game from the rest of the world.
17That could cause an exodus of non-European players, and Blatter reminded clubs of that.
18"UEFA is the biggest (confederation), UEFA is the richest one, you have the best competition, you have the best players," Blatter said.
19"If you don't have players from the other continent, then they will not be so rich or so good in football."
20There was no immediate retribution toward Europe, though, as FIFA's executive committee voted to make no changes to the format for World Cup qualifying spots.
21The decision ensures there will still be 13 European countries, plus host Russia, at the 32-team tournament in 2018.
22Europe will also have 13 teams at the World Cup in Qatar in 2022.
23The executive committee also cleared the way for European countries to bid for the 2026 World Cup, despite previous comments by Blatter that it would be too soon after Russia hosts the 2018 tournament.
24UEFA's strategy for how to deal with Blatter may become clearer next week when the European body holds meetings in Berlin ahead of the Champions League final.
25"There should be some kind of reaction," said Dutch federation president Michael van Praag, who had been running for FIFA presidency before withdrawing last week to back Prince Ali.
26But opposition to Blatter is not unanimous in Europe.
27Russia is fiercely loyal to Blatter, having been awarded the 2018 World Cup under his watch.
28The Kremlin said President Vladimir Putin sent a telegram congratulating Blatter on his re-election and expressing confidence in his "experience, professionalism and high authority."
29France's football federation also voted for Blatter, ignoring Frenchman Platini's public appeals for the world to back Prince Ali.
30Some of the strongest criticism of Blatter has come from England, and Blatter took a swipe at former Manchester United chief executive David Gill for rejecting the FIFA vice presidency he assumed directly before the 79-year-old Swiss was re-elected.
31Gill said the pre-announced move was to defend his "professional reputation," and that he didn't want to serve under the current leadership.
32Blatter said Gill had not informed FIFA directly of his resignation and was counted as a missing participant from Saturday's executive committee meeting.
33"You cannot take (responsibility) when you are elected and you don't even come to the first meeting," Blatter said.
34"You are elected you have to come, whoever is the president of FIFA. ... He has given no excuse or reasons for not being present."
