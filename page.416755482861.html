7BALTIMORE (AP) - Months before Freddie Gray died of the broken neck he suffered during what Baltimore's top prosecutor called an illegal arrest, the city's mayor and police commissioner said the department needed reform and asked the Justice Department for help reviewing officer misconduct.
8Now that Gray is buried, six officers are charged in his death and an uneasy calm has returned to the streets, critics are wondering whether city leaders are capable of implementing the change the city needs without the direct, intensive oversight that comes with a full-fledged civil rights investigation resulting in a federal consent decree.
9Democratic Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake has already pushed back against this possibility, saying it would deprive Baltimore's leaders from having a say in fighting crime in one of the nation's most violent major cities, with more than 200 homicides a year.
10"Nobody wants the Department of Justice to come and take over our city," she said last week.
11Baltimore's leaders should welcome federal oversight, because it's doubtful any police department can fix itself from within, said Erwin Chemerinsky, the dean of the University of California-Irvine School of Law.
12Consent decrees have been mostly effective since Congress responded to the Rodney King beating in Los Angeles by granting the Justice Department the power in 1994 to sue police departments for civil rights violations.
13Los Angeles went through it, and proved that it works, said Chemerinsky, who has studied reform efforts there.
14"I think that there is less likelihood of excessive force today, less racist policing today in Los Angeles, than prior to the consent decree," he said.
15The Justice Department has negotiated settlements with 21 other police departments since then; Seattle and New Orleans are currently under consent decrees, and Cleveland's police department is negotiating one.
16Justice officials are also negotiating with the department in Ferguson, Missouri, where an officer's shooting of 18-year-old Michael Brown sparked a national debate about use of force by police against blacks.
17The federal civil rights investigation in Ferguson found patterns of racial bias and discriminatory law enforcement tactics.
18A consent decree is a form of negotiated settlement with the Justice Department that averts a civil-rights lawsuit.
19Police departments agree to implement a series of reforms under the watch of an independent monitor.
20The Justice Department already announced a separate federal probe of Gray's death.
21And a broad civil rights investigation would not begin unless federal authorities conclude the ongoing voluntary review is insufficient.
22Federal consent decrees also create new challenges.
23It can take more than a decade for police departments to satisfy their requirements, and meanwhile, expenses add up: It can cost tens of millions of dollars to retrain officers, hire new ones and modify use-of-force policies.
24"Cities don't want to invest their scarce resources in the costly process of reforming a police department," said Stephen Rushin, a visiting assistant professor of law at the University of Illinois who is working on a book about police reform.
25"Typically, it takes away from investments in schools, roads, parks, other things the city is going to value."
26Then again, the city is already spending millions in legal settlements with people alleging officers have injured them or killed family members.
27The mayor and Police Commissioner Anthony Batts asked for the Justice Department review last year after The Baltimore Sun tallied $5.7 million in payouts to resolve more than 100 police misconduct lawsuits since 2011.
28The voluntary review should result in recommendations and give the city access to federal funds to implement them, but they would not be enforced by any court order or independent monitor.
29Meanwhile, Baltimore faces financial challenges so serious that in 2013, the mayor hired outside consultants who forecast that the local government was on the path to insolvency.
30The mayor has implemented spending reforms, but has little leeway to raise taxes.
31The city's property taxes are already the highest in Maryland after being raised repeatedly between 1950 and 1985, in part to cover the cost of city services as the city lost manufacturing jobs and shed population.
32Some residents and legal advocates think the city's government won't be able to impose change unless it's forced to.
33"There's skepticism about how thorough and effective any kind of collaborative process will be," said Sonia Kumar, a staff attorney with the ACLU of Maryland.
34"The measure of success is really whether people feel that things are different on the street in their interactions with police."
35Randy Howell, 55, who grew up in the Sandtown-Winchester neighborhood where Gray was arrested, says far more than federal intervention is needed to set things right.
36The city, state and federal governments need to address what he calls the root causes of crime: high unemployment, poor schools, grinding poverty and a lack of amenities.
37"The first thing they need to do is get rid of them vans," Howell said.
38"Those vans are a death trap. ... Other police departments don't use them. And body cameras. But they also need to change the people and the neighborhood."
