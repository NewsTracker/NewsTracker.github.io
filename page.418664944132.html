Greek banks reopen, taxes on basic goods soar

1Greek banks opened their branches across the country on Monday after a three-week shutdown, while German Chancellor Angela Merkel called for swift aid talks so Athens could also lift withdrawal limits.
2Greek banks opened their doors Monday for the first time in over three weeks, a move that the government hopes will help the economy get back to normal following a period dominated by fears over the country's future in the euro.
3Still, strict controls on the amounts individuals can withdraw remain and new austerity taxes demanded by the country's European creditors mean that most everyday items are more expensive - from coffee to taxis to cooking oil.
4In downtown Athens, people queued up in an orderly fashion as the banks unlocked their doors at 8 a.m., but restrictions on most transactions remained.
5Though the daily cash withdrawal limit stayed at 60 euros, the government has given individuals a new weekly limit of 420 euros from this coming Sunday so they don't need to trudge to the ATM every day.
6Ready cash is something Greeks will need as new taxes also came into effect on a wide array of goods and services Monday.
7Sales taxes have risen from 13 per cent to 23 per cent on many basic goods - including some meats, cooking oils, coffee, tea, cocoa, vinegar, salt, flowers, firewood, fertilizer, insecticides, sanitary towels and condoms.
8Popular services were also hit by the new taxes: restaurants and cafes, funeral homes, taxis, ferries, cram schools and language schools.
9The new taxes are part of a package of confidence-building measures that the Greek government had to introduce in order for negotiations on a third bailout to begin.
10Since the Greek parliament passed the measures, creditors have sought to relieve the pressure on Greece.
11The European Central Bank has raised the amount of liquidity assistance on offer to Greek banks while Greece's partners in the 19-country eurozone agreed to give Athens a short-term loan so it wouldn't default on a 4.2 billion-euro debt due to the ECB on Monday.
12Mina Andreeva, a spokeswoman at the European Commission, confirmed Monday that the 7.16 billion-euro three-month loan has been sent to Athens so it can repay the ECB and also to clear arrears with the International Monetary Fund.
13Andreeva said she was confident Greece will make the payments "in the course of today."
14Paying off the IMF and ECB will give Greece some breathing room but the country will need to secure the bailout, which is expected to be worth around 85 billion euros, to meet upcoming debts.
15After months of negotiations with creditors, Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras agreed last week to accept a series of demands such as pension cuts and sales tax hikes, in order for the bailout discussions to begin.
16"The government was obliged to make a tactical retreat to save the country," new Labor Minister Giorgos Katrougalos said Monday.
17"This was the result of a soft, post-modern financial coup that was handled by the prime minister in a responsible way."
18Getting the Greek economy back to normal will take time though especially as credit - the lifeblood of a modern economy - remains restricted.
19Louka Katseli, head of the Greek Banking Association, said Monday it was too early to say how long the cash controls would last.
20"I totally understand people who are anxious," she told state-run ERT television.
21"But acting with fear produces the circumstances that people are afraid of."
22Katseli noted that since 2008, 124 billion euros worth of deposits has been withdrawn from Greek banks and that 40 billion of that money has been removed in the last few months.
23"If that had not been removed, the Greek banks would not have had a liquidity problem," she said.
24The Greek government introduced the capital controls because negotiations with creditors had reached an impasse, fueling speculation that the country was going to default on its debts and exit the euro.
25It has cleared most of the hurdles demanded of it, but still faces another vote in parliament this Wednesday to impose more austerity measures.
26Amid the pressure, Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras is struggling to contain a growing revolt in his Syriza party that threatened to topple the government last week in the first round of austerity voting.
27Cabinet-level dissenters were replaced in a reshuffle Friday, but even their replacements have angrily denounced the new austerity measures.
