As South Sudan conflict rages, UN expands refugee camp in Kenya

1A policeman patrols as refugees from South Sudan queue to attend World Refugee Day celebrations at the Kakuma refugee camp in Kenya.
2Kakuma, Kenya: Kakuma camp in northern Kenya is expanding by nearly a half, the United Nations refugee agency said on Saturday, to house refugees fleeing nearby South Sudan, as hopes fade for peace in the world's newest nation.
3The arrival of some 44,000 South Sudanese refugees since late 2013, when fighting broke out between forces loyal to President Salva Kiir and rebels allied with former vice-president Riek Machar, has stretched the camp to its limits.
4"Before the resumption of the current crisis, what we hoped was that the camp would shut. But what we've seen, since December 2013, is actually the contrary," said Raouf Mazou, the UNHCR's Kenya representative.
5An aerial view shows recently constructed houses at the Kakuma refugee camp in Kenya.
6Kakuma is home to some 185,000 people, mostly from South Sudan, and with the planned expansion it will soon accommodate up to 80,000 more.
7The extra space will help ease congestion as well as make room for new arrivals.
8The sprawling Kakuma camp was set up in Kenya's arid Turkana County in 1992 to accommodate thousands of "Lost Boys" who fled the southern part of what was then Sudan and walked hundreds of kilometres to Kenya.
9A 2005 peace deal paved the way for South Sudan's 2011 independence and the UN launched a repatriation programme.
10By 2008, officials contemplated the camp's closure.
11The Kakuma refugee camp in north-western Kenya, near the border with South Sudan.
12But the crisis involving Mr Kiir and Dr Machar has led to fierce fighting along ethnic lines.
13Thousands have been killed and more than 1.5 million people have been displaced, while another 500,000 have fled to neighbouring countries, especially Ethiopia and Uganda.
14"Nobody thinks that they can go back," said Andrew Riek Wal, 24, who lived in Kakuma for a decade before returning home in 2011, only to come back to the camp in 2013.
15"I am very worried about going back because I may be killed."
16During his brief return, Mr Wal got a job teaching English in the capital Juba and began searching for family members.
17But when fighting resumed, he feared he was especially vulnerable.
18Because he had spent his adolescence in Kakuma, he was never given the distinctive horizontal forehead marks that distinguish Nuer men - many of whom support Dr Machar - and he feared he would be mistaken for a Dinka, the ethnic group to which Mr Kiir belongs.
19"Living this life of a refugee, it seems like I am in prison," he said.
