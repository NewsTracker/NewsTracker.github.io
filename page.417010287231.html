Fin24.com | Facebook accused of trampling on privacy

1Facebook has emerged as the most popular social media destination.
2Brussels - Facebook tramples on European privacy laws by tracking people online without their consent and dodges questions from national regulators, Belgium's privacy watchdog said on Friday.
3Belgium's Privacy Protection Commission (CPVP/CBPL), which is working with German, Dutch, French and Spanish counterparts, launched the blistering attack after trying to find out more about the US tech giant's practices.
4It urged internet users to install privacy software to shield themselves from Facebook's tracking systems, whether they have an account with the social network or not.
5The show of strength from the Belgian regulator, which does not have the power to levy fines, highlights a growing willingness across the 28-member bloc to ensure big US tech companies abide by European laws.
6"Facebook tramples on European and Belgian privacy laws," the Commission said in a statement after analysing changes the company made to its privacy policies in January.
7It said Facebook had refused to recognise Belgian and other EU national jurisdictions, with the company insisting it was subject only to the law in Ireland, the site of its European headquarters.
8"Facebook has shown itself particularly miserly in giving precise answers," the watchdog said, adding that the results of the study by a group of researchers were "disconcerting".
9A Facebook spokesperson questioned the Belgians' authority but said it would review the study's recommendations with the Irish data protection commissioner: "We work hard to make sure people have control over what they share and with whom."
10"Facebook is already regulated in Europe and complies with European data protection law, so the applicability of the CBPL's efforts are unclear," she said.
11Ireland has come under criticism from EU states for policies some say are soft on the multinational companies Dublin wants to attract, whether on data protection or corporate taxation.
12The Commission said Facebook would not explain in detail how it uses data it collects and highlighted problems with plug-ins such as Facebook's "like" button, which it said affects many who do not have an account with the social network.
13Facebook is among a number of firms under fire in Europe over how it uses data it collects.
14It places tracking "cookies" when anyone visits a Facebook page, meaning it can track the online activities of a huge number of non-customers.
15Facebook later said this was a bug and it is working to fix it.
