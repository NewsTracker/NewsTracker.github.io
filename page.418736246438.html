Despite negativity, Apple Watch shows signs of familiar path to success

1The Apple Watch might not have flown off shelves like new iPhones do, but that's not really a fair comparison.
2Asking if the Apple Watch will become a hit or a flop is a bit like asking if my 2-year-old daughter is destined to go to Yale or to jail.
3Interested parties can speculate on the basis of thin evidence - she learned to walk pretty early, though on the other hand, she still thinks cats say "bow wow" - but youth is inherently unpredictable, and anyone venturing a long-term forecast based on short-term performance runs the risk of looking quite silly.
4Technology pundits tend to be a rash bunch, though, so there has been no shortage of prognostication about the Apple Watch, a device that went on sale three months ago.
5Because reviews (including mine) were mixed and the device hasn't proved to be culturally revolutionary, some are declaring the watch dead on arrival.
6The watch sold more in its first nine weeks on the market than either the iPhone or the iPad did in that same period.
7Apple has declined to provide sales figures for the watch, its newest product.
8In its earnings report for the fiscal third quarter -the first to include sales of the Apple Watch - the company was cagey about sales, with Tim Cook, chief executive, saying the device had "a great start."
9Analysts' estimates vary wildly, with many originally predicting that Apple sold 3 million to 5 million watches from April to the end of June.
10After studying Apple's opaque earnings report, several analysts revised their estimates down to about 1.5 million to 3 million watches.
11Even at the lower end, that's the opposite of instant death: Luca Maestri, Apple's chief financial officer, pointedly said the watch sold more in its first nine weeks on the market than either the iPhone or the iPad did in that same period.
12Yet the future of the Apple Watch will not be determined by first-quarter sales.
13Apple's product debuts tend to follow a well-worn script: A first-generation device is always criticised as overpriced and a bit lacking in utility and is often vulnerable to the charge that it is a solution in search of a problem.
14Then, over a few years, Apple and its customers figure out the best uses for the gadget, and the company methodically improves design and functionality to meet those needs.
15It also tends to lower its prices.
16Correspondingly, sales explode.
17Given this history, the question to ask about the Apple Watch isn't how well it has sold so far, but how well Apple is following that script.
18Is it moving quickly to address the early criticism of the watch and to expand access to and functionality of the device?
19The answer here is far more definitive than the murky sales figures: So far, Apple is following exactly the same playbook for the watch that it did for the iPod, the iPhone and the iPad.
20If it sticks to that pattern - trying to add new capabilities to the device while perhaps lowering prices and expanding distribution - the future of the Apple Watch could be bright.
21The only catch is that determining success will take months, if not years.
22Waiting may be too much to ask of itchy tech observers, especially because Cook's legacy is riding on his ability to introduce a new product category to the world's tech-obsessed masses.
23But waiting may be the only option.
24Of the data we have on the watch so far, much of it leans positive.
25Owners of Apple Watches, for one, seem to like them.
26Creative Strategies and Wristly, two research firms, recently conducted a study of more than 800 Apple Watch owners.
27About 97 per cent reported being either very or somewhat satisfied with the device.
28That level is better than corresponding levels of satisfaction for the original iPhone and iPad, both of which scored in the low 90s in early customer-sentiment studies.
29Ben Bajarin, an analyst at Creative Strategies, said the study also revealed a point of perhaps deeper importance.
30There is a split, the research found, between people who describe themselves as tech-obsessed - people who work in the industry or build apps for a living - and those outside the tech world, also known as normal people.
31Early adopters were pickier about the watch's shortcomings, the study found.
32Only 43 per cent of app developers described themselves as "very satisfied" with the watch, compared with 73 per cent of nontech users who were delighted by the device.
33The split between the tech elite and mainstream users echoes a story that has been repeated throughout Apple's history.
34The iPod, the iPhone and the iPad all confounded tech insiders, who loudly criticised faults with the devices that they claimed would be fatal - and that, it turned out, didn't much matter to ordinary people, or to Apple's long-term success.
35The first iPod, which sold for $US399, was called too expensive, and it worked only with Macs.
36The iPhone didn't have a copy-and-paste function; it ran only the slow Edge network; and it didn't have a removable battery.
37Also, it couldn't run third-party apps.
38The iPad didn't load websites with Flash animation, and it didn't have a camera.
39A few of these were genuine shortcomings, and Apple eventually addressed them.
40The company created a Windows version of the iPod, added an app store to the iPhone and put a camera on the iPad.
41But in many instances it ignored the techies: Apple never added Flash to iOS, and you'd be hard-pressed to find anyone in tech these days who thinks that was a bad idea.
42The point here isn't that the tech critics are always wrong.
43Instead, the pattern suggests the early criticism isn't necessarily the best long-term guide.
44It takes time to figure out which shortcomings in a new product are significant and which are merely symptoms of an outmoded way of looking at technology.
45With the Apple Watch, the company is clearly studying these patterns now, and it has already shown off some coming changes.
46There have been two main criticisms of the watch thus far: It's too slow, and it's not necessary - it doesn't do all that much you can't do on your phone.
47A more pernicious problem is that developers have not yet hit on killer ideas for the watch, and some of the world's largest tech companies are moving cautiously to create apps for it.
48Apple is fixing these problems faster than it addressed major shortcomings with other first-generation products.
49At its developer conference in June, the company unveiled an update to the watch's operating system that Apple said would greatly improve its speed.
50The new operating system, appearing later this year, will also let developers use more of the sensors and other technologies on the watch, allowing for more powerful apps.
51These advances might sound small, but in tech, incremental improvements can drive big changes.
52Jan Dawson, an independent tech analyst, told me the new operating system would "be a big part of generating more compelling-use cases for the watch for mainstream users, because that's when we'll really start to see a flood of third-party apps that make sense."
53What's more, bit-by-bit improvements are part of Apple's modus operandi.
54We saw it with the iPod, the iPhone and the iPad.
55After creating something new, the company continually adds small new features over time.
56Over a few years, this turns an initial burst of interest about Apple's newest thing into a long-term tech institution that just about everyone can use and enjoy.
57That's happening with the watch, and the strategy just might work.
