Lufthansa insurers to set aside $300 million for Germanwings crash

1One week ago 150 people died in the Germanwings airliner crash in the French alps on its way from Barcelona to Duesseldorf.
2PARIS -- French aviation investigators said Tuesday they will examine "systemic weaknesses" like cockpit entry rules and psychological screening procedures that could have led to the Germanwings plane crash -- issues that could affect the worldwide aviation industry.
3Lufthansa, meanwhile, said its insurers had set aside $300 million to deal with possible costs from the March 24 crash.
4The announcement by the French aviation agency BEA signalled the latest re-think about airline procedures in the wake of the Germanwings crash, which jolted an aviation industry already reeling after one passenger plane disappeared into an ocean and another was shot out of the sky over war-torn eastern Ukraine.
5Authorities say Germanwings co-pilot Andreas Lubitz, who in the past had been treated for suicidal tendencies, locked his captain out of the cockpit before deliberately crashing the Airbus 320 into a mountain in the French Alps.
6All the 150 people aboard Flight 9525 from Barcelona to Duesseldorf were killed that day.
7BEA said it aims to provide a "detailed analysis" of the Germanwings cockpit voice recorder and any other flight data -- but it also plans to widen its search to examine issues that could be problematic for all airlines.
8"(We will study) systemic weaknesses (that) might possibly have led to this aviation disaster," BEA said in its first statement since prosecutors detailed the co-pilot's suspected role in the crash.
9The agency is studying both psychological screening procedures and rules applied to entering and leaving the cockpit, as well as cockpit door locking systems.
10BEA's investigation runs parallel to a criminal probe being led by prosecutors.
11Both are investigating what happened with the flight.
12The end goal of the criminal investigation is to hold someone responsible.
13The end goal of the BEA investigation is to make recommendations to aviation authorities to prevent similar accidents from happening again.
14If the French agency discovers wider aviation problems, that could spark industry regulators and airline companies worldwide to make changes.
15Several airlines and the European aviation regulator have already changed procedures after the Germanwings crash, announcing last week that they now recommend having two people in the cockpit at all times.
16In Frankfurt, Lufthansa spokeswoman Kerstin Lau said $300 million is the amount currently reserved by insurers to deal with "all costs arising in connection with the case."
17Lufthansa -- Germanwings' parent company -- offered immediate aid last week of up to 50,000 euros ($54,250) per passenger to relatives of the victims.
18Those payments are separate from eventual compensation payments.
19Airlines are required under a treaty governing deaths and injuries aboard international flights to compensate relatives of victims for proven damages of up to a limit currently set at about $157,000 -- regardless of what caused the crash.
20However, higher compensation is possible if a carrier is held liable.
21German prosecutors say Lubitz, 27, received psychotherapy before obtaining his pilot's license and that medical records from that time referred to "suicidal tendencies."
22They have given no dates for his treatment, but said visits to doctors since then showed no record of any suicidal tendencies or aggression against others.
23They also have found torn-up sick notes from doctors, including one that would have kept Lubitz off work on the day of the crash.
24Lufthansa has declined to say whether it knew anything about Lubitz's health problems.
25But it says the young pilot had passed all required medical checks since starting work for its subsidiary in September 2013.
26The airline on Tuesday cancelled plans to celebrate its 60th anniversary on April 15 "out of respect for the victims of the crash."
27At the crash site in the French Alps, investigators said Tuesday they hope to have found DNA samples for everyone killed on the flight in the next 24 hours.

29Jean-Marc Menichini, speaking in the nearby town of Le Vernet, said the search was still on for the plane's second black box -- its data recorder.
30French President Francois Hollande, speaking to reporters during a previously planned trip to Germany, said: "By the end of the week at the latest, it will be possible to identify all the victims thanks to the DNA samples taken."
31Hollande said German and French ministers also discussed the need to improve checks of air passengers within Europe's visa-free Schengen zone, and to "ensure that we can also strengthen our safety rules for piloting planes."
32Construction workers on Monday cut a road to the steep, mountainous crash site to speed up recovery efforts, since previously emergency workers had to rely on helicopters.
33"We can accelerate the work. It will be much easier not to have to depend on the weather," said Lt. Col.
34Philippe Sansa of the rescue service.
35German investigators from a special team tasked with identifying the victims and determining their cause of death are expected to travel to the crash site on Wednesday.
