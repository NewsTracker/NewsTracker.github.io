Greek debt crisis: Violence erupts as Greek parliament debates bailout

1RAW VISION: Greek anti-establishment protesters throw petrol bombs at police in front of parliament after thousands demonstrate against austerity.
2Athens: Police clashed with protesters in central Athens as parliamentarians debated a new bailout of up to 86 billion euros ($194 billion) that will impose further austerity on a country already ravaged by recession.
3Riot police fired tear gas and protesters threw petrol bombs in central Athens as parliamentarians were voting on the bailout bill, which will endorse tax increases and spending cuts.
4Greek anti-establishment protesters threw petrol bombs at police in front of parliament on Wednesday.
5The protests show the challenges Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras faces convincing a country whose economy has shrunk by a quarter in the last five years to accept further spending cuts.
6In a July 5 referendum, 61 percent of Greeks voted "no" to a package of cuts less onerous than the one Mr Tsipras accepted on Monday after capitulating to creditors' demands.
7"I want to say something to my comrades," Greek Finance Minister Euclid Tsakalotos told lawmakers.
8"On Monday morning, at 9.30 am, it was the most difficult moment of my life. It was a decision which will be a burden for me for the rest of my life. I don't know if we did the right thing. But I know we did something to which there was no alternative."
9The scenes outside parliament were reminiscent of the turmoil that rocked Greece at the height of the crisis in 2011, when hooded protesters in gas masks fought running battles with police.
10About 13,000 people gathered to protest in central Athens on Wednesday night, police said.
11Riot police try to avoid petrol bombs thrown by anti-austerity protesters in Athens on Wednesday.
12Hotels and shops around the square pulled down shutters and deployed security guards as night fell.
13A few blocks away, business continued as normal in the tourist-heavy Plaka district.
14Television images showed a protester throwing a Molotov cocktail shortly before the tear gas was fired.
15Protesters thew dozens of petrol bombs.
16The events highlight the changes in Greek politics since Mr Tsipras and his Coalition of the Radical Left, or Syriza, came to power in January.
17As the sound of percussion grenades reverberated across Syntagma Square, lawmakers who had campaigned against austerity just six months before now prepared to vote for just the opposite.
18Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras sells the bailout deal in the Greek parliament on Thursday.
19Mr Tsipras is likely to lose the support of 30 to 40 Syriza lawmakers in the bailout vote, and to be forced to rely on opposition lawmakers to pass the bill, Eurasia Group analyst Mujtaba Rahman said in a note to clients.
20As a result, "Mr Tsipras will reshuffle his cabinet and may call a vote of confidence," allowing him to rule with a minority of the legislature at least for a few weeks, Mr Rahman said.
21After that, a "national unity" government comprising the major Greek parties could be formed.
22The vote is a key milestone in the plan to unlock fresh aid for Greece.
23Banks have been closed for more than two weeks to stem withdrawals and without an injection of funds the government will miss a July 20 payment of 3.5 billion euros to the European Central Bank.
24The Frankfurt-based ECB plans to make a decision on extending emergency liquidity to Greek banks on Thursday, after the parliamentary vote, which may allow Greek lenders to gradually re-open.
25The bailout deal must also still be approved by six other euro-area national parliaments, including the lower house in Germany.
