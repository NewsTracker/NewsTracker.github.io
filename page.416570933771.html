In quake-hit Nepal, rescue workers struggle to recover the dead as toll climbs

1KATHMANDU, Nepal - For hours after Nepal was shaken by one of the most intense earthquakes here in nearly a century, 12-year-old Neha Chumda screamed for help.
2She had been inside her family's four-story villa in Kathmandu when the tremor struck unexpectedly Saturday.
3And in the life-or-death seconds that followed - when her home swayed at its walls began cracking apart - she and 11 members of her family rushed to get outside.
4They all made it - except Neha, who was pinned in a room between the second and third floors, which had collapsed onto of each other.
5"She was crying for so long, we tried to help her," Transport Minister Tek Bahadur Garung said of the girl, who is the daughter of one of his cousins.
6The cries turned faint, then ceased by the time Nepalese army troops and security forces began trying to break through the front of the house to pry Neha out at 4 p.m. Saturday.
7Then for a day and a half the soldiers used hammers, crowbars, shovels and a generator-powered saw and small jack-hammer to tear open the front of the house, then bust through the ceiling into the second floor.
8By Monday morning, they could see Neha's lifeless body curled up, covered in dust, a single elbow poking out above them.
9Then, at 10 a.m. - 42 hours after the rescue operation began - they finally pulled her out.
10They lowered her gently into a rainbow colored blanket that served as a makeshift stretcher, then placed her on the grassy ground outside, where four members of her family wept, their red-eyes filled with tears.
11"We have manpower. But we don't have nearly enough equipment to do the job," Garung said of his government's rescue efforts.
12"We need help from every country that will give it. We need bulldozers, cranes, scaffolding, machinery."
13Speaking outside the girl's home in a black tracksuit, with dozens of distraught residents looking on, Garung said: "There are a lot of dead bodies here and it's very difficult to get them out. We can't do it with crowbars" alone.
14The estimated death toll from Nepal's earthquake soared past 4,000, and could go much higher if it turns out that vulnerable mountain villages - where information is still scarce and rescue workers are still struggling to reach - were hit hard.
15The catastrophe has overwhelmed Nepal's government.
16Garung said the Cabinet was meeting daily, but "we don't know what to do in such a situation ... this is very hard. It's a big problem. It's a mess."
17Although the vast majority of Kathmandu's five and 10-story buildings actually withstood the quake and still stand, many have been damaged and it's unclear whether they are still safe.
18On the four-lane road that runs up a hill near Neha's home, most buildings looked undamaged.
19But half a dozen were in ruins and several multi-story apartment blocks were tilting precariously, one at a 60-degree angle.
20Many people have camped outdoors in the chilly night cold since the quake, some in tent cities in grass parks where thousands have congregated.
21Tens of thousands more squat on sidewalks and roads - anywhere but their own houses - fearing aftershocks that have shaken Nepal repeatedly.
22At one of the city's international hotels, foreign guests have done the same, dragging bed-sheets and pillows into gardens outside and sleeping on lounge chairs by the pool.
23For many, uncertainty reigns.
24It is unclear what is happening and when help might be on the way.
25Power lines are down, cell phone connections are sporadic at best, and most shops and banks are closed.
26Long lines have also formed at gas stations as critical fuel supplies run low.
27Just behind Neha's villa, another much larger operation to recover bodies was underway.
28Nepalese troops and Indian emergency rescue crews were scaling part of a green, three-story building with orange columns that had been destroyed.
29Authorities estimated a couple dozen people died inside the structure, most of them were Christians attending a church meeting.
30P.B. Bista, a police officer in the area, said 13 bodies had been recovered since the previous day, and six had been pulled from the rubble.
31On the ground outside, the emergency workers brought a shattered wooden donation box filled with Nepalese rupees.
32The words "Youth Gospel" and "Luke 6:38" were written across its top.
33Beside it sat a black-bound Hindi language bible covered in dirt and a plastic bag of yellow biscuits.
34Nepal is an overwhelmingly Hindu nation and has a small Christian population who comprise 1.4 percent of the country's 27 million people.
35Amir Amang, who worked at the church, which was on the second floor, said one of those rescued had been saved after midnight.
36But he said four of his family members had been found and taken to a morgue.
37He said they were a niece, a sister, and two aunts.
38His father was still missing.
39"I am shattered. I'm getting very emotional," he said, before waving his hand to indicate he could no longer speak, and walking away.
40As he spoke, rescue workers brought down body after body - seven in the space of one hour.
41When the first man came on a yellow plastic stretcher, wrapped in a curtain with yellow and blue flowers, his brother began sobbing, putting his palms across his eyes.
42The dead man was face down, one of his fists still tightly clenched.
43He was placed, like the rest, on a plastic Pepsi advertisement torn from the building's wreckage.
44Navraj Bhatta said the victim was a 25-year-old relative, a high-school science teacher named Rajendra Bhatta.
45He said the man had just eaten at a second-floor restaurant called Daniel's Cafe with one of Bhatta's relatives when the quake hit.
46The two were on the way out, but the teacher stopped to go to the bathroom.
47"He never came out," Navraj said forlornly.
48Navraj said the family would take Rajendra's body to Hindu temple where he would be burned on a pyre by the Bagmati River.
49But he said there were long lines there and he was unable to say when it could happen.
50Navraj said Rajendra might have survived if the city's notoriously lax building codes were enforced.
