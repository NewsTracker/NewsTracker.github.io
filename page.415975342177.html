Purported Islamic State video: Militants shot, hammered away Iraq's ancient city of Hatra

1Militants attacked Hatra, a UNESCO World Heritage site, last month, officials and local residents said, though the extent of the damage remains unclear as it is in territory still controlled by the Islamic State group.
2The video, released overnight Friday, shows a militant on a ladder using a sledgehammer to bang repeatedly on the back of one of the carved faces until it crashes to the ground and breaks into pieces.
3The video also shows a militant firing a Kalashnikov rifle at another, while men chop away the bases of some of the larger wall sculptures.
4The video corresponded with Associated Press reporting on the attack and was posted to a militant website frequently used by the group.
5One of the militants, who speaks Arabic with a distinct Gulf accent on the video, declares they destroyed the site because it is "worshipped instead of God."
6The Islamic State group, which holds a third of Iraq and neighboring Syria in its self-declared caliphate, has been destroying ancient relics they say promote idolatry that violates their fundamentalist interpretation of Islamic law.
7Authorities also believe they've sold others on the black market to fund their atrocities.
8Local government officials told the AP last month the militant group had looted and destroyed several ancient sites, including the 3,000 year-old Nimrud, another UNESCO World Heritage site.
9United Nations Secretary-General Ban Ki-Moon called the Nimrud attack "a war crime."
10Another video released in February showed militants smashing artifacts in the Mosul Museum and in January, the group burned hundreds of books from the Mosul library and Mosul University, including many rare manuscripts.
11The majority of the artifacts destroyed in the Mosul Museum attack were from Hatra.
12Hatra, located 110 kilometers (68 miles) southwest of the Islamic State-held city of Mosul, was a large fortified city during the Parthian Empire and capital of the first Arab kingdom.
13The site is said to have withstood invasions by the Romans in A.D. 116 and A.D. 198 thanks to its high, thick walls reinforced by towers.
14The ancient trading center spanned 6 kilometers (4 miles) in circumference and was supported by more than 160 towers.
15At its heart are a series of temples with a grand temple at the center - a structure supported by columns that once rose to 100 feet.
16The video's release comes after the Iraqi government this week claimed victory against the Islamic State group in Saddam Hussein's hometown Tikrit.
17Tikrit is 80 miles (130 kilometers) north of Baghdad on the main highway to Mosul, Iraq's second-largest city.
18Seizing Tikrit was key to an eventual campaign to retake Mosul - and the historic sites near it.
