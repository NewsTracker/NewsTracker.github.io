Emails: UN health agency resisted declaring Ebola emergency

1GENEVA (AP) - By early June of last year, the Ebola epidemic in West Africa was the deadliest ever recorded.
2There weren't enough beds to treat patients and many were refusing to seek treatment, driving the outbreak underground.
3Senior staffers in Africa at the World Health Organization raised the prospect of declaring an international emergency.
4The answer from WHO's Geneva headquarters: Wait.
5According to internal emails and documents obtained by The Associated Press, the U.N. health agency resisted sounding the international alarm until August, a two-month delay that some argue may have cost lives.
6More than 10,000 are believed to have been killed by the virus since WHO announced the outbreak a year ago.
7FILE - In this Friday, Aug. 8, 2014 file photo, Director-General of the World Health Organization (WHO) Dr. Margaret Chan, left, and Assistant Director-General of Health Security Keiji Fukuda, right, confer during a news conference after an emergency meeting at the WHO headquarters in Geneva, Switzerland.
8In a delay that some say may have cost lives, the World Health Organization resisted calling the Ebola outbreak in West Africa a public health emergency until the summer of 2014, two months after staff raised the possibility and long after a senior manager called for a drastic change in strategy, The Associated Press has learned.
9WHO has acknowledged acting too slowly to control the Ebola epidemic.
10In its defense, the agency says the virus's spread was unprecedented and blames several factors, including lack of resources and intelligence from the field.
11Internal documents obtained by AP, however, show WHO's top leaders were informed of how dire the situation was.
12But they held off on declaring an emergency in part because it could have angered the countries involved, interfered with their mining interests or restricted the Muslim pilgrimage to Mecca in October.
13Declaring an emergency was "a last resort," Dr. Sylvie Briand, who runs WHO's pandemic and epidemic diseases department, said in a June 5 email to a colleague who floated the idea.
14"It may be more efficient to use other diplomatic means for now."
15Five days after Briand's email, WHO Director-General Dr. Margaret Chan was sent a memo that warned cases might soon appear in Mali, Ivory Coast and Guinea Bissau.
16But it went on to say that declaring an international emergency or even convening a committee to discuss it "could be seen as a hostile act."
17Critics and former WHO staff dismiss that reasoning.
18"That's like saying you don't want to call the fire department because you're afraid the trucks will create a disturbance," said Michael Osterholm, a prominent infectious diseases expert at the University of Minnesota.
19The signs this was no ordinary outbreak - the panic, the health workers' deaths, and the urban spread of the virus - were there from the beginning.
20Many are recorded in a memo sent to WHO's Africa director on March 25.
21In mid-April, an experienced Ebola expert with WHO's Africa office wrote to Geneva, with disturbing news that many staffers at a leading hospital in Guinea had been exposed to the virus.
22"What we see is the tip of an iceberg," Jean-Bosco Ndihokubwayo wrote in an email, writing in all caps: "WE NEED SUPPORT."
23The Ebola outbreak erupted at a difficult time for the agency.
24WHO was also struggling to contain polio outbreaks - which it had declared an international emergency in May - and Middle Eastern Respiratory Syndrome.
25At a June 2 meeting, one senior scientist warned the agency was "overwhelmed."
26Declaring an international emergency might not necessarily have fixed all those problems - some argue it can create new ones - but it acts as a global distress signal.
27Dr. Joanne Liu, the international president of Doctors Without Borders, said she had been lobbying Chan to sound the alarm.
28"I told her, 'Dr. Chan, the Ebola epidemic in West Africa is out of control,'" Liu recalled telling Chan in a July 30 meeting in Geneva.
29The emergency was eventually declared on August 8, by which point nearly 1,000 people had already died from Ebola.
30WHO's Ebola response leader, Dr. Bruce Aylward, couldn't say whether an earlier declaration would have made a difference, noting that "the overall response slowed down" after the emergency was declared.
31"Had we declared it earlier, it is very, very difficult to say how perceptions would have affected the response."
32Dr. Matthieu Kamwa, a former WHO scientist, describes that kind of reasoning as contradictory.
33If emergencies don't work, why declare them at all?
34The declaration triggered a global deployment to contain the virus.
35U.S. President Barack Obama ordered up to 3,000 troops to West Africa and promised to build more than a dozen 100-bed field hospitals.
36Britain and France also pledged to build Ebola clinics; China sent a 59-person lab team and Cuba sent more than 400 health workers.
37Kamwa says it's clear the alarm should have been sounded sooner.
38"People died because things were not done," he said.
