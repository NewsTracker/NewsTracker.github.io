The Latest on Nepal Quake: Gorkha toll could change picture

1A man takes a selfie at the historic Dharahara Tower, a city landmark, that was damaged in Saturday's earthquake in Kathmandu, Nepal, Monday, April 27, 2015.
2A strong magnitude earthquake shook Nepal's capital and the densely populated Kathmandu valley on Saturday devastating the region and leaving tens of thousands shell-shocked and sleeping in streets.
3Foreign tourists in Nepal are getting anxious as food, water and power remain scarce.
4Hotel rooms are in short supply too so Pierre-Anne Dube, a 31-year-old from Quebec, has been sleeping on the sidewalk outside a hotel.
5Friends had been staying there for the first two days so she could use the bathroom and shower there.
6But they have checked out.
7Like many others she's scared and wants to get out on the first flight she can get.
8"We can't reach the embassy. We want to leave. We are scared. There is no food. We haven't eaten a meal since the earthquake and we don't have any news about what's going on."
9She had just returned from a trek to Everest base camp, which had been the "best experience of her life," but living the experience of the massive earthquake was definitely the "worst."
10- Katy Daigle, Kathmandu, Nepal.
11The Israeli military said it is sending a search and rescue crew to Nepal on Monday to help locate survivors in the rubble, set up a medical field hospital for locals, and bring Israeli travelers home.
12A total of 260 Israeli military personnel are traveling to Nepal for the mission.
13The military says about 150 Israeli travelers have yet to establish contact after the earthquake and are believed to be missing.
14"The idea is to arrive and to try to establish communication with them," said Col.
15Yoram Laredo, head of the Israeli military mission.
16In addition, Israel's emergency response service, Magen David Adom, is flying home a group of 18 Israelis who travelled to Nepal to receive babies born to Nepalese surrogate mothers, spokesman Zaki Heller said.
17International aid agency Oxfam says it is is gearing up to deliver clean water and sanitation supplies to thousands of Nepalis now left homeless.
18They estimate that some 30,000 people are currently living in makeshift shelters in 16 government camps, too scared to return to their homes for fear of aftershocks.
19"We are managing to reach out to people in Kathmandu, but it is extremely difficult to provide support on a larger scale to the most affected areas - a lot of the main roads have been damaged," said Cecilia Keizer, Oxfam country director in Nepal.
20"Our staff is still checking on their families and the partners we work with. At the moment, all the death count reports are coming from Kathmandu Valley. Sadly, I fear that this is only the beginning," she said.
21There's a lot that the world still doesn't really know about the Nepal quake.
22The key thing is this: How significant is the destruction in Gorkha district, 80 kilometers (50 miles) from the capital and the location of the quake's epicenter?
23Roads to the area, difficult on good days, are damaged.
24Learning about the level of destruction and human toll in the vulnerable mountain villages there could change the whole picture.
25"Villages like this are routinely affected by landslides," he says, "and it's not uncommon for entire villages of 200, 300, up to 1,000 people to be completely buried by rock falls."
26Jagdish Pokhrel, the clearly exhausted army spokesman, says nearly the entire 100,000-soldier army is involved in rescue operations.
27"90 percent of the army's out there working on search and rescue," he said.
28"We are focusing our efforts on that, on saving lives."
29Fears are growing that thousands of people may remain cut off in isolated, devastated mountain villages.
30Udav Prashad Timalsina, the top official for the Gorkha district where Saturday's quake was centered, says he is in desperate need of help.
31"Things are really bad in the district, especially in remote mountain villages. There are people who are not getting food and shelter. I have had reports of villages where 70 percent of the houses have been destroyed," he said when contacted by telephone.
32"We have been calling for help, but we haven't received enough from the central government."
33He says 223 people had been confirmed dead in the district but he presumed "the number would go up because there are thousands who are injured."
34Australian Foreign Minister Julie Bishop says Australia has dispatched a 9-person crisis response team that is scheduled to arrive in Kathmandu later Monday and "will assist in establishing the safety and welfare of Australians currently unaccounted for" after Saturday's earthquake.
35She says the team will also assist with humanitarian assessments to support Nepal's government and the international relief efforts.
36New Zealand is sending 37 urban search and rescue experts to Kathmandu.
37They are scheduled to leave Monday evening.
38Included are experts in rubble-pile rescues and technical rescues, as well as a structural engineer, a doctor, and paramedics.
39New Zealand is also contributing 1 million New Zealand dollars ($761,000) to the relief effort.
40Officials have made contact with over 200 New Zealanders in Nepal and are seeking contact with others.
41They say they have no reason to believe at this point that any New Zealanders died in the earthquake.
42On Monday morning, some pharmacies, groceries and shops selling basic provisions opened while bakeries began offering fresh bread.
43Long lines of people desperate to secure fuel formed outside gasoline stations.
44Fuel prices remained the same as they were before the quake.
45A sense of eerie calm and uneasiness hangs over the capital, Kathmandu, as the aftershocks keep coming for a third day after the massive quake on Saturday.
46People are still trying to absorb what's happening to them and their city.
47Part of their anxiety appears to be fueled by a near complete absence of any real information.
48Power lines are down and there is almost no Internet connectivity.
49Phone connections are spotty at best.
50Most people are camped outdoors so even where there is limited power back up there's no TV news to watch.
51This has made them anxious to buy newspapers every morning.
52Near Kathmandu's famed Dharahara Tower, reduced to an enormous pile of red brick dust, dozens of people were clambering around the debris clicking smiling selfies and photos of their friends posing.
53"This is earthquake tourism. This is not right," said 21-year-old business student Pawan Thapa who arrived from the suburbs to see how he could help.
54"They are more interested in clicking their selfies than understanding that it is a tragedy."
55Facebook has activated its "Safety Check" feature in response to the earthquake in Nepal.
56The feature, launched in October, allows users to tell friends and family they are safe if they are in the middle of a disaster area.
57Facebook engineers in Japan started development on the feature after the 2011 Japan earthquake and tsunami.
58The Kathmandu earthquake was one of the first natural disasters where it has been used.
59Google, which lost an employee in an avalanche as a result of the earthquake, has activated its own disaster tool called "Person Finder" http://google.org/personfinder/2015-nepal-earthquake/ and is updating satellite imagery to help with relief efforts.
60Google is also donating $1 million toward relief efforts, the company said.
61Japan says it will provide emergency relief goods such as tents and blankets worth 25 million yen ($210,000) to Nepal, through the Japan International Cooperation Agency following the massive earthquake on Saturday.
62In Kathmandu, those who came to help are frightened, too.
63Kathmandu district chief administrator Ek Narayan Aryal says tents and water are being handed at 10 locations in Nepal's capital, but that the many aftershocks were leaving everyone jittery.
64He says that "even the rescuers are scared and running because of them."
65There's a bottleneck in the efforts to get relief to Nepal.
66The good news: Kathmandu's airport is open.
67The bad news: It's been a bumpy, frustration-filled experience for those arriving.
68The second bit of good news: Some aid vehicles have been able to travel overland from Indian to the stricken Nepalese city of Pokhara.
69Ben Pickering, Save the Children's humanitarian adviser in Britain, calls that a positive sign.
70He emphasizes this, though: "The airport opening is a small miracle."
71Nepal's Deputy Inspector General of Police Komal Singh Bam says at least 3,218 people have been confirmed killed in Saturday's earthquake.
72So far 18 people have also been confirmed dead in an avalanche that swept through the Mount Everest base camp in the wake of the earthquake.
73Another 61 people were killed in neighboring India.
74In a western part of Kathmandu, dozens of Nepali soldiers and Indian rescue workers pulled 16 bodies and one survivor from a collapsed green, three-story building that housed a church.
75Officials say a Christian meeting was being held on the second floor when the quake hit on Saturday.
76One Indian national disaster response force, who did not want to be named, said "I really doubt anybody is alive here, but we have to keep trying and look."
