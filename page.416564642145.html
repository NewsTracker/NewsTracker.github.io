Prosecutor: 2 exams found James Holmes to be sane

1A prosecutor declared Monday that two mental health evaluations found Colorado theater gunman James Holmes to be sane.
2It was the first public word on what different psychiatrists determined after examining the former neuroscience student accused of killing 12 people and wounding 70 at a midnight "Batman" premiere.
3The statement by District Attorney George Brauchler marked the start of a long-awaited, lengthy and emotionally wrenching trial to determine if he'll be executed, spend his life in prison, or be committed to an institution as criminally insane.
4Holmes pleaded not guilty by reason of insanity to 166 counts, including first-degree murder, attempted murder and an explosives offense, after the mayhem he caused in suburban Denver on July 20, 2012.
5It remains one of America's deadliest shootings, and that Holmes was the lone gunman has never been in doubt.
6He was arrested at the scene, along with an arsenal of weapons on his body and in his car.
7His fate depends on whether a jury agrees that he was unable to know right from wrong because of a mental illness or defect three years ago, when he slipped into the midnight Batman premiere, unleashed tear gas and marched up and down the aisles, firing at people who tried to flee.
8"Through this door is horror. Through this door are bullets, blood, brains and bodies. Through this door, one guy who thought as if he had lost his career, lost his love life, lost his purpose, came to execute a plan," said Brauchler, standing before a scale model of the theater.
9"Four-hundred people came into a boxlike theater to be entertained, and one person came to slaughter them," the prosecutor said.
10Holmes sat quietly as Brauchler played a video recording of one of the psychiatric evaluations.
11In it, Holmes told a doctor he "only counts fatalities," and described the people he wounded as "collateral damage."
12Each side was allowed two hours for opening statements, with public defenders Daniel King and Katherine Spengler following his presentation.
13Defense lawyers say Holmes was in the grips of a psychotic episode and could not tell right from wrong when he went on the rampage.
14His parents, Robert and Arlene Holmes, in pleading for his life, have called their son a "human being gripped by a severe mental illness."
15Under Colorado law, the burden falls on the state to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that he was "NOT insane," Judge Carlos A. Samour Jr., told the jury.
16And that, in turn, depends in part on "a culpable state of mind:" If Holmes acted with deliberation and intent - willfully taking actions that he knew would kill people - then even if he had mental problems, he should be found guilty of murder, the judge said.
17Prosecutors allege that Holmes planned the violence for months, buying a rifle, a shotgun, two pistols, tear gas canisters, body armor, thousands of rounds of ammunition and a chemical stockpile that turned his 800-square-foot apartment into a booby trap that might have caused a conflagration.
18"He tried to murder a theater full of people to make himself feel better and because he thought it would increase his self-worth," Brauchler said.
19"I would like to focus on the victims," he said, but instead he must prove that Holmes was not insane.
20The state has already spent millions seeking that verdict, managing an outsized number of victims, witnesses and more than 85,000 pages of evidence.
21Nearly three years passed hundreds of motions were filed in legal debates over capital punishment and insanity pleas.
22Insanity defenses are successful in only 25 percent of felony trials nationally, even less so in homicides.
23"Lay people tend to think of people with mental illness as extremely dangerous, and that also influences jurors, especially if someone has killed someone," said Christopher Slobogin, who teaches law and psychiatry at Vanderbilt Law School.
24"Usually there's evidence of intent and planning that seems to be counterintuitive to the lay view of mental illness."
25Winning a trial on mental-health grounds is rare, but then again, so are jury trials for mass shooters.
26Most are killed by police, kill themselves or plead guilty.
27A review of 160 mass shootings found killers went to trial 74 times, and just three were found insane, according to Grant Duwe, a Minnesota corrections official who wrote the book "Mass Murder in the United States: A History."
28Just one has won a mental-health case in the last two decades, Duwe said: Michael Hayes, who shot nine people, killing four, in North Carolina in 1988.
29Based on that, Holmes "faces some pretty long odds," he said.
30Brauchler began laying out how the once-promising doctoral candidate in neuroscience plotted and planned for months, amassing guns, ammunition, tear gas grenades and enough chemicals to turn his dingy apartment into a potentially lethal booby trap that could have caused even more carnage.
31Holmes was arrested almost immediately, while stripping off his body armor in the parking lot outside the Century 16 movie theater where he replaced Hollywood violence with real human carnage.
32His victims included two active-duty servicemen, a single mom, a man celebrating his 27th birthday, and an aspiring broadcaster who had survived a mall shooting in Toronto.
33Several died shielding friends or loved ones.
34At 6 years old, the youngest to die was Veronica Moser-Sullivan.
35Her mother, Ashley Moser, was left paralyzed and lost her unborn child.
36Holmes' trial could take at least four months or more and is sure to be emotionally wrenching.
37The 12 jurors and 12 alternates - chosen from a pool of 9,000 because it was so difficult to find people who weren't personally affected - won't know if they'll join the deliberations until after the trial.
38Some survivors want Holmes executed, even if that means reliving horrific details.
39"It still doesn't bring him back, but we want justice," said W.
40David Hoover, who wants to avenge the death of his 18-year-old nephew, A.J. Boik.
41"Real justice is going to happen when this animal leaves this Earth."
42Fire Chief Larry Trujillo, whose daughter, Taylor, survived the shooting when a friend threw her to the floor, said while entering the courthouse that his faith enables him to forgive, but that this may be easier for him to say: His daughter survived.
