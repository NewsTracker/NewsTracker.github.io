Google Plus gets dismantled by tech giant

1Google is moving features once integrated into Google Plus into other services.
2For many users, Google Plus accounts don't get all that many visits.
3Now, Google seems to be indicating it, too, will be paying less attention to its social network.
4On Monday, Google said it would move features once integrated into Google Plus out of the social network and into other Google services.
5Photo features have already been moved to the newly introduced Google Photos.
6Location-sharing will go to Google Hangouts, the company's chat app.
7Users will also no longer need a Google Plus account to comment on YouTube, long a point of contention among customers who felt as if they were being roped into using a social network they did not ask for.
8Altogether, the moves, announced in a blog post on Monday, are a moment of reckoning for Google Plus and those who created it.
9"While we got certain things right, we made a few choices that, in hindsight, we've needed to rethink," Bradley Horowitz, who oversees Google Plus, wrote about the moves.
10He was even more contrite on his personal Google Plus page: "We want to formally retire the notion that a Google+ membership is required for anything at Google."
11Driving home that point, Google said it would make it easier for users to delete Google Plus accounts, if they choose to.
12Horowitz was put in charge of Google Plus in March.
13Started in 2011 and promoted as Google's answer to Facebook, Google Plus initially appeared popular on paper.
14The social network reached 300 million monthly active users in just two years, according to the company.
15But much of that growth, analysts say, came because users needed to create Google Plus accounts to use some of the company's other services, like YouTube, annoying many longtime users of those services.
16"When you say that one service requires another, that's not a tenable approach," said Bradley Shimmin, a business analyst at Current Analysis.
17But Google's decision to dismantle Google Plus may have less to do with appeasing users' anger than with meeting the demand of mobile phone owners, who expect apps for each individual service.
18Rather than make an app or website that is a one-stop shop, tech companies instead are introducing stables of services.
19Twitter has Vine, a video-sharing app, and Periscope, a live-streaming app, separate from the main Twitter platform.
20Facebook not only broke out photos and messaging into separate apps, but also decided to acquire Instagram and WhatsApp, direct competitors to those services, and maintain them both as yet another set of options for smartphone owners.
21Even though it has been dismantled, Google Plus is not dead and Google promised to continue adding new features.
22Despite often being portrayed as a ghost town, Google Plus still draws groups of hard-core users, often focused on forums dedicated to discussing other Google products, like Chromebook or Android.
23After Google's announcement, those die-hard Google Plus users sounded a note of optimism.
24"Today's news surrounding the upcoming changing to Google+ could be seen as cause for concern," Derek Ross, an Android enthusiast with 1.2 million followers on Google Plus, said in an email.
25"But myself and many other users of the platform aren't concerned for the future of Google+."
