Bali Nine execution move edges nearer

It is not who killed Russia's Boris Nemtsov, it is why

1The dreaded date had been delayed by Indonesia's ill preparations for the mass execution and hope had been invested in legal appeals and pleas from as high up as the Australian prime minister.
2But after a string of co-ordination meetings and shifting time frames, Bali's chief prosecutor, Momock Bambang Samiarso, on Tuesday set the date.
3"Co-ordination has been completed, the transfer will be tomorrow at noon," he told reporters.
4Foreign Minister Julie Bishop said it was devastating news.
5"I am dismayed by reports that Andrew and Myuran are to be transferred by Indonesian authorities in preparation for their execution," she said in a statement on Tuesday evening.
6She said she would continue to contact her counterparts in Indonesia to press for a stay of execution.
7"Given their rehabilitation, it is callous for these executions to proceed," she said.
8Prison governor Sudjonggo told reporters the Bali Nine pair were aware the day had come to leave Kerobokan jail, their home since the failed 2005 heroin smuggling plot.
9In that time, they have become its model prisoners, Sukumaran guiding others through art classes and Chan with religious ministry.
10Sudjonggo said Sukumaran had joked that he now didn't have time to paint him.
11"They already know," he told reporters.
12"When Myuran was talking to me, he was still laughing."
13The men had left their belongings - such as clothes, food, bedding and kitchen utensils - with their inmate friends, including other members of the Bali Nine.
14They were saying their goodbyes, Sudjonggo said, with Sukumaran overheard telling friends to be careful.
15"I didn't ask if he was ready or not, but I can see from his gestures," the governor said.
16"He was still talking, still laughing, still chatting with his friends."
17Sukumaran's mother Raji and Chan's brother Michael had visited the prison before the news was made public.
18The pair's lawyers have just lodged another appeal in a bid to have the facts of their case - the rehabilitation of Chan and Sukumaran - considered in the courts.
19In Jakarta, Todung Mulya Lubis begged for the executions to be stopped.
20"I plead to the president, I plead to the attorney-general, please wait for the legal process to be completed," he told reporters.
21But on Monday, the attorney-general rejected the idea the executions should be delayed for those among the 10 drug offenders scheduled for execution who have legal appeals pending.
22"If there's already clemency (rejected), there should be no more other legal challenges submitted," HM Prasetyo told reporters on Monday.

2Why was Boris Nemtsov shot dead?
3The committee investigating the chilling murder of the vocal Kremlin critic within view of its red brick walls has already established a number of possible motives.
4Among them is his outspoken opposition to the fighting in Ukraine.
5For Nemtsov, a long-time opposition leader briefly considered as a possible successor to Boris Yeltsin until Vladimir Putin edged him out, the war next door had become a central, electrifying issue among a litany of complaints against Putin's leadership.
6And Nemtsov was adamant about documenting proof that the Russian president is sending troops into Ukraine's churning conflict.
7According to his friends, including the Ukrainian President Petro Poroshenko, Nemtsov was on the verge of disclosing new information in a document he was to call "Putin and the war."
8"Boris had declared that he would provide the clear evidence of Russian armed forces' participation in Ukraine," said Poroshenko following Nemtsov's shooting.
9"Somebody was afraid of this, Boris wasn't afraid. Killers and executioners were afraid."
10Russia has consistently denied stoking the Ukraine conflict, or feeding it with men and materiel - despite mounting evidence, mostly produced abroad, to the contrary.
11But while Russia's involvement is a subject often discussed outside Russia, inside, it is a difficult - even dangerous - conversation to have.
12A Russian woman in Saint Petersburg carries a placard with the image of opposition leader Boris Nemtsov who was gunned down on Friday near the Kremlin, setting Russia on edge.
13The rumours have been swirling for months.
14Russian mothers reporting their enlisted sons calling from inside Ukraine, many others reporting their sons missing.
15In recent months, independent media have begun reporting on the whispers of Russian casualties secretly buried near the border.
16But what discussion there is inside Russia about all this has lacked hard proof, though there have been some in Russia, in addition to Nemtsov, trying to find it.
17But how do you prove a country is engaged in war if it won't acknowledge it?
18A few months ago, a group of Russian journalists tried by investigating reports of secret soldier burials.
19They found at least one grave, before thugs scared them off - an act of intimidation the journalists caught on camera.
20"They [were] very aggressive," Vladimir, the young videographer who filmed the attack for the independent Rain TV, told CBC News on a recent visit to Moscow.
21"Some people don't want this information [to go] to the world. They don't want that people know."
22Another opposition figure says he was brutishly beaten for asking similar questions.
23He believes the attack had everything to do with his investigation into the deaths of paratroopers from his home in Pskov.
24He had attended a secret funeral held for one of them, and is certain they all died fighting in Ukraine.
25Russia insists any of its men fighting on the rebel side of the conflict in eastern Ukraine are volunteers fighting of their own volition.
26But last fall, journalists at Rain TV's website edition began compiling a list of Russian soldiers confirmed either missing in action or dead.
27In most of the cases, the journalists say, relatives were told the soldiers died during "exercises" "on the border" with Ukraine.
28The journalists spent considerable time to confirm each case, to ensure they had the information right.
29But most Russians, who largely watch state television, aren't exposed to that view, and certainly not to any of the data from Western sources, including NATO, said to show Russian involvement in Ukraine.
30There has been speculation that the Kremlin is behind the murder of opposition leader Boris Nemtsov.
31But while Russian President Vladimir Putin might want to bring such attention on his regime is not at all clear.
32Even amidst the wall-to-wall coverage of the Ukraine conflict on Russian television, they aren't likely to have seen an exhibit mounted in Kyiv last weekend of a mountain of Russian hardware, including grenade launchers and drones, that were apparently seized in eastern Ukraine by government forces.
33And the vast majority would not have attended the protest Nemtsov was to lead on Sunday, which he planned partly to amplify the criticism of Russia's policy in Ukraine.
34"If you support stopping Russia's war with Ukraine, if you support stopping Putin's aggression, come to the Spring March," he wrote in a blog linked to his final tweet.
35What kind of proof did Nemtsov have?
36And is that the reason he was silenced?
37Though the answer isn't known - and may never be - in a sense, that may be beside the point.
38Because Russia's most brazen political assassination in years silences a potent critic of the Kremlin, and strikes fear into anyone else trying to get at the truth - notwithstanding the posters asserting otherwise at Sunday's rally.
39"I am not afraid," was a common slogan at the gathering.
40But friends say even Nemtsov was concerned for his own safety.
41He said as much in an interview earlier this month when he said "I'm afraid Putin will kill me."
42Was it Putin, as his critics allege, via someone acting on his behalf?
43Or as others have suggested, was it nationalists who believed Nemtsov, as a Putin critic, was a traitor?
44And if so, was killing him the best way to manage the problem?
45A killing like this wouldn't be the first in a country that is no stranger to violence and intimidation in pursuit of political goals.
46The journalists at Rain TV weren't dissuaded by the intimidation they faced, but they were also very concerned their work would eventually land them in trouble.
47"We don't get any response, and it's scary in a way," the web edition's editor in chief Ilya Klishin told CBC last fall.
