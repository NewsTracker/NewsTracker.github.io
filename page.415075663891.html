Protests after US blogger hacked to death in Bangladesh

1Hundreds of protesters have gathered in Dhaka to denounce the murder of a prominent American blogger of Bangladeshi origin, who was hacked to death with machetes after he allegedly received threats from Islamists.
2The demonstrators - including teachers, publishers and fellow writers - met near the spot where Avijit Roy, founder of Mukto-Mona (Free-Mind) blog, was attacked by unknown assailants as he returned home from a book fair with his wife on Thursday evening.
3No one has yet claimed responsibility for the vicious attack, which also seriously wounded the 44-year-old blogger's wife and has shocked many in a country already reeling from weeks of violent political protest.
4The atheist writer's father Ajoy Roy said he had received numerous threats from Islamists via email and social media before his death.
5"The attack on Roy and his wife Rafida Ahmed is outrageous," the head of a Bangladeshi bloggers' association, Imran H Sarker, said.
6"We strongly protest this attack and are deeply concerned about the safety of writers."
7Dr Roy, a US citizen, is the second Bangladeshi atheist blogger to have been murdered in two years and the fourth writer to have been attacked since 2004.
8US State Department spokeswoman Jen Psaki called it "a shocking act of violence" that was "horrific in its brutality and cowardice".
9Ms Psaki told a regular news briefing she had no information as to the motive for the attack, but said the US was ready to assist the investigation.
10Hardline Islamist groups have long demanded the public execution of atheist bloggers and sought new laws to combat writing critical of Islam.
11Police have launched an investigation and recovered the machetes used in the attack, which they compared to that on atheist blogger Ahmed Rajib Haider in 2013.
12Mr Haider was hacked to death by members of a little-known Islamist militant group, triggering nationwide protests by tens of thousands of secular activists.
13"The pattern of the killing appeared to be the same as that of a previous attack on a celebrated writer," Dhaka police assistant commissioner Shiblee Noman said.
14Assistant Commissioner Noman also said police were investigating a tweet by the pro-Islamist group Ansar Bangla Seven that appeared to celebrate Dr Roy's murder.
15"Target Down here in #Bangladesh," the group tweeted from the @AnsarBn7 handle.
16Dr Roy had written about 10 books, including the best-selling "Biswasher Virus" (Virus of Faith), as well as his blog, which championed liberal secular writing in the Muslim-majority nation.
17The Centre for Inquiry, a US-based charity promoting free thought, said it was "shocked and heartbroken" by the brutal murder.
18"Dr Roy was a true ally, a courageous and eloquent defender of reason, science, and free expression, in a country where those values have been under heavy attack," it said in a statement.
19The killing also triggered strong condemnation from fellow writers and publishers, who lamented the growing religious conservatism and intolerance in Bangladesh.
20Pinaki Bhattacharya, a fellow blogger and friend of Dr Roy, said one of the country's largest online book retailers was being openly threatened for selling Dr Roy's books.
21"In Bangladesh, the easiest target is an atheist. An atheist can be attacked and murdered," he wrote on Facebook.
22After Mr Haider's death, Bangladesh's hardline Islamist groups started to protest against other campaigning bloggers, accusing them of blasphemy and calling a series of nationwide strikes to demand their execution.
23The secular government of Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina reacted by arresting some atheist bloggers.
24The government also blocked about a dozen websites and blogs to stem the furore over blasphemy, as well as stepping up security for the bloggers.
25Bangladesh is the world's fourth-largest Muslim majority nation with Muslims making up some 90 per cent of the country's 160 million people.
