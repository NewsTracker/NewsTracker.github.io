Previous FIFA Presidential Elections

1ZURICH (AP) " Sepp Blatter would certainly argue that being president of FIFA is the best job in sports. Becoming FIFA president is one of the hardest.
2On May 29, soccer's world governing body will hold only its fourth contested presidential election in the past 50 years.
3Only three men have led FIFA in that time, and only eight have occupied the president's office in its 111-year history.
4Two candidates are trying to stop Blatter from extending his 17-year reign at the top: Prince Ali bin al-Hussein of Jordan and Luis Figo of Portugal.
5Michael van Praag of the Netherlands pulled out on May 21.
6Joao Havelange of Brazil beat Stanley Rous of England 68-52 in the second round of voting in Frankfurt, Germany.
7By FIFA election rules, Havelange's 62-56 first-round lead was not decisive.
8After 13 years of Rous as president, Havelange wooed voters outside Europe by promising more places at the World Cup and increased FIFA funding.
9Victory for the imperious Havelange heralded a new era, driven by key FIFA sponsors like Adidas and Coca-Cola.
10Gone was the Rous style of gentlemen amateurs leading with a sense of civic duty.
11It was swept aside by aggressive commercialism in a soon-to-be booming sports marketing industry.
12Havelange faced no election challengers during his 24-year presidency.
13Now 99 years old, he resigned as FIFA honorary president in 2013 ahead of being stripped of the honor for taking millions of dollars in kickbacks from World Cup marketing deals in the ISL scandal.
14Sepp Blatter beat Lennart Johansson of Sweden 111-80 in the first round of voting in Paris.
15Though Blatter, then the long-time FIFA secretary general, did not have the required majority, UEFA President Johansson conceded by declining to contest a second round.
16The election is remembered for allegations that Blatter supporters from the Middle East offered some African voters $50,000 to switch sides on the eve of the vote.
17Blatter beat Issa Hayatou of Cameroon 139-56 in Seoul, South Korea.
18Blatter retained his presidency during a period of bitter internal turmoil and financial crisis at FIFA after the collapse of World Cup marketing agency ISL the previous year.
19Hayatou, the Confederation of African Football president, and Johansson led an 11-member group in the FIFA executive committee accusing Blatter of financial mismanagement.
20Blatter's successor as secretary general, Michel Zen-Ruffinen, filed a criminal complaint against his boss with Swiss prosecutors.
21After Blatter's big victory " with support even from many African voters " Zen-Ruffinen left FIFA and the criminal complaint came to nothing.
22Hayatou is now a FIFA senior vice president, closely allied with Blatter and would become interim president if the position was vacated.
23Blatter ran unopposed for re-election in Zurich.
24Again, Blatter was unopposed in Zurich but it was a very different story.
25He had been challenged by former ally Mohamed bin Hammam, the Asian Football Confederation president from Qatar, who was a key campaigner in Blatter's 1998 and 2002 wins.
26Bin Hammam declared his candidacy months after helping persuade his executive committee colleagues to pick Qatar as 2022 World Cup host.
27Days before what looked to be a tight vote, Bin Hammam withdrew and FIFA suspended him over accusations that Caribbean voters were offered $40,000 bribes.
28An attempt by England to postpone Blatter's "coronation" election failed.
29Blatter was the only name on the ballot paper and needed just a single valid vote to win.
30He got 186 of the 203 votes cast.
