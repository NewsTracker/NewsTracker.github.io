Former US general David Petraeus to be sentenced over leaking secrets

1David Petraeus and Paula Broadwell, co-author of "All In: The Education of General David Petraeus."
2WASHINGTON: Former US military commander and CIA director David Petraeus will appear in federal court in North Carolina to face sentencing for allegedly leaking secrets to a mistress who was writing his biography.
3The now-retired US Army general has already agreed to plead guilty to a criminal misdemeanour charge of unauthorised removal and retention of classified material.
4As part of the agreement with prosecutors filed in March, the government will not seek any prison time.
5Instead, General Petraeus will agree to pay a $40,000 fine and receive two years of probation, according to court documents.
6David Petraeus with his wife Holly seated behind him at his Senate Armed Services Committee confirmation hearing to become commander of US forces in Afghanistan in 2010.
7The recommendations are not binding on the federal judge who will preside at the hearing Thursday afternoon in Charlotte.
8General Petraeus, who served stints as the top US commander in the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan, resigned from the CIA in 2012 after it was revealed that he was having an affair with the biographer, Army Reserve officer Paula Broadwell.
9A court document signed by General Petraeus and prosecutors says that, in 2011, before he became the CIA director, the four-star general illegally gave Broadwell access to binders, known as "black books," that included classified information.
10He also is accused of improperly storing classified materials at his residence and falsely telling the FBI in October 2012 that he had not shared any classified information with Broadwell.
11General Petraeus' lawyer, David Kendall, had no comment before the plea and sentencing hearing.
12Civil liberties and government transparency advocates say the government's lenient treatment of General Petraeus suggests prosecutors maintain double standards.
13Other leak case defendants have received harsher punishments, such as former CIA officer John Kiriakou, who was sent to prison.
14Ben Wizner, an American Civil Liberties Union lawyer who represents former National Security Agency contractor Edward Snowden, said he was not against lighter sentences for people involved in leaking, so long as such treatment is handed out equally.
15"The problem is not that David Petraeus is getting lenient treatment," Mr Wizner said.
16"The problem is that lenient treatment is only available to people in high places."
