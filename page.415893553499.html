5The revelation that officials Lufthansa had been informed of Lubitz's psychological problems raises further questions about why he was allowed to become a pilot for its subsidiary, Germanwings, in September 2013.
6Authorities say the 27-year-old Lubitz, who in the past had been treated for suicidal tendencies, locked his captain out of the cockpit before deliberately crashing the Airbus 320 into a mountain in the French Alps on March 24.
7All 150 people aboard Flight 9525 from Barcelona to Duesseldorf were killed.
8Earlier Tuesday, Lufthansa said it had set aside $300 million to deal with possible costs from the crash as French aviation investigators said they were examining "systemic weaknesses" like cockpit entry rules and psychological screening procedures that could have led to the Germanwings plane crash -- issues that could eventually change worldwide aviation practices.
9French aviation agency BEA signaled the latest re-think about airline procedures in the wake of the Germanwings crash, which jolted an aviation industry already reeling after one passenger plane disappeared into an ocean and another was shot out of the sky over war-torn eastern Ukraine.
10The goal of the BEA investigation is to make recommendations to aviation authorities, not just in France but anywhere, about what can be done to prevent similar crashes.
11French prosecutors are carrying out a separate crash probe to pinpoint possible criminal wrongdoing.
12The Germanwings crash has already produced some changes in aviation procedures.
13Europe's aviation regulator now says all airlines in Europe should require two people in the cockpit at all times during a flight.
14Many airlines have already imposed the new rule, which has been in place in the U.S. since the Sept.
1511 attacks.
16The International Civil Aviation Organization, which brings together 191 nations, said state agencies like the BEA must officially determine the causes and contributing factors of crashes and give recommendations on ways to avoid recurrences.
17ICAO could then bring such recommendations to its member states -- possibly leading to changes in international aviation standards.
18BEA said it aims to provide a "detailed analysis" of the Germanwings cockpit voice recorder and any other flight data -- but it also plans to widen its search, to examine issues that could be problematic for all airlines.
19"(We will study) systemic weaknesses (that) might possibly have led to this aviation disaster," BEA said in its first statement since prosecutors detailed the co-pilot's suspected role in the crash.
20The agency is studying both psychological screening procedures and rules applied to entering and leaving the cockpit, as well as cockpit door locking systems.
