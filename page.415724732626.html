Search resumes at Germanwings Flight 9525 crash site

1PARIS, March 25 (UPI) -- As crews began a second-day recovery operation Wednesday, searching on an alpine mountain where Germanwings Flight 9525 crashed, investigators said the aircraft's black box was damaged but the information was usable.
2"We can confirm deaths of two U.S. citizens, are reviewing records to determine whether other U.S. citizens might have been on board," the State Department said on Twitter.
3The Airbus crashed Tuesday in the French Alps in southern France, with all 150 people aboard, including two babies and 16 high-schoolers, presumed dead.
4Of those aboard were 45 Spaniards, 67 Germans, two Australians and several British nationals.
5Among the dead are two teachers and two German opera stars.
6The plane was enroute to Barcelona, Spain, from Dusseldorf, Germany.
7Lufthansa Vice President Heike Birlenbach said the company is treating the crash as an accident.
8The plane left the airport 30 minutes late, but there was little more authorities could say.
9French Prime Minister Manual Valls said "no hypothesis" could be ruled out.
10At first light Wednesday, helicopters scoured the wide debris field as bereaved families arrived at the site.
11The icy and rocky terrain, with a threat of more snow and rain, made the search difficult.
12French Interior Minister Bernard Cazeneuve said the cockpit voice recorder was recovered.
13"The black box is damaged and must be reconstituted in the coming hours in order to be usable," Cazeneuve said.
14President Barack Obama expressed his condolences to people of Germany and Spain, saying "America stands with them at this moment of sorrow."
