Dozens missing feared dead after mine blast in Ukraine: union

One dead, dozens trapped after Ukraine coal mine blast: officials

1The early morning blast, believed to be caused by a build-up of methane gas, hit the Zasyadko mine near the city's bomb-scarred airport in what officials described as a "terrible tragedy".
2By Wednesday evening 17 bodies had been found, according to the regional authorities, and a search was ongoing despite the late hour.
3"The fate of 16 other miners is still unknown," spokeswoman of the rebel emergency ministry in Donetsk Yuliana Bedylo told AFP.
4The head of the rebel Donetsk People's Republic administration, Maksim Leshchenko, told reporters that 197 workers, out of 230 in the mine when the explosion occurred shortly before 0400 GMT, had been rescued alive.
5Emergency workers were forced to call off the rescue operation late Wednesday due to the threat posed by the high methane levels, Mykhaylo Volynets of the Ukrainian Independent Miners Union said.
6A spokesman for another mining union, who did not wish to be named, told AFP the chances of finding more survivors were "practically zero".
7In Donetsk, anxious family members gathered outside the mine to await news of their loved ones.
8Valentina Dzyuba, 72, told AFP she had been waiting for seven hours for news of her 47-year-old son Vladimir.
9"We have no information. I fear he's dead," a tearful Dzyuba said.
10Vyacheslav Baloban, one of two injured miners who AFP met at Donetsk hospital, said he was knocked unconscious by the blast.
11"I just remember the explosion... I just remember the dust," Baloban, who was being treated for a head injury and burns, told AFP.
12President Petro Poroshenko sent condolences on the "tragedy" to the victims' families in a Twitter message.
13The coal mines dotted across Ukraine's eastern rustbelt have a history of disasters involving high numbers of casualties.
14Zasyadko, which is one of the country's biggest, was the scene of the worst mining accident in Ukraine's post-Soviet history in 2007, in which 101 workers were killed.
15Prime Minister Arseniy Yatsenyuk complained that the rebels did not let government rescue teams help in the rescue operation, casting doubt that the separatists could handle the crisis.
16The rebel authorities however claimed they had everything they needed.
17"We don't need the help," said Leshchenko.
18The head of Donetsk hospital, Alexander Oprichenko, said he had 51 beds available, with another 300 already occupied by patients, around half of whom had been wounded by fighting.
19"The treatment system has not been affected by the war," he declared.
20"All we're missing is some drugs. Russia helps but our stocks are low."
21The mine lies just a few kilometres from the frontline, where sporadic clashes between government forces and the pro-Russian separatists have been reported in recent days, despite both parties backing a February 12 ceasefire deal.
22A military spokesman in Kiev reported Wednesday that another soldier had been killed by rebel fire in the previous 24 hours, bringing to at least five the number of troops killed since Sunday.
23The separatists do not provide regular accounts of their losses.
24Over 6,000 people, mostly civilians, have been killed since the conflict began in April 2014, according to the United Nations.
25Kiev and the West blame Russia for fuelling the insurgency, but Moscow denies sending troops across border.
26In fresh accusations, US Assistant Secretary of State Victoria Nuland told a congressional committee that Russia had deployed "thousands and thousands" of troops to neighbouring Ukraine.
27She also accused Russia of sending "hundreds of pieces of military equipment" to Ukraine since December, including tanks, armoured vehicles and rocket systems.
28Since the ceasefire took effect the rebels have seized the transport hub of Debaltseve but the truce has largely held since, despite mutual accusations of violations.
29Western countries have warned that further ceasefire breaches would be met with new sanctions against Moscow.
30"If the Minsk ceasefire accord does not work then (EU) member states are quite prepared to move to new sanctions," German Chancellor Angela Merkel said Wednesday.
1DONETSK, Ukraine -- A rebel-held city scarred by months of conflict in eastern Ukraine suffered more tragedy Wednesday when a methane gas explosion in a coal mine killed at least 24 workers and left nine missing.
2As rescue efforts stretched past sundown, separatist authorities were accused by Ukraine's government of failing to do enough to save the lives of the miners.
3The blast occurred before dawn more than 1,000 metres underground at the Zasyadko mine in the city of Donetsk in the coal-rich Donbass region.
4Nearly a year of bitter fighting by pro-Moscow rebels and Ukrainian troops in the east has killed more than 6,000 people.
5Rebel officials said the accident was caused by the ever-present danger of methane gas, rather than artillery fire.
6There were contradictory accounts of the toll of dead and missing by the rival authorities.
7The rebel government that controls Donetsk was slow to divulge information, while a senior official in the capital of Kyiv was swift to give a death toll of 32, only to retract it several hours later.
8The blast occurred as 230 workers were in the mine, and nearly 200 of them were quickly evacuated, but uncertainty lingered throughout the day about dozens of others.
9Rebel officials insisted into the afternoon that only one person had died.
10But a slightly wounded miner who gave his name only as Sergei told The Associated Press that he saw five bodies being pulled out.
11By nightfall, Yuliana Bedilko, a representative for the rebel-managed rescue services at the site of accident, said another 23 bodies had been located below ground, bringing the overall number of confirmed dead to 24.
12Under cover of darkness, a truck pulled up in the heavy rain to the mine's opening in preparation to take the bodies away.
13A woman emerging from the mine was heard wailing in grief from a distance.
14Rebel officials had said earlier that 32 workers were unaccounted for, suggesting 16 still remained trapped as of the evening.
15A news agency run by the separatist government reported that 14 people were injured in the accident.
16Igor Murygin, a 42-year-old miner being treated for burns at a hospital in Donetsk, said he was blown off his feet by the explosion.
17"When I came to, there was dust everywhere. People were groaning," said Murygin, who suffered burns over 20 per cent of his body.
18The mine had recently installed new equipment and nothing appeared to be out of order, he added.
19Speaking in Kyiv, Ukrainian Prime Minister Arseniy Yatsenyuk accused rebels of preventing a team of 60 Ukrainian rescuers from reaching the mine to provide assistance.
20But leading rebel representative Denis Pushilin denied that Ukrainian authorities had offered any help.
21"If we truly need assistance, we will turn to Russia," Pushilin was quoted as saying by the rebel-run Donetsk News Agency.
22Miners arriving for their morning shift ended up doing most of the work to clear away debris.
23Reaching the stricken section was complicated because the entrance that was closest to the accident had been shut by the artillery fire that has beset Donetsk.
24Separatist officials arrived at the mine throughout the morning, but all refused to respond to questions, frustrating relatives of miners looking for answers.
25Valentina Petrova came to the mine looking for her 47-year-old son, Vladimir.
26"He was supposed to retire next year. Everyone is angry that they say on TV that 32 people died, but nobody tells us anything," she said.
27The mine has a history of deadly accidents, including one in November 2007 that killed 101 workers, and two more the following month that killed a total of 57.
28Workers complained about many safety violations at the site.
29"We work like crazy for peanuts. We want this place to be safe. We want our children to be able to work here," said a miner who only gave his first name, Kostya.
30He told the AP that two of his brothers had been injured in earlier explosions at the mine.
31Safety officials say 99 people were killed in Ukraine's coal mines in 2014, with 13 of those deaths directly attributable to the fighting in the east, where mines have frequently been hit in artillery duels.
