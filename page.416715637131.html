'We want to heal our city' – Baltimore's mayor lifts curfew

1Baltimore mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake announces the lifting of the curfew.
2Baltimore: The mayor of Baltimore on Sunday lifted a night curfew imposed on the city last week after the looting and arson that followed the funeral of a young black man who died from injuries suffered while in the police custody.
3Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake said she believed sufficient calm had returned to allow her to end the 10pm to 5am curfew, which went into effect last Tuesday after protests over the death of 25-year-old Freddie Gray turned violent on Monday.
4"My goal has always been to not have the curfew in place a single day longer than was necessary," the mayor said.
5"I believe we have reached that point today."
6Parishioners at a service at Southern Baptist Church on Sunday.
7A nearby senior centre being built by the church was burned during the riots following Freddie Gray's funeral.
8Speaking in front of a mall that was looted on Monday, the mayor also said the Maryland National Guard would begin withdrawing from the streets over the next week.
9As of Saturday, there were about 2,600 troops helping law enforcement officers patrol the city and enforce the curfew, the guard said.
10The surprise announcement on Friday by the city's chief prosecutor that she was bringing criminal charges against the six police officers involved in Mr Gray's arrest has helped to defuse outrage over his death.
11The protests in the mostly black city of 625,000 have been a reprise of nationwide anger that erupted last year after the deaths of unarmed black men in confrontations with police in Missouri, New York and elsewhere.
12A shopper outside the Mondawmin Mall, which was being patrolled on Sunday by a member of the Maryland National Guard.
13"What we saw over the past few days is not just the resiliency of our city, but also our communities coming together," the mayor told a news conference, when asked if how confident she was that the time was right to lift the curfew.
14"We want to heal our city."
15Larry Hogan, the Republican governor of Maryland, welcomed the decision by the Democratic mayor, who had faced criticism for what some saw as a slow initial response to Monday's violence.
16Mr Hogan was among those who expressed concern over the financial impact of the curfew, which forced bars and restaurants to close early.
17Baltimore's downtown Inner Harbour district, a tourist destination, resembled a ghost town on Friday and Saturday nights, normally the busiest nights of the week.
18Twitter instantly lit up with reaction: "Baltimore curfew is seriously hurting businesses glad to see it lifted," wrote someone using the Twitter name God First.
19But many tweets accused authorities of having applied the curfew selectively and mostly in black neighbourhoods.
20The looting and arson on Monday contrasted with what had been mostly peaceful protests in the Maryland city since Mr Gray's death a week after his April 12 arrest.
21People at a rally in Baltimore on Saturday praised the decision by prosecutor Marilyn Mosby to charge one of the officers involved in Mr Gray's arrest with murder and five others with lesser crimes.
22Ms Mosby said the state medical examiner had ruled Mr Gray's death a homicide.
23She said he was unlawfully arrested and the officers repeatedly ignored his pleas for medical help while he was handcuffed, shackled and lying face down in the back of a police van.
24Her swift decision to bring charges contrasted with decisions last year by grand juries to clear officers in the Missouri and New York deaths.
25The mood of Saturday's rally was almost celebratory, in sharp contrast to the outrage expressed earlier in the week, especially on Monday, when more than a dozen law enforcement officers were hurt and more than 200 people were arrested.
26Still, Baltimore police arrested at least a dozen people on Saturday for violating the curfew.
