Eagles: 60-minute man Chuck Bednarik has died at 89

New Mexico Legislature wrangles with capital outlay

1He was 89.
2Known as "Concrete Charlie," Bednarik epitomized the tough-guy linebacker and also was an outstanding center for the Eagles from 1949 to 1962.
3He is best remembered for a game-saving tackle at the 9-yard line on the final play of the 1960 title game, and it was typical Bednarik.
4He threw Green Bay running back Jim Taylor to the ground and refused to let him up while the final seconds ticked off as the Eagles held on for a 17-13 win.
5"Everybody reminds me of it and I'm happy they remind me of it," Bednarik once said.
6"I'm proud and delighted to have played in that game."
7He died at an assisted living facility in Richland, Pennsylvania, following a brief illness, the Eagles said in a statement.
8Bednarik, who frequently criticized modern athletes, said he played on all but two kickoffs against the Packers and could have kept playing if he needed to, unlike today's players who "suck air after five plays."
9He missed only three games in his 14-year career.
10The tackle on Taylor actually was the second hit that season that drew headlines.
11Earlier in 1960, he knocked out New York Giants running back Frank Gifford with a blow so hard that Gifford suffered a concussion and didn't play again until 1962.
12An iconic photograph captured Bednarik pumping his fist over Gifford's prone body, though the linebacker insisted he wasn't gloating.
13He said he didn't notice what happened to Gifford after the hit and only saw that he had fumbled and another Eagle recovered the ball.
14Bednarik was the last NFL starter to play regularly on both offense and defense until Deion Sanders did so for Dallas in 1996.
15Sanders' achievement hardly impressed Bednarik.
16"The positions I played, every play, I was making contact, not like that ... Deion Sanders," Bednarik said.
17"He couldn't tackle my wife. He's back there dancing out there instead of hitting."
18Born May 1, 1925, in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania, Bednarik flew 30 combat missions over Germany as a gunner during World War II.
19He then played center for Penn from 1945 to 1948, and was selected first overall in the 1949 NFL draft by the Eagles.
20In 1950, he was All-NFL as a center, then he was voted All-NFL as a linebacker in 1951 through 1957, and again in 1960.
21Bednarik, whose gnarled fingers in retirement stood as a reminder of the ruggedness of his profession, said he never made more than $27,000 in a season and supplemented his income by selling concrete, earning his nickname.
22At one point, he pawned his championship ring and his Hall of Fame ring.
23In early 2005, when the Eagles won the NFC championship and had Philadelphia in a Super Bowl frenzy, Bednarik was bitter enough to root for the Patriots in the Super Bowl.
24He later apologized to owner Jeff Lurie and was a welcomed visitor at training camp and other alumni functions.
25"Philadelphia fans grow up expecting toughness, all-out effort and a workmanlike attitude from this team and so much of that image has its roots in the way Chuck played the game," Lurie said in a statement released by the team.
26The Maxwell Football Club presents an award in Bednarik's honor to the defensive player of the year in college football.
27Bednarik is survived by his wife, Emma, and five daughters -Charlene Thomas, Donna Davis, Carol Safarowic, Pam McWilliams, and Jackie Chelius, as well as 10 grandchildren and a great-grandchild.

1The Republican-controlled New Mexico House voted to pass a funding measure that Senate leaders vowed to ignore and with the clock ticking down didn't have enough time to consider.
2The House voted 36-32 in the final minutes to approve a nearly $270 million capital outlay package despite heated opposition from Democrats.
3The vote came after House Democrats failed to put money back into the bill for some senior centers and for community colleges.
4Republicans said millions of dollars for such projects had previously gone unspent.
5The House Ways and Means Committee amended the legislation that included highway improvements and upgrades at prison facilities.
6Democrats say the House bill borrowed too much money, but Republicans countered that using bonds to fund road projects showed foresight.
7Lawmakers did finish work, however, on what is considered their most important assignment - a $6.2 billion spending plan that pays for public education and government services.
8The budget is now in the hands of the governor, who can cut spending in the package using her line-item veto powers.
9The sticking point over the state projects bill came down to how the highway work should be paid for: through severance tax bonds or from state reserves.
10In news conferences afterward, each side blamed the other for stalling the package.
11The bill approved by the House would have been stripped in the Senate of "all those amendments that help the big oil and gas companies in southeastern New Mexico," said Senate Majority Leader Michael Sanchez, D-Belen.
12"The (Democratic) caucus was pretty firm: we were not gonna let the governor take away from families, schools, to help her friends down in the oil patch."
13About $800 million in capital-outlay projects that hasn't been spent is still available, Sanchez said.
14But House Republicans and Gov. Susana Martinez said the gridlock was solely the fault of the Democratic-controlled Senate's opposition.
15"Senate Democrats chose to obstruct, they chose to delay, chose to gridlock, chose to be partisan and they chose not to compromise," Martinez said.
16Martinez said she had no plans to call for a special session to revisit the capital-funding issue.
17The last-minute bickering came as little surprise as the two chambers found little common ground during the session.
18With the GOP in control of the House for the first time in decades and Democrats at the helm in the Senate, the chambers battled on major issues that included a right-to-work initiative, abortion rights, social promotion and driver's licenses.
19At times, contentious bills resulted in hours of testimony in one chamber only for them to end up languishing or getting blocked in a committee.
20Those included measures to ban late-term abortions and require parental notification before a minor ends a pregnancy and a measure to end the state's practice of issuing driver's licenses to people in the country illegally.
21"We had a hard session this year," Senate President Pro Tempore Mary Kay Papen, D-Las Cruces, said during the closing minutes.
22"We will be back next year."
23Legislation on ride-booking services, warrantless drones, forced mental health treatment and sheriff's qualifications also failed to pass.
24Still, the Legislature passed dozens of bills, including a measure to adjust tax credits for film and television production companies to encourage them to buy and hire locally.
25A bill to require health insurance companies to help the state purchase vaccines for privately insured children in New Mexico was also approved.
26In addition, lawmakers passed a bill calling for reorganizing the New Mexico Department of Public Safety to make the agency more efficient given limited resources.
27Martinez late Friday signed that measure.
28Lawmakers also voted to create a five-member board to oversee grant applications to eradicate bullying in New Mexico schools, colleges and communities.
29With less than 48 hours left before adjournment, lawmakers came together to approve a gambling compact that clears the way for a handful of American Indian tribes to continue operating casinos.
30That measure still requires final consideration by the U.S. Interior Department.
