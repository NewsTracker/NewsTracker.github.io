Bird flu detected in northeast Kansas

Part-time Ferguson mayor in full-time spotlight

1(AP) - Bird flu has been detected in a backyard flock of chickens and ducks in northeast Kansas.
2The Kansas Department of Agriculture said in a news release late Friday that the discovery was made in Leavenworth County.
3Backyard poultry owners in the county are urged to contact the department.
4Additionally, parts of Cherokee and Crawford counties in the southeast part of the state are under surveillance after a confirmed case of the H5N2 strain of bird flu was found just across the state line in Jasper County, Missouri, this month.
5Avian influenza is common in wild migratory waterfowl but doesn't usually harm them.
6But the H5N2 strain is deadly when it spreads to commercial poultry.
7It can wipe out a flock of tens of thousands of birds in a few days.

2FERGUSON, Mo.
3(AP) - In the days since the release of a Justice Department report that found widespread racial bias in the Ferguson Police Department, the mayor of the St. Louis suburb has become a part-time public servant in a full-time spotlight.
4The attention has only intensified as six city employees - most notably the police chief and city manager - have been fired or stepped down.
5But Mayor James Knowles III remains, making just $4,200 a year in a job he called basically ceremonial before a white police officer shot an unarmed black 18-year-old in August, prompting weeks of sometimes-violent protests and the Justice Department inquiry.
6He's now so involved that he's opened a City Hall office and insists he will stay to see the city through the changes it must make.
7On Friday, five residents filed an affidavit seeking to recall Knowles.
8They have 60 days to collect enough signatures - 15 percent of registered voters in the last mayoral election - to qualify for a special election.
9Knowles said he has no plans to step aside.
10"Obviously there are people on the street calling for my resignation, but my voicemail, my text messages and my Facebook are full with literally hundreds of people who want me to stay," Knowles said in an Associated Press interview Friday.
11"Somebody has to show leadership, and I'm focused on how we can move this community forward."
12He argues that Ferguson's city manager form of government made him more figurehead than administrator, leading the city council but lacking the power to do much else.
13But critics say he must have known about the lax police oversight, racial profiling and profit-driven court practices cited in the Justice Department report released March 4.
14"I want the mayor out," said Kayla Reed, 25, of the Organization for Black Struggle.
15"True accountability means clean house, top to bottom."
16Knowles, who grew up in Ferguson, was just a few years out of Truman State University when he defeated an incumbent for election to the city council in 2005.
17He was elected state chairman of the Missouri Federation of Young Republicans in 2008 and was on the fast-track, earning mention in 2009 in the St. Louis Business Journal's "30 Under 30" list.
18He was elected to his current post in 2011, becoming one of Missouri's youngest mayors, and re-elected without opposition in 2014.
19But he was little known outside the area until Aug. 9, when Officer Darren Wilson shot 18-year-old Michael Brown and Ferguson found itself suddenly in the national spotlight.
20After the shooting, Knowles and Police Chief Tom Jackson became the public face of Ferguson, holding news conferences, hosting public forums and reaching out to civil rights leaders and protesters.
21Meanwhile, City Manager John Shaw, who made $120,000 a year until he resigned Tuesday, remained out of sight.
22Assistant city manager, Pam Hylton, has been named the interim city manager.
23Knowles has a full-time job: general manager of the state-contracted motor vehicle license office in Ferguson.
24He said he typically works about 60 hours a week, evenly splitting the time between the two duties.
25Knowles has sought to be a reassuring presence for supporters and critics alike.
26At a council meeting Tuesday, he sat patiently as some residents loudly called for his ouster.
27He was equally passive as others praised him and the council.
28When a brief shouting match began between a city critic and supporter, he urged both sides to calm down.
29Knowles, who is white, has deep roots in the city of 21,000, where two-thirds of residents are black.
30He was a top wrestler at McCluer High School and has been an assistant wrestling coach at another mostly black high school for several years.
31He was drawn to politics in fourth grade, when his father ran for city council (he lost but won on a subsequent try.)
32Former Mayor Brian Fletcher, who worked alongside him when he was a young councilman, said Knowles has a bit of ego, like most politicians.
33He isn't up for re-election for another two years, and Fletcher believes he'll ride out the storm.
34"He's in a tough situation," Fletcher said.
35"He's taken a lead. From the people I've talked to they're very pleased with the way he's handled himself under a very difficult circumstance, a very difficult time for our community."
36But Rasheen Aldridge, director of Young Activists United St. Louis and a member of the Ferguson Commission appointed by Gov. Jay Nixon, believes Knowles needs to follow the police chief and city manager out the door.
37The Justice Department report - a companion to one that cleared Wilson of any wrongdoing - laid bare instances of racial profiling and bias in the police department and a municipal court system that profited largely on the backs of blacks.
38"When all of this stuff was going on, there's no way you can be the mayor of the town and not look at how much money is coming in, and even if you see it you don't raise a concern," Aldridge said.
39"This is his city. He's the mayor, and he needs to be held accountable."
