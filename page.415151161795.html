Florida group to sue FBI in death of friend of Boston bombing suspect

Video reports conflict in probe of Putin critic's slaying

1Elena Teyer, mother-in-law of Ibragim Todashev, speaks to reporters following a pre-trial conference for Boston Marathon bombing suspect Dzhokhar Tsarnaev at the federal courthouse in Boston, Massachusetts December 18, 2014.
2(Reuters) - A Florida Islamic group announced on Monday it has filed a formal notice with the FBI that it plans to sue the agency for $30 million in the death of Ibragim Todashev, a friend of one of the Boston Marathon bombing suspects.
3Todashev, 27, a Muslim Chechen immigrant, was killed in an Orlando apartment in May 2013 during FBI questioning about his links with the Boston suspects.
4The FBI said the agent shot Todashev after he attacked him.
5The notice was filed by the Council on American-Islamic Relations Florida (CAIR-Florida), a civil rights group, on behalf of Todashev's parents who accused the FBI in a statement on Monday of killing their son "in cold blood."
6Thania Diaz Clevenger, civil rights director for CAIR Florida, said the group was "seeking answers and justice for someone who was shot seven times by an FBI agent in his own home after hours of interrogation."
7An FBI spokesman said Monday the agency does not comment on pending litigation.
8CAIR spokesman Ali Kurnaz told a press conference in Orlando "there are a lot of things that happened that day that we don't know ... there are things that don't add up."
9For example, the autopsy report and video from the interrogation have not been publicly released, he said.
10CAIR accused the FBI of "careless hiring practices" involving FBI agent Aaron McFarlane as well as a lax internal review that cleared him in Todashev's death.
11CAIR said in a statement that during his time with the Oakland, California, Police Department he was involved in two police brutality lawsuits, four internal affairs investigations, allegations of beating suspects and witnesses falsified police reports.
12Boston Marathon bombing suspect Dzhokhar Tsarnaev was in court on Monday for a hearing before his trial starts later this week.
13Tsarnaev, 21, is accused of killing three people and injuring 264 with bombs in the largest mass-casualty attack on U.S. soil since Sept.
1411, 2001.
15He is also accused of killing a police officer.
16Todashev was a friend of Tsarnaev's brother, Tamerlan, an alleged co-conspirator in the Boston bombing, who was killed in a police shootout.
17Todashev's parents in Russia issued a statement through CAIR on Monday, saying their son "was killed by the FBI in cold blood."
18"Today, together with CAIR Florida, we are starting a process that will bring, as we hope, justice to our son, our family, and our world."
1The emotional account by Anna Duritskaya came amid a swirl of speculation about who was responsible for the high-profile assassination and what it means for Russia.
2While state-run and Kremlin-controlled media focused on a theory that the killing was a provocation aimed at staining President Vladimir Putin, his critics are holding the Russian leader responsible for creating an atmosphere that encouraged the crime by fanning nationalist, anti-Western sentiments and vilifying the opposition.
3Duritskaya said she has been questioned extensively by authorities.
4Shortly after midnight Tuesday, Duritskaya flew into an international airport in the Ukrainian capital, Kiev, from where she was swiftly whisked away by a security detail in a car with blacked-out windows.
5Vadim Prokhorov, a lawyer for Nemtsov who traveled with Duritskaya, said she had been left emotionally drained by investigators' questioning.
6"She has given a full and exhaustive account of her last hours with Boris," Prokhorov said.
7"If any further investigative action is required, she has promised - something she has done publicly - that she will cooperate. The main thing is that the guilty parties be tracked down."
8In her first public comments since the killing, Duritskaya said in an interview with Russia's independent Dozhd television that she waited for Nemtsov to meet her Friday night at the Bosco Cafe, a pricey restaurant in the former GUM department store on Red Square.
9He had just given a radio interview in which he had slammed Putin's "mad, aggressive policy" on Ukraine.
10They dined and then walked across a bridge near St. Basil's Cathedral, heading for Nemtsov's apartment across the Moscow River from the Kremlin, she said, her eyes welling with tears.
11Duritskaya said didn't see the man who shot Nemtsov, only a car speeding up.
12"I don't know where he came from, but he was behind," she said of the gunman.
13"I didn't see the man. I turned round and all I saw was a light-colored car. I saw neither the brand nor the license plate of the car that was driving away."
14After Nemtsov was shot, she saw a snowplow approaching them on the bridge and she said she asked its driver how to call police.
15The driver gave her the number, then drove away, she added.
16TV Center, a station controlled by the Moscow city government, broadcast a poor-resolution video from a web camera that it said showed Nemtsov and Duritskaya shortly before he was killed.
17A vehicle that TVC identified as a snowplow moved slowly behind the couple, obscuring the view of the shooting.
18TV Center then circled what it said was the suspected killer jumping into a passing car.
19The video, which could not be independently verified, contradicted the initial statement by police, who said Nemtsov was shot from a passing car.
20Investigators said they are looking into several possible links for Nemtsov's slaying, including an attempt to destabilize the state, Islamic extremism, the Ukraine conflict and his personal life.
21They have offered a reward of 3 million rubles (nearly $50,000) for any information.
22Tens of thousands of supporters marched Sunday through central Moscow in a silent tribute to Nemtsov.
23Others mourned him in St. Petersburg and other European cities.
24Putin quickly sent condolences to Nemtsov's 86-year old mother, promising her that the perpetrators of the "vile and cynical murder" will be brought to justice.
25State television stations focused on allegations that Russia's enemies could be behind the hit, following comment by Putin's spokesman that the president saw the attack as a "provocation" aimed to destabilize the country.
26Chechnya's Moscow-backed strongman, Ramzan Kadyrov, openly blamed Western special services.
27Kremlin critics pointed out that the site of the killing is one of the most heavily secured parts of the Russian capital, packed with police, plainclothes agents and security cameras.
28"The choice of place in front of the Kremlin ... points at the perpetrator's sense of impunity and his link to law enforcement structures," opposition lawmaker Ilya Ponomaryov wrote in his blog.
29"It was made for a picture: a prominent Russian opposition leader lying dead with the Kremlin stars and St. Basil's Cathedral as the backdrop."
30Many commentators said that like other key opposition leaders, Nemtsov was constantly shadowed by police, so it would be hard to imagine his killing could go unnoticed by them.
31Some noted that Nemtsov died on the newly established holiday commemorating the Special Operations Forces, honoring troops who swept through Ukraine's Crimean Peninsula, setting stage for its annexation by Russia a year ago.
32Nemtsov's killing was the biggest political assassination in Russia since another Kremlin foe, journalist Anna Politkovskaya, was shot to death in the elevator of her Moscow apartment building on Putin's birthday in 2006.
33Five Chechens were convicted in the case last year, but it has remained unclear who ordered the killing.
34Some observers speculated that certain members of a hawkish, isolationist wing of the government could have had a hand in Nemtsov's death, possibly counting on it to provoke outrage abroad and further strain Russia's ties with the West.
35Those relations already are at their lowest point since the Cold War because of the Ukrainian crisis.
36"Russia is at crossroads," Irina Khakamada, who once co-led a liberal party with Nemtsov, said on RBC TV.
37"Those who did it favored a more aggressive path. It was a demonstration of their omnipotence."
38Western officials have called for Russia to conduct a prompt, thorough, transparent and credible investigation into the slaying.
39Putin has ordered law enforcement chiefs to personally oversee the probe.
40White House spokesman Josh Earnest on Monday described Nemtsov as a "tireless advocate for his country, an opponent of corruption, and an advocate for human rights and greater transparency."
41The business newspaper Kommersant quoted anonymous sources in the Interior Ministry as saying there was no closed-circuit TV video of the killing because the cameras in question were not working at the time.
42However, Yelena Novikova, a spokeswoman for Moscow's information technology department, which oversees the city's surveillance cameras, said all cameras "belonging to the city" were operating properly when Nemtsov was killed.
43She said federal authorities also had surveillance cameras near the Kremlin that are not under her organization's control.
44Novikova would not confirm the existence of any video of the killing, saying the police investigation was still underway.
45Duritskaya's mother, Inna, who spoke to The Associated Press in the Ukrainian capital of Kiev, said she worried that Russian authorities were "putting psychological pressure" on her daughter.
46"I am worried that they want to use her, as a citizen of Ukraine, use her as though this situation is somehow something to do with Ukraine," she said Monday.
47"She has absolutely nothing to do with this killing. She is innocent. Stop abusing my child."
