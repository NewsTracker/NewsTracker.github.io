More than 30 killed in mine blast in rebel east Ukraine: local authority

1Mine officials said the explosion was most likely caused by gas and not fighting in the war between Moscow-backed rebels and Ukraine government forces.
2Nevertheless, Kiev suggested the war had made the disaster worse, accusing the separatists of holding up a rescue effort by restricting access.
3Outside the gates of the Zasyadko mine, about 30 relatives clamored for information about any survivors.
4Sergei Baldayev, a miner injured in the blast, mingled with the crowd, his face covered in scratches and one arm hanging motionless by his side, the result of a broken collarbone.
5The sister of one miner who was in the pit at the time of the explosion, Alexei Novoselsky, stood in tears.
6"Tell me, are there survivors? Why are you concealing the truth?" she asked as a rescue worker tried to calm her.
7The Donetsk regional administration said 16 injured people were in hospital.
8Earlier in the day, after the body of one miner had been retrieved, the emergence services said 32 people remained trapped in the mine.
9But hour after hour the death toll kept rising.
10"The total deaths amount to 33 people," Interfax news agency reported, citing the region administration's press service.
11Eduard Dmitrenko, a miner who went down the affected shaft after the blast, said it was unlikely any of the missing miners would be found alive.
12"But they will of course find bodies," said Dmitrenko, his face covered with coal dust.
13Trucks and buses were driven to the mine in preparation for transporting the bodies to local morgues.
14Asked what were the chances of trapped miners surviving, a medical worker said: "It's getting smaller and smaller all the time, because of the methane, the hot air, burns to the airways."
15Donetsk has been the scene of heavy fighting between Moscow-backed separatist rebels, who control the area, and forces loyal to the government in Kiev.
16A ceasefire has sharply reduced the violence in the past week.
17The neighborhood around the mine has come under artillery fire, with fragments from Grad rockets visible on surrounding roads, but mine officials said the explosion was unrelated to the fighting and most likely caused by gas.
18In Kiev, Ukrainian Prime Minister Arseny Yatseniuk said rescue workers had been dispatched by the central authorities, "but the Russian terrorists did not let them reach the scene of the accident," he said, using a term commonly used by Kiev to describe the separatists.
19The mine, now owned by a private firm, has a history of fatal accidents in the 57 years it has been operating.
20An explosion at Zasyadko in 2007 killed 106 people.
21A cemetery next to the pit holds the graves of many miners killed in the past.
22"When there's an accident, we bury them all here," said the head of security at the shaft where Tuesday's blast happened, who did not give his name.
23"Coal is a costly business."
24A welder at the mine, who gave his name as Oleg, said outside the entrance: "I've been down the pit for 23 years, and this is the fourth explosion that I can recall. If they didn't get them out straight away, then later they will only retrieve bodies. An explosion is a terrible thing."
25The Zasyadko coal mine produced 1.4 million tonnes of coal in 2013.
26The mine is in the center of a Donbass region which is Ukraine's industrial and coal-producing heartland.
27Ukrainian coal production fell 22 percent in 2014 to 65 million tonnes as the conflict disrupted mining operations, leading to some shortages of coal at power plants.
