Clinton Stops at Iowa Coffee Shop to Kick off 2016 Campaign

1In this image taken from video posted to hillaryclinton.com on Sunday, April 12, 2015, Hillary Rodham Clinton announces her campaign for president.
2The former secretary of state, senator and first lady enters the race in a strong position to succeed her rival from the 2008 campaign, President Barack Obama.
3The big rallies and massive fundraising blitzes have to wait.
4Hillary Rodham Clinton is getting her 2016 campaign for president started with a few caffeinated beverages and says she's ready to "drink my way across Iowa."
5"Hi, everybody," Clinton said Tuesday after popping into the Jones Street Java House, a coffee shop in the Mississippi River town of LeClaire.
6"I'm happy to be here."
7Fresh from a two-day road trip, Clinton's coffee shop stop Tuesday morning is the first event in her return to presidential politics.
8It sticks with her strategy to hold small "retail" style events that allow her to speak to individual voters.
9She doesn't plan to hold a large kickoff rally for several weeks, and will tour a community college and hold a roundtable discussion with students and teachers in Monticello, Iowa, later Tuesday.
10It's a debut reminiscent of the "listening tour" that opened her campaign for Senate in 2000, when she ventured into small upstate towns to convene meetings with voters and local leaders.
11At the coffee shop, she asked for recommendations on what to order, and decided on a Masala chai and a Carmela latte, along with some water with lemon.
12Her total: $6.96.
13Among those she spoke with was LeClaire Mayor Bob Scannell, an independent.
14"I always vote for the person who I think will do best for the country, and she has my vote," he said.
15In a fundraising email to supporters on Monday, Clinton promised not to take anything for granted and to "work my heart out to earn every single vote."
16Clinton is taking that same low-key approach to fundraising, forgoing the celebrity-studded fundraisers that marked her husband's presidency, as well as the high-dollar private events put on this year by former Florida Gov. Jeb Bush, a potential GOP rival.
17Instead, Clinton's initial appeals for money will be for small-dollar donations collected over the Internet instead of in swanky fundraising blowouts in New York, Los Angeles and Silicon Valley.
18Advisers have set a modest goal of raising $100 million for the primary campaign and will not initially accept donations for the general election.
19"Everyone knows that over time Hillary Clinton will raise enough to be competitive," said Tom Nides, a top Wall Street supporter and former State Department adviser to Clinton.
20"Her objective is not to raise money to prove that she can. It's to build the grassroots organization."
21Clinton retains deep ties to the party's top fundraisers, including those cultivated by her husband, former President Bill Clinton, during the 1990s.
22During its first call with donors Monday, Hillary Clinton's team noted that some of those listening in helped President Barack Obama's campaigns, while others had raised money for Clinton's own White House bid in 2008.
23Others, they said, were new to the fundraising circuit.
24With those relationships well established, her aides on Monday outlined steps to cast their net as widely as possible to broaden their list of potential contributors, according to several donors who took part.
25They spoke on condition of anonymity to describe a private conference call.
