1At Kasumigaseki, one of the hardest hit subway stations, staff and relatives of some of those who died in the biggest attack on post-WWII Japan fell silent at 8:00 am to remember those who were lost and those still affected.
2"Twenty years have passed but I think the victims are still suffering," said Fumiko Suzuki, who was there to pay tribute to a friend made ill by the invisible clouds of sarin that spread through rush-hour trains.
3"We should not forget about the attack."
4Prime Minister Shinzo Abe also visited the station in the afternoon to place flowers in memory.
5"I offered my condolences to the victims as we marked the 20th anniversary of the attack," he said.
6"The incident isn't over yet because many are still suffering from the after-effects of the sarin."
7Thirteen members of the Aum Supreme Truth cult remain on death row for their part in the March 20, 1995 terror and for other murders and kidnappings the group carried out.
8Cult leader Shoko Asahara, a blind mystic who espoused a blend of Hinduism and Buddhism, was convicted of ordering five teams of followers to dump packets of sarin on trains, puncturing them with the sharpened tip of an umbrella.
9Thousands of commuters were unknowingly exposed to the colourless, odourless nerve gas, which even in low doses can cause paralysis of the lungs and neurological damage.
10Some died almost instantly, while others suffered gradually worsening symptoms.
11More than 6,000 people were treated in hospital after having inhaled sarin -- an agent developed in Nazi Germany and used by Saddam Hussein against the Kurds in Iraq.
12Kasumigaseki station master Mitsuaki Ota led tributes to Tokyo Metro worker Kazumasa Takahashi who unknowingly picked up a punctured packet of the nerve gas from the floor of one of the trains.
13He and another colleague died.
14"I'd like to offer my deep condolences to those who died, and I'm praying from the bottom of my heart for the quick recovery of those who are still suffering," Ota said.
15Takahashi's widow, Shizue, told reporters that the 20th anniversary of the attack should serve as a wake-up call over the risk of terrorism.
16"It's possible we'll face the danger of terror attacks. I want (Japanese people) to be more aware," she said after laying a flower on a makeshift altar.
17Her comments came after three Japanese tourists were among 21 people killed when gunmen stormed a museum in the Tunisian capital.
18Chief Cabinet Secretary Yoshihide Suga said at a regular press conference that the government would do its best to prevent similar attacks from happening.
19The final trial relating to the episode is under way, with Katsuya Takahashi facing a murder charge following his arrest in 2012 after years on the run.
20But after two decades, the exact reasons behind the incident remain opaque.
21Prosecutors said Asahara, now 60 years old, had become paranoid that the authorities were coming to close down his sprawling cult compound at the foot of Mount Fuji, and ordered the subway attack to disrupt attempts to capture him.
22Japan still puzzles over why the cult, which had 10,000 members at the height of its popularity, was able to attract some of the country's brightest minds, including scientists and doctors who had graduated from top universities.
