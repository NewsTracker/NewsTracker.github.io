Q&A: Kurds fight Islamic State and endure Turkish attacks

1U.S. and Turkish plans to create an "Islamic State-free zone" in Syria along the Turkish border could escalate the conflict between Turkey and Kurdish fighters in Syria and Iraq.
2With the help of U.S. airstrikes, the Kurds have proven to be among the most effective ground forces against the IS group.
3But their advance across northeastern Syria in recent months has alarmed Ankara, which fears they could revive a decades-long insurgency in pursuit of statehood.
4Syria's main Kurdish fighting force is affiliated with the Kurdistan Workers' Party, or PKK, which waged a decades-long insurgency in Turkey and maintains bases in remote parts of northern Iraq.
5Since Friday, Turkey has struck the IS group in Syria and PKK positions inside Iraq.
6On Monday, the main Syrian Kurdish militia, known as the YPG, claimed that it had been shelled by Turkish troops.
7A Turkish official said the military was only returning fire, and that the campaign does not include the YPG.
8WHO ARE THE KURDS?
9The Kurds are an ethnic group with their own language and customs whose nomadic past led to their modern-day dispersal across several countries, mostly Turkey, Iraq, Syria, Iran and Armenia.
10Sunni Muslims make up the vast majority, but there is a sizeable Shiite population, particularly in Iran.
11After the collapse of the Ottoman and Qajar empires and the drawing of modern borders, Iraq, Iran and Turkey each agreed to oppose the creation of an independent Kurdistan, making the Kurds the largest stateless minority group in the world.
12With nearly 25 million people living in five countries, they continue to push for self-rule.
13WHAT IS THEIR ROLE IN TURKEY?
14Turkey is home to an estimated 15 million Kurds, about one-fifth of the country's population of 76 million.
15Most are Sunni Muslim.
16The PKK has fought a three-decade war, initially for independence and later for autonomy and greater rights for Kurds.
17The conflict with the PKK has killed tens of thousands of people since 1984.
18Turkey and its U.S. and European allies consider the PKK - which has Marxist origins - a terrorist organization for killing civilians in urban bombings.
19In 2012, Turkey launched secret talks with the PKK's imprisoned leader, Abdullah Ocalan, to end the conflict.
20The talks were made public in 2013 and the PKK declared a cease-fire a few months later.
21The Kurds later accused Turkey of not doing enough to help Syrian Kurds during their battle against Islamic State militants over the Syrian border town of Kobani, prompting violent clashes and straining the fragile peace process.
22Tensions flared again after an Islamic State suicide bombing in the southeastern Turkish city of Suruc last week killed 32 people.
23Kurdish groups held the Turkish government responsible, saying it had not been aggressive in battling the Islamic State group.
24Turkey's pro-Kurdish party, the People's Democratic Party, said the strikes on the PKK in Syria and Iraq amounted to an end of the two-year-old truce.
25It called on the government to end the bombing campaign and resume a dialogue with the Kurds.
26Turkey views Kurds in Iraq as an ally but is suspicious of Syrian Kurds who are affiliated with the PKK.
27Ankara is worried that Kurdish gains in Iraq and in Syria will encourage the aspirations of its own Kurdish population.
28WHERE DO THEY STAND IN IRAQ?
29Five million Kurds have their own government in Iraq's semi-autonomous north and have significant representation in the central government with several key posts including the presidency, which is allocated to Kurds.
30They currently represent about 20 percent of Iraq's population, making them the largest ethnic minority.
31There are two main Iraqi Kurdish factions: The Kurdistan Democratic Party is led by Kurdish Regional Government President Massoud Barzani, and the Patriotic Union of Kurdistan is led by former Iraqi President Jalal Talabani.
32The factions fought a bloody war for power over northern Iraq in the mid-1990s, before agreeing to a power-sharing deal that ended the fighting in 1998.
33The Iraqi Kurdish militia, known as the peshmerga, has been a major force in repelling the Islamic State group's onslaught in recent months, with nearly a dozen countries rushing to its aid with weapons and training in the absence of genuine support from a strained Iraqi military.
34The United States has been one of the most ardent protectors of Iraqi Kurds for over a generation, helping establish and enforce a safe haven in northern Iraq to protect them from Saddam Hussein.
35WHAT ABOUT THE KURDS IN SYRIA?
36Kurds are the largest ethnic minority in Syria, making up more than 10 percent of the country's pre-war population of 23 million people.
37They are centered mostly in the impoverished northeastern province of Hassakeh, between the borders of Turkey and Iraq.
38The Kurdish Democratic Union party, or PYD, is the most powerful political force among Syria's Kurds.
39The party is a deeply secular, and affiliated with the PKK.
40The People's Protection Units, known by the Kurdish acronym YPG, is the main Kurdish fighting force in Syria.
41Since Syria's civil war began, the Kurds have made unprecedented gains, strengthening their hold on the far northeast reaches of the country and carving out territory where they declared their own civil administration.
42They showed resilience in their fight against IS group militants in Kobani, pushing them out in January with the help of U.S.-led airstrikes.
43Last month, they ejected the Islamic State group from their stronghold of Tal Abyad along the border with Turkey, robbing the IS of a key avenue for smuggling oil and foreign fighters.
44Associated Press writers Suzan Fraser in Ankara, Turkey, and Zeina Karam in Beirut contributed to this report.
