Greece in limbo: Shuttered banks keep lifeline

1ATHENS, Greece (AP) " Greece braced for more chaos on the streets outside its mostly shuttered banks Thursday, as Athens and its creditors halted talks on resolving the country's deepening financial crisis until a referendum this weekend.
2Banks have been closed all week to prevent a crash from mass money withdrawals, while a few have been reopened to help pensioners without ATM cards.
3But they are still in business.
4The European Central Bank left the terms of its emergency $100 billion cash support to Greece unchanged, a day after Athens slipped into arrears with the International Monetary Fund and its bailout program expired.
5The move kept chances alive for a settlement between Greece and creditors.
6And Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis publicly thanked the ECB and its president, Mario Draghi, for the decision.
7"This allows us to breathe. It's a very positive move and a move of good will on the part of the European Central Bank. I welcome it," Varoufakis told state television.
8Draghi, he said, had faced down "hawks" among eurozone members who had demanded that Athens increase collateral needed to receive continued assistance.
9Greece is seeking a third bailout from the eurozone rescue fund after the previous deal expired this week without agreement on the terms of final pay outs.
10The impasse left billions bailout money frozen or canceled and saw Greece forced to close banks and its stock market for at least a week, while the country's left-wing government called a referendum urging voters to denounce the previous deal offered by creditors.
11Eurozone finance ministers decided to put the talks with Greece on hold till the vote takes place.
12"Given the political situation, the rejection of the previous proposals, the referendum which will take place on Sunday, and the recommendation by the Greek government to vote 'No,' we see no grounds for further talks at this point," Dutch Foreign Minister and eurogroup chairman Jeroen Dijsselbloem said after a late-night teleconference.
13"There will be no talks in the coming days."
14In Athens, crowds of anxious elderly Greeks thronged banks Wednesday beginning before dawn, struggling to withdraw their maximum of 120 euros ($134) for the week after the government reopened some banks to help pensioners who don't have bank cards.
15Business associations and the country's largest labor union urged the government to cancel the vote.
16And the Council of Europe " an independent body that monitors elections and human rights " told The Associated Press the referendum would fall short of international standards.
17In a sign of serious financial deterioration, Greece suffered another sovereign downgrade, the fourth this week, as Moody's slashed the country's rating from Caa2 to Caa3, or just above default.
18Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras resisted the pressure, however, insisting a "No" vote would give the government a stronger negotiating position.
19"There are those who insist on linking the result of the referendum with the country's future in the euro," Tsipras said.
20"They even say I apparently have a secret plan to take the country out of the EU if the vote is 'No.' They are lying with the full knowledge of that fact."
21Greece is in a financial limbo now that its bailout program has expired, cutting it off from vital financing and pushing it one step closer to leaving the euro.
22It also has become the first developed country to fail to repay a debt to the International Monetary Fund on time.
23The last country to miss an IMF payment was Zimbabwe in 2001.
24As long as it is in arrears on the IMF payment, Greece cannot get any more money from the organization.
25The referendum has thrown additional confusion into the already fraught negotiations.
26German Chancellor Angela Merkel said a deal remained impossible before the referendum, while French President Francois Hollande said creditors should seek an accord before then.
27"We have to be clear. An accord is for right now, it will not be put off," Hollande said.
28Menelaos Hadjicostis and Gregory Katz in Athens, Geir Moulson in Berlin, Angela Charlton and Lorne Cook in Brussels contributed to this report.
