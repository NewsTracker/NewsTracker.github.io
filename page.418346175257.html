All Greek to you? A guide to Greece debt crisis lexicon

1All Greek to you?
2A demonstrator shouts slogans as she holds a placard reading 'YES to Greece, Yes to Euro' during a rally organized by supporters of the YES vote to the upcoming referendum in front of the Greek Parliament in Athens, Tuesday, June 30, 2015.
3NEW YORK -- Greece's debt crisis has spawned its own awkward argot.
4For those in the know, clunky terms like "Grexit" are shorthand, a quick way of saving a bundle of words.
5For everybody else, they're as mysterious as hieroglyphs.
6Wall Street types and economists have been wrestling with language to explain Greece's crisis since it started more than five years ago.
7First came the strained metaphors.
8Greece, the birthplace of drama, was the setting for a three-act tragedy.
9Now, it seems like every new twist in the story gets the prefix "Gr" slapped on it.
10Social media sites egg the process on.
11On Twitter, a bunch of words crammed together with a hash mark make messages easier to find.
12It's not the Greek crisis anymore, just (hash)grisis.
13"It's part of the gallows humor that the financial world is fond of," says Guy LeBas, chief fixed income strategist at Janney Montgomery Scott.
14"Sticking a `gr' in front of a word makes it slightly amusing."
15GREXIT: Made by combining Greece with exit.
16Used when discussing the possibility that Greece could choose to drop the euro instead of acceding to demands for more budget cuts from its European creditors.
17This word is guaranteed to draw at least a few blank looks if said aloud.
18But it's shorter than saying "Greece's departure from the group of 19 countries that share a single currency, the euro."
19GREXIDENT: An accidental Grexit.
20A default or a bank run could wind up forcing Greece to quit using the euro, even if it wanted to keep it.
21GREFERENDUM: Arguably the silliest of the bunch, created by adding the G from Greece to referendum.
22Took off on Twitter, when Greece's prime minister called for the public to vote on the terms offered by the country's lenders in exchange for more loans.
23Greek voters delivered a loud "No" on Sunday, opening up the latest stretch of uncertainty.
24GRIMBO: So does that vote mean Greece is going to be in or out of the currency union?
25At the moment, say Citigroup economists, they're sitting somewhere in between.
26And since we're talking about Greece, it's not limbo but "Grimbo."
27Good grief.
