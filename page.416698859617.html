13Their faces were gaunt, their hair tinted orange, their stomachs distended, all signs of malnutrition.
14They looked ragged, lost, shattered.
15But the girls were alive and free.
16They were among a group of 275 children and women rescued from Boko Haram extremists, the first to arrive at a refugee camp Saturday after a three-day journey to safety, brought by Nigeria's military.
17They came from the Sambisa Forest, the last stronghold of the Islamic extremists, where the Nigerian military said it has rescued more than 677 girls and women and destroyed more than a dozen insurgent camps in the past week.
18"Boko Haram killed the father of this child," Lami Musa said, cradling a 4-day-old girl with black curls glistening with sweat in the 104-degrees Fahrenheit heat.
19Tears came to her eyes when she was asked if she has other children: "Three of them. Boko Haram killed my husband and grabbed me. I have no idea where my other children are."
20She said she lost her family in an attack by the militants on her village of Lassa in December.
21The baby was born the day before the group set off from the Sambisa for a refugee camp in Yola, the capital of Adamawa state, crammed into the backs of rickety, open pick-up trucks.
22On the trip's first day, one military vehicle escorting the group exploded a landmine, wounding two soldiers, according to a soldier traveling with them.
23He spoke on condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to speak to journalists.
24Soldiers on foot then swept the road ahead of the convoy, he said, so it took three days to travel potholed roads for the 200 miles southwest to Yola.
25She's among several dozen in the group taken first to the clinic at the refugee camp, set up in an unused boarding school.
26As night crept in, the camp is lit by a generator that powered the clinic and a bulb in a tree under which the military handed over the females to the National Emergency Management Agency at a brief ceremony.
27For nearly a year, Nigeria's President Goodluck Jonathan had been promising to bring home all the people kidnapped by Boko Haram home - especially 219 schoolgirls abducted from their boarding school in the town of Chibok.
28That mass kidnapping a year ago outraged many around the world.
29It still was not clear Saturday whether any of the students who have come to be called "the Chibok girls" are among the newly freed.
30The military has spent days "processing" them and trying to identify them.
31Now they will get medical and psychological care to begin their rehabilitation, said Air Commodore Charles Otegbade, the emergency agency's director for search and rescue.
32The women were exhausted, too traumatized to realize they were safe, or be questioned about their experiences under Boko Haram.
33They lined up for tea, water and a stew of baobab leaves.
34Some women shot at their rescuers and were killed, as Boko Haram used them as an armed human shield for its main fighting force.
35Nigerian soldiers were shocked when women opened fire on troops who had come to rescue them in the village of Nbita last week, The Associated Press was told by a military intelligence officer and a soldier who were at the scene.
36The women killed seven soldiers and 12 of the women were killed, they said.

41Nigerian troops ran away before their advance, complaining they were not given enough ammunition or food to fight, and leaving civilians with no defense against an uprising that killed as many as 10,000 people last year.
42Some 1.5 million people have been driven from their homes.
