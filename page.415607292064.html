Liberia reports first Ebola case in weeks

Visual effects artist's Hollywood path began in Albania, with detour in Kosovo

1Liberian Information Minister Lewis Brown said that a 44-year-old woman had been transferred to the ELWA Ebola treatment unit in the capital Monrovia after testing positive for the virus.
2"Initial suspicion is that she may have contacted the virus through sexual intercourse with a survivor," the minister said.
3Liberia had not reported any new cases for a few weeks.
4However, health officials have warned that even after areas are declared free of the disease new cases are possible due to sexual transmission.
5A spokesman for Medecins Sans Frontieres, which runs the unit, confirmed that a patient had tested positive at a transit centre in the government-run Redemption Hospital in Monrovia.
6The Ebola outbreak, which began in eastern Guinea over a year ago, is the worst on record and has killed more than 10,200 people, mostly in Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone.
7Guinea has recently reported a rash of new cases of the disease, which had appeared to be on the wane.
8In Liberia, the government had hoped to declare the country Ebola-free next month, 42 days after the last patient tested negative for a second time, which would mark double the length of the virus's incubation period.
9However, while the disease typically takes 15 to 21 days to run its course, traces of Ebola can remain in semen for around two months after recovery, so transmission by sexual intercourse would still pose a risk, health officials say.
10Brown said that once the new case was detected on Friday, surveillance teams had been deployed to the woman's neighbourhood of Caldwell in Monrovia -- not far from the last recorded cluster of cases in the St Paul's Bridge suburb -- to track down people who had come into contact with her.
11Liberia had previously gone for 16 days without any new infections, according to the World Health Organization (WHO).
12The government put the figure at 27 days without cases.
13It released its last known patient on March 5.
14Monrovia became the epicentre of the outbreak in the middle of last year but since then hundreds of millions of dollars in aid and the deployment of U.S. troops have helped officials control the spread of the virus.
1Tanks clattered.
2Paramilitaries roamed.
3Kuke had spent days translating for journalists.
4He had escaped death at least once, and now, with a bump of cash in his pocket, he hailed a battered cab at the Kosovo border and headed south.
5He arrived in Tirana, Albania, hours later, stepping into the house of his father, a well-known director.
6Although he spoke six languages, Kuke had been aimless before the war.
7He partied too much and drifted through the cafes of Europe's poorest country.
8But upon his return from Kosovo in 1999, he spent long hours in his father's studio and grew fascinated by the art of special effects.
9"You had to teach yourself. There was no Internet, no way to learn it," said Kuke, a child of communism who embraced an emerging democracy by shaving question marks in his hair and binge-reading Jack Kerouac.
10"Whoever went abroad brought a book back on technology."
11Kuke today is creative director and special-effects supervisor at Mind Over Eye studio in El Segundo.
12He was part of a team nominated last year for an Emmy for the documentary "Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey."
13He has worked on big-budget films, including "The Polar Express" and "Superman Returns," commercials for Jaguar and Chrysler, and music videos for Britney Spears and Beyonce, whom Kuke described as "her own perfectionist."
14Countless roads lead to Hollywood.
15Many are straight shots down interstates, some squiggle through the heartland, and others are curious odysseys spanning cultures and nations.
16Few arrive at fame, but livelihoods are built, passions sated.
17Kuke landed here 14 years ago with the promise of a green card and acceptance into Gnomon School of Visual Effects not far from Paramount Pictures.
18He moved in with his best friend from back home, Dorian Ahmeti, a poet and film student.
19Hollywood parses success in uneven degrees.
20Actors and celebrities can become global brands, but most of its workers, like Kuke, make their names in smaller, specialized worlds.
21They are the unseen forces behind the spectacle: artists, designers, writers, programmers, gaffers and armies of others whose ingenuity propels ideas, scripts, music - and often whole franchises - into the mass consciousness.
22"This business is like any other. It has its dirty laundry, but if you work hard you can make it," said Kuke, 39, who is skilled in live-action and animation.
23"I'm not a special-effects director racking up Oscars. I'm respected in my profession for what I do, and I think I'm good."
24"He's a phenomenal hybrid," Jennifer Chavarria, head of production at Mind Over Eye, said of Kuke.
25"He's a great artist and can visually conceptualize. He's extremely passionate and driven. There's a certain honesty in his passion that is rare in this industry."
26Kuke's journey has had its dark parts and ragged stretches.
27Years ago, he told a reporter he was traveling with in Kosovo that one day they'd meet in L.A. Several months ago they did.
28Kuke smiled and said, "Man, it's been a while. A lot's happened."
29The two sat in a downtown tavern and talked.
30It was a warm night and Kuke's sleeve was rolled up, revealing a tattoo on his forearm written in his mother tongue: "We are getting ready for the new crusades. We are captains of starships."
31Kuke is lean, speaks in whispers, moves like candlelight; he has combed-back hair, an on-again, off-again beard and the sly charm of a Balkan cardsharp.
32He has taken to L.A., flitting through traffic on a Kawasaki motorcycle, a satchel slung over his shoulder.
33He looks to his homeland with a vagabond's nostalgia.
34Albania is a land of clans and mountain myths.
35Kuke grew up in the capital, Tirana, a city of drizzle and fascist-inspired architecture that drew in communists, gangsters and provincial men with outsized ambitions.
36Albania was Europe's most hermetic nation, led for decades by Enver Hoxha, an invasion-fearing former partisan who built hundreds of thousands of army bunkers that rose like gray blisters across the land.
37Hoxha died in office in 1985 and the ensuing years were marked by a shift from state control to capitalism that culminated in a series of pyramid schemes that bankrupted peasants and professionals and sparked an uprising.
38Kuke's father, Pali, who accepted a loan from a tribal baron to buy studio equipment imported from Germany, was known for documentaries and television shows.
39"It took us five years to pay back the loan," said Kuke.
40His mother, Zerina, was a TV variety host and a book editor.
41"There were always books around." he said.
42"I wrote poetry in high school and published an underground newspaper. I was also into sci-fi and fantasy."
43He eventually won a scholarship to the American University in Bulgaria in the unsettled era that followed the end of the Cold War.
44"Angst, that was our lives," he said.
45"Generation X-type bull. No money. My room was a library of stolen books. We were out every night. I started using heroin and by my third year in college ended up failing four out of five classes. I lost my scholarship and returned to Tirana."
46He worked for his father, designing computer graphics and conjuring "spinning globes" on TV shows.
47Kuke had known film and television since he was a child.
48When he was 10, he auditioned and won the lead in a "communist family movie" but didn't take to acting.
49His favorite boyhood scent, he said, was not the spotlight but the "dust burning off the back of overworked tape decks and studio racks."
50Kuke was 24 when war broke out in neighboring Kosovo, another in a string of unresolved territorial disputes from the dissolution of the former Yugoslavia.
51Journalists streamed into Tirana to pick up supplies, fixers and translators before heading north into the mountains.
52It was good money for the locals, especially multilinguists like Kuke.
53He hired on with two U.S. journalists, following tanks into villages, stepping past mass graves, watching tracers streak the night.
54The images have bothered him ever since.
55"I've tried to block that out," he said.
56"But it's like a film in the back of my memory."
57Upon his return from Kosovo, Kuke stayed busy with studio work.
58Despite his lack of training in anatomy and color theory, he was accepted as a part-time student at the Gnomon school.
59He received his visa on Sept.
6010, 2001.
61"The next day my dad woke me up," he said.
62"He told me, 'You won't believe what's happened.'" Two weeks later, Kuke was on a plane to Los Angeles, where for a century foreign artists and filmmakers had been welcomed by Hollywood.
63He moved in with his friend in Valencia.
64"Ergin was a scrawny guy who smoked like a chimney," said Yas Koyama, a fellow student at Gnomon who later started a special-effects company with Kuke.
65"He's artistic and has a great eye. He always had a knack for visual effects, color correction and finalizing shots."
66School tuition and other expenses rose to $40,000.
67Gnomon couldn't grant Kuke an extended student visa because he wasn't enrolled full time, so even though he spoke perfect English, he signed up for a Korean-English language school to get one.
68After graduation from Gnomon, he worked at Sony for two years, most of it spent on the special-effects team for "The Polar Express."
69He was hired by Rhythm & Hues Studios in 2005 and spent six months of 12-hour days to perfect special effects on five shots that amounted to about 15 seconds of screen time for "Superman Returns."
70Kuke grew restless.
71"I was a prisoner of the visa," he said.
72"Whoever I worked for held my visa. But I wanted to be out on my own."
73Retracing the past was like pulling out a long ago map of blurred lines and indelible symbols.
74Kuke paused.
75His eyes wandered and came back.
76He had stopped using heroin before he left Albania, a promise to his father.
77But the drug stayed part of his life in unexpected, tragic ways, the kind of ways people find difficult to fit into the narrative of who they've become.
78"The only option for a green card was a fake wedding," he said.
79"I met a girl online. We had been dating for about two months. She agreed to marry me so I could get a green card. We were wed in a strip mall in the city of Industry. We had fake flowers and lollipop rings. She was a photographer and ex-junkie. She was a tortured soul.... I went with her to police stations and rehabs. But I couldn't take it anymore. We divorced."
80Kuke also lost his best friend, Ahmeti, the countryman he shared an apartment with.
81"He didn't make it in L.A. and he returned to Albania's gloom and doom," said Kuke.
82"He went back to the bad ways. He became self-destructive, and he died. He was a mad poet. He was epic. Something in him gave up."
83Kuke pushed his hair back and looked ahead.
84In 2006, he and Koyama started Angst Vfx, a visual-effects company.
85Angst was a play on words; shorthand for "angstrom," a unit to measure wavelengths, that also described the anxiousness around a venture founded in Kuke's bedroom with two computers and a few USB sticks.
86Their first big job was for McDonald's, followed by music videos.
87The company moved to an office in Venice, but the recession hurt business and Angst folded in 2009.
88Kuke said he was free of obligations.
89He decided to take a trip across his adopted land.
90He and the woman he had fallen in love with, Allie Nelson, packed up a motorcycle and drove 16,000 miles in 41 days, traveling the four corners of the U.S., from the glacier region of Montana to the Florida Keys.
91The couple returned and Kuke signed on with Mind Over Eye, a multiplatform media company where surfboards and guitars hang on the walls and the staff works with drones and 3-D augmented reality and things that blip and buzz in a long building that has the blue-glow aura of the inside of a submarine.
92Kuke has lately been concentrating on "anthem films" - three- to four-minute clips for car companies that feature sleek men and women, shiny automobiles, cityscapes and mountain vistas.
