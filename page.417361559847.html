Greek exit would change nature of euro - Moody's

1A Greek exit from the euro would not mark a return to the debt crisis of 2012, a senior Moody's rating analyst has said.
2But it would create risks of contagion and change the nature of the monetary union, which was supposed to be permanent.
3"We don't think that a Greek exit would be inconsequential," Kathrin Muehlbronner, vice-president of sovereign risk at Moody's told Reuters in an interview in Lisbon.
4Many analysts worry that Greece will be forced to leave the euro zone if it fails to reach agreement with its creditors and receive more funds.
5"A Greek exit would change the face and the nature of monetary union, which was supposed to be permanent and would then turn out not to be," Muehlbronner said.
6She added that Portugal would be at risk of contagion in the case of a Greek exit.
7She said the impact of a Greek exit would be limited by the European Central Bank's quantitative easing programme but might hit corporate operations.
8"It's unclear how it would play out," she said.
9"The sovereigns in a way are protected by the ECB's QE. It's less certain about bank funding and corporate funding and their ability to access markets."
10The ECB's programme has provided a "huge help" to the euro zone and will continue to have a large influence on interest rates until September 2016, when the plan ends.
11"So interest rates will remain anchored at very low levels, but they can now only go in one direction, which is up," she said.
12The ECB's programme has also reduced pressure on governments, she said.
13"With interest rates so low and funding conditions improving, the pressure has been taken off governments to pursue aggressive fiscal policy measures. That's clear for the whole euro zone," she said.
