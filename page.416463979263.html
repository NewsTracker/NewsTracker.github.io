Calls mount for a special session of New Mexico Legislature

1A growing number of cities are urging Gov. Susana Martinez to overcome an impasse and call a special session to consider funding more than $260 million in public improvement projects around the state.
2The capital outlay bill stalled on the final day of the legislative session in March and much finger-pointing has followed.
3Martinez initially resisted the call for a special session, saying she wanted to make sure the outcome would be different.
4Her office says it has been working with leaders from both parties to find a solution.
5Legislative leaders also have been talking, but there's no indication this week that any agreements have been reached.
6Farmington was the latest city to join the call this week, following dozens of mayors and business groups who have urged the governor to call lawmakers back into session.
7If a special session is called, the Legislature needs to have approved and the governor has to sign off on a capital projects bill no later than May 15, according to the Legislative Finance Committee.
8That's because the state is required to advertise the sale of bonds for 30 days and take into account time for other procedural details, all work on capital outlay must be finished by the end of this fiscal year on June 30.
9The capital outlay bill that started in the Senate called for nearly $300 million in spending.
10The House version called for nearly $265 million.
11Finance Committee documents show both versions financed about $212 million of the projects using severance tax bonds.
12The biggest difference was in the use of general fund dollars for some public projects, with $45 million in the Senate version and less than $9 million in the House version.
13Should the public projects bill not get hammered out in a special session, some state-operated facilities will sustain a hit for a second straight year.
14Money for improvements and equipment for the veterans' home in Truth or Consequences, hospitals, juvenile detention centers and other state facilities was left out of a nearly $400 million capital outlay bill approved last year.
15Late discussions between the governor's staff and lawmakers that year led to more than $71 million being shifted from those state facilities to various water projects, according to a post-session fiscal review.
