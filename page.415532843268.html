24What made you want to work for women's rights?
25Memory: In my community in southern part of Malawi the tradition is that once a girl reaches puberty, you go to an initiation camp where we are taught how to be a woman - how to satisfy a man.
26As part of that you go through a sexual initiation with a man.
27Memory: I did not.
28This was a hard decision.
29My family and friends were calling me a stubborn little girl because it felt to them like I was embarrassing the family.
30But for me it was a life decision.
31I knew that some girls come back pregnant, they get married, they cannot go to school, and if the men run away from their responsibility the girls are left on their own with the children.
32That was not for me.
33But when my younger sister reached puberty, she went to the camp.
34She ended up getting pregnant and had to marry to the person who impregnated her.
35She was 11.
36This is what I saw and what I wanted to change.
37What are you doing to help make change happen?
38Memory: I had the idea to put up posters in my neighborhood offering free lessons to the adolescent mothers.
39And 20 girls joined the class.
40That led to my working with Let Girls Lead to help create networks for girls and advocating to help stop child marriage.
41... So if you ask me what is it like to be a teenager here, it was a struggle.
42You get anxious as adolescence approaches because you know what you're going to go through.
43Achie, tell us about your life in Addis Ababa.
44Achie: I live in a nice neighborhood, and go to a good school, but this is not the life that many young girls in Ethiopia have.
45Early marriage is also a problem in my country.
46There are traditional views about women, and they are not expected to go to school.
47There is also female genital mutilation.
48In my family there is nothing like that, but I would volunteer in organizations [to tutor] and I would talk to girls and hear their stories.
49Listening, you just have a feeling of how heavy a burden they are carrying and you cannot be quiet about it.
50When they share with you what they have experienced, you feel part of it and you want to act on it.
51Achie: I used to be a really shy girl and even if an opportunity was in front of me I would underestimate myself and not do it.
52That is how I was until I was 14.
53At that time, I was working as a summer volunteer tutoring children from ages 5 to 16, and even though I was among the youngest of the volunteers, I was asked to lead one of the programs as a school coordinator.
54I was afraid, but I said yes, and it was my best decision.
55I opened up.
