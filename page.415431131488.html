Ukraine's grinding war stains innocence of childhood

1Like many 7-year-olds in eastern Ukraine, he has trouble recalling a time before the war.
2"They've always been shooting," he says, vigorously scratching with the brightest of pencils.
3Yelena Nikulenko, the director of the children's home in the rebel-held town of Khartsyzk, says kids like Seryozha have been let down twice.
4First orphaned or abandoned by their parents, they were then dumped by their new families when the Ukrainian government stopped paying benefits to foster families in separatist-controlled areas.
5"On top of that, you have the war, the shelling, the fear," Nikulenko says.
6"It will be a scar for the rest of their lives, that's for sure."
7The conflict that erupted in Ukraine last year between government troops and Russian-backed separatists has claimed at least 6,000 lives and displaced nearly 1.8 million people.
8The United Nations Children's Fund estimates that 1.7 million children on both sides of the front line have been harmed through lack of proper shelter, nutrition, medicine or schooling.
9Children struggle to understand what is going on around them, and why.
10Fighting has abated drastically since a new cease-fire came into effect last month, but the suffering, loneliness and terror remain.
11In the government-held town of Popasna, 70 kilometers (45 miles) north of the children's home, only a few people walk along deserted streets between apartments gutted by rocket fire, a grim contrast to the days when the town bustled with 30,000 people.
12One of those destroyed homes belongs to Tatyana Belash, who has now taken shelter in a basement with her 3-year old daughter, Zlata.
13As the adults talk politics, Zlata, a shy girl with blonde pigtails, darts around the basement cluttered with battered mattresses.
14Asked about the shelling, Zlata shies away and seeks comfort in stroking her cat.
15"When we first came here, she kept saying: 'Let's go home!'" Belash says.
16"I couldn't explain to her that we couldn't go home because there was fighting going on."
17Children in the Ukraine-controlled village of Chermalyk play war and scuttle in and out of the craters made by falling Grad rockets.
18As 11-year old Tolik Tokar shimmies into one crater, his head disappears from view.
19Then he raises his head and pretends to shoot at the baddies: the separatists.
20The boys take aim with make-believe guns fashioned from sticks - but Tokar has seen shooting first-hand, not just in play.
21"When shelling was raging, we went to check it out, and then they opened fire on me," Tokar says, stammering as he tells his tale.
22"The bullets tore through the cloth on my shoulder here and flew past."
23A few dozen kilometers away on the rebel side, children play the same games - but with roles reversed.
24There, Ukrainian soldiers are the bogeymen, with "Nazi" one of the favorite slurs.
25The 22 children under Nikulenko's tutelage, in Khartsyzk, are some of the most vulnerable anywhere in the war-wracked region, and she asks that they be identified only by their first names.
26Before fighting began, the home served as a shelter for children rescued from the streets, or seeking respite from dysfunctional families.
27More have been abandoned in recent months.
28Veronika, a freckled and gap-toothed 6-year-old, smiles and pulls down on her red-checked dress as she recalls life before the war - the visits to the amusement park and zoo, her mother's home cooking.
29She even has fond memories of her father, who returned home after a stint in prison for slashing her mother's shoulder with a knife.
30Since then, her father enlisted with the rebel army and her mother left her at the children's home.
31Veronika relies on her own inner strength to ward off the terror brought on by war.
