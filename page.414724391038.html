'Mosul Eye' Claims City 'Quietly Slipping Away' From IS

US: Islamic State fighters killed by Iraqi forces

1The account of life in Mosul was posted on the Mosul Eye Facebook page on February 13.
2Mosul Eye is the pseudonym of a local historian who has been secretly documenting IS's activities in Mosul since the militants overran the city in June.
3While it is not possible to verify the blogger's identity, Iraq watchers believe the accounts are credible.
4According to Mosul Eye, IS's recent murder of Kasasbeh was "the main direct factor" -- though there have been others -- that led to the significant change in the attitude of religious Mosul residents toward the extremist Sunni group.
5Kasasbeh was captured by IS on December 24 during a bombing raid, after his F-16 war plane developed mechanical problems and crashed.
6A video showing Kasasbeh being burned alive in a cage was released by IS on February 3.
7Activists in the Syrian town of Raqqa reported that IS showed the immolation video on large screens in the city.
8The objections being expressed by Mosul residents toward IS are toward the militants' theory and application of Islamic Shari'a law, Mosul Eye said.
9"The big question people are asking today in Mosul is how [IS] is well-correlated to true Islam," Mosul Eye reported.
10When IS first took over Mosul, local people were afraid to get involved with the militants or even mention them, but that has changed, according to Mosul Eye.
11"Nowadays, people talk openly in the market about [IS] and its cruelty," Mosul Eye said.
12While talking about religion had become almost taboo in the Iraqi city over the past months, the burning of Kasasbeh led people to question IS's interpretation of Shari'a.
13Mosul Eye quotes a 25-year-old university student, named only as Samir, who said that he did not sleep the night that Kasasbeh was burned alive.
14"The idea that Allah, the merciful God I learned about in my childhood, is giving the pretense and justification to kill in his name, has scared me a lot," Samir was quoted as saying.
15Samir said that he told everyone that the immolation of Kasasbeh was "not an act of Islam."
16"I went to the nearby mosque...and I talked to the imam there. I asked him about the legitimacy of burning. The imam kept silent and did not want to give me an answer.... I left the mosque knowing that this is the last time I'll ever enter the place," Samir said.
17According to Samir, an IS imam gave the sermon in the mosque the next day and told the congregation that burning was legitimate and that God had authorized it.
18Mosul Eye described Samir as "one among a small but very significant population in Mosul who was struck by [IS's] brutality."
19It is not only in Mosul that local people have expressed resentment and anger about the burning of Kasasbeh.
20Residents of other IS-controlled areas have demonstrated similar views, as have people from elsewhere in Syria and Iraq.
21A young man from the Syrian city of al-Bab told Radio Free Iraq that the burning of Kasasbeh has resulted in discontent among the town's residents.
22In response, IS has tried to justify the punishment by distributing leaflets that cite the Islamic scholar Ibn Taymiyyah and some Koranic verses.
23Radio Free Iraq also spoke to a young woman from the Idlib countryside who described the burning of Kasasbeh as a "criminal act."
24Some of IS's own clerics were reportedly opposed to the burning alive of Kasasbeh.
25One cleric, a Saudi national, faces trial by an IS Shari'a court for voicing his objections to the immolation of the Jordanian pilot in al-Bab, according to the U.K.-based group Syrian Observatory for Human Rights.
26Another shift in the attitude of Mosul residents to IS is regarding the militants' presence in the city.
27When the gunmen first took over Mosul, locals thought that the group would never leave, but now "people are convinced that [IS] is only staying for so long and their departure from the city is inevitable," Mosul Eye wrote.
28While residents initially fled from Mosul, now they are determined to stay and say that the militants are the ones who should leave, according to Mosul Eye.
29Mosul residents are engaging in forms of protest against the militants, including by refusing to attend schools and the university.
30In particular, parents prevented their daughters from attending school where they would be "under the control of [IS's] barbaric, uncivilized insurgents," Mosul Eye said.
31Another sign of rebellion is that some Mosul residents are also smoking cigarettes, a practice that IS had banned.
32However, the militants failed to stop cigarettes from reaching Mosul and "smokers no longer find it difficult to get their cigarettes," the blogger wrote.
33Mosul Eye concludes on a positive note: "The city of Mosul is quietly slipping away from" IS.

2John Kirby, the Pentagon spokesman.
3Kirby said an estimated 20-25 Islamic State militants were involved in the attack on al-Asad air base in Iraq's Anbar province.
4He said the attack was led by "at least several" suicide bombers, some of whom managed to detonate their bombs and others were killed by Iraqi troops.
5"Early indications are that yes, some of them did detonate their vests, detonate themselves," he said.
6"And then they were followed by roughly something on the order of 15 or so other fighters."
7It appeared that most, if not all, of the militants were wearing Iraqi uniforms, Kirby said.
8No Iraqi or U.S. troops were killed or wounded, Kirby said, and no US troops were involved in the gunfight.
9Kirby said Islamic State fighters had taken control of al-Baghdadi, a town near the al-Asad air base.
10He said this represented "the first (time) in at least a couple of months, if not more, where they have had any success in taking any new ground."
11It was not clear whether the attackers at al-Asad managed to penetrate the perimeter of the base, which is a sprawling series of compounds.
12"Information is still coming in," he said, that may clarify some details.
