Oklahoma football players denounce Sigma Alpha Epsilon

Obama Discusses Ferguson With Jimmy Kimmel

1With the blessing of their coach, Bob Stoops, the players skipped practice Thursday to hold a silent protest against the bigotry tarnishing their school.
2"I think this is bigger than practice and these guys have demonstrated that, and whether it makes us better or not is irrelevant in their eyes and in mine," said Stoops, who has lost at least one star high school recruit over the controversy.
3The team applauded the swift expulsion of Levi Pettit, 20, and Parker Rice, 19, who were filmed directing the bigoted song Saturday night on a Sigma Alpha Epsilon party bus.
4But they said more heads need to roll.
5"The chant was not invented by the two that led it, but was taught to underclassmen by people of higher authority," reads a statement released by the team.
6"It is our passionately expressed desire as members of the football team for the leadership of SAE to be expelled, suspended, or otherwise disciplined severely."
7Rice, a freshman, issued an apology, partly blaming alcohol consumption for the bigoted behavior and confirmed, "Yes, the song was taught to us..."
8The SAE frat was booted from the Norman campus after the video surfaced of SAE frat brothers and their sorority guests singing about lynching and chanting the N-word.
9University President David Boren, who expelled Rice and Pettit on Tuesday, has vowed to discipline others once their identities are determined.
10On Thursday Boren announced that Clark Stroud, Vice President of Student Affairs for the university, will investigate each individual officer of the fraternity to "determine their level of responsibility and to make recommendations on appropriate disciplinary action."
11The football team, including coach Stoops, staged a protest, marching arm-in-arm onto Gaylord Family Oklahoma Memorial Stadium dressed in black clothes.
12Once they reached the end zone, they fanned out across the field and held a moment of silence.
13Senior Ty Darlington, a white member of the team, said the controversy has "undeniably brought our team closer together."
14But the player said racism is not an exclusive problem of the University of Oklahoma.
15"From personal experiences and from experiences of people around the nation, we know that this is a nationwide issue and that there are instances that happen like this every year. This one was caught on tape," Darlington said following the protest.
16"This is our chance to use our platform as student athletes to promote a positive change around the nation," he said.
17"Before we can change the whole nation, we want to change what's going on here."
18Stoops said he was proud of how his players have taken a unified stand against racism.
19"It wasn't just thrown together," Stoops said of the players' protest and statement.
20"These guys have put a lot of time into what the message is and how they can effect change and how they want to go about doing it."
21Meanwhile, a lawyer familiar with the troubled history of the SAE fraternity nationwide blamed overall lack of oversight and a culture of secrecy for the scandal that has enveloped the University of Oklahoma.
22"There is a quantum lack of mature management of fraternities," said Douglas Fierberg, who represents the family of a Brooklyn student who was killed during a hazing event by Cornell University's SAE chapter in 2011.
23"They manage and govern themselves, which means there is a tendency to get dangerously out of control," Fierberg told the Daily News on Thursday.
24Unlike campus dorms, which have paid adult staffers helping student leaders, fraternities are run almost exclusively by students, Fierberg noted.
25"So you have teenagers governing themselves behind closed doors," he said.
26"There's no balance check from someone without sworn allegiance to the fraternity."
27Fierberg represents the family of George Desdunes, who was found unconscious inside Cornell University's SAE house in February of 2011.
28The 19-year-old sophomore died after fraternity brothers bound his wrists and ankles with duct tape and zip ties and forced him to guzzle booze, according to a lawsuit the family filed.
29After Desdunes' death, the Cornell officials suspended the frat until at least 2016.
30SAE, which has roots in antebellum Alabama, was once named the nation's deadliest fraternity by Bloomberg News after nine people died at SAE events between 2006 and 2013 - more than any other fraternity in the country.
