Israel's Netanyahu struggles to govern with narrow majority

1FILE - In this Tuesday, July 14, 2015 file photo, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu speaks during a news conference at his Jerusalem office.
2On course to become the longest serving prime minister in Israeli history, Netanyahu's legacy has not been marked so far by bold measures of war and peace that defined his predecessors.
3But the hallmark mission of his professional career, keeping Iran from attaining a nuclear weapon, is seen as having taken a big hit with last week's U.S.-led nuclear deal.
4JERUSALEM (AP) - When Benjamin Netanyahu called early elections last year, he said his unwieldy coalition was untenable and that Israel needed a more stable government to carry out vital reforms.
5But two months into his new term in office, Netanyahu finds himself depending on an even narrower majority, susceptible to the extortion of practically any lawmaker.
6His political footing is so tenuous that he had to keep his defense minister from attending an international security conference in June because he couldn't spare the minister's vote.
7Another time, an ailing Cabinet minister was forced out of bed to save the government from the humiliation of losing a crucial late-night budget vote.
8Unruly backbenchers in Netanyahu's own Likud party have embarrassed him with their antics, and now he is facing growing criticism at home for failing to stop the international community's nuclear deal with Iran.
9Insiders say Netanyahu is in no danger of being toppled anytime soon, despite calls by opposition politicians that he step down over the Iran issue.
10However, it remains to be seen whether he will actually be able to get anything done in his fourth term in office.
11"He can survive. The question is what quality of life he will have," said Amit Segal, a political commentator for Channel 2 TV.
12"It's hard to see him pushing forward with anything big."
13Some would say that is just how Netanyahu likes it.
14On course to become the longest serving prime minister in Israeli history, his legacy thus far is one of longevity rather than bold measures of war and peace that defined his predecessors.
15His lobbying against the Iran nuclear deal - which he insists will not keep Tehran from attaining an atomic weapon - took a big hit with last week's U.S.-led agreement.
16The Iran issue has been a hallmark of his political career.
17The fact that Netanyahu is still standing is an impressive feat itself given his situation in the days leading up to the March 17 election.
18Trailing in opinion polls and facing a potential uprising within the Likud, Netanyahu rebounded and surged to victory, in part through fiery election-day rhetoric that shunned Palestinian statehood and railed against Israel's own Arab minority.
19By warning that Arabs were heading to the polls "in droves," Netanyahu scared passive right-wing voters into voting.
20But his victory came with a price.
21The votes Netanyahu earned in the final hours were largely sapped from his traditional nationalistic allies, leaving him with fewer coalition options.
22Former Foreign Minister Avigdor Lieberman's ultranationalist party then abruptly chose to join the opposition, leaving Netanyahu's government with the smallest of majorities, just 61 out of parliament's 120 members.
23To ensure the votes of his shaky majority, Netanyahu barred coalition members from traveling abroad without his approval.
24In the closest call yet, a Cabinet minister had to filibuster aimlessly in parliament while the coalition whip scrambled to gather supporters for a vote that could have technically toppled the government.
25Eventually, Netanyahu's closest ally, Cabinet minister Yuval Steinitz, was awoken from his sick bed and rushed in wearing slippers at 1:30 a.m. to save the day.
26Ironically, Netanyahu's defeat over the Iran nuclear deal could work in his favor.
27He has stated hopes of expanding his coalition and has been urging opposition politicians to join him in a show of unity as the country confronts the Iranian threat.
28Netanyahu has argued that the deal, by easing sanctions, will allow the Iranians to funnel newfound cash to hostile militant groups and continue to develop its nuclear infrastructure in the coming years.
29Netanyahu has already met with opposition leader Isaac Herzog following the nuclear deal, raising speculation that a unity government could be in the works.
30But Herzog threw cold water on that idea this week, rejecting a partnership with Netanyahu, despite their shared opposition to the Iran deal.
