Russia's Lavrov says fighting in east Ukraine abating

1Moscow stands accused by the West and Kiev of driving the separatist pro-Russian rebellion in east Ukraine, providing it with troops and arms, training and intelligence.
2Moscow denies this.
3Fighting has picked up in recent weeks despite a ceasefire in place since February, with hot spots including around the airport in the rebel stronghold of Donetsk and the village of Shyrokyne, en route to the city of Mariupol on the Azov Sea.
4"We are disquieted at the continued ceasefire violations, which have been very intense indeed in recent days. Now the situation is calming down. We will actively facilitate the strengthening of this tendency," Lavrov told a news conference.
5Ukrainian military spokesman Andriy Lysenko said in Kiev on Monday no Ukrainian servicemen had been killed or injured in fighting over the previous 24 hours.
6"The number of ceasefire violations by the enemy has fallen considerably," he told a news conference.
7The Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe, which is monitoring the ceasefire, said on Monday its staff were able to access Shyrokyne for the seventh consecutive day on Sunday, indicating relative calm in the area.
8But the truce is tenuous, with both sides on the ground in east Ukraine expecting fighting to pick up again.
9Western diplomats in Moscow, however, say that is unlikely before the May 9 military parade in Moscow to honour the 70th anniversary of the end of World War Two in Europe.
10They say Russian President Vladimir Putin would not want to further jeopardise a chance to show off Russia's military prowess and fuel national pride after most Western leaders decided to skip it over Ukraine.
