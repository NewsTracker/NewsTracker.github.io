7New Jersey agreed last month to settle the litigation for $225 million, prompting broad criticism that the Christie administration had taken pennies on the dollar to end the hard-fought case.
8Not only was the sum less than what the state offered to accept in 2012, but it also came at an odd moment: A ruling on damages was believed to be imminent, after an eight-month trial last year at which the state sought to prove its damages claim.
9Environmental advocates and some lawmakers have accused Mr. Christie, a Republican, of sacrificing the environment to help the state address more immediate shortfalls in its budget, allegations the Christie administration has vigorously rejected.
10On Thursday, the Assembly Judiciary Committee held a hearing on the proposed settlement, which still requires a judge's approval.
11Records of the settlement proposals are not public; the state official and others interviewed for this article who had knowledge of the talks spoke on the condition of anonymity because the talks were also not public.
12Whatever the political and legal analysis that led to the $225 million deal, the seeds of an agreement were planted years earlier.
13Familiar to drivers on the New Jersey Turnpike, the Linden refinery, known as Bayway, has reinforced the image of the state as almost irretrievably toxic.
14There, marshland adjacent to a creek was "now mostly covered with a tar of petroleum products or filled with other hazardous constituents and debris," according to a state environmental official quoted in a 2006 affidavit.
15He also cited 45 acres of "sludge lagoons," former tidal marshes that had been used as hazardous-waste disposal facilities.
16Exxon agreed in 1991 to clean up both the Bayway and Bayonne sites, subject to state approval, with "no cap" on what it would pay, as Mr. Christie recently pointed out.
17The company says that to date, it has spent about $260 million on those efforts.
18But the 1991 agreement did not block the state from suing for the loss of natural resources, which it did in 2004.
19A news release issued at the time said Exxon had "balked at the opportunity" to reach "a reasonable settlement" to resolve liability for the natural resource damage at the sites.
20The release added that the state's environmental commissioner, Bradley M. Campbell, had issued an administrative subpoena for information from Exxon about its "hundreds of potentially contaminated sites to ensure that the public is fully compensated for natural resource damage claims."
21The release cited nearly 1,000 gas stations and other sites.
22As the case proceeded, Exxon was held strictly liable, that is, regardless of fault, for potential restoration costs and damages to compensate for the public's "loss of use" of the natural resources.
23Exxon argued that no damages should be awarded.
24In spring 2008, the administration of Gov. Jon S. Corzine, a Democrat who was Mr. Christie's predecessor, made Exxon a settlement offer that the state valued at roughly $550 million (about $150 million in cash and $400 million in land restoration projects), according to the senior Christie administration official.
25Exxon rejected the proposal.
26The senior official said the 2008 offer served as a "framework" for how New Jersey officials would arrive at their 2012 settlement demand of $325 million.
27The Corzine administration proposal, however, was made before a ruling that bolstered the state's position on potential damages.
28In 2009, a judge rejected Exxon's arguments that New Jersey could only seek damages for contamination that had occurred after the 1970s-era law that authorized such suits was enacted.
29That meant the state could seek damages dating to when it claimed the contamination had begun: in the 1870s at Bayonne, and the early 1900s at Bayway.
30Exxon did win a ruling in 2009 that said it was not responsible for natural resource damages linked to physical modifications at the refinery properties.
31A few years would pass before the possibility of a settlement was broached again.
32During that time, the attorney general's office lost the only two natural resource damages cases it had taken to trial, strengthening Exxon's argument for New Jersey to settle.
33In April 2012, Exxon's lead lawyer, Theodore V. Wells Jr., met with Christopher S. Porrino, then an official in the attorney general's office, to lay out the company's position.
34Mr. Wells, co-chairman of the litigation department of the Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison law firm, had been hired in 2006.
35His argument was that any deal should fall significantly below $100 million, roughly the aggregate value of all of the state's natural resource damage settlements from 2004 to that point.
36He and his team offered data showing that the largest settlement reached by the state in such a case was $17 million, while the average of the 10 highest such deals was $6.3 million.
37At the meeting in August 2012, Exxon's team, which had litigated the case through three Democratic administrations, included a new face: George Gilmore, a lawyer and influential fixture of state Republican politics who had served on Mr. Christie's transition team.
38Mr. Gilmore had been retained by Exxon after the governor's election to assist in the case.
39He ended his relationship with the company before the trial began in 2014.
40The state's legal team included Mr. Porrino and John J. Hoffman, another top official in the attorney general's office.
41Both would later fill even more senior posts: Mr. Porrino as the governor's chief counsel and Mr. Hoffman as the acting attorney general.
42Mr. Wells said in the meeting that he considered the $325 million offer "outrageous," a person with knowledge of the discussions recalled.
43Mr. Wells largely repeated the arguments he had made before and said the company did not feel it was being treated fairly or consistently with how other major corporations had been treated in such cases.
44During these early talks, Exxon also indicated that it wanted any deal to resolve potential natural resource liability claims related to the gas stations and other sites, where, it asserted, contamination had been minimal.
45If that assertion proved to be true, state officials said, such claims could be included in a deal.
46The Christie administration lawyers also made it clear that any settlement had to exceed $100 million for the state to take it seriously.
47Exxon countered later in 2012 with an offer of $20 million, which the state rejected.
48In January 2013, at a court hearing in Elizabeth, N.J., Allan Kanner, an outside lawyer retained by the state, told a judge that the parties had made offers, and that Mr. Porrino, who was present in court, had attended all of the meetings.
49"We just are at an impasse," Mr. Kanner said.
50In December 2013, Exxon raised its offer to $66 million, in cash and land, according to one person with knowledge of the settlement talks.
51The state countered with an offer of about $290 million, which also included land.
52In January 2014, as the damages trial was beginning, Mr. Porrino became Mr. Christie's counsel.
53In April, during the trial, Exxon raised its offer to $100 million.
54Exxon then raised its offer to $200 million, and as Mr. Wells and Mr. Kanner and their teams continued to fight in court, the settlement was negotiated by other lawyers.
55The final deal, under which Exxon agreed to pay $225 million, would also resolve New Jersey's claims related to the service stations and other facilities, which the state was now describing as "relatively minor," according to a news release from the attorney general's office.
56The state has not released a list of those sites.
57Mr. Hoffman said last week that had the state not settled the litigation, the matter would have been "litigated for many years to come with an uncertain outcome that could threaten the state's recovery in this case" and potentially jeopardize other significant natural resource damage recoveries the state was pursuing.
58Recently, Mr. Campbell, the former environmental commissioner, wrote an Op-Ed article in The New York Times that was critical of Mr. Porrino's role, saying the governor's counsel "inserted himself into the case, elbowed aside the attorney general and career employees who had developed and prosecuted the litigation and cut the deal favorable to Exxon."
59In response, the senior state official said last week that Mr. Porrino, while in the attorney general's office, had "significant involvement in trying to force Exxon Mobil into meaningful settlement talks."
60Later, the official said, Mr. Porrino, as Mr. Christie's counsel and working with others, "appropriately continued those efforts in his new role."
61At the Assembly hearing on Thursday, Mr. Hoffman and Bob Martin, the environmental commissioner, were absent: They had declined an invitation to testify, the committee chairman, John F. McKeon, said.
62One witness, Debbie Mans, executive director of NY/NJ Baykeeper, questioned Exxon's cleanup efforts.
63"Anyone who thinks that Exxon Mobil will complete a comprehensive cleanup of these sites is dreaming," she testified.
64An earlier version of this article misstated the time frame for which Exxon argued New Jersey could seek damages for environmental contamination.
65Exxon argued that damages could be sought only for contamination after the enactment of a 1970s-era law authorizing such suits, not before.
66Because of an editing error, a picture caption with an earlier version of this article misstated the status of the Bayway refinery in Linden, N.J. It was once owned by Exxon Mobil; that company no longer owns it.
