Mexico march marks 6 months since 43 students disappeared

1Protesters marked the six-month anniversary of the disappearance of 43 students with marches in Mexico City and other cities in Mexico and asked that elections scheduled for June in the southwestern state of Guerrero be suspended.
2Federal investigators say local police handed the students over to a drug gang, which killed them and incinerated their remains.
3Nearly 100 people have been detained in the case.

5A protester defaces a campaign election poster during a march in Chilpancingo, Mexico, Thursday, March 26, 2015.
6MEXICO CITY (AP) - Parents of 43 missing students marked the six-month anniversary of their disappearance with a march of a few thousand supporters Thursday, urging the country not to abandon them but drawing far smaller numbers than rallies last year.
7At the march's conclusion, Maria Elena Guerrero, the mother of a missing student, stood atop a stage and said her pain had turned to fury against Mexico's government in the months since her son disappeared.
8Earlier in the day parents and supporters demonstrated at the federal elections office in Mexico City to ask that voting scheduled for June in the southwestern state of Guerrero be suspended.
9Dozens of protesters delivered a letter to the office asking that the June 7 elections not move forward because people could be voting for politicians tied to drug trafficking, as was the case with former mayor of Iguala, Jose Luis Abarca, who remains in custody.
10Parents and their supporters later staged a protest march in the capital, while small bands of activists defaced campaign posters and burned a small bus in the Guerrero state capital of Chilpancingo.
11The students from a rural teachers college were last seen in Iguala.
12On Thursday, the Attorney General's Office issued a statement reiterating that the government had conducted a transparent and exhaustive investigation.
13It said 104 people had been detained in the case, including 48 from the Iguala police force.
14Only one victim's remains have been identified, however, and parents of the young men have continued to demand answers about the events of Sept.
15"We came to tell authorities and the Mexican government that as parents we cannot allow the elections," said Meliton Ortega, parent of a student.
16"They have been six months of torture, of suffering for us."
17Students from the Rural Normal School at Ayotzinapa went to Iguala on Sept.
1826 to collect money and hijack buses, a common practice, so that they could attend events in Mexico City.
19But police confronted them in Iguala, firing on the buses.
20Six people were killed.
21Federal investigators determined the police turned the students over to members of a drug gang who took them to a remote garbage dump near the town of Cocula, killed them, burned the bodies and threw the remains into a river.
