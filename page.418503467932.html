After all is said in theater attack trial, was Holmes sane?

1FILE - In this July 23, 2012 file photo, James Holmes, who is charged with killing 12 moviegoers and wounding 70 more in a shooting spree in a crowded theatre in Aurora, Colo., in July 2012, sits in Arapahoe County District Court in Centennial, Colo.
2Jurors in the Colorado theater shooting case soon will retreat into the largest jury room in the courthouse to determine whether Holmes was legally insane at the time of the killing spree.
3DENVER (AP) - They saw more than 1,500 photographs.
4They heard more than 250 witnesses.
5They listened to 10 psychiatrists.
6They handled three guns.
7Jurors in the Colorado theater shooting case will hear closing arguments and instructions from the judge this week.
8Then they'll retreat into an oversized jury room - the only one in the courthouse that can hold all the evidence - to decide what of all this matters as they determine whether James Holmes was legally insane when he killed 12 people at a crowded midnight movie premiere.
9Jurors have been deluged with information during nearly three months of testimony, and it's impossible to know how all of it will play into their deliberations.
10The only question they must answer is whether Holmes met Colorado's threshold for an insanity verdict, by suffering from mental illness so severe that it rendered him unable to tell right from wrong at the time of the shootings.
11Colorado law puts the burden of proof in these cases on the state: Prosecutors must show, beyond a reasonable doubt, that despite Holmes' mental disorder, he was capable of appreciating the wrongfulness of the shootings, which also wounded 58 people and left 12 others injured in the chaos that ensued.
12The hideous details of the attack, the heartbreaking survival stories, the signs of mental illness that Holmes showed well before the events of July 20, 2012 - they shouldn't matter.
13In legal terms, all the jury should focus on is whether the state has met its burden.
14But extraneous details that prosecutors and defense attorneys pile onto jurors often can make or break a case.
15"This was a terrible tragedy in which great harm was caused for large numbers of people. It's virtually impossible to divorce that question of insanity from its context," said Valerie Hans, a Cornell Law School professor who has studied juries and the insanity plea.
16"I really feel for jurors who have to listen to wrenching testimony and steel themselves and look at the law and see which legal option really is the best match."
17If they agree Holmes is insane, he would be committed indefinitely to a state mental hospital.
18If they convict him, he faces life in prison, or execution.
19A copy of Colorado's insanity statute will guide them in the jury room as they consider Holmes' mental state that night, analyzing his medical records, family history, police reports and journal.
20Four of the psychiatrists who testified were asked to test his sanity.
21Two declared him sane and two others declared him insane.
22Colorado allows jurors to question witnesses during trial, and they often did.
23A defense psychiatrist fielded more than 50 questions from jurors last week, about delusions, schizophrenia, mental illness and her methods for determining someone's sanity.
24Merely having a mental illness, or suffering from delusional thinking, does not mean someone is insane, she said.
25This hazy line between severe mental illness and legal insanity can be difficult to distinguish, especially for people without expert guidance.
26Hans did opinion surveys after John Hinckley Jr. was found not guilty by reason of insanity for trying to assassinate President Ronald Reagan in 1981.
27Many people had wildly exaggerated ideas about what the term meant.
28"We asked people what the legal definition was of legal sanity, and they just didn't know," she said.
29In Holmes' case, jurors have seen more than 70 shooting survivors, some in wheelchairs or missing limbs.
30They heard doctors diagnose Holmes with a spectrum of mental illnesses, from schizophrenia to full-blown psychosis.
31They were told he had a delusion that he could increase his own self-worth by murder.
32They thumbed through his spiral notebook, which contained elaborate plans for the massacre, along with incoherent ramblings and strange symbols.
33They handled Holmes' arsenal of weapons and body armor.
34They saw a jail video of him running head-first into walls.
