Nurse Treated For Ebola To Sue Texas Hospital

Five Qld greyhound trainers banned

1Nurse Nina Pham tells the Dallas Morning News that while she is Ebola free, she suffers residual effects from contracting the disease from a patient she cared for last fall at Texas Health Presbyterian Hospital Dallas.
2Pham's patient, Thomas Eric Duncan, was the first person in the United States diagnosed with Ebola.
3Duncan contracted Ebola in Liberia and later traveled to Dallas.
4Pham, 26, was the first person to contract Ebola on U.S. soil.
5Pham tells the paper in an interview published Saturday that she will file a lawsuit Monday in Dallas County against Texas Health Resources, the parent company of the Dallas hospital where she worked.
6She says the hospital lacked proper training and proper equipment.
7She also says the hospital violated her privacy - making her "a symbol of corporate neglect - a casualty of a hospital system's failure to prepare for a known and impending medical crisis."
8Texas Health Resources responded to the article with a statement from spokesman Wendell Watson.
9"Nina Pham bravely served Texas Health Dallas during a most difficult time. We continue to support and wish the best for her, and we remain optimistic that constructive dialogue can resolve this matter."
10In October, Texas Health Resources issued a a full-page letter published in the Dallas Morning News saying it was "deeply sorry" for missing the Ebola diagnosis of Thomas Duncan.
11Pham wants unspecified damages for physical pain and mental anguish, medical expenses and loss of future earnings.
12She was treated at the National Institutes of Health in Bethesda, Md.
13Before heading back to Texas, Pham met President Obama at the White House, where he embraced her.
14She had hugged Dr. Anthony Fauci, director of the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases at NIH, earlier in the day.
15The embrace was as much to combat the stigma surrounding the deadly virus as to celebrate her being free of Ebola.
1The five were previously issued with show cause notices as to why they should not be warned off.
2Reg Kay, Debra Arnold, James Harding, Tom Noble and Tony McCabe received the life bans on Monday night after the Queensland All Codes Racing Industry Board considered the matters.
3Greg Stella made a verbal submission to the show cause notice and his case has been referred back to stewards while Michael Chapman's case has been adjourned to consider further evidence.
4Queensland Racing boss Kevin Dixon said his board considered the five trainers unworthy of being involved in the industry.
5"As a board we determined the actions of these individuals proved they should not be considered fit and proper persons to continue to be involved in the greyhound industry," Mr Dixon said in a statement.
6"The conduct we saw from these people in the evidence provided to us is not only against the rules of greyhound racing, it is deplorable by its very nature."
7Earlier on Monday, the Queensland government announced it will conduct an independent review of the state's greyhound industry.
8Racing Minister Bill Byrne says the $3 million review, to be fully funded by the racing industry, would be headed by barrister Alan McSporran SC.
9He said the review, to take about three months, would determine how misconduct that led to the suspension of trainers allegedly involved in live-baiting occurred without earlier detection.
