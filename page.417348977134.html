Why Blatter will win FIFA election again

1FIFA's 209 members are expected to elect Sepp Blatter to a fifth term as president on Friday, despite a string of scandals including the indictment of nine soccer officials this week by the US government.
2But why does Blatter enjoy such loyalty?
3Thanks to the vast increase in television payments during his 17 years of heading the organisation, Blatter has redistributed billions back to national governing bodies and regional confederations.
4In addition to influxes of cash for their soccer programs, officials from even the smallest territories have been able to attend FIFA events, stay at the world's finest hotels, eat in top restaurants, all while receiving large per diems.
5They also get to compete to stage dozens of global and regional tournaments.
6FIFA reported revenue for the 2011-14 cycle of $US5.72 billion ($A7.50 billion), and it distributed $US1.05 billion ($A1.35 billion) for development.
7FIFA said its Goal Programme funded 200 projects worth at least $US500,000 ($A653,500) each during the period.
8That money has made many FIFA officials intensely loyal to Blatter.
9Nations vote by secret paper ballot.
10A two-thirds majority is necessary on the first ballot, and a simple majority on succeeding ballots.
11While Europe has the world's wealthiest leagues and clubs, it has not held FIFA's top spot since 1974, when Brazil's Joao Havelange ousted England's Stanley Rous 68-52.
12Havelange held office for 24 years.
13Sepp Blatter, a 79-year-old Swiss native who had been Havelange's top aide, defeated Sweden's Lennart Johansson, then the president of UEFA, 111-80 on the first ballot in 1998.
14There were accusations even before the election that Blatter's associates were buying votes.
15A majority of the Union of European Football Associations is expected to back Jordan's Prince Ali bin Hussein - UEFA president Michel Platini estimates at least 45.
16A former French national team captain, Platini replaced Johansson as UEFA president in 2007 and has been viewed as a possible Blatter successor.
17Platini opposed Blatter but announced last August that he declined to run against him, saying "now is not my time, not yet."
18He met with Blatter on Thursday and urged him to resign.
19Long considered a soccer backwater, CONCACAF gained influence during the presidency of Trinidad and Tobago's Jack Warner, who was among those indicted this week.
20The 1994 World Cup in the US boosted media and corporate attention, but leagues in Mexico and the US trail European counterparts in revenue.
21Sunil Gulati, the US Soccer Federation president in 2006, won North America's spot on FIFA's executive committee two years ago in an 18-17 vote over Mexico's Justino Compean and is viewed as a reformer.
22But Gulati has cautioned that views differ around the world as to what constitutes necessary reform.
23Gulati says the US and Canada will vote for Ali, but most of CONCACAF is expected to support Blatter.
24At a CONCACAF meeting last month, Dominican Football Federation President Osiris Guzman compared Blatter to Moses, Abraham Lincoln, Winston Churchill, Martin Luther King, Jesus and Nelson Mandela.
25Blatter has strong backing in Africa, where few nations have powerful leagues.
26The Confederation of African Football issued a statement Thursday reiterating its support for Blatter.
27When CAF President Issa Hayatou of Cameroon ran against Blatter in 2002, he lost 139-56 but Africa has since staged its first World Cup, in South Africa, in 2010.
28Ali may be from Asia, but that doesn't mean he will have great support from his own federation, which issued a statement Thursday backing Blatter.
29Qatar's Mohamed bin Hammam, president of the Asian Football Confederation from 2002-11, ran against Blatter four years ago, then withdrew after accusations he helped arrange bribes to Caribbean voters.
30One of its affiliates Australia have indicated they will support Ali in light of the recent controversy.
31The continent's soccer is dominated by Brazil and Argentina, and the confederation is expected to support Blatter.
32With only 10 votes, South America has the fewest of any confederation.
33It has far greater influence on FIFA's executive committee, where it has three of 25 votes.
34A small and relatively weak confederation became smaller and weaker when Australia left in 2006 and joined the AFC, leaving the remaining small nations loyal to Blatter and the development money whose distribution he has led.
35A statement in January said all 11 nations planned to vote for Blatter.
36Amid the humiliation brought on FIFA following a high-profile corruption investigation, the sporting body holds the opening ceremony for the 65th FIFA congress.
