EU adds 19 more names to sanctions list over Ukraine conflict

North Korean escapee accepts blame for hurting defectors' credibility

1The EU published the list, which also included nine entities, on Monday, one week after the EU foreign ministers decided on the issue.
2There was a week's lapse in the publishing of the list because of the negotiations which led to an agreement between Ukraine and Russia last Thursday.
3The additions bring the total of individuals hit with a travel ban and asset freeze to 151 while 37 entities are now also hit with restrictive measures.
4The latest batch includes Russian Deputy Defense Minister Anatoly Antonov, an outspoken critic of Ukraine, two more high-level officials linked to the military, and two state Duma officials who backed the annexation of Ukraine's Crimean Peninsula last year.
5Among the officials from the Duma, Russia's lower house of parliament, was Iosif Kobzon, who has been supportive of the separatists, and is also well known as a crooner.
6The entities targeted consist of eight armed separatist groups and one Russian movement backing the separatists in eastern Ukraine.
7A cease-fire in eastern Ukraine went into effect on Sunday but on Monday the two sides were accusing the other of violations.
1Shin Dong-hyuk, who shot to international fame with the publication of "Escape from Camp 14" by former Washington Post correspondent Blaine Harden in 2012, last month recanted key elements of his account of torture and subsequent escape from a North Korean prison camp.
2"Of course there's been damage," Shin told Reuters in his first multimedia interview since he admitted his story was not entirely truthful.
3"And I know a lot of people are criticizing me. But I did not have a choice."
4In official propaganda, North Korea has blasted Shin as a liar and a criminal.
5Shin, in his early 30s, is one of the best known defectors from the North and a key witness to the U.N. inquiry that issued an indictment of the North's rights abuses last year, leading to a U.N. resolution urging the referral of Pyongyang to an international criminal tribunal.
6Shin said he made a conscious decision in 2006 to omit parts of his story that he wanted to forget and hide.
7He said he had not had the courage to tell the full truth because his memories of torture by prison guards and confessions that led to the execution of his mother and brother were too painful.
8"I have heard all of the criticism, and I know I will continue to receive criticism," he said.
9"And it was so hard because of that, and that's why I thought about taking my own life."
10Shin now says he escaped twice from Camp 18, the second time making it to China where he was captured and sent back to the North, and to Camp 14, a much harsher facility, where he suffered painful torture.
11He then says he escaped from Camp 14.
12In his original account, he said he had lived all his life in Camp 14 until his escape.
13"Torture using fire was bearable, now I that I look back. But what I could not bear was pulling out the fingernails," Shin said in the interview.
14North Korea is one of the most secretive places in the world and it is extremely difficult to verify the accounts of defectors.
15Shin's admission last month led to concern in the defector community in South Korea that their credibility had been damaged.
16About 25,000 defectors from North Korea live in the South.
17Shin, who married recently and said he lives modestly in Seoul, also said he wants to renew fighting for improvement in rights conditions in North Korea, including taking part in any future trip by U.N. investigators to the North.
18"I have to be included, because the people who lived through the suffering know best."
19Harden, in an updated foreword to his book, said at the weekend the new disclosures may still not be the whole truth of Shin's story.
20"It seems prudent to expect more revisions," Harden said in the foreword he posted on his blog and will be printed in future editions of the book.
21Shin said: "There will be no more changes. Even if there's something I am keeping to myself, I won't be changing anything now ... the whole truth will come out when history proves everything about the pain suffered by the North Korean people."
