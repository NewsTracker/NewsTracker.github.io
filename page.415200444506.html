Kenya: Businessmen, ex-officials charged for fraud and abuse of office

Motherlode Blog: Letting Children Take Risks: When Does It Make You a Risky Parent?

1Eight suspects were charged with conspiracy to commit an economic crime for a scheme to defraud the government of $44.6 million in contracts to modernize police equipment, including computerizing police force operations, according to charge sheets from the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission.
2Two suspects, including a senator, were charged with abuse of office and breach of public trust for the procurement of communication services, said the sheets presented in court by public prosecutors.
3Seven suspects denied charges.
4The others weren't in court.
5Two businesses were also charged.
6In 2006, former presidential adviser on corruption, John Githongo, released documents alleging high level corruption in the issuance of 18 contracts dubbed the "Anglo Leasing scandal".
7The officials and businessmen were charged Wednesday for their alleged involvement in some of these contracts.
8An audit of 18 government contracts showed that all were awarded without seeking bids from competitors and suppliers were not required to submit invoices, delivery notes or certificates of completion of works, a local newspaper quoted then Controller and Auditor-General Evan Mwai as saying in 2006.
9Some of the security contracts were awarded to companies whose offices could not be traced at addresses indicated in the agreements, according to the report.
10Githongo has linked the failure to set up the security projects- which were part of the awarded contracts- to the country's struggle to contain extremist attacks.
11"If the Anglo Leasing security contracts that are still being investigated today 10 years later, hadn't been predated upon, hadn't been 'eaten', we'd (be) far more ready than we are for the potentially existential threat al-Shabab poses to Kenya's State," Githongo told The Associated Press by email from Stanford University, California, where he is a visiting scholar.
12Al-Shabab extremists from Somalia have carried out 129 attacks in Kenya since Oct. 2011 saying its retribution for the Kenyan troop presence in Somalia.
1We were all minding our own business, looking at the screens of our mobile devices, until the local news on a television there aired a story that united a group of strangers.
2It turns out the staff members and customers of that repair shop had a fair amount in common: We'd all been children, we were all parents, and we were all horrified by the sanctioning of two fellow Maryland parents for the crime of letting their kids (10 and 6 years old) walk home alone from the park.
3Danielle and Alexander Meitiv were found responsible for "unsubstantiated child neglect" after a two-month investigation by Montgomery County Child Protective Services.
4As far as the waiting room could tell, the finding means the Child Protective Services will keep a file on the family for many years.
5It also seems that the family could face repercussions if they do anything to catch Child Protective Services' attention, like letting their children walk unaccompanied again.
6Everyone in the waiting room started talking.
7We told stories.
8The estimator said he couldn't have been more than 7 or 8 when he and an older friend rode their bikes all the way from suburban Maryland to the zoo in Washington.
9It must have been nearly 10 miles each way.
10The rest of us nodded in understanding.
11Back in the 1970s and '80s, we'd disappear for hours as children.
12The front desk person said she used to climb trees and read for hours in the branches, unnoticed, content.
13My friends and I went through a phase where we'd go around collecting neighborhood road kill, burying our dead squirrels and birds with solemn funerals.
14Someone raised the subject of our own children.
15I recalled how my son described a recent snowstorm as one of the best days of his life.
16He and friends were outside for four hours; they jumped creeks, built forts, sledded, threw snowballs.
17It seemed as if everyone in the room had a snow story; we had a good time with those.
18And then one of the men offered a different kind of story.
19He'd been a single parent raising his son, and he couldn't afford child care.
20So from a pretty early age (the legal limit in Maryland) his boy got himself home from school, let himself in the apartment, and waited until his dad came home.
21He smiled then, at the memory of his boy.
22"He wasn't lonely, though, he was always outside, playing with his friends."
23Then the guy shook his head.
24"What would they do to me now if they saw him out there playing? Would they try to take him?"
25It's not an absurd question.
26A South Carolina mother was arrested last summer for leaving her child at a nearby park while she was at work.
27She also could not afford child care.
28I don't mean to scapegoat child service workers, who were no doubt just following their rules.
29But we need to recognize that well-intended laws aimed at protecting vulnerable children from real abuse and neglect can have unintended consequences, and one of those consequences is that parents will believe that letting kids be kids - the kinds of kids they themselves often were - will put their family in danger, even if the children themselves are at statistically little risk of anything but poor choices.
30(That stuff still gets back to parents via phone calls, texts and informative neighborhood list-serves - the latter, in fact, is how I heard that some of the snowball throwing that was part of my son's excellent day was not appreciated by all our neighborhood drivers. The children won't do that again.)
31My new waiting room acquaintances and I agreed that parents should be able to send their children to the park to play without fear from the authorities, just as our parents used to do.
32To us children of the 1970s and '80s, it's feeling a little personal.
33It's starting to seem as if someone's saying the way we were raised is no longer O.K. And speaking for a lot of us, we disagree.
34We liked our way.
35I've been sensing a healthy backlash against the kind of helicopter parenting that smothers a child and doesn't let him or her grow, learn from their mistakes, even figure out how to be bored.
36Unfortunately, while more and more parents seem to want to just say no to excessive hand-holding, that's clearly not the case with all our bureaucracies.
37As one fellow parent noted, looking wistfully out the repair shop window at the snow-covered landscape, "It's almost like they'd rather our kids sit in the basement and play video games for hours than wander outside."
38Here in Maryland, it does kind of feel like that.
39And it feels wrong.
