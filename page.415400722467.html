1Officers on duty who witnessed the March 4 incident wanted to arrest the agents and conduct sobriety tests, according to a current and a former government official familiar with the incident.
2But the officers were ordered by a supervisor on duty that night to let the agents go home, said these people, who spoke on the condition of anonymity to discuss the sensitive internal matter.
3The episode presents an early test for the Secret Service's new director, Joseph Clancy, who was appointed by President Barack Obama last month following a string of security lapses at the White House and other embarrassing missteps and had vowed to restore the agency's once-stellar reputation.
4Clancy on Monday directed that the inquiry be led by the Department of Homeland Security's inspector general, in part because the incident involves such senior officials, a law enforcement official said.
5Agency spokesman Brian Leary confirmed the investigation after questions from The Washington Post, saying the Secret Service was "aware of the allegations of misconduct involving two of our employees."
6He declined to specify which allegations are the focus of the inquiry.
7"If misconduct is identified, appropriate action will be taken based on established rules and regulations," Leary said.
8He added that the agency "will fully cooperate" with the inspector general's office.
9An administration official said that in "recent days," the White House had been "made aware of the allegations and the steps being taken by the Secret Service to ensure that the incident is thoroughly investigated."
10The agents under investigation are Mark Connolly, the second-in-command on Obama's detail, and George Ogilvie, a senior supervisor in the Washington field office.
11Connolly and Ogilvie declined to comment through the Secret Service spokesman.
12An agency official said the two employees had been moved to "non-supervisory, non-operational assignments."
13They were not put on administrative leave, which has been a typical procedure when agents are under investigation for alleged misconduct.
14The incident happened at about 10:30 p.m. on the White House compound near 15th Street NW and Freedom Park, as Metropolitan Police Department and Secret Service officers were trying to clear the scene due to an investigation of a suspicious package, according to the people familiar with the incident.
15Witnesses reported that the car's overhead flashing lights had been activated and both agents were showing their badges to get through the section of the grounds that had been closed off, according to people familiar with the incident.
16The vehicle ran through security tape before hitting the barricades, which an agency official said had been set up temporarily during the investigation of the package.
17Secret Service rules prohibit turning on flashing lights without a security reason and driving a government vehicle after drinking alcohol.
18The two men had been attending a party at a nearby Washington bar to honor the retirement of departing Secret Service spokesman Edwin Donovan.
19Two people familiar with the incident said it was likely captured on Secret Service video.
20They said all of the agents' activities on that part of the compound would have likely been streamed live to the agency's emergency command center.
21The incident violated security protocols, according to several veteran agents.
22At the time that the agents drove into the barricade, disrupting an active investigation, officers had not yet determined whether the suspicious package was a threat.
23If a civilian drives through a White House security barrier, officers are trained to release an attack dog or point loaded guns at the driver.
24In October 2013, a Connecticut woman was shot and killed by police after she rammed a temporary security barrier outside the White House with her car, and then struck a Secret Service uniformed division officer.
25She fled in her car, prompting a police chase that ended with both Capitol Police and Secret Service officers shooting multiple rounds and killing her.
26Her 1-year-old child was inside the car but not hurt.
27Last week's incident is the first major misstep to take place since the management shake-up that began last October, when then-Director Julia Pierson abruptly resigned in the weeks after a man was able jump the White House fence and race through much of the mansion's main floor.
28Clancy took over as acting director, moving later to force out much of the senior management team.
29An independent panel created by the Department of Homeland Security to examine the Secret Service's problems strongly recommended late last year that the president choose an outsider as the new director.
30The panel said the agency was "starved for leadership" with fresh perspectives.
31But Obama went with a trusted hand, picking Clancy, a 27-year veteran of the agency who had headed Obama's security detail early in his administration and who had also worked with Connolly.
