US high court hears Obamacare case

1The US Supreme Court appears sharply divided over a healthcare law challenge that could threaten health insurance for eight million Americans.
2The challenge rests on the distinction between federal and state-run insurance marketplaces set up as part of President Obama's 2010 law.
3During arguments the two justices expected to make the deciding vote did not make clear how they would rule.
4The high court previously upheld a separate element of the law in 2012.
5But many of its provisions have begun since then and an estimated 9.5 million people are enrolled in the programme.
6In King v Burwell, the justices are being asked to determine whether the wording of the law makes people in all 50 states eligible for federal tax subsidies that make insurance premiums more affordable.
7The 2010 law, often known as Obamacare, set up a federally run insurance exchange where Americans who were not covered by employers or other governmental could buy health insurance.
8Individual states also set up exchanges, but most states declined to do so.
9Residents in thirty-four states would be affected by the decision.
10Challengers at the court say five words of the law - "exchange established by the State" - means only those covered by state-run exchanges are eligible for federal subsidies.
11The Obama administration argues this is a too-narrow reading of the law, which spans near 1,000 pages, and the rest of the legislation makes clear subsidies are intended for those who meet income requirements, regardless of which exchange insurance was purchased from.
12Independent studies by the Urban Institute and the Rand Corporation estimate eight million people would lose insurance if the court rules for the plaintiffs.
13During arguments on Wednesday, lawyers on both sides were aggressively questioned by the court's justices in a clear show of the court's ideological divide.
14Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg said giving subsidies only to people in some states would be "disastrous" while Justice Antonin Scalia argued the wording "may not be the statute Congress intended, but it may be the statute Congress wrote".
15Justice Anthony Kennedy, who often provides the deciding vote in close cases, said he saw a "serious constitutional problem" with plaintiffs' argument.
16He argued removing subsidies would unlawfully pressure states but said the law's challengers may win based on the plain text reading.
17Justice John Roberts, who cast the deciding vote in the 2012 case, said very little during arguments.
18Protesters, both in support and in opposition to the law, rallied outside the court.
19"You shouldn't have to choose between a roof over your head or healthcare - or do I go to the doctor today, or do I make sure my kids can eat something today?" one protester, Michelle Boyle, a nurse from Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, said.
20Republicans and conservatives see the law as a government overreach.
21Marsha Blackburn, a Republican congresswoman from Tennessee, told the gathering that she was confident the court would rule against the subsidies in the law.
