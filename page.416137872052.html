Online excitement but no long lines for Apple Watch debut

1PALO ALTO, Calif.
2(AP) - An online rush replaced the traditional overnight queues outside Apple stores Friday as the iconic tech company began taking orders and letting shoppers get their hands on its much-vaunted smartwatch for the first time.
3Eager customers placed online orders for the Apple Watch as soon as Apple's website began accepting them, shortly after midnight Pacific Time.
4Within half an hour, the company appeared to sell out the initial batch of watches that were available for the first official day of shipping on April 24.
5By midmorning, Apple's website was showing the earliest shipping date for many watch models would be in June or later.
6Demand was difficult to gauge, since Apple hasn't said how many watches were available for shipping in the first wave on April 24.
7And in contrast with earlier releases of new Apple products, there were no big lines of shoppers waiting all night outside the company's retail stores.
8That's because Apple encouraged customers to make appointments for a 15-minute opportunity to try on different models - which are priced starting at $349 and go up to $17,000 for a luxury edition - while specially trained employees explained their features.
9Apple is only accepting orders online, for now.
10But the debut still came with some of the anticipation and marketing sizzle for which Apple Inc. is famous.
11Apple retail staffers in New York, Atlanta and other cities clapped and cheered as the first customers streamed through their stores' signature glass doors just before 10 a.m. Eager shoppers also examined the watch at shops in London, Shanghai and Tokyo.
12Student Victor Leung was grinning from ear to ear after finishing his watch appointment and placing his order for a lower-priced sport model at the Apple store on New York's 5th Avenue.
13He said he'd been waiting eagerly for the opportunity to buy one.
14"It's awesome," Leung said.
15"You get texts on your watch, make phone calls."
16While he has tried other smartwatches, he thinks "Apple is different."
17Despite Leung's enthusiasm, analysts said Apple may have good reasons to sell the watch through pre-orders and appointments.
18Online ordering should help Apple manage its inventory and manufacturing.
19The try-on visits should help ensure that early buyers know what to expect and how to use the watch, said Carolina Milanesi, a tech analyst at Kantar Worldpanel.
20She said that could build positive "word of mouth" recommendations.
21And some analysts said the lag time in shipping may help build anticipation while the company works to convince other customers - those who aren't early tech adopters - why they should want this new category of gadget.
22"Our view is it's going to take time for the consumer to adopt wearable technology," said Angelo Zino, analyst at S&P Capital IQ.
23"We're very positive on the long-term trends for wearables and we think Apple is going to be a clear leader in the category," Zino said this week.
24But he added, "We think initial sales are going to be below expectations."
25Apple, which is based in Cupertino, California, hasn't offered any estimates, but some analysts have predicted the company could sell 10 million to 20 million watches this year.
26By comparison, it sold more than 10 million of its new iPhone 6 and 6 Plus smartphones in the first weekend they were available last September, and a record total of 74.5 million iPhones in the fourth quarter last year.
27Some shoppers needed no convincing on Friday.
28Jack Weber, a retired University of Virginia professor, said he woke up at 3 a.m. to order a $1,000 stainless steel Apple Watch online.
29He and his wife, Carol, were first in line for a 9 a.m. appointment to see the watches at a New York Apple store.
30Carol Weber tried on a $15,000, 18-karat gold model that Jack thought would be nice to buy for their upcoming 50th anniversary.
31Carol also liked the steel, though, since it would go with her white gold wedding ring.
32She said she hasn't decided which to get yet, but is leaning toward both.
33AP writers Anick Jesdanun, Barbara Ortutay reported from New York, Kathleen Foody from Atlanta, Sylvia Hui from London, Noriko Kitano from Tokyo and Fu Ting from Shanghai.
