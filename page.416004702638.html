Germanwings crash pilot's profile prompts questions, deep unease

1As more becomes known about Germanwings co-pilot Andreas Lubitz, more questions arise.
2The profile that has emerged of Germanwings co-pilot Andreas Lubitz has become more troubling by the day.
3In the hours after Flight 9525 crashed in the French Alps two weeks ago, Lubitz was regarded as one of 150 victims in an unexplained disaster.
4Two days later he was the prime suspect of an unfathomable act.

10By now, French and German prosecutors have little doubt the 27-year-old intentionally slammed the Airbus A320 into a mountain, killing everyone on board, and there is growing evidence that his actions weren't just a split-second decision but the result of days of planning.
11The revelations have raised questions about who knew what, when, and whether Lubitz could have been stopped.
12The crash has prompted particular soul-searching in Germany.
13Seventy-two of those killed were German citizens, the worst air disaster in the nation's history since the Concorde crash of 2000, in which 97 Germans died.
14"Germany, a byword for technical perfection where security isn't just a hallmark of quality but an article of faith and a measure of value, has been traumatized," historian Michael Stuermer wrote in an op-ed Saturday for Die Welt newspaper.
15Investigators have pieced together a picture of Lubitz by analyzing the voice and flight data recorders found at the crash site, searching his homes in Duesseldorf and Montabaur, and by interviewing friends, relatives, colleagues and doctors.
16- Medical records show that before he received his pilot's license, Lubitz suffered from depression, with doctors recording "suicidal tendencies."
17It forced him to take a break of several months from his pilot training.
18Lufthansa has said Lubitz informed their flight school when he returned in 2009 that he had experienced an episode of "severe depression."
19But Germanwings, the Lufthansa subsidiary Lubitz joined in 2013, said it was unaware of this.
20But both airlines say Lubitz passed all medical tests and was cleared by doctors as fit to fly.
21- In the months before the crash Lubitz sought help from several doctors, including specialists at Duesseldorf's University Hospital.
22The hospital declined to confirm reports that Lubitz was experiencing vision problems, but said he had come in for tests.
23Duesseldorf prosecutors say there was no evidence the co-pilot had any physical ailments.
24- In the week leading up to the crash Lubitz spent time online researching suicide methods and cockpit door security.
25Safety rules introduced after the Sept.
2611, 2001, terror attacks in the United States allow someone in the cockpit to deny others entry.
27- Shortly after takeoff from Barcelona on March 24, Lubitz offered to take over the controls while the pilot went to the toilet.
28Finding himself unable to enter the cockpit on his return, the pilot pleaded with Lubitz to let him back in.
29Lubitz ignored him and repeatedly accelerated the plane before it slammed into a mountainside near the village of Le Vernet.
30With pilots responsible for the lives of 10 million air travelers worldwide every day, the idea that one might be harboring a dark desire to harm himself and others has prompted deep unease.
31"A technical malfunction, a terrorist act, a natural disaster or human error - none of these would be so troubling," German weekly Der Spiegel wrote in its latest edition.
32As the extent of Lubitz's illness became known in the days following the crash, German prosecutors took the unusual step of providing regular updates on their investigation to reassure the public.
33German newspapers, normally at pains to protect the identity of even convicted criminals, spelled out the co-pilot's full name to indicate the gravity of his alleged deeds.
34Meanwhile, the country's airlines swiftly mandated that two people have to remain in the cockpit at all times, while politicians debated the need to relax patient privacy rules - prompting a swift rebuke from medical associations who warned that rash changes to the rules could undermine patients' confidence in doctors and prevent them from seeking help, doing greater harm than good.
35Few commentators, however, have raised the possibility that Lufthansa, a national institution, may have made any avoidable mistakes.
36Instead, they have pointed to similar incidents that are believed to have taken place in Namibia, the United States and Indonesia over the past two decades.
37Conservative daily Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung concluded that "ultimately Lufthansa's system failed."
38However, given that Lubitz appeared to have gone to great lengths to conceal the extent of his illness, the paper questioned whether any system could ever account for what goes on inside a person's head.
39Others evoked the phenomenon of the "Geisterfahrer" - a German term meaning "ghost driver" describing someone who drives the wrong way down the Autobahn, endangering himself and others.
40The notion of an unstoppable yet ultimately rare incident resonated in Spain, which counted 51 of its citizens among the dead.
41"Who could have known that he was determined to destroy himself on that day?" said Mercedes Valle, a 54-year-old doctor in Madrid.
42She added that the content of the black box had confirmed "there was a deranged person with suicidal tendencies left in charge of a plane."
