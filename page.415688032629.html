Friends pay tribute to Evita Sarmonikas, who died after surgery in Mexico

1Evita Sarmonikas has died in Mexico during surgery.
2Evita Sarmonikas had just attended a funeral when she felt moved to send a text message to one of her dear friends.
3Evita Sarmonikas sent this text message to a close friend.
4The message read: "Hey Jules. I went to a funeral today. This is just a short message to say I think of you and treasure you. You are unique, you make me happy, and I look forward to more memories with you :) xoxo love Eva."
5Now her friends are facing the heartbreaking reality that a funeral will soon be held for the 29-year-old Gold Coast woman, who died after undergoing a medical procedure in Mexico.
6"I never thought I would read that message and have to accept that you are the one that is gone now," Jules wrote in a tribute to Ms Sarmonikas, known to her friends as Eva.
7Evita Sarmonikas' family have been told she signed a waiver before surgery.
8"May you rest in peace as the angel you always were. You will never be forgotten."
9Ms Sarmonikas' family has posted a statement on Facebook saying she died on March 20 after suffering a heart attack following surgery in Mexicali, the capital of the Mexican state of Baja California, near the Mexican-US border.
10The exact procedure Ms Sarmonikas was undergoing is not known, however the statement hints that Ms Sarmonikas, who was "filled with certain inadequacies" about her body, may have been undergoing cosmetic surgery.
11The family said the only response they had received from the hospital was to say Ms Sarmonikas had signed a waiver before surgery, which released them of any liability.
12"There have been no answers and their only offer of 'co-operation' was an urgency to return her to us in the form of ashes," the family's statement says.
13"The Australian embassy has strongly advised we do not allow this to happen."
14They are now frantically trying to raise funds so Ms Sarmonikas' sister, Andrea, and mother can travel to Mexico to bring her body home to Australia.
15Ms Sarmonikas, who was travelling with her boyfriend, did not have travel insurance, and the estimated cost of returning her body to Australia is up to $20,000, her family says.
16Following her sudden death, family and friends have inundated a Facebook page with tributes and messages of support.
17"Eva always carried a smile wherever she went, and that's how I'll remember her," a friend, Claud, wrote.
18Brooke wrote: "Evita shone so bright and her smile lit up the room. I hope the way that she changed everyone's lives around her will help ease your suffering."
19Another friend, Sasha, wrote: "How do you improve on perfection? Eva was perfect, just as God made her and no man should ever have adulterated His masterpiece. The world is poorer for having lost her."
20Mark wrote: "No matter how far life takes us, don't forget the people who helped you get there; who shared in your achievements, comforted you in your grief, laughed at your jokes, and led you in your dance. We are all born into family. Sometimes, we get to choose it. Rest now in eternal peace, my sister. I was truly blessed."
21Ms Sarmonikas was a former school captain at Coombabah High School on the Gold Coast, and also worked as a receptionist at real estate company LJ Hooker in Surfers Paradise.
22Staff there said they were deeply saddened to hear of her death.
23"She was rightly positioned as our receptionist and the role suited her to a T," the company said in a statement.
24"With the bubbliest and brightest personality anyone could ever imagine, it was a pleasure to hear her wonderful voice at the other end of the line ... We will all miss her very much, Rest In Peace beautiful girl."
25Thousands of Australians are thought to travel overseas, often to Thailand, each year to undergo surgical procedures at a cheaper rate than in Australia.
26But the vice-president of the Australian Medical Association, Dr Stephen Parnis, urged those people to ask themselves: "What price is one's health?"
27"In general terms, we are concerned about the numbers of Australians heading overseas for medical procedures, and that's because we feel on balance that the risks significantly outweigh any potential benefits," he said.
28Dr Parnis said he could not comment specifically on Ms Sarmonikas' case.
29But as a Melbourne emergency physician, he had seen first-hand the complications that could arise when patients returned to Australia after undergoing surgery overseas.
30Some of those patients he had treated were very sick, he said.
31"We have a system here which is subject to very rigorous scrutiny, and also has among the world's best outcomes, whether it's medical or surgical care," Dr Parnis said.
32"We can vouch for our services. We don't want to cast aspersions or doubts about our colleagues overseas, but we can't vouch for the standards that are in another part of the world.
33"If you have a surgical procedure, you really need to have the involvement of the surgical team before, during and after the procedure. If you have your procedure in another country, that clearly can't occur if you return to Australia, and it is not uncommon for Australian doctors to have to try to deal with complications that occur from procedures overseas. That has a number of issues, because the documentation is often inadequate. If it's, say, an infection, you've got a greater chance of having a superbug, a multi-resistant organism, because they are an even bigger problem overseas than they are in Australia."
