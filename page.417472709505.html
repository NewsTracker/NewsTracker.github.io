In wake of Snowden leaks, NSA domestic spy program limits pass Congress

1Washington: The US Senate on Tuesday passed a bill reforming a government domestic spying program that swept up millions of Americans' telephone records, sending the bill to the White House for President Barack Obama to sign into law.
2Reversing US security policy that had been in place since shortly after the September 11, 2001, attacks, the bill would end a system exposed by former National Security Agency contractor Edward Snowden in 2013.
3The eavesdropping agency collected and searched records of phone calls looking for terrorism leads but it was not allowed to listen to the content of calls.
4The passage of the USA Freedom Act, the result of an alliance between Senate Democrats and some of the chamber's most conservative Republicans, was a victory for Mr Obama, a Democrat, and a setback for Senate Republican Majority Leader Mitch McConnell.
5Edward Snowden is credited with sparking the debate which has led to reform of surveillance in the US.
6Before passing the bill, Senators defeated three amendments proposed by Republican leaders.
7By a vote of 67-32, the Senate approved the new act, which the US House of Representatives had passed overwhelmingly last month.
8A federal appeals court on May 7 ruled the collection of "metadata" illegal.
9The new law would require telephone companies, such as Verizon Communications Inc and AT&T Inc, to collect and store telephone "metadata" the same way that they do now for billing purposes.
10But instead of routinely feeding US intelligence agencies such data, the companies would be required to turn it over only in response to a government request approved by the secretive Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court.
11Passage of the bill is the first major legislative reform of US surveillance practices since Mr Snowden's revelations two years ago this month.
12Along with the phone records program, two other domestic surveillance programs authorised under the USA Patriot Act have been shut down since Sunday.
13The Senate missed the deadline to extend legal authorities for certain data collection by the NSA and the Federal Bureau of Investigation.
14Before the final vote, the Senate defeated three amendments proposed by security hawks, who argued that the reforms in the USA Freedom Act went too far.
15"Before scrapping an effective system that has helped protect us from attack in favour of an untried one, we should at least work toward securing some modest degree of assurance that the new system can, in fact, actually work," Senator McConnell argued.
16But legislators defeated by 44-54 a measure that would have doubled to 12 months from six months the amount of time the NSA would have to wind down the existing telephone metadata collection system.
17They also defeated by 42-56 a measure that would have changed the treatment of independent advocates in the secretive surveillance court, and rejected by 56-43 a proposal that would have mandated that the US Director of National Intelligence certify the new phone records system works.
18Telephone companies had been less than thrilled about potentially overhauling their record-keeping systems to become the repositories of surveillance records, and resisted the prospect for month.
19The sensitivity of their push against powerful government agencies is so high that few industry officials or experts are willing to discuss the matter.
20Privacy advocates who have opposed the phone records program since Mr Snowden exposed it balked at the amendments.
21They said the changes would weaken privacy protections in the Freedom Act and hold up the bill.
22It was not immediately clear how soon the NSA program would be restarted for six months, until the new system is put in place.
23After Mr Obama signs the bill, the executive branch would have to apply to the surveillance court for reauthorisation of the program.
24And officials have said it would take time logistically to bring the system back online.
25A senior US intelligence official said the bulk telephone data collection system had been shut down since shortly before 8 pm Washington timeon Sunday.
