EU leaders optimistic as Greece says it will propose reforms in coming days

1BRUSSELS, Belgium -- Top leaders of the European Union emerged from a meeting with the Greek prime minister early Friday saying much what they have been for weeks: Greece needs to come up with a reform plan if it wants more of their money.
2But now it's on a tighter timetable.
3The EU leaders said Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras committed to providing a list of specific reforms in the coming days that would improve his country's balance sheet.
4German Chancellor Angela Merkel said the leaders did not discuss specific numbers.
5Hanging over the summit of 28 heads of state and government are fears that the hard line of the Greek government formed in January could cause the country to drop out of the euro, something that would trigger a crisis for the currency shared by 19 nations.
6Tsipras made a last-minute request for a mini-summit Thursday night with half a dozen top EU officials.
7Those officials agreed to the meeting -- over the objections from some of the other leaders, who complained they were being left out.
8In addition to Merkel and Tsipras, the meeting included French President Francois Hollande, European Central Bank President Mario Draghi and EU President Donald Tusk.
9The leaders emerged more optimistic than when they went in, but gave little evidence of why, other than the timetable of "the next days" for Tsipras to present them with his intended reforms.
10"The Greek government will take full responsibility for the reforms and submit a list of these reforms, which will be a comprehensive list of specific reforms, in the coming days," Merkel said.
11Tsipras was upbeat as well.
12"We are more optimist after this deliberation," he said.
13"All the sides confirmed their intention to try their best to overcome the difficulties of the Greek economy as soon as possible."
14European leaders have become increasingly exasperated by what many see as foot-dragging on the part of Tsipras' government.
15Greece agreed a month ago to push through reforms in exchange for EU help in keeping it solvent, but has delayed submitting the list.
16"A deal is a deal," Dutch Prime Minister Mark Rutte said ahead of the meeting.
17Adding to the European frustration, the Greek parliament approved an anti-poverty bill on Wednesday.
18It was the first piece of legislation that the government has put through -- and it did so without full consultation from its creditor partners.
19Greece's economic policies drew criticism even from nations outside the eurozone.
20British Prime Minister David Cameron marveled Thursday at how poorly the Greek economy has fared compared to his own.
21"When I first came here as prime minister five years ago, Britain and Greece were virtually in the same boat. We had similar-sized budget deficits," he said.
22"The reason we are in a different position is we took long-term, difficult decisions and we had all of the hard work and effort of the British people."
23Greece is banking on the fact that its European partners all want to keep the eurozone intact, fearing that if Greece pulls out others might as well.
