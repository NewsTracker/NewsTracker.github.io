3It's a great record that doesn't try too hard.
4Barnett's songs make a virtue of casualness, an off-handedness that can turn razor sharp in the middle of a run-on-sentence.
5Somehow, the Australian singer-guitarist has made something fresh out of everyday vignettes performed on everyday instruments (guitar-bass-drums).
6She sounds like she's day-dreaming out loud instead of singing, but she's deceptively incisive as a lyricist.
7Her guitar-playing, while never particularly showy, can be subtle or scalding.
8Her minimalist style nicely counterpoints the maximalist wordplay.
9The amiable stomp of "Elevator Operator" creates a template for everything that follows.
10It's a short story spoken as much as sung, packed with descriptive details and too many words to safely cram inside a three-minute song.
11A 20-year-old blows off work and converses in an elevator with an aging femme fatale.
12She's worried about why he's heading for the rooftop.
13"I'm not suicidal, just idling insignificantly," he explains.
14"Idling Insignificantly" would've been another great album title, though the one Barnett chose based on a quote attributed to either "Winnie the Pooh" author A.A. Milne or baseball sage Satchel Paige sums up the album's disarmingly wry tone.
15Like "Elevator Operator," "Pedestrian at Best" turns romantic calamity into a shrug, a deadpan joke.
16"Give me all your money, and I'll make some origami, honey," Barnett sings over fuzzed-up chords and feedback mainlined from Nirvana's "Bleach."
17In these songs not much seems to happen the narrator stares at a hotel-room ceiling in "An Illustration of Loneliness (Sleepless in New York)," contemplates the fate of a beached seal in "Kim's Caravan," sizes up her lawn on "Small Poppies," examines the vegetables at the supermarket in "Dead Fox."
18All the action takes place inside the narrator's head, a zone racing with indecision, conflict, feelings of inadequacy and inertia.
19She's the unassuming hanger-on at the party, the one who notices everything but is never noticed herself.
20She builds modest songs that turn on a telling line.
21The gently lilting "Depreston" evokes the wide-open spaces of a country song.
22It describes a couple house-hunting in a suburban cul de sac when they notice "a photo of a young man in a van in Vietnam," and quietly their modest dreams darken.
23In "Boxing Day Blues," a guitar sounds like it's providing the soundtrack for a hanging, and a couplet encapsulates a dying relationship: "When's the funeral? Do you want me to come?"
24The turbulence lurking in all these songs bubbles over in the guitar-stoked finales of "Small Poppies" and "Kim's Caravan," the garage-rock rumble of the magnificently tossed off "Nobody Really Cares if You Don't Go to the Party," the forced cheerfulness of the pop bauble "Debbie Downer."
25Barnett may sometimes act like she's distracted or can't be bothered, but don't be deceived.
26Her songs refuse to be brushed off.
