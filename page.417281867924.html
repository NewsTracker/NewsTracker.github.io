4WASHINGTON (AP) - A Supreme Court ruling due in a few weeks could wipe out health insurance for millions of people covered by President Barack Obama's health care law.
5But it's Republicans - not White House officials - who have been talking about damage control.
6A likely reason: Twenty-six of the 34 states that would be most affected by the ruling have Republican governors, and 22 of the 24 GOP Senate seats up in 2016 are in those states.
7Obama's law offers subsidized private insurance to people without access to it on the job.
8In the court case, opponents of the law argue that its literal wording allows the federal government to subsidize coverage only in states that set up their own health insurance markets.
9Most states have not done so, because of the intense partisanship over "Obamacare" and in some cases because of technical problems.
10Instead, they rely on the federal HealthCare.gov website.
11If the court invalidates the subsidies in those states, an estimated 8 million people could lose coverage.
12The results would be "ugly," said Sandy Praeger, a former Kansas insurance commissioner.
13"People who are reasonably healthy would just drop coverage," she said.
14"Only the unhealthy would keep buying health care. It would really exacerbate the problem of the cost of health insurance."
15Praeger, a Republican who retired this year, called it "a classic death spiral," using a term for market collapse.
16Oral arguments on March 4 revealed a divided court.
17Chief Justice John Roberts and Justice Anthony Kennedy seemingly are key to the outcome, which won't be known until late June.
18If the subsidies survive, the Affordable Care Act will look like settled law to all but its most passionate opponents.
19But if they are overturned, the shock could carry into next year's elections.
20Around the time when the court announces its decision, insurers will be working to finalize premiums and plans for the coming year.
21Contracts with the government for 2016 health law coverage have to be signed by early fall.
22If the subsidies are overturned, insurers would have to tear up their projections about markets in more than half the states.
23State lawmakers could mitigate the impact by setting up their own insurance markets, or exchanges.
24But that can't be done overnight.
25States might try authorizing an exchange, and then contracting with the federal government to run it.
26But that sort of end run might prompt lawsuits from opponents of the law.
27During arguments, Justice Samuel Alito raised the possibility that the court might be able to delay the effective date of its decision.
28Even a delay through the end of this year wouldn't buy much time.
29Enrollment for 2016 health law plans is scheduled to start Nov. 1.
30The health law was designed as a balancing act.
31Insurers can't turn people away because of health problems, but most healthy people are required to contribute to the insurance pool, and the government subsidizes most of the premium for low- to middle-income households.
32Take away subsidies, and the other two parts become unstable.
33The law's requirement to carry insurance, never popular, would probably become the biggest target for repeal.
34"My guess is there would be overwhelming political support for the elimination of the individual mandate if people can't afford the premiums," said former Sen. Tom Daschle, D-S.D., who was an influential Obama adviser on health care.
35Insurers would demand relief from provisions of the law intended to limit premium increases, or they might drop out of the insurance exchanges.
36Many people still buy individual health care policies directly from an insurance company, bypassing the law's markets and paying the full cost.
37They tend to be small-business owners, self-employed professionals and early retirees.
38But even they would not escape the tumult in states losing subsidies.
39The health law created one big insurance pool in each state, combining customers who purchase their policies directly with those who buy through the government market.
40If healthy people exit the insurance exchanges in droves, premiums for those buying directly would go up.
41Some may be unable to afford the higher cost.
42"It would set off cascading events," said Larry Levitt of the nonpartisan Kaiser Family Foundation.
43"The individual market would empty out as premiums rise significantly."
44REPUBLICANS TO THE RESCUE?
45Leading congressional Republicans have been walking a fine line, opposing the law in the Supreme Court case while pledging to protect consumers if their side wins.
46If the subsidies are overturned, Republicans will first try blaming Obama and the Democrats for writing flawed legislation and then trying to paper over problems with regulations.
47Then they'll move ahead with a patch to appease angry constituents.
48A bill introduced by Sen. Ron Johnson, R-Wis., would continue the subsidies for existing customers only on the federal exchange until Sept.
492017. That would open a window for states to act, but it would ultimately leave the problem for the next president and Congress.
50Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell, R-Ky., is a co-sponsor.
51Johnson's bill would repeal the requirements for individuals to have insurance and for larger employers to offer coverage to workers.
52Obama is unlikely to accept any of those changes.
53"The president is likely to veto whatever we would propose, because we don't have a willing partner," said Sen. John Barrasso, R-Wyo., leader of a GOP working group on health care.
