Islamic State releases more than 200 captive Yazidis in Iraq

1The freed prisoners said that they had been led to believe they were being led to their execution, but instead, were piled onto a minibus that drove them to peshmerga positions.
2The Yazidis, made up of children and elderly people, are said to be in poor health and bearing signs of abuse and neglect.
3The 216 prisoners have been held captive in the Islamic State since last summer when militants attacked their villages in the area around Sinjar in northwestern Iraq.
4General Hiwa Abdullah, a peshmerga commander in the northern Iraqi city of Kirkuk, said that about 40 children are among those released, while the rest were elderly, some of whom were too exhausted and disoriented to speak.
5No reason was given for the release of the prisoners, which took place in Himera just southwest of Kirkuk, 180 miles north of Baghdad.
6The freed Yazidis were taken away by ambulances and buses to receive treatment and care.
7One elderly woman said she had been captured by the insurgents last August when they overpowered Kurdish forces in the Sinjar area and proceeded to purge its Yazidi population, killing hundreds and taking thousands captive.
8The woman, who asked not to be named, said she had told her son and two young daughters to run away as the militants closed in, but stayed behind herself because she was unwell and did not want to slow them down.
9'I had lost hope of seeing my children again, but today it has happened,' she said as they embraced her and wept.
10It was not clear why the radical jihadists had decided to release the Yazidis, whom they consider devil-worshippers, but the group previously freed 200 more it was holding under similarly mysterious circumstances.
11Some of the Yazidis said they had been held in the Islamic State stronghold of Tel Afar most of the time, but in the days leading up to their release, they were moved from one town to another in Islamic State's self-proclaimed caliphate.
12Yazidi community leaders were there to receive them and an ambulance was on standby.
13Yazidi activists say many remain in the hands of Islamic State, which has often subjected women to rape or sexual slavery.
14The United Nations said last month Islamic State may have committed genocide against the minority.
15The Yazidis are an ancient, predominantly Kurdish people who follow their own religion derived from Islam, Christianity and Zoroastrianism.
