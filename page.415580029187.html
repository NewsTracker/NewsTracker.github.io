Honda adds 105,000 vehicles to driver's air bag recall

US, Israel have no choice but to work together: Netanyahu

1Honda is adding nearly 105,000 vehicles to its U.S. recall of driver's side air bag inflators that can explode with too much force.
2The added vehicles include nearly 89,000 Pilot SUVs from the 2008 model year, as well as about 11,000 Civics from 2004 and another 5,000 Accords from the 2001 model year.
3DETROIT (AP) - Honda is adding nearly 105,000 vehicles to its growing U.S. recall of driver's side air bag inflators that can explode with too much force.
4Honda said that it's the first recall of 2008 Pilots for potential problems with driver's air bags made by Takata Corp. of Japan.
5The inflators can blow apart a metal canister and spew shrapnel into drivers and passengers.
6At least six people have died worldwide due to the problem.
7Dealers will replace the driver air bag inflators for free.
8With the added vehicles, Honda has now recalled 5.5 million Honda and Acura cars and SUVs nationwide from the 2001 to 2011 model years because of the air bag problems.
9So far, 10 different automakers have recalled over 17 million cars and trucks in the U.S. and 22 million worldwide because of the air bag problem.
10Honda said in documents posted Thursday by U.S. safety regulators that it found the additional vehicles by checking Takata inflator part numbers against Honda vehicle identification numbers in its factory records.
11Some 2001 Accords and 2004 Civics already were included in a recall from last year.
12After announcing the driver's side recall last year, Honda started checking part numbers, spokesman Chris Martin wrote in an email.
13"The process required a significant amount of time to cross-check large databases, but helped to assure that each vehicle had been identified," he wrote.
14Honda has no new reports of inflator ruptures related to the recall expansion, and has no reason to believe further expansions will be needed, Martin wrote.
15The company said it will send letters to owners of vehicles in the expanded recall "over time" as replacement parts become available.
16The recalls are nationwide for driver's side air bags.
17There were separate recalls announced last year for passenger air bags in high-humidity areas including Alabama, California, Florida, Georgia, Hawaii, Louisiana, Mississippi, South Carolina, Texas, Puerto Rico and the U.S. Virgin Islands.
18Takata uses ammonium nitrate to create a small explosion that quickly inflates its air bags.
19But government investigators say the chemical can burn faster than designed if exposed to prolonged airborne moisture.
20That can cause it to blow apart a metal canister meant to contain the explosion.
21Automakers, Takata and the government all want to find out just how much humidity and time it takes to cause the problem, both of which are unknown.
22Takata, NHTSA and the auto industry are investigating to figure out just how much humidity over how much time can cause the problem to happen.
23The 10 automakers, fed up with slow progress by Takata in finding the cause, hired a Virginia rocket science company to investigate the matter.
24Orbital ATK has the ability to quickly simulate the impact of humidity on chemicals over long periods of time.
25NHTSA began fining Takata $14,000 per day on Feb. 20 for allegedly failing to fully cooperate in the government's investigation.
26The fines have grown to $392,000 as of Thursday.
27The Justice Department also is investigating Takata for possible criminal prosecution.
28The company has known about the air bag problems since at least 2004.
