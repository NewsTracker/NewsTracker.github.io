Al-Shabaab gunmen targeted Christian prayer site in Kenya attack, survivor says

1Anger is growing in Kenya over the government's inability to protect people from violent incursions by Al-Shabaab.
2The militants who slaughtered 147 people in a Kenyan school appeared to have planned extensively, even targeting a site where Christians had gone to pray, a survivor said Friday.
3Police on Friday were at the campus of Garissa University College, taking fingerprints from the bodies of the four assailants and of the students and security officials who died, for thorough identification purposes.
4The northeastern Kenyan town lacks the facilities to store all those bodies.
5In Nairobi, Kenya's capital, family members were lining up at a morgue where about 20 bodies had already been airlifted from Garissa.
6A government source told Reuters that the death toll is likely to rise Friday.
7One of the first things that the assailants did early Thursday, survivor Helen Titus said, was to head for a lecture hall where Christians were in early morning prayer.
8"They investigated our area. They knew everything," Helen Titus told The Associated Press at a hospital in Garissa where she was being treated for a bullet wound to the wrist.
9Titus, a 21-year-old English literature student, said she covered her face and hair with the blood of classmates and lay still at one point during al-Shabaab's deadliest attack on Kenyan soil in hopes the gunmen would think she was dead.
10The gunmen also told students hiding in dormitories to come out, assuring them that they would not be killed, said Titus, who wore a patient's gown as she sat on a bench in the hospital yard.
11"We just wondered whether to come out or not," she said.
12Many students did, whereupon the gunmen started shooting men, saying they would not kill "ladies," Titus said.
13But they also shot women and targeted Christians, said Titus, who is a Christian.
14Also Friday, a small group of demonstrators walked down a main road in Garissa with signs that read "We are against the killing of innocent Kenyans!!!! We are tired!!" and "Enough is enough. No more killing!! We are with you, our fellow Kenyans."
15"We feel very sorry for them and we condemn the attack," said demonstrator Abdullahi Muktar.
16Anger over the massacre was compounded by the fact there were warnings last week that an attack on a university was imminent.
17Local residents accused the authorities of doing little to boost security in this little developed region.
18"It's because of laxity by the government that these things are happening. For something like this to happen when there are those rumours is unacceptable," said Mohamed Salat, 47, a Somali Kenyan businessman.
19Elsewhere in Garissa, there was activity in a military-controlled area where medical vehicles and a UNHCR truck made their way in and out, and some civilians, possibly relatives of the dead, gathered.
20Security was tight at the gate.
21A Garissa University College bus was parked outside.
22At one point, a group approached the gate and was blocked by soldiers.
23Several women began shrieking and collapsed in apparent grief in the dust for several minutes.
24A bystander said the son of one of the women had died in the attack.
25The masked attackers - strapped with explosives and armed with AK-47s - singled out non-Muslim students at Garissa University College and then gunned them down without mercy, survivors said.
26The gunmen took dozens of hostages in a dormitory as they battled troops and police before the operation ended after about 13 hours, witnesses said.
27Al-Shabaab spokesman Ali Mohamud Rage said fighters from the Somalia-based extremist group were responsible.
28The al-Qaida-linked group has been blamed for a series of attacks in Kenya, including the siege at the Westgate Mall in Nairobi in 2013 that killed 67 people, as well as other violence in the north.
29The group has vowed to retaliate against Kenya for sending troops to Somalia in 2011 to fight the militants staging cross-border attacks and kidnappings.
