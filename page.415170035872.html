1Gissendaner was scheduled to be executed Monday, March 2, 2015, but citing concerns about the drug to be used in a lethal injection, corrections officials in Georgia postponed Gissendaner's execution for the second time in a week.
2ATLANTA (AP) - Georgia officials were indecisive about whether to proceed with a cloudy lethal injection drug, at one point saying they weren't sure whether they checked "this week's or last week's" batch, according to a court filing.
3Ultimately, they postponed the execution of Kelly Renee Gissendaner late Monday night.
4A day later, they decided to temporarily halt executions until they could more carefully analyze the pentobarbital, which is supposed to be clear.
5The cloudy drug bolstered death penalty opponents, who have been vocal in their opposition after three botched executions in other parts of the country.
6Gissendaner, who was convicted of murder in the February 1997 slaying of her husband, had originally been set for execution last week on Feb. 25, but it was postponed because of a threat of bad weather.
7Attorneys for Gissendaner said in a filing with the U.S. Supreme Court that a lawyer for the state called them around 10:25 p.m. Monday to say the execution would be postponed several days because the state's pharmacist had looked at the drug an hour earlier and determined it was cloudy.
8The state's lawyer called back about five minutes later to say the prison wasn't sure which drugs they had checked, "this week's or last week's," and that they were considering going forward, the filing says.
9The lawyer called a third time, saying "this particular batch (of drugs) just didn't come out like it was supposed to" and they weren't going to proceed, according to the court filing.
10About 11 p.m., the state told reporters an independent lab checked its potency and it was acceptable, but it later appeared cloudy and the execution was postponed "out of an abundance of caution."
11The back and forth was detailed in Gissendaner's emergency motion for a stay.
12Corrections spokeswoman Gwendolyn Hogan said "there was never any confusion within the department about the drugs which were to be used," but also indicated the department was not part of the conversations between the attorney general's office and defense attorneys.
13Georgia Attorney General Sam Olens said in a statement it is "essential that executions are carried out in a constitutional manner" and that he approved of the decision to temporarily suspend executions.
14The cloudiness could be contamination by bacteria or some impurity, said Michael Jay, a professor at the University of North Carolina's Eshelman School of Pharmacy.
15If the particles were big enough, they could clog blood vessels when injected or could lodge in the lungs, Jay said.
16It could also make the drug less potent, making an inmate very sleepy but not kill them, he said.
17"If it's a solution that's supposed to be clear and it's not clear, it should never be injected," Jay said.
18"So they did the right thing by not injecting it."
19Compounded drugs have a shelf life of 30 days, Jay said.
20Corrections officials declined to say when the department obtained the drug or when it was set to expire.
21Georgia and other death penalty states have bought execution drugs from compounding pharmacies in recent years after pharmaceutical companies stopped selling to U.S. prisons.
22And many of them have adopted laws to hide the identity of their drug providers, saying those laws are necessary to protect their suppliers and maintain a source of the drugs.
23Critics say executions should be as transparent as possible.
24The cloudy drug "raises significant concerns about Georgia's ability to carry out executions in compliance with the Constitution, but we don't know what really happened," said Megan McCracken of the University of California, Berkeley, School of Law's Death Penalty Clinic.
25"Because of the secrecy surrounding Georgia's procedures, it is impossible to say if this is indicative of a larger problem," she said.
