Greek debt talks go into weekend as default deadline looms

1The bitter standoff between Greece and its international creditors was extended into the weekend, just days before Athens has to meet a crucial debt deadline which could decide whether it goes bankrupt and gets kicked out of the euro currency club.
2A key meeting of eurozone finance ministers broke up without agreement on Greece's rescue package on Thursday, intensifying doubts about whether Athens can pay the International Monetary Fund a debt worth 1.6 billion euros (NZ$2.6b ) on Tuesday.
3An agreement on a drastic Greek tax and austerity reform package is necessary for creditors to unfreeze 7.2 billion euros (NZ$11.6b ) in bailout money that would get Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras off the hook.
4"European history is full of disagreements, negotiations and at the end, compromises," Tsipras said.
5"So, after the comprehensive Greek proposals, I am confident that we will reach a compromise."
6Lower-level negotiations will continue and a new meeting of eurozone finance ministers is tentatively scheduled for Saturday.
7The breakup of the eurozone meeting was the latest in a series of negotiating roadblocks, and a major setback since there had been hopes to reach a deal in time for European leaders to approve it at a summit later Thursday.
8The blockage happened after leaders from the IMF, the European Central Bank and the European Commission raised the stakes by putting forward their joint position on the reforms they would accept to offer Greece a financial lifeline.
9But Greece, objecting to what it saw as outside interference, was still not on board, and wanted to stick to a previous plan it had offered.
10Greek Finance Minister Yanis Varoufakis said several ministers within the 19-nation eurozone found that Athens was being pushed too hard.
11"Interestingly, several colleagues disagreed and criticised not only our text but also the text of the institutions," he said.
12Greece's most influential creditor, Germany, had never been optimistic about a breakthrough by Thursday.
13The Greek government so far has "not moved, rather moved backward," said German Finance Minister Wolfgang Schaeuble.
14He said the chances for success now "lies exclusively with those responsible in Greece."
15In Washington, the IMF said Greece wouldn't get any extra time to make the debt repayment due Tuesday.
16"We're expecting the payment to be made June 30," IMF spokesman Gerry Rice said, adding that Greece will be granted no extensions.
17A default on its debts could force Greece out of the eurozone, which would be hugely painful for the country.
18Some experts say it could be manageable for Europe and the world economy, but that remains unclear and any failure would likely shake global markets.
19"When Greece, Europe, the eurozone is at stake, we have to know how to finish negotiations," French President Francois Hollande said.
20Tsipras is under massive pressure from Greeks themselves as the compromises suggested so far will mean more hardship for citizens already suffering the impact of past austerity measures to bring public spending into line.
21According to Greek officials at the talks, creditors are seeking a different mix of austerity measures than those proposed by Athens, making the cuts more immediate.
22They include broad pension cuts, higher revenue from sales tax, and a faster elimination of tax exemptions " demands that are likely to fuel dissent within the government if accepted.
23Representatives from almost every Greek party were in Brussels, following developments blow by blow, to see whether they would be able to back any new deal in the Greek parliament, where a vote must pass by Monday.
24"We are at a critical moment," Greek Labor Minister Panos Skourletis warned on private Antenna television.
25He said on of the key issues Greece wanted included in an agreement was making the country's existing debt load more sustainable.
26Creditors have said in the past they would consider that, by either lowering the interest rates or extending repayment dates on Greece's bailout loans.
27But they are balking at discussing the issue now, preferring to first get Greece the loans it needs to avoid default next week.
28Amid the uncertainty over Greece's future, bank deposit holders have been pulling money out of the banks.
29That has forced the European Central Bank to increase emergency credit to the Greek banks on a daily basis since last week.
30Experts say that if Greece does not reach a deal, it could have to put limits on money withdrawals soon.
31Miranda Xafa, senior fellow at the Center for International Governance Innovation and a former IMF official, said that the talks were now "more a matter of signing a deal, even a painful deal" that would cover Greece for three to nine months.
32If no deal is struck quickly, she said, Greece could see "capital controls as early as next week."
33Despite the uncertainty, investors held their nerve.
34The Stoxx 50 index of European shares closed unchanged on the day while the Athens Stock Exchange edged up 0.1 per cent.
