Why Germanwings flight might have crashed over the Alps

1The Germanwings plane that crashed Tuesday in France was at the safest part of flight.
2Yet, something went horribly wrong, leading to the death of 150 passengers and crew.
3The first clue about what could have happened will be the wreckage itself.
4If the debris field is pretty compact, the plane most likely hit the mountains intact.
5If, it is scattered, the plane probably broke up midair.
6"If you see a wing here and three miles later you see a fuselage, one doesn't have to be an expert to speculate that it didn't happen on the ground - that something happened before it impacted," says Todd Curtis, a former Boeing safety engineer who is now director of the Airsafe.com Foundation.
7People comfort each other as they arrive at the Barcelona airport in Spain, Tuesday, March 24, 2015.
8A Germanwings passenger jet carrying 150 people crashed in the French Alps region as it traveled from Barcelona to Duesseldorf in Germany.
9Germanwings Flight 9525 from Barcelona, Spain, to Duesseldorf, Germany, was flown on an Airbus A320, a workhorse of modern aviation, similar to the Boeing 737.
10There are over 3,600 of them in operation worldwide, according to Airbus, which also makes nearly identical versions of the plane: The smaller A318 and A319 and the stretched A321.
11An additional 2,500 of those jets are flying.
12The A320 family has a good safety record, with just 0.14 fatal accidents per million takeoffs, according to a Boeing safety analysis.
13This particular jet was delivered to Lufthansa - the parent company of Germanwings - in 1991 and had about 58,300 flight hours over 46,700 flights.
14It is way too early to know for sure what happened, but here are some options.
15This jet averaged more than 5.3 flights a day over its 24 years.
16That isn't unusual for a plane making short trips around Europe but is on the higher end of what is normal.
17Each takeoff and landing cycle and the pressurization and depressurization associated with it adds stress to the skin of the plane.
18Aircraft that fly short, frequent routes go through more of these cycles than planes flying long distances.
19In 1988, a 19-year-old Aloha Airlines Boeing 737-200 that had made frequent, short hops among the Hawaiian islands lost a large part of its roof.
20Corrosion and metal fatigue were to blame.
21Flight 9525 reportedly descended at 3,000 feet per minute from its cruising altitude of 38,000, twice the normal speed of a descent.
22If there was some type of rapid decompression - whether from metal fatigue or a bomb - the pilots' first move would be to get the plane down below 10,000 feet, where the air is breathable.
23The masks that come down from the overhead bins provide about 10 minutes worth of oxygen.
24So, a decent rate of 3,000 feet per minute would get the plane down to breathable air just in time.
25Curtis says that initial rate of decent is consistent with what you would see in a decompression situation.
26Airbus jets have one of the most sophisticated cockpits.
27However, there have been issues in the past with some of the instruments there.
28The 2009 crash of Air France Flight 447 started because pilots were getting false air speed indications as it flew through a storm.
29Ultimately, pilot error led the plane into a stall but only after they were getting bad data.
30More recently, a Lufthansa A321 - the slightly larger version of the A320 - dropped about 4,000 feet in one minute after the autopilot unexpectedly lowered the jet's nose.
31Following the November incident, the European Aviation Safety Agency issued a safety directive warning pilots about the possibility of an error with the angle of attack sensors on the plane.
32Pulling up on the jet's sidestick - a device similar to a yoke - would not pull the jet out of the dive.
33The safety agency noted the only way to stop it was to turn off two of the three air data reference units in the cockpit.
34These units process the flight speed, up or down angle of the nose, and the altitude.
35There is no indication that this crash is tied to any criminal act but such actions can't yet be ruled out.
36The pilots might have somehow accidently put the plane into a dive or stall and not be able to recover from it.
37Separately, the pilots could have been so focused on getting the plane below 10,000 feet that they didn't realize how close they were to the mountains until it was too late.
