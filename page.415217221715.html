US clears officer in Ferguson case, criticizes police force

1ST. LOUIS (AP) " The federal government's withering report on the Ferguson Police Department issued a stern mandate to city leaders: Reform your law-enforcement practices and rebuild relations with the black community.
2It won't be swift or simple, particularly if the same police chief is in charge and many of the same officers are on the beat.
3Some residents and civic leaders want to see wholesale changes in leadership or even complete dissolution of the department.
4At the very least, experts said Thursday, Ferguson must move quickly, and publicly, to prove it is serious about regaining public trust.
5The Justice Department on Wednesday cleared Darren Wilson, the white former Ferguson officer who shot Michael Brown, of federal civil rights charges in the death of the 18-year-old, who was black and unarmed.
6But a separate report released simultaneously found patterns of racial profiling, bigotry and profit-driven law enforcement and court practices in the St. Louis County suburb that has come to represent the tension between minorities and American police nationwide.
7Most of Ferguson's police officers and city leaders are white, but two-thirds of the 21,000 residents are black.
8Meanwhile, attorneys for Brown's parents on Thursday announced plans for a wrongful-death lawsuit against the city and Wilson.
9They did not say specifically when the complaint would be filed.
10Brown's parents attended the news conference announcing the case but did not speak or take questions.
11Attorney Anthony Gray said the lawsuit will offer a "more accurate picture" of the fatal confrontation between Brown and the officer.
12"He had other options to him," Gray said of Wilson.
13"He chose deadly force as his option."
14Gray called that choice "unreasonable and unnecessary."
15The Justice Department found that black drivers were more than twice as likely as others to be searched during routine traffic stops.
16Minority residents bear the burden of fines and court costs expected to generate $3 million this fiscal year.
17Black residents were more likely to face excessive force from police, often during unwarranted stops.
18Police Chief Tom Jackson, in a reply to a texted interview request, said only, "I'm still looking into this report."
19Messages seeking comment from Ferguson Mayor James Knowles III and attorneys for Wilson were not returned.
20The report also included seven racially tinged emails that did not result in punishment.
21Knowles said Wednesday that three employees were responsible for those emails.
22One was fired Wednesday, and the other two are on administrative leave pending an investigation, he said.
23The report spurred calls from some for St. Louis County or another municipality to take over the department.
24Others urged Jackson to resign, or for the city to fire him.
25"The chief has no credibility," said state Sen. Maria Chappelle-Nadal, a Democrat who represents Ferguson.
26"How in the world did the chief of police not know that there was an undercurrent of racism that existed in his police department?"
27Other political leaders were more measured in their assessment but still pressed for improvements.
28"Facts exposed in the Department of Justice's report on the Ferguson Police Department are deeply disturbing and demonstrate the urgent need for the reforms I have called for, some of which the General Assembly is now considering, including reforms to municipal courts," Gov. Jay Nixon said in a statement.
29At a brief news conference Wednesday, the mayor tried to show that Ferguson is serious about reform, saying the city has been working for some time to find more black officers.
30Only four of the current 54 commissioned officers are black.
31Associated Press Writer Jim Suhr in St. Louis contributed to this report.
