Baltimore asks Justice Department to review police practices

1Officers put up police tape on Monday, May 4, 2015 in Baltimore, Maryland.
2The city mayor on Wednesday asked the Justice Department to investigate its police department for civil rights violations after the death of a black man from injuries sustained in police custody.
3The mayor of Baltimore called on federal investigators Wednesday to look into whether the city's beleaguered police department uses a pattern of excessive force or discriminatory policing.
4Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake said even though complaints of excessive force and lawsuits alleging misconduct are down over the last few years, "we all know that Baltimore has a fractured relationship with community."
5The mayor's request came a day after new Attorney General Loretta Lynch visited the city.
6In response to the mayor's call for a federal investigation, the Justice Department released a statement saying that it would "actively" consider launching one.
7"The Attorney General is actively considering that option in light of what she heard from law enforcement, city officials, and community, faith and youth leaders in Baltimore yesterday," the statement said.
8Baltimore saw days of unrest after Freddie Gray, a black man, was taken into custody on April 12 and suffered fatal spinal injuries.
9He died a week later.
10Following Gray's funeral on April 27, protestors threw bottles and bricks at police, injuring dozens of officers.
11More than 200 people were arrested as cars and businesses burned.
12The Justice Department is already investigating whether Gray's civil rights were violated, and six officers face charges in his arrest and death, ranging from assault to second-degree murder.
13The new investigation that Rawlings-Blake called for is similar to the one that was done in Ferguson, Missouri, following the shooting death of an unarmed, black teen by a white police officer.
14Such wide-ranging investigations look for patterns of discrimination within a police department.
15They can examine how officers use force and search and arrest suspects.
16Baltimore City Council President Jack Young has been calling for such an investigation since October, said his spokesman Lester Davis.
17"The only way we're going to get the kind of lasting and meaningful reforms that are going to produce results is through a full-scale civil-rights investigation," Davis said.
18At the time the Ferguson inquiry was announced in September, it was described as part of a broader Justice Department effort to investigate troubled police departments and, when pervasive problems are found, direct changes to be made.
19The department said then it has investigated 20 police departments for a variety of systemic misconduct in the past five years, more than twice the number of cases opened in the previous five years.
20The investigations can sometimes result in a settlement known as a consent decree, in which the department agrees to make specific changes, and an outside monitor is appointed to make sure the police force complies with the agreement.
21The Justice Department reached a court-supervised agreement in 2012 with the New Orleans Police Department that required the agency to overhaul its policies and procedures for use of force, training, interrogations, searches and arrests, recruitment and supervision.
22In April, it issued a harshly critical report of the police department in Albuquerque, New Mexico, that faulted the agency for a pattern of excessive force and called for an overhaul of its internal affairs unit.
