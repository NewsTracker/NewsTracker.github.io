Riots in Baltimore raise questions about police response

1Rioters National Guard troops fanned out through the city, shield-bearing police officers blocked the streets and firefighters doused still-simmering blazes early Tuesday as a growing area of Baltimore shuddered from riots following the funeral of a black man who died in police custody.
2The violence that started in West Baltimore on Monday afternoon - within a mile of where Freddie Gray was arrested and placed into a police van earlier this month - had by midnight spread to East Baltimore and neighborhoods close to downtown and near the baseball stadium.
3It was one of the most volatile outbreaks of violence prompted by a police-involved death since the days of protests that followed the death of Michael Brown, an unarmed black man who was shot and killed during a confrontation with a white police officer in Ferguson, Missouri, last summer.
4At least 15 officers were hurt, including six who remained hospitalized late Monday, police said.
5Two dozen people were arrested.
6State and local authorities pledged to restore order and calm to Baltimore, but quickly found themselves responding to questions about whether their initial responses had been adequate.
7Baltimore Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake was asked why she waited hours to ask the governor to declare a state of emergency, while the governor himself hinted she should have come to him earlier.
8"We were all in the command center in the second floor of the State House in constant communication, and we were trying to get in touch with the mayor for quite some time," Gov. Larry Hogan told a Monday evening news conference.
9"She finally made that call, and we immediately took action."
10Asked if the mayor should have called for help sooner, however, Hogan replied that he didn't want to question what Baltimore officials were doing: "They're all under tremendous stress. We're all on one team."
11Rawlings-Blake said officials believed they had gotten the unrest that had erupted over the weekend under control "and I think it would have been inappropriate to bring in the National Guard when we had it under control."
12But later on, Baltimore Police Commissioner Anthony Batts made it clear events had become unmanageable.
13"They just outnumbered us and outflanked us," Batts said.
14"We needed to have more resources out there."
15Police certainly had their work cut out for them: The rioters set police cars and buildings on fire in several neighborhoods, looted a mall and liquor stores and threw rocks at police with riot gear who responded occasionally with pepper spray.
16U.S. Attorney General Loretta Lynch, in her first day on the job, said she would send Justice Department officials to the city in coming days.
17A weeklong, daily curfew was imposed beginning Tuesday from 10 p.m. to 5 a.m., the mayor said, and Baltimore public schools announced they would be closed Tuesday.
18Maj.
19Gen. Linda Singh, adjutant general of the Maryland National Guard, said up to 5,000 troops would be available for Baltimore's streets.
20"We are going to be out in massive force, and that just means basically that we are going to be patrolling the streets and out to ensure that we are protecting property," Singh said at a news conference Monday night.
21Singh said they will be acting at the direction of Baltimore police.
22Col.
23William Pallozzi, the superintendent of the state police, said a request for up to 500 additional law enforcement personnel in Maryland had been sent.
24Pallozzi added that the state is putting out a request for up to 5,000 more law enforcement personnel from around the mid-Atlantic region.
25Maryland Congressman Elijah Cummings and about 200 others, including ministers, tried unsuccessfully to quell the violence at one point Monday night, marching arm-in-arm through a neighborhood littered with broken glass, flattened aluminum cans and other debris.
26As they got close to a line of police officers, the marchers went down on their knees.
27They then rose to their feet and walked until they were face-to-face with the police officers in a tight formation and wearing riot gear.
28But the violence continued, with looters later setting a liquor store on fire and throwing cinder blocks at fire trucks as firefighters labored to put out the blazes.
29Monday's riot was the latest flare-up over the death of Gray and came amid a national debate over police use of force following the high-profile deaths of several black men in encounters with police - from the Brown death in Ferguson to the deaths of Eric Garner in New York and Walter Scott in North Charleston, South Carolina.
30Gray was black.
31Police have declined to specify the races of the six officers involved in his arrest, all of whom have been suspended with pay while they are under investigation.
32While they are angry about what happened to Gray, his family said riots are not the answer.
33"I think the violence is wrong," Gray's twin sister, Fredericka Gray, said late Monday.
34"I don't like it at all."
35The attorney for Gray's family, Billy Murphy, said the family had hoped to organize a peace march later in the week.
36Hours before the riots began Monday, mourners filled the 2,500-capacity New Shiloh Baptist church to attend Freddie Gray's funeral.
37Gray was arrested on April 12 after making eye contact with officers and then running away, police said.
38He was held down, handcuffed and loaded into a van without a seat belt.
39Leg cuffs were put on him when he became irate inside.
40He asked for medical help several times even before being put in the van, but paramedics were not called until after a 30-minute ride.
41Police have acknowledged he should have received medical attention on the spot where he was arrested, but they have not said how he suffered a serious spine injury.
