EU officials discuss possible Greek debt default

1Senior EU officials have formally discussed for the first time a possible Greek debt default as negotiations between Athens and its creditors have stalled ahead of an end-month repayment deadline, several officials have told Reuters news agency.
2The government representatives, preparing next week's Eurogroup meeting of euro zone finance ministers, concluded at talks in Bratislava last night that there were three possible scenarios for what would happen with Greece at the end of June.
3The least likely, they think, is a successful cash-for-reform deal next week in time to meet end-June legal deadlines.
4The second possibility was a further extension of the current bailout programme, which expires this month at the same time as Greece must repay 1.6bn to the IMF.
5The third - discussed formally for the first time at such a senior level in the EU - was to accept Greece could default.
6The meeting reached no decision or concrete conclusion.
7Most officials argued that it was unlikely creditors would strike a deal on reforms with Athens in time to disburse the 7.2bn that remains available to Greece under a rescue programme extended in February for four months.
8"It would require progress in a matter of days that has not been possible in weeks. The reaction of the ECB, the IMF and several member states was extremely sceptical," one official familiar with the discussions said.
9Greece has said it is ready to make "counter-proposals" on a deal to unlock vital bailout funds.
10A government statement said representatives of Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras would make the proposals tomorrow in Brussels.
11Meanwhile, the European Commission President said that stalled debt talks between Athens and its creditors would restart but put the ball firmly in the Greek government's court to come up with an acceptable deal.
12Jean-Claude Juncker spoke after the International Monetary Fund quit negotiations in Brussels yesterday.
13The European Union leader bluntly told Athens to stop "gambling" with its future.
14Time is fast running out for Greece to strike a cash-for-reforms deal with its creditors and stave off a default at the end of June that could see it tumbling out of the euro zone.
15But Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras is showing no signs of alarm.
16His first engagement after rushing home from Brussels last night was an open air concert celebrating the revival of the ERT state TV station, closed exactly two years ago under cuts ordered by the country's EU and IMF lenders.
17Both sides tried to keep hope alive today.
18A senior minister and close adviser to Tsipras said he hoped for a deal on June 18, at a meeting of euro zone finance ministers.
19Mr Juncker also stressed the process was not yet over.
20"Negotiations will start again, first at a technical level, then a political one," he told French radio.
21"A deal is necessary in the coming days," adding: "The ball is in the Greek court."
22Greece needs a deal to unlock aid or loosen curbs on how much it can borrow in short term debt before a 1.6 billion repayment to the IMF falls due by the end of this month.
23Renewed uncertainty sent Greece's top share index down 4% this morning.
24The German tabloid Bild reported that the German government was holding "concrete consultations", including about introducing capital controls restricting bank withdrawals in Greece and transfers abroad should the country go bust.
25Tsipras faces major problems in meeting the promises he made before his election in January.
26He has vowed to end the waves of austerity imposed by previous governments at the lenders' behest.
27But he also needs to keep the country in the euro zone - a poll this week showed an overwhelming 77.4% favoured keeping the euro.
28But the creditors are demanding yet more austerity and refusing to release any aid until Athens backs down, raising the risk of default and a euro zone exit.
29Major sticking points in the talks remain.
30Athens has balked at measures such as curbing pension benefits and raising value-added tax, and pressed lenders for more help to attempt to reduce the debt burden.
31Its own delegation also flew home from Brussels yesterday.
32For a deal to work, EU officials said Tsipras's government needed to come up with new savings and tax measures to replace those that Athens finds unacceptable.
33People familiar with the talks said the two sides have come closer to agreeing a primary surplus target but cannot agree on how to achieve it.
34Juncker said he had given Tsipras a set of propositions in discussions yesterday evening, but it was not immediately clear how Greece would respond, or whether it would send counter proposals to take the talks forward.
