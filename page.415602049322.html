6The team used a process called 'use-wear analysis' which involves examining the surfaces and edges of the tools to determine their function.
7The researchers studied three tools found in the Revadim quarry in Israel - a flake, biface or 'handaxe' and scraper.
8No use-traces or identifiable residue was found on the flake.
9The biface and scraper both revealed 'well preserved and clear use-wear traces'.
10The biface use traces indicate the processing of 'medium-hard material' believed to be evidence of hide scraping.
11The scraper instead featured traces of 'soft or medium material'.
12This is believed to be animal tissue.
13Both tools showed residue of adipocere, or animal fat and on the scraper, both tissues and vegetable residues were found.
14It also used so-called Fourier Transform InfraRed (FTIR) residue analysis to identify organic residue.
15FTIR uses infrared to identify bio-signatures of prehistoric compounds.
16From this, the researchers were able to demonstrate for the first time direct proof of animal exploitation by flint tools.
17'There are three parts to this puzzle: the expansion of the human brain, the shift to meat consumption, and the ability to develop sophisticated technology to meet the new biological demands,' Professor Barkai said.
18Fracturing rocks in order to butcher and cut animal meat is said to represent a key biological and cultural milestone.
19'At the Revadim quarry we found butchered animal remains, including an elephant rib bone which had been neatly cut by a stone tool, alongside flint handaxes and scrapers still retaining animal fat,' continued Professor Barkai.
