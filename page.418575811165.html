James Holmes jury's choice: Was Aurora theater massacre murder or madness?

1James Holmes jury's choice: Was Aurora theater massacre murder or madness?
2It belongs to James Eagan Holmes, a failed neuroscience graduate student who became one of the nation's worst mass shooters when he opened fire at a midnight show in a crowded movie theater in July 2012.
3Looking into the depths of Holmes' psyche hasn't been a pleasant or comfortable journey, and now nine women and three men have the unenviable task of deciding this question: Was Holmes' movie theater massacre an act of murder -- or madness?
4Some 400 people had gone to the midnight premiere of the Batman movie "The Dark Knight Rises" on July 20, 2012 in Aurora, Colorado.
5They hoped to be entertained by a hero dressed in black, "someone who would fight insurmountable odds for justice," as prosecutor George Brauchler told jurors Tuesday in his closing argument.
6Instead, a shooter -- also dressed in black -- came gunning for them.
7Twelve people died and 70 others were wounded.
8For many of the survivors, going to the movies would never again be simple or fun.
9"He came there with one thing in his heart and his mind and that was mass murder," Brauchler said, pointing at Holmes, who sat passively at the defense table, occasionally rocking back and forth.
10"That guy was sane beyond a reasonable doubt, and he needs to be held accountable for what he did."
11Nobody disputes that Holmes was the gunman in black.
12And nobody is disputing that he was and is severely mentally ill.
13But was he so sick that he couldn't appreciate that what he was doing was wrong?
14So sick that he shouldn't be convicted of murder?

70In Colorado, the burden rests on the prosecution to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that Holmes was sane.
71Most states and the federal government place the burden on the defendant to prove insanity.
72Not surprisingly, the experts called to the witness stand were divided in their opinions.
73Two court-appointed psychiatrists -- William Reid and Jeff Metzner -- said Holmes was mentally ill but sane at the time he committed the crime.
74He knew what he was doing, and he knew that it was wrong, they testified.
75The judge told jurors they should find Holmes legally insane if "he was so diseased or defective in mind at the time of the commission of the act as to be incapable of distinguishing right from wrong with respect to that act."
76Jurors can also find him insane under an alternative theory, that a mental disease or defect "prevented him from forming a culpable mental state."
77Jurors were cautioned not to confuse insanity with "moral obliquity, mental depravity or passion growing out of anger, revenge, hatred or other motives and kindred evil conditions."
78In other words, there's insanity -- and then there's just plain old evil.
79Indeed, Holmes acknowledged to Reid during hours of videotaped sessions that killing was "legally wrong," adding, "You get punished for killing."
80And, he said, "It's wrong to kill children."
81Two defense psychiatrists -- including the University of Pennsylvania's Raquel Gur, a nationally recognized expert on schizophrenia -- testified that Holmes was so psychotic, his view of reality so warped, that he fit the criteria for legal insanity.
82"The severe defect in his brain made him incapable of distinguishing right from wrong by societal standards," she said.
83Defense attorney Daniel King urged jurors to set aside their fears, prejudices and discomfort and confront the issue of mental illness head on in their deliberations.
84Society is not comfortable talking about mental illness, King said, and even Holmes feared the stigma enough to hide his mental illness from his family and doctors.
85"Accept the mental illness," he urged the jury.
86"Acknowledge the fact that the overwhelming evidence is that Mr. Holmes was in the throes of a psychotic episode. We can't separate the mental illness from him, or from this crime, because the mental illness is the sole reason for this crime taking place."
87Jurors began grappling with the question on Wednesday morning.
88By the end of their first day of deliberations, they'd already sent back four questions for the judge.
89Their written instructions from Judge Carlos Samour Jr. are more than an inch thick, and the verdict form is 650 pages long.
90Jurors must consider 165 counts -- murder, attempted murder and weapons offenses related to the massacre, and explosives charges in connection with Holmes' effort to booby-trap his apartment as a diversion.
91If jurors decide Holmes is sane -- and guilty -- the case will proceed to a penalty phase, estimated to last a month, and then they will be asked to make another, even more difficult decision: Should Holmes pay for his crimes with his life?
92If the jury finds Holmes guilty of a lesser charge -- second-degree murder or manslaughter -- then he will likely spend the rest of his days in prison.
93And, if his insanity defense results in a "not guilty" verdict, Holmes will be sent to a state mental hospital.
94The defense asserts Holmes has struggled with mental illness and homicidal thoughts since his early teens.
95By the time he opened fire, he had been battling what he called his "broken brain" for a decade.
96He was exhausted and only getting sicker.
97He had always felt something was wrong with him, according to testimony, and his studies in neuroscience may have been his way of trying to fix himself.
98His childhood seemed normal, but he became withdrawn at age 12, when his family moved from Castroville to San Diego.
99He didn't want to leave his friends and slid into depression.
100His concerned parents sent him to a therapist, and he was diagnosed with something called "oppositional defiant disorder."
101He described his battle with mental illness in a notebook he mailed hours before the shootings to Lynne Fenton, a University of Colorado psychiatrist who was treating him at a campus clinic.
102He had sought help for his social awkwardness but did not tell her about his homicidal thoughts.
103The notebook wasn't discovered until days after the movie theater massacre.
104Holmes began seeing Fenton in early 2012, but by then his mental illness was winning the battle.
105He'd always done well in school, but he was failing.
106He broke up with the first girlfriend he'd ever had, and he lost his best friend at grad school.
107Why? Because he told them about his homicidal thoughts -- the first time he'd dared articulate them to anyone.
108In his twisted mind, defense attorney King said, Holmes now felt compelled to act on his homicidal impulses because he had spoken out loud about them.
109He had made them real.
110He now viewed killing as his "mission," King said.
111And so, the defense attorney argued, Holmes was beyond reason at the time he opened fire in the theater.
112He had lost touch with reality.
113Holmes bought a ticket and entered Theater 9 in the Century Aurora 16 movie complex around midnight, choosing a front-row seat.
114He pretended to take a phone call and left through an emergency exit, propping the door open with a plastic doorstop.
115He returned 18 minutes into the movie, tossing a tear gas grenade into the audience.
116He then opened fire with a 12-gauge pump action shotgun and then an AR-15 style semi-automatic rifle with a high-capacity clip that jammed.
117He also fired several shots with a Glock 40-caliber pistol.
118Starting at about 12:30 a.m., 41 calls came in rapid succession to 911.
119The Aurora police station is just three minutes away, and the first officers arrived to find a horrific scene: Blood, body tissue and spilled popcorn were everywhere, the air was filled with tear gas and gun smoke, and panicked people were screaming.
120Cell phones were going off but weren't being answered.
121Ten people lay dead in the theater, and two more would be pronounced dead at hospitals.
122Holmes had brought 700 rounds of ammunition.
123Metal piercing bullets went through seats -- and even through walls into Theater 8.
124He dressed in protective gear and took a painkiller in case he was injured during his capture by police.
125He feared being killed, and he did not want to feel pain.
126The defense acknowledged that some of Holmes' thoughts and actions might seem rational.
127He was able to plan intricately.
128But, they argue, the planning supported his primary delusion -- what Holmes called his "life capital" theory.
129It was spelled out in the notebook he'd mailed to Fenton.
130Holmes also shared details of his thinking during hours of videotaped sessions with Reid, the court-appointed psychiatrist.
131In Holmes' skewed worldview, each life he took was worth a point, adding value to his own life.
132If his life had value, he reasoned, he wouldn't have to kill himself.
133And so, by the end of the theater shooting, Holmes believed he had raised his "life capital" to 13, adding all the qualities and experiences of the people he killed to his own.
134"The dead can't be repaired," he told Reid.
135"It's kind of irreversible."
136He had accomplished his mission, even if getting arrested was the price he had to pay.
137But he said he gained nothing from injuring people or leaving them behind to grieve for the dead.
138He spoke of the 70 people wounded as "collateral damage."
139Prosecutor Brauchler says Holmes' actions -- dressing in protective gear and taking painkillers for fear of being shot or killed by police -- sounds like the thinking of a rational person.
140A sane person.
141But the defense argued that Holmes didn't need to be catatonic or babbling gibberish to fit the criteria for legal insanity.
142Some of his actions seem sensible -- until one considers they are being done to support his delusional "life capital" plan.
143"His delusional process cannot be separated from him. It's part of his being," said defense attorney King.
144"You cannot divorce the mental illness from this case, or from Mr. Holmes, because the mental illness caused this to happen. Only the mental illness caused this to happen, and nothing else."
145Surely only a madman could do such a thing, to shoot into a crowded movie theater.
146And there's a lot about Holmes' story that seems, well, crazy.
147He dyed his hair orange, posed for selfies wearing diabolical black contact lenses, armed himself to the teeth and donned what a prosecutor called his "kill suit."
148He fired metal-piercing bullets into the crowd.
149He would have fired more but the gun jammed.
150And so he walked calmly outside to his car and surrendered to police.
151Brauchler says surrendering is what any rational person would do in that situation.
152He points to the uneventful arrest as a sure sign the defendant was sane at the time of the shooting.
153And Holmes was able to plan the attack with meticulous detail, he added.
154Could an insane person do that?
155But just hours later, a different side of Holmes emerged at the Aurora police station.
156He seemed almost childish.
157When detectives covered his hands with paper bags to preserve gunshot residue, he played with the bags as if they were puppets.
158Would any sane person accused of multiple murders do that?
159The defense attorneys maintain that Holmes was in the throes of a psychotic breakdown.
160It had been building up for a long time and had gotten progressively worse between March and late July 2012.
161He exhibited some symptoms at that time, but many more emerged during the first few months he was in jail.
162The psychosis was so intrusive, according the defense experts, that Holmes' high IQ dropped by seven points.
163In hindsight, there may have been some red flags.
164Fenton and a colleague treating Holmes at the university clinic grew concerned he was headed toward a breakdown.
165But he stopped seeing them when he withdrew from school.
166And he hid aspects of his disease from them, saving his homicidal thoughts for the notebook.
167The loss of his friends after he told them about his homicidal thoughts should have put Holmes on warning that society frowned on killing, that what he was planning was legally and morally wrong, Brauchler argued.
168But a person in the grips of a psychotic delusion doesn't pick up on social cues, defense attorney King countered.
169Once he was in jail, Holmes was a compliant prisoner, but his mental illness worsened.
170He experienced what doctors believe was a psychotic break nearly five months after his arrest -- on November 12, 2012.
171By then, shadows and voices haunted the walls of his cell, urging Holmes not to eat, especially the red food.
172He banged his head on the cell walls, raising big bruises on his forehead, and fell over backward from his bunk to the hard floor.
173Asked why he'd done that, he responded that he was saving his captors from himself.
174He smeared feces throughout his cell and then curled into a catatonic ball on the floor, his limbs contorted and seemingly frozen at odd angles.
175In an ambulance, on the way to a hospital, he babbled nonsense.
176"You can't teach an old dog new tricks," he mumbled, over and over, like a mantra.
177He seems better now, or at least his behavior does.
178He has been a passive presence during 50 days in court.
179But, his lawyers say, Holmes still holds on to the delusion that he can add value to his life by killing others.
