1The development Thursday came as both groups - among the most ruthless in the world - are under increasing military pressure and have sustained setbacks on the battlefield.
2Islamic State seized much of northern and western Iraq last summer, gaining control of about a third of both Iraq and Syria.
3But it is now struggling against Iraqi forces seeking to recapture Saddam Hussein's hometown of Tikrit, while coming under fire from U.S.-led coalition air strikes in other parts of the country and in Syria.
4Boko Haram, meanwhile, has been weakened by a multinational force that has dislodged it from a score of northeastern Nigerian towns.
5But its new Twitter account, increasingly slick and more frequent video messages, and a new media arm all were considered signs that the group is now being helped by IS propagandists.
6Then on Saturday, Boko Haram leader Abubakar Shekau posted an audio recording online that pledged allegiance to IS.
7"We announce our allegiance to the Caliph of the Muslims & and will hear and obey in times of difficulty and prosperity, in hardship and ease, and to endure being discriminated against, and not to dispute about rule with those in power, except in case of evident infidelity regarding that which there is a proof from Allah," said Boko Haram's message.
8On Thursday, the Islamic State group's media arm Al-Furqan, in an audio recording by spokesman Abu Mohammed al-Adnani, said that Boko Haram's pledge of allegiance has been accepted, claiming the caliphate has now expanded to West Africa and that "no one can stand in its path."
9Al-Adnani had urged foreign fighters from around the world to migrate and join Boko Haram.
10He also sent a message to Christians and other non-Muslims in IS lands to convert to Islam or pay a special tax - something the extremists have already put into practice in IS-held territory in Iraq and Syria.
11"If you insist on being arrogant and stubborn & soon you will bite your fingers off in regret," he added.
12Peter Pham, director of the Africa Center at the Atlantic Council, a think tank in Washington, noted the Islamic State group's quick acceptance of Boko Haram's allegiance and said that the bond highlights a new risk.
13"Militants finding it increasingly harder to get to Syria and Iraq may choose instead to go to northeastern Nigeria and internationalize that conflict," Pham wrote in an email to The Associated Press.
14In the past - as was the case with IS affiliates in Egypt, Yemen and Libya - it took weeks for the Islamic State to respond to a pledge of allegiance.
15"The prompt - one might even say 'fast-tracked' - acceptance by the so-called Islamic State of Boko Haram's pledge of allegiance" underscores that both needed the propaganda boost from the affiliation, Pham added.
16The Boko Haram's pledge comes as the militants reportedly were massing in the northeastern Nigerian town of Gwoza, considered their headquarters, for a showdown with the Chadian-led multinational force.
17Boko Haram killed an estimated 10,000 people last year, and it is blamed for last April's abduction of more than 275 schoolgirls.

16Kosovo is one of the poorest countries in Europe with an unemployment rate of 55.9 percent among young people aged 15-24.
17Among young women, 68.4 percent are unemployed, according to the World Bank.
18Both economic and political tensions in Kosovo sparked this staggering exodus.
19"The mass emigration during the winter of discontent 2014-15, paired with a series of violent anti-government protests and strikes in key sectors, including health and education, reflect the social tension that has been brewing over the years and the perceived lack of an economic perspective even for the well-educated," Jan-Peter Olters, the World Bank country manager in Kosovo, said.
20Kosovo, which declared independence from Serbia in 2008, is recognised by 108 UN member states.
21Europe's youngest country has been stricken by political unrest and turmoil.
22The new government was formed six months after inconclusive elections in June.
23"Kosovo is the most isolated country in Europe. It is the only country that needs a visa to travel to the EU," Olters said.
24In September, Serbia relaxed travel rules for Kosovars.
25Crowded buses started leaving Pristina every night, bound for Belgrade.
26From there, they went to the Serbian-Hungarian border by bus or taxi, where many crossed on foot.
27Once inside Hungary, they can travel freely to other EU countries thanks to the Schengen Agreement.
28The destination for most Kosovars is Germany, the perceived economic powerhouse of Europe and home to the largest Kosovar diaspora aside from Switzerland.

35Haziro, who is now waiting in an asylum centre in Augsburg, decided to leave Kosovo after he heard rumours that Germany was looking for Kosovars, and that it has an "open door" policy.
36Some even heard that Germany will give them 4,000 euros ($4,340) as a welcome gift.
37"Smugglers and human traffickers have profited from the rumour mill and spurred it on," said Germany's Representative for the UN Refugee Agency UNHCR, Hans ten Feld.
38"They created false hopes among people that a better life was waiting for them in the EU."
39Smugglers charged Kosovars about 250-500 euros ($270-$540) per head to help them get into the EU.
40Some rumours are even true.
41Germany provides accommodation for every asylum seeker, gives them daily meals, and pocket money.
42After three months, they are allowed to work.
43However, most won't be allowed to stay for long.
44The approval rate for Kosovars is currently at 0.3 percent.
45In January, only two Kosovar asylum requests were approved.
46That month, 3,630 had officially applied for asylum - the number of yet unregistered Kosovars is much higher.
47In February, German authorities even decided to prioritise and expedite Kosovar asylum claims, to accept or reject them within 14 days.
48Most Kosovars are being rejected because they are economic migrants and aren't fleeing from war or ethnic or religious persecution - the prerequisite for asylum.
49Some Roma, members of the minority also called Gypsies, have been granted asylum because they could prove discrimination and that they fear for their safety.
50Haziro was surprised when he heard most Kosovars have slim chances of getting accepted.
51He's been waiting for one month now, but, he said, "I think they will say yes because they need people for work."
52Ten Feld said: "Seeking the asylum route isn't a viable option to gain access to the EU. The solution lies within Kosovo. It is in the interest of the government to retain its labour force and to be able to provide a future for its people and the country."
53Germany, Austria and others have started an awareness campaign in Kosovo to dispel misinformation.
54Kosovo's President Atifete Jahjaga urged people not to leave the country during a visit to Mitrovica with a German representative.
55"We know that Kosovo is not the country we wished to have, but 15 years ago we have come out of a terrible war, which has left huge consequences. These are challenges of the transition, transformation process. These are the challenges of our state building," Jahjaga said in a statement.
56"Our citizens must not fall prey of criminal groups and illegal groups, seeking to find a better life, because their life and their future is within Kosovo," she added.
57"The message is that all our citizens who have taken their road towards illegal roads, their asylum will be rejected and they will be returned to Kosovo."
58The awareness measures seem to have been effective and numbers leaving have declined.
59New arrivals from Kosovo into Germany have stagnated.
60However, the tens of thousands that have left are still waiting in EU countries for the outcome of their asylum claims.
61Deportations have begun in Germany, Austria and Hungary, with many being put on charter flights back to Pristina.
62German Foreign Minister Frank-Walter Steinmeier pledged one million euros to Kosovo, which will be used in projects to reintegrate returnees and to help people to set up their own businesses.
63"Reintegrating the returnees will be difficult," Olters said.
64"Their disappointment will be worse than before they left. They have been unsuccessful in trying to start a new life abroad and they are returning poorer, because many sold their possessions and took on debt to pay for the journey."
65"The government was shocked by this sudden exodus. They didn't expect buses full of people leaving daily. It can affect the country's stability. It could also be a wake-up call.
66"Now, a page could be turned to create better opportunities in the country and provide people with a perspective."
67It's a long road ahead that requires patience before any improvements can be seen.
68But if the tens of thousands who have left in a matter of just weeks are any indication, patience is a virtue that Kosovars have little left of.
