Second shot: Hillary Clinton set to run again for president

1The former first lady, U.S. senator and secretary of state planned to enter the 2016 Democratic race with an online video posted on social media, to be followed in the days ahead with small events with voters in early-voting states.
2Campaign officials said she would seek the White House as a results-oriented "tenacious fighter."
3The opening strategy was described ahead of the announcement by two senior advisers who requested anonymity to discuss her plans.
4Clinton's strategy has parallels to the approach President Barack Obama took in 2012.
5He framed his re-election as a choice between Democrats focused on the middle class and Republicans who sought to protect the wealthy and return to policies that led the country into recession.
6Republicans pounced even before Clinton's announcement.
7Republican National Committee chairman Reince Priebus has outlined plans for a broad effort to try to undermine Clinton's record as secretary of state while tying her to Obama's most unpopular policies.
8Early Sunday, potential GOP rivals Jeb Bush and Rand Paul previewed their case against Clinton.
9"We must do better than the Obama-Clinton foreign policy that has damaged relationships with our allies and emboldened our enemies," Bush, a former Florida governor, said in a video.
10Paul, a Kentucky senator who launched his presidential campaign last week, pointed to the Clinton family's foundation, saying it was hypocritical for the Clintons to accept money from Saudi Arabia, which places public restrictions on female movement and activity.
11"I would expect Hillary Clinton if she believes in women's rights, she should be calling for a boycott of Saudi Arabia," Paul said on NBC's "Meet the Press."
12"Instead, she's accepting tens of millions of dollars."
13Clinton intends to sell herself as being able to work with Congress, businesses and world leaders, the advisers said Saturday.
14That approach could be perceived as a critique of Obama, who has largely been unable to fulfill his pledge to end Washington's intense partisanship and found much of his presidency stymied by gridlock with Congress.
15Clinton was not expected to roll out detailed policy positions in the first weeks of her campaign.
16Advisers said she planned to talk about ways families can increase take-home pay, the importance of expanding early childhood education and making higher education more affordable.
17It's not clear whether that would include a noticeable break with Obama on economic policy.
18The GOP has hammered Obama's approach as anti-business and insufficient in the wake of the recession.
19The White House says the economy has improved significantly in recent years.
20Clinton is seen as the overwhelming favorite for her party's nomination.
21Still, her team has said her early strategy is designed to avoid appearing to take that nomination for granted.
22The early events were expected to include discussions at colleges, day care centers and private homes, and stops at coffee shops and diners.
23After about a month of such events, Clinton planned to give more specifics about her rationale for running.
24Clinton's husband, Bill, and daughter, Chelsea, are unlikely to appear at her early events.
25Bill Clinton, the former two-term president, said recently that he wanted to play a role as a "backstage adviser" in his wife's campaign.
26Advisers said Bill Clinton has been engaged with his wife in some of the policy discussions leading up to this weekend's rollout.
27To prepare for the campaign, Clinton has spent months meeting with economic policy experts.
28Clinton's growing team of staffers began working Friday out of a new campaign headquarters in Brooklyn.
29They gathered Saturday to hear from campaign manager-in-waiting Robby Mook, who told them the campaign would value teamwork, respect, diversity, discipline and humility.
30A Democratic official in attendance described the meeting on condition of anonymity because it was a private strategy session.
31AP White House correspondent Julie Pace in Chicago contributed to this report.
