My memory of Myuran Sukumaran: Chilean's tribute to friend

1Deportes Valdivia shows its support for mercy for Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran.
2People will remember the names Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran long after they are gone.
3In their fight for life they commanded global attention, but they also left a lasting impression upon the many whose paths they crossed on the road to their execution.
4Nicolas Osnovikoff is one such person.
5The 30-year-old Chilean football manager met Sukumaran in 2013, after a long chain of email correspondence.
6In the art room of Kerobokan prison, where Sukumaran spent so much of his time on death row, Mr Osnovikoff bought one of the inmate's paintings - a self-portrait, for $500.
7Nicolas Osnovikoff with the self-portrait of Myuran Sukumaran he bought for $500 in 2013.
8"I felt very comfortable with him and after watching most of the paintings, I chose one and made him an offer," he says.
9" 'Rupiah, you mean?' " he recalls Sukumaran saying.
10" 'No, dollars!' " he answered.
11"He [Sukumaran] looked very happy and grateful and I was also very happy with my painting."
12Mr Osnovikoff today took the painting to the Indonesian embassy in Santiago, hoping to see ambassador Aloysius Lele.
13It was his way of showing support for his friend, who, along with Chan, is due to be executed in the early hours of Wednesday morning, Sydney time.
14The self-portrait outside the Indonesian embassy in Santiago, Chile.
15He could not see the ambassador but is determined to try again tomorrow.
16"I don't know what I am going to do, but I will do something," he says.
17Mr Osnovikoff has something of a fascination with Australians imprisoned in Indonesia.
18The story of the Bali nine first came to his attention after watching the documentary Ganja Queen, about convicted drug smuggler Schapelle Corby.
19He began to research Chan and Sukumaran's trial and imprisonment and was immediately impressed with their apparent rehabilitation.
20In October 2013, following a working holiday in Australia, Mr Osnovikoff visited Bali and met Sukumaran at Kerobokan.
21"I needed to discover, to see with my eyes, to feel how he was dealing with all this. I needed to learn from him," he says.
22The experience changed his life forever, he says.
23It is politeness that figures most prominently in his assessment of Sukumaran - a man who was "a little shy", but unfailing in his civility and grace.
24News about the 10 death row prisoners in Indonesia had been limited in Chile, Mr Osnovikoff says, until the 72-hour notice was given at the weekend.
25Since then there has been significant coverage in the country's newspapers.
26Still, not many people were familiar with the prisoners' full stories, he says.
27Mr Osnovikoff says when he tells people about his experience with Sukumaran, they tend to change their opinion of the pair and support clemency for them.
28A director of Deportes , a Chilean football club, his team has pledged its support for the "mercy" campaign, which has called on Indonesian President Joko Widodo to grant clemency.
29"I think that Indonesia is wrong. The world will make them know that they are not taking the right decision," Mr Osnovikoff says.
30"If I can directly say to Joko Widodo, it's not just Australia, it's not just France or Brazil, it's the world, it's Chile, it's all the Latin American countries, it's everyone that has love, that doesn't want you to kill people. Everything can be solved in different ways."
31Mr Osnovikoff says "there are still many hours of hope" although he admits he feels powerless, especially so far away in Santiago, to intervene in any meaningful way.
32But he says he has learned from Sukumaran, whom he calls an example of humanity, strength, solidarity and humility.
33"And an example of forgiveness," he adds.
34"Because we have to start loving and forgiving first."
