Pakistan judge: Charge CIA lawyer, agent for drone strike

1A Pakistani judge on Tuesday ordered criminal charges be filed against a former top CIA lawyer who oversaw its drone program and a former station chief in Islamabad over a 2009 strike that killed two people.
2Former acting general counsel John A. Rizzo and ex-station chief Jonathan Bank must face charges including murder, conspiracy, waging war against Pakistan and terrorism, Justice Shaukat Aziz Siddiqui of the Islamabad High Court ruled.
3A court clerk and a lawyer involved the case, Mirza Shahzad Akbar, confirmed details of the judge's ruling.
4Rizzo and Bank could not be immediately reached for comment.
5The CIA declined to immediately comment when reached by The Associated Press.
6Bank fled Pakistan in 2010 after his cover was blown when a Pakistani man named Kareem Khan initially threatened to sue the CIA and others for $500 million over the deaths of his 18-year-old son, Zaenullah Khan, and his brother, Asif Iqbal, in a purported Dec. 31, 2009, strike on the North Waziristan tribal region.
7The AP and other media organizations reported at the time that three people were killed in a missile attack that day in Mir Ali in North Waziristan.
8Pakistani intelligence officials said then that the men were militants, but offered no proof.
9As the outrage over the lawsuit grew, protesters in Islamabad began carrying placards bearing Bank's name as listed in the lawsuit, urging him to leave the country.
10The CIA didn't move Bank, who had previously served in Baghdad, until he began receiving death threats.
11The station chief's outing spurred questions at the time of whether Pakistan's spy service might have leaked the information, something the Pakistanis denied.
12The disclosure didn't prevent Bank from landing another sensitive job: He became chief of the Iran operations division at CIA headquarters at Langley.
13He later was removed from that post after CIA officials concluded he created a hostile work environment in the division.
14He has since been detailed to the Pentagon's intelligence arm.
15Rizzo was the CIA's acting general counsel overseeing its drone program.
16He later left the agency and wrote a book about his experiences at the agency.
17The CIA's drone strike program killed Pakistani Taliban fighters and other militants hiding in its tribal regions, but caused major anger across Pakistan over civilian casualties from the strikes.
18Since 2004, the U.S. has carried out nearly 400 suspected drone strikes in the country, according to the New America Foundation's International Security Program, which tracks the American campaign.
19The foundation says the last U.S. drone strike in Pakistan happened on Jan. 29, which killed at least six suspected militants.
20It's not clear how the judge's ruling will affect relations between Pakistan and the U.S., especially over the drone program.
21While Pakistan's government often decries the strikes, many believe it allows them in order to target the militants that threaten it.
22Massive protests against the drone program previously have blocked a land route used by NATO forces to resupply troops in neighboring Afghanistan.
23Any legal action stands no chance of success unless U.S. officials cooperate with the court, something highly unlikely given the secretive nature of the missile strike program.
24The CIA rarely publicly discusses its drone program.
25Under the judge's order, Pakistan's federal police force must file the charges against the two former CIA officials, though they've so far refused over what lawyers describe as a reluctance to upset the country's diplomatic relations with the U.S. Pakistani police officials could not be immediately reached for comment Tuesday.
26Pakistani lawyer Khawaja Naveed Ahmed, who is not involved in the case, said the government also could appeal the judge's order to a higher court to try and stop having to file the charges.
