Greek invites IMF to join bailout talks, should begin Monday

1Greece's Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras speaks with a journalist during a meeting with the Greek President and other political party leaders, ahead of a meal to mark the 41st anniversary of the country's return to democracy after the 1974 fall of the junta then ruling Greece, at the presidential palace in Athens, Friday, July 24, 2015.
2High-level talks between Greece and its European creditors on a third bailout for the cash-strapped country have been delayed until logistical matters are sorted out.
3ATHENS, Greece (AP) - Greece on Friday invited the International Monetary Fund to participate in its negotiations with European creditors over a vital third bailout - talks that are expected to start next week after a few days' delay and must conclude before Greece faces another big repayment Aug. 20.
4Negotiators are now expected to arrive in Athens over the weekend with talks probably starting Monday, Greek officials said.
5In a statement Friday evening, the IMF confirmed it had received Greece's request for a new IMF loan and said it would work to arrange talks with Greece and its European creditors.
6Athens is looking to secure yet another bailout - the third since its finances imploded in 2009 - worth 85 billion euros ($93 billion) over three years.
7Without the money, the country faces imminent bankruptcy and a probable exit from the shared euro currency.
8The letter to the IMF, signed by Finance Minister Euclid Tsakalotos, formally requests a new bailout from the fund.
9That is in accordance with the preliminary third bailout agreement Greece struck with its European partners on July 12, which called for IMF financing and monitoring for Greece from March 2016 - when current IMF financing ends.
10The letter said Athens believes it will take "several quarters" before the Greek economy faces up to its challenges "and returns to a vigorous and sustainable path to growth with fairness and social inclusion."
11"We look forward to continued cooperation with the Fund," it added.
12Greek government spokeswoman Olga Gerovasili said the final third bailout agreement will be brought to Greece's parliament for approval on Aug. 18.
13"Clearly, the negotiations will be constant until then," she said Friday.
14Greece has a debt repayment of around 3.2 billion euros ($3.5 billion) to the European Central Bank on Aug. 20.
15Greece's five-year financial crisis took a dramatic turn for the worse this summer, after talks between its radical left-led government elected in January and the country's creditors nearly collapsed amid sharp disagreements over the reforms required in return for the rescue loans.
16The new bailout was only possible after Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras made a sharp U-turn from years of vehemently opposing further cutbacks.
17Since its first bailout in 2010, Greece's economy has shrunk by a quarter, while unemployment has rocketed to record peacetime highs and incomes have shrunk an average of 40 percent.
18Much of the pain has been attributed to the income cuts and tax hikes demanded by creditors.
19It's unclear to what extent IMF participation complicates the latest bailout talks.
20The fund has been critical of many of the demands insisted upon by Greece's European creditors but it also says Greece needs deep, meaningful debt relief - something that Germany and other European nations are dead set against.
21Gerovasili said the IMF position on Greece's crippling debt load - the highest in the 19-country eurozone - "is clearly something we agree with."
22But she added the fund "is tougher in negotiations, with harsher terms, and is not very agreeable to us as a negotiator."
23Gerovasili dismissed reports that security concerns delayed the talks Friday, blaming "technical issues" instead.
24"Greece is a safe country," Gerovasili said.
25"Both sides are trying to expedite the start of talks."
26The final hurdle Greece had to clear before talks could restart came early Thursday when Greek lawmakers approved creditor-demanded judicial and banking reforms.
27A week earlier, parliament approved new laws introducing steep sales tax increases.
28In spite of a revolt from his own Syriza party, Tsipras managed to get both reforms passed with the help of pro-European opposition parties.
29Top Greek banking and finance ministry officials met with Greek business leaders Friday to discuss ways of easing financial transactions, which are being restricted by the capital controls introduced last month.
