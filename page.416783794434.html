Engineers inspect quake-damaged homes in Nepal's capital

1An elderly Nepalese walks past a damaged temple performing rituals to end the mourning period of a family member who died in the April 25 earthquake, in Bhaktapur, Nepal, Wednesday, May 6, 2015.
2The April 25 earthquake killed thousands and injured many more as it flattened mountain villages and destroyed buildings and archaeological sites in Kathmandu.
3KATHMANDU, Nepal (AP) - More than a thousand engineers were checking damaged houses in Nepal's capital Wednesday and advising people about whether they are safe.
4About 13,000 families have requested inspections of their homes since the massive magnitude-7.8 earthquake near Kathmandu on April 25, Nepal Engineers Association General Secretary Kishore Kumar Jha said.
5More than 7,600 people died in the quake.
6The association, which has 2,000 civil and structural engineers, has set up phone lines for people to request inspections.
7"We have been receiving calls constantly. We are trying to reach as many people as possible," Jha said.
8About 40 percent of the damaged houses inspected so far were considered safe, he said.
9It is still unclear how many houses were damaged in the capital and how many are repairable.
10Some modern buildings - including upscale hotels and expensive homes - appear to have escaped largely unscathed.
11But there is widespread damage in poorer neighborhoods.
12Much of Kathmandu's so-called old city area, home to many of its precious world heritage buildings, was destroyed.
13Many villages outside the capital also were completely flattened.
14As aftershocks continue to shake the capital, many people remain afraid to return to their homes.
15Police say about one-third of Kathmandu's population has left the city since the earthquake.
16Many others have moved in with relatives, while some are staying in tents in open areas.
17The confirmed death toll from the quake rose to 7,652, with more than 14,500 injured, Deputy Inspector General of Police Kamal Singhbam said in Kathmandu.
18On Wednesday, some families of victims marked the end of the traditional Hindu mourning period.
19Many others will mark the day on Thursday, the 13th day since the earthquake.
20It is customary among Hindus in Nepal to mourn for 13 days.
21During this time close family members stay at home, do not touch outsiders and refrain from eating salt.
22No entertainment is allowed.
