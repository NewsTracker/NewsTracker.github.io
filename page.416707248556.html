101-year-old man found after Nepal quake

1Photo: Funchu Tamang, 101, was rescued seven days after his house collapsed.
2A 101-year-old man was taken to hospital after being found alive in the grounds of his home in Nepal seven days after it collapsed in a deadly earthquake, police say.
3Funchu Tamang was rescued on Saturday with only minor injuries and airlifted to a district hospital, local police officer Arun Kumar Singh said.
4"He was brought to the district hospital in a helicopter. His condition is stable," Mr Singh said.
5"He has injuries on his left ankle and hand. His family is with him."
6Police had initially said that Mr Tamang was trapped under the rubble of his home ever since the quake struck on April 25, but they later said he was in fact rescued from his garden where he had been sheltering since the disaster.
7"We are far from the place where the 101-year-old man was rescued. It is very hard for us to communicate well among each other," Mr Singh said on Monday.
8"The rescue is the prime duty and it does not matter now whether security personnel pulled him from rubble or a garden."
9The government said the death toll from the earthquake had reached 7,040 and 14,123 people had been injured.
10A police team from Nepal pulled out the bodies of about 50 people, including some foreign trekkers, from an avalanche-hit area on Saturday, officials said.
11None of the bodies have been identified, deputy superintendent of police in the northern district of Rasuwa, Pravin Pokharel, said.
12Kathmandu's tiny international airport has been operating round the clock to allow aid flights to land, but a shortage of parking space and damage to the runway has meant some aircraft have been turned away.
13The manager at the airport said large planes carrying relief supplies had been banned from landing because of pot holes on the runway.
14"This runway is the only lifeline for Kathmandu," airport manager Birendra Prasad Shrestha said.
15Authorities announced that larger aircraft of 196 tonnes and over will not be allowed to land or take off because of the condition of the runway.
16This would mean a 747-size plane, full of emergency essentials, would not be able to get into Nepal.
17Some officials have denied reports of cracks appearing on the runway, saying the move to stop larger planes from landing is simply a precautionary measure.
18More than a week after the magnitude-7.8 earthquake, large swathes of the Himalayan nation have yet to receive any outside help as aid workers battle landslides, avalanches and a helicopter shortage to reach communities in some of the world's most remote terrain.
19Relief workers have said it is now a race against time to get desperately needed shelter, food and clean water to survivors in the far-flung mountain villages flattened by the disaster before the monsoon rains begin in June.
20But many of the worst-hit communities are tiny villages perched on the side of mountains that are inaccessible by road and where it is difficult or impossible for helicopters to land.
21The country's poor infrastructure and a weak national government beset by in-fighting among coalition partners have compounded the difficulties of mounting a vast emergency relief operation in the world's highest mountains.
22"One of the big challenges of working in Nepal, and we knew that this would happen should a major earthquake happen here, is the nature of the terrain," the UN humanitarian chief Valerie Amos said in Kathmandu.
23"There are a lot of villages which are on the top of hills, they're quite small villages but there are no real roads that go up to those villages, and we know that there are quite a few areas like that because the epicentre is in a mountainous region.
24"It's not been possible to land even small helicopters [in some places] because there have been landslides."
25Indian Air Force pilot Avik Abhijit S Bali described how he had to abandon efforts to rescue survivors from a remote village in Gorkha, one of the worst-hit districts, because he could not land.
26"We tried for 20 minutes but there was no possibility of landing in a village that was on a slope and covered in debris from collapsed houses," he said.
27The Nepal government has said it faces a severe shortage of both helicopters and heavy machinery to clear roads blocked by landslides.
28The army has just seven helicopters of its own and while India has lent the country another six for rescue and relief efforts it desperately needs more help to transport aid materials.
29"This was a calamity of enormous proportions and the relief operations have been a challenge given the resources we have," information minister Minendra Rijal said.
30"We are putting all our resources into increasing the effectiveness of our relief efforts.
31"We have deployed people to reach every affected district by air, road or even on foot."
32With authorities saying up to 90 per cent of homes in the two worst-affected districts have been destroyed, the United Nations said providing shelter was the priority.
33It estimates more than eight million survivors are in need of aid, and says it has received reports of desperate survivors clamouring to get on to helicopters evacuating the badly wounded from rural areas and forcing relief trucks off the road.
34"Remarkably, few modern, concrete, buildings have been affected in the capital," said Nepal expert and former UN official John Bevan in a blog post this week.
35"In the countryside, however, most buildings are old, made of mud or weak bricks and stand on vertiginous slopes.
36"These are the remote hill villages which it would appear have borne the main brunt of the quake."
37Ms Amos said there had been reports of some areas getting repeated aid deliveries, while others had still received nothing.
38"What we do know is that there is a lot more need out there than the places we are able to get to," she said.
39"Our priority now is really to try to reach those people, get immediate assistance to them."
40The government of landlocked Nepal has also faced criticism for holding up foreign relief deliveries at customs, with reports of aid trucks being turned away at the border with India because they did not have the correct documentation.
41Editor's note (4/5/2015): An earlier version of this story quoted police as telling AFP news agency Funchu Tamang had been trapped under the rubble of his home for several days.
42They later said he was rescued from his garden, where he had been trapped for several hours.
