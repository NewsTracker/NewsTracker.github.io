Global finance officials see rising risks to recovery, including possible Greek debt default

1WASHINGTON?
2World finance officials said Saturday they see a number of threats on the horizon for a global economy still clawing back from the deepest recession in seven decades, and the Greek debt default presents the most immediate risk.
3After finance officials wrapped up three days of talks, the International Monetary Fund's policy committee set a goal of working toward a "more robust, balanced and job-rich global economy" while acknowledging growing risks to achieving that objective.
4The Greek finance minister, Yanis Varoufakis, held a series of talks with finance officials on the sidelines of the IMF and World Bank meetings, trying to settle his country's latest crisis.
5Mario Draghi, head of the European Central Bank, said it was "urgent" to resolve the dispute between Greece and its creditors.
6A default, he said, would send the global economy into "uncharted waters" and the extent of the possible damage is hard to estimate.
7He told reporters that he did not want to even contemplate the chance of a default.
8Earlier in the week, IMF Managing Director Christine Lagarde had rejected suggestions that her agency might postpone repayment deadlines for Greece.
9But on Saturday, she cited constructive talks with Varoufakis and said the goal was to stabilize Greece's finances and assure an economic recovery.
10Greece is in negotiations with the IMF and European authorities to receive the final 7.2 billion euro ($7.8 billion) installment of its financial bailout.
11Creditors are demanding that Greece produce a credible overhaul before releasing the money.
12The country has relied on international loans since 2010.
13Without more bailout money, Greece could miss two debt payments due to the IMF in May and run out of cash to pay government salaries and pensions.
14Fears that Greece could default and abandon the euro currency group sent shockwaves through global markets Friday.
15After being down nearly 360 points, the Dow Jones industrial average recovered a bit to finish down 279.47, a drop of 1.5 percent.
16U.S. Treasury Secretary Jacob Lew said that a Greek default would "create immediate hardship" for Greece and damage the world economy.
17In a speech Saturday to the IMF panel, Lew also warned South Korea, Germany, China and Japan to do more to increase consumer demand in their own countries instead of relying on exports to the United States and elsewhere for growth.
18"We are concerned that the global economy is reverting to the pre-crisis pattern of heavy reliance on U.S. demand for growth," Lew said.
19"As we all know, such a pattern will not lead to strong, sustainable and balanced global growth."
