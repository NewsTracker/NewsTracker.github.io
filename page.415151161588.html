Ferguson News Guide: US to make Ferguson announcements soon

1The federal government has undertaken two separate investigations.
2One looks at whether criminal charges should be filed against the officer involved in the shooting, Darren Wilson.
3The other is a broader examination of the city's police department, including a search for discriminatory practices by officers.
4Attorney General Eric Holder, the nation's first black to hold that office, has made civil rights a cornerstone of his six-year tenure.
5He says he expects to release results in both investigations before he leaves office, which means announcements could be coming within days.
6Already, federal officials have been clear that they see troubling signs in a police department that is overwhelmingly white yet serves a majority-black community, and the report is likely to detail problems found during the Justice Department's months-long probe.
7A 2013 report by the Missouri attorney general's office found that police stopped and searched black drivers far more often than white motorists, even though they were less likely to find contraband among the black drivers.
8Investigations like this one aren't uncommon.
9Under Holder, the feds have delved into about 20 police departments, including those in Albuquerque, Cleveland and Newark, New Jersey, usually for discriminatory policing practices and large numbers of police-involved shootings.
10Most such cases end with police departments committing to make changes, though the Justice Department maintains the prerogative to take them to court when departments refuse to commit to reforms.
11When he announced the Ferguson investigation in September, Holder revealed that the probe would focus on police use of force as well as stops, searches and arrests of suspects and the treatment of inmates at the city jail.
12He said that a "deep mistrust" had taken hold between law enforcement officials and members of the community.
13In October, Holder said publicly that the Ferguson department was in need of "wholesale change."
14Last month, in a forum with reporters at the National Press Club, he described the investigation as "independent, thorough and based only on the facts and the law."
15He predicted that "people will be satisfied with the results that we announce."
16It's hard to bring a successful federal prosecution for criminal civil rights violations against a police officer, particularly in shootings that arise during rapidly unfolding confrontations.
17Police are given wide latitude to use deadly force when they feel endangered, and the U.S. Supreme Court has said police confrontations should be evaluated through the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, rather than 20/20 hindsight.
18Officer Darren Wilson is seen as unlikely to face criminal charges, in part because of the high threshold needed for a civil rights case.
19Prosecutors would need to establish that Wilson willfully deprived Brown of his civil rights by using excessive force, a tough standard that Justice Department prosecutors have been unable to meet in some high other high-profile police shootings.
20Wilson told a state grand jury that investigated the case that he feared for his life during the confrontation, which began after he directed Brown and a friend who were walking in the street to move to the sidewalk.
21As the two struggled, Wilson said Brown reached inside the driver's-side window of his patrol car, struck him in the face and reached for his service weapon.
22Brown ran, and Wilson said he shot at him after the teenager charged at him.
23Other witnesses said Brown never posed a threat and was standing with his hands up before he was shot.
24A grand jury cleared Wilson of wrongdoing in November.
25He resigned from his job days later.
26The shooting touched off weeks of protests in the streets of Ferguson and other cities.
27Along with the police chokehold death of a New York City man suspected of selling untaxed cigarettes and the December killings of two New York police officers, the Ferguson case became part of a national conversation about race and policing.
28The shooting and its aftermath focused attention on how police departments use military surplus equipment and on whether more training is needed to help officers de-escalate situations.
29It accelerated a push for the use of body cameras by police departments nationwide and led to brainstorming discussions about how to build trust between officers and the communities they serve.
30Public statements came from officials including President Barack Obama, who said the frustrations of the protesters "have deep roots in many communities of color who have a sense that our laws are not always being enforced uniformly or fairly."
31Holder has called for more complete record keeping on the number of police shootings and how often officers are themselves shot at.
32FBI Director James Comey, in a blunt speech last month on race and law enforcement, echoed that suggestion and said police officers may be informed by unconscious biases and tempted by "lazy shortcuts of cynicism."
33"We must better understand the people we serve and protect, by trying to know deep in our gut what it feels like to be a law-abiding young black man walking down the street and encountering law enforcement," Comey said.
