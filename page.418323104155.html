Oil tumbles 6 percent after Greek 'No' vote, ahead of Iran deadline

1NEW YORK Oil prices tumbled their most in three months on Monday, with U.S. crude falling 6 percent, after Greece's rejection of debt bailout terms and China's rolling out of emergency measures to support its stock markets shook global markets.
2Adding to the pressure on oil, Iran and global powers were trying to meet a July 7 deadline on a nuclear deal, which could bring more supply to the market if sanctions on Iran are eased.
3The self-imposed deadline could be extended again, officials at the negotiations said.
4U.S. crude has fallen 10 percent in all over three straight sessions and Brent over 7 percent in two consecutive days, the biggest rout since January.
5The slump brought oil out of its narrow trading band of the past three months, risking a deeper slide ahead.
6"Even without Greece, China's stock market woes and Iran priming to hit the market with more barrels, the demand picture in oil has only been okay while the supply picture has been phenomenal," said John Kilduff, partner at New York energy hedge fund Again Capital.
7"With these number of bearish elements weighing on the market now, the only thing of support has been the seasonal demand in gasoline, and even that will be going away soon."
8U.S. crude CLc1 was at $53.50 a barrel by 12:51 p.m. EDT, down $3.43 from its settlement on Thursday before Friday's U.S. Independence Day holiday and below the 100-day moving average.
9If the 6 percent drop is retained at the close, it will be the biggest daily loss in U.S. crude since a 7 percent drop on April 8.
10"A closing break below $54 could pave the way for a move down to $50," said Fawad Razaqzada, a London-based technical analyst for forex.com.
11Brent LCOc1 fell $2.67, or 4.4 percent, to $57.65, also snapping its 100-day average.
12Greeks voted a resounding no to a referendum on an international bailout that also put in doubt its membership in the euro.
13The euro EUR= fell against the dollar, weighing on demand for dollar-denominated commodities from holders of the single currency.
14Commodities were also sucked into market turmoil that has seen Chinese shares .CSI300 fall as much as 30 percent since June due in part to the economy growing at its slowest pace in a generation.
15In Vienna, a dispute over U.N. sanctions on Iran's ballistic missile programme and a broader arms embargo were among issues holding up a nuclear deal between Tehran and six world powers.
16Iran is seeking to restore oil exports that have dropped from 2.5 million barrels per day in 2011 to about 1 million bpd in 2014.
17Morgan Stanley analysts said up to 700,000 bpd in new Iranian exports were likely to arrive between late 2015 or early 2016, delaying the recovery in oil prices and U.S. output by 6 to 12 months.
18Oil prices were also weighed down by signs that U.S. shale drillers were returning to the field, as the rig count for oil rose last week for the first time since December.
19[RIG/U] It is unclear whether the latest price decline will give drillers pause, though, as many oil producers had been counting on $60 or $65 a barrel prices to support new wells.
