Strong turnout for world's first glimpse at Apple Watch

1The Apple Watch, CEO Tim Cook's first new major product, was available for pre-order online and to try out in stores - but not take home.
2On April 24, consumers will be able to buy it online or by reservation at retail locations including high-end fashion boutiques in Paris, London and Tokyo, part of Apple's strategy of positioning the wearable computer as a must-have accessory.
3On Friday morning Apple's Australian flagship store in Sydney's financial district was packed with those hoping to peek at the device, although just around 20 die-hard fans queued out front, modest by the standards of a major Apple launch.
4Alexander Bock, a backpacker from Germany, stood outside the shop's towering glass facade.
5He hopes to save enough money to buy the sports version of the watch, he said.
6The Apple Watch sport starts at $349 while the standard version comes in at $549 in the U.S. High-end "Edition" watches with 18-karat gold alloys are priced from $10,000 and go as high as $17,000.
7"I feel naked without a watch. I think I will buy the Apple watch with the sports band ... I'm working very hard right now so I can buy this watch," Bock told Reuters.
8The watch marks the Cupertino, California company's debut in a fledgling wearable technology market.
9Based on recent customer interest at its stores, Apple expects demand for the watch, which allows users to check email, listen to music and make phone calls when paired with an iPhone, to exceed availability at launch, it said on Thursday.
10Reviewers this week praised the watch, which also helps users monitor their health and exercise, as "beautiful" and "stylish" but gave it poor marks for relatively low battery life and slow-loading apps.
11For women, the various sizes and wrist bands make this smartwatch more pleasing than earlier versions from Samsung Electronics Co Ltd and others, said Kantar World Panel market analyst Carolina Milanesi, who has been wearing the watch for a few days.
12At the Apple Store in Omotesando, a high-end shopping district in the heart of Tokyo, dozens of shoppers huddled around two glass displays, snapping pictures of the range of options available on the different watch models.
13"I actually tried it on briefly, and it's so light it doesn't feel like it's on my wrist," 26-year-old salesman Yosuke Hosoi told Reuters.
14Sales estimates for 2015 vary widely.
15Piper Jaffray predicts 8 million units and Global Securities Research forecasts 40 million.
16By comparison, Apple sold nearly 200 million iPhones last year.
17"Is it for everybody? No, but I don't think any wearables are yet," Milanesi said.
18Apple's watch is widely expected to outsell those by Samsung, Sony Corp and Fitbit, that have attracted modest interest from consumers.
19It will likely account for 55 percent of global smartwatch shipments this year, according to Societe Generale.
20Underscoring its marketing strategy, Apple is selling the watch through a handful of high-end stores including Selfridges in London, Galeries Lafayette in Paris and Isetan in Tokyo.
21Still, experts said Apple's offering was unlikely to displace the market share of luxury-brand watch makers.
22"This is just Apple's interpretation of the watch, I don't think watch makers should be worried at all," Stephen Fenech, editor of the Tech Guide website, told Reuters.
23"This is just Apple's expression where they've combined what they are good at with the fact that it's a watch with style so I think there's going to be room for everyone."
24JMP analyst Alex Gauna said he and others on Wall Street would be at stores this weekend to gauge consumer reaction.
25But the typically long queues at its stores on product launch days could be a thing of the past as the company emphasizes online sales, according to a leaked internal memo from retail chief Angela Ahrendts, reported by Business Insider.
26"The days of waiting in line and crossing fingers for a product are over for our customers," she wrote.
27"This is a significant change in mindset, and we need your help to make it happen. Tell your customers we have more availability online, and show them how easy it is to order."
28A spokesman for Apple in Sydney declined to comment on the memo, citing company policy.
29At an Apple Store in Hong Kong there was no queue, although half a dozen customers gathered near the shop door, cheering when it was opened.
30Demos were by online registration, after which a Reuters reporter received a demo without having to wait.
31"I didn't know it's the first day. I am here to buy an iPad," said a 26-year-old customer who gave her name only as Ms Jian, visiting from China's Chongqing.
32"I will take a look at the watch later."
33Apple shares closed 0.76 percent higher at $126.56 on Thursday.
