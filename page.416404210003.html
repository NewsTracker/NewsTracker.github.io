10CAIRO (AP) - An Egyptian criminal court on Tuesday sentenced ousted Islamist President Mohammed Morsi to 20 years in prison on charges linked to the killing of protesters in 2012, the first verdict to be issued against the country's first freely elected leader.
11The conviction, which can be appealed, and muted Islamist reaction following it, underscore the dramatic downfall of Morsi and Egypt's once-powerful Muslim Brotherhood group.
12Morsi escaped being sentenced to death in the case.
13Morsi and the Brotherhood rose to power in the elections that followed the overthrow of Hosni Mubarak in a 2011 Arab Spring uprising.
14But the military overthrew Morsi in 2013 amid massive protests against his divisive yearlong rule, and now he and other top Brotherhood leaders are in prison facing a raft of charges, many of which carry the death penalty.
15Now, as Mubarak and members of his government increasingly find themselves acquitted of criminal charges, Morsi and the Brotherhood are at the receiving end of heavy-handed sentences.
16Tuesday's verdict sparked no immediate street protests, reflecting the toll of a heavy security crackdown on any show of dissent - either by Islamists or other activists.
17During the hearing, Judge Ahmed Youssef issued his verdict as Morsi and other defendants in the case - mostly Brotherhood leaders - stood in a soundproof glass cage inside a makeshift courtroom at Egypt's national police academy.
18Seven of the accused were tried in absentia.
19In addition to Morsi, 12 Brotherhood leaders and Islamist supporters, including Mohammed el-Beltagy and Essam el-Erian, were sentenced to 20 years in prison.
20Youssef dropped murder charges and said the sentences were linked to a "show of force" and unlawful detention.
21The case stems from violence outside the presidential palace in December 2012.
22Morsi's supporters attacked opposition protesters demanding that Morsi call off a referendum on an Islamist-drafted constitution.
23Clashes escalated into deadly confrontations overnight that killed at least 10 people.
24In a statement, Amnesty International called the case flawed and full of loopholes, while describing the trial as a "sham."
25"Convicting Mohammed Morsi, despite fundamental flaws in the legal process and what seems to be at best flimsy evidence produced in court under a gag order, utterly undermines this verdict," said Hassiba Hadj Sahraoui, Amnesty's deputy Middle East and North Africa director.
26Morsi's questioning took place without his lawyers present during his detention in an undisclosed location for four months following his ouster in July 2013.
27Amnesty said Morsi's legal team was only able to access case files days before the trial began.
28It also documented irregularities, such as where abuses of his supporters - not his opponents - were the only evidence documented.
29The court also ignored what Amnesty said were deaths among Morsi's supporters during the same protests.
30During Tuesday's hearing, Morsi and the rest of the defendants in white jumpsuits raised the four-finger sign symbolizing the sit-in at the Rabaah al-Adawiya mosque, where hundreds were killed when security forces violently dispersed the sprawling sit-in by Morsi's supporters on Aug. 14, 2013.
31They also smiled for cameras filming the hearing.
32It was a far cry from when the trial first began, when Morsi repeatedly shouted to the court: "I am the president of the republic!"
33During subsequent court appearances, Morsi and other defendants turned their backs to the court when Youssef played several videos of the clashes outside the palace in 2012.
34From his exile in Turkey, top Muslim Brotherhood figure Amr Darrag called the ruling "a sad and terrible day in Egyptian history."
35"They want to pass a life sentence for democracy in Egypt," Darrag said.
36Under the government of President Abdel-Fattah el-Sissi, who as army chief overthrew Morsi, Brotherhood members and Islamists have faced trials that end with mass death sentences, sparking international condemnation.
37The heavy sentences have raised questions about the independence of Egypt's judiciary, something analysts say could have contributed to the sentence Morsi received Tuesday.
38"Morsi's trial gripped international attention and a heavy sentence would have put the judiciary under a spotlight," said Sameh Eid, a former Brotherhood member who researches Islamic movements.
39"Today, the judiciary seemed keen in preserving its image."
40Political science professor Hassan Nafaa said average Egyptians have seen the differences between the trials of Morsi and Mubarak.
41"People are not reassured of the fairness of these trials," Nafaa said.
42Morsi faces four other trials on charges that include orchestrating a prison break and undermining national security by conspiring with foreign groups.
43Thousands of Brotherhood members are in jail facing a variety of charges, most linking them to violence that followed Morsi's 2013 overthrow.
44Morsi is being held at a high security prison near the Mediterranean city of Alexandria.
45Tuesday's hearing took place amid heavy security, as hundreds of black-clad riot police deployed at the police academy, backed by armored vehicles.
