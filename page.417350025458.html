Amid Ukraine denials, Putin orders Russian peacetime troop deaths be kept secret

1MOSCOW - President Vladimir Putin ordered on Thursday that the deaths of Russian soldiers during special operations in peacetime should be classified as a state secret, a move that comes as Moscow stands accused of sending troops to fight in eastern Ukraine.
2Putin, who has repeatedly denied any involvement of Russian troops in a pro-Russian separatist rebellion there, amended a decree that had previously classified only deaths during war time as secret.
3Asked to explain the rationale behind Putin's move, his spokesman, Dmitry Peskov, denied any link to Ukraine.
4"The improvement of the state secret law is underway," he said in a conference call.
5U.S. State Department spokesman Jeff Rathke said at a news briefing the law was a blow to free press and transparency.
6"We see this as a misplaced effort to cover up what everyone knows, and that is that Russian active duty military personnel are fighting and dying in eastern Ukraine and that the Russian government is denying it," he said.
7Russia's role in the turmoil in east Ukraine, where more than 6,100 people have been killed in over a year of fighting, has been one of the most contentious issues in a conflict that has thrown ties between Moscow and the West into disarray.
8Russian opposition activists released a report this month saying at least 220 serving Russian soldiers were killed in fighting in two hot spots in east Ukraine last summer and earlier this year.
9Boris Nemtsov, an opposition leader who initiated work on the report, was gunned down in February.
10His close aide who helped finalize the document, Ilya Yashin, told Reuters by telephone: "This shows that the war with Ukraine - undeclared but ongoing - is a major sore spot for Putin."
11The presidential decree refers to information held by the defense ministry, but could, he said, find broader application.
12"This & should be seen as a threat to activists, politicians and journalists who deal with this."
13Sergei Krivenko, who heads a soldiers' rights group and sits on the Kremlin's advisory council on human rights, termed the presidential decree "within the logic of the current situation in Russia."
14"If Russian authorities officially say there are no Russian troops in Ukraine, then, all the more so, there can be no losses," he said.
15Both cited the case of Svetlana Davydova, a Russian national charged with treason after calling the Ukrainian Embassy to say she had overheard a soldier's conversation about troops from a nearby military base being sent to Ukraine.
16Russia annexed Crimea from Ukraine last year after wresting control over the peninsula using troops with no insignia.
17Moscow initially vehemently denied they were Russian.
18Putin only publicly admitted Russian soldiers' involvement nearly a month after signing legislation formally completing the peninsula's annexation.
19A Reuters reporter witnessed earlier this week the Russian army massing troops without insignia and hundreds of pieces of unmarked weaponry on the border with Ukraine.
20Asked by Reuters if this indicated Russia planned an invasion of Ukraine, Peskov said: "I find the wording of this question & inappropriate as such."
21A fragile cease-fire has been in force in east Ukraine since February, but each side accuses the other of violations.
22Kiev fears Russia could commit troops to a push to extend control by separatist forces deeper into Ukrainian territory.
