1(AP) - The protesters who spent eight months pressing for changes in Ferguson's police practices after the fatal shooting of Michael Brown take credit for this week's resignations of the city manager and the police chief.
2And they insist they still have unfinished business, with many planning to stay in the streets until the mayor of the St. Louis suburb is forced out and the entire police force dissolved.
3"We will protest until we see everything in our favor. This movement has legs," Derrick Robinson, a protest organizer, declared Friday.
4"We're out here fighting for justice and equality, and that's what we'll continue to fight for."
5Part of the movement has also been channeled into legislative change.
6On Wednesday, about two dozen people from the Don't Shoot Coalition and the American Civil Liberties Union traveled to the Missouri Capitol in support of the "Fair and Impartial Policing Act," a measure that would strengthen state laws about racial profiling by police and require law officers to undergo "anti-bias" training.
7The Justice Department fueled the sense of achievement among activists, announcing in a scathing report last week that it had found widespread racial bias in the city's policing and in a municipal court system driven by profit extracted from mostly black and low-income residents.
8That same report also cleared former Ferguson officer Darren Wilson in the Aug. 9 death of Brown, the unarmed 18-year-old who for protesters became a symbol of unjustified use of force and unfair treatment of minorities by police.
9The Justice Department's conclusions drew a muted response among activists.
10Only a few dozen protesters gathered that night outside Ferguson's police station - the nexus of many demonstrations.
11That was in sharp contrast to the throngs that turned out there and across the nation in the days after Brown's death and in November, when a Missouri grand jury declined to indict Wilson.
12The comparatively sparse turnout raised questions about whether the movement had lost momentum.
13Organizers said the federal government's conclusions had been expected after some of the findings were leaked in the weeks before the formal announcements.
14Rasheen Aldridge, a 20-year-old regular at the protests, considers Ferguson his generation's civil-rights movement.
15That idea, he said, seemed to gain resonance in the last week after the killing of an unarmed biracial man by a white officer in Madison, Wisconsin, and the suspension of a University of Oklahoma fraternity chapter that was caught on video singing a racist chant.
16Aldridge, head of Young Activists United St. Louis, said he did not expect the protest movement "to grow into the energy that it has, empowering young people not to be silent anymore and to take action and fix it."
17"This is the new time to make changes," added Aldridge, also a member of the Ferguson Commission, a group tasked by Missouri's governor to address underlying social and economic problems.
18"This is our time."
19In the months after Brown's death, some demonstrations were marred by looting and arson fires that targeted businesses.
20Organizers blamed those incidents on outside agitators.
21That was the case again Thursday, when two police officers helping monitor protests outside the police department were shot in an attack that was still under investigation.
22The officers were later released from the hospital.
23On Friday, St. Louis County Police Chief Jon Belmar said detectives were working around the clock to crack that case.
24The department's top priority, he said, was to continue "a tempo of service and protection and relationships" that ensure Ferguson does not "have a regression from all that we've done since last fall."
25The gunfire drew instant, broad condemnation from activists.
26Dozens of protesters gathered again Thursday night in Ferguson, expressing sympathy for the wounded officers and praying for peace during a candlelight vigil.
27"We cannot afford these kinds of incidents happening. That gets us absolutely nowhere," said St. Louis activist John Gaskin III.
28He called the gunfire "disgraceful and cowardly" and said people "need to be working for reforms and justice, not revenge."
29Robinson said the wounding of the officers "definitely set us back."
30But he believes protesters can regain what they lost.
31"Even last night as we protested, police were very gracious to us," he said Friday.
32"That shows a lot of trust to us."
33Gaskin said protesters should take stock in the Ferguson house cleaning they helped achieve.
34Associated Press Writer Alan Scher Zagier contributed to this report from Clayton, Missouri.

2New Delhi: An Indian bride walked out of her wedding ceremony after the groom failed to solve a simple math problem, police said on Friday.
3The bride tested the groom on his math skills and when he got the sum wrong, she walked out.
4The incident took place late on Wednesday in Rasoolabad village near the industrial town of Kanpur in northern Uttar Pradesh state, local police officer Rakesh Kumar said on Friday.
5The groom's family tried persuading the bride to return, but she refused.
6She said the groom had misled them about his education.
7"The groom's family kept us in the dark about his poor education," said Mohar Singh, the bride's father.
8"Even a first grader can answer this."
9Local police mediated between the families and both sides returned all the gifts and jewelry that had been exchanged before the wedding, Kumar said.
10Last month, another bride in Uttar Pradesh married a wedding guest after the original groom had a seizure and collapsed at the wedding venue.
11The groom's family had not revealed that the groom was epileptic.
12While the groom was rushed to a hospital in Rampur town, the bride asked one of the wedding guests to step in and married him.
13Most marriages in India are arranged by the families of the bride and groom.
14Except for brief meetings, the couple rarely gets to know each other before the nuptials.
