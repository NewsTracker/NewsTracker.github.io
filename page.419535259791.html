16DAKAR, Senegal (AP) - Lingering health problems afflicting many of the roughly 13,000 Ebola survivors have galvanized global and local health officials to find out how widespread the ailments are, and how to remedy them.
17The World Health Organization calls it an emergency within an emergency.
18Many of the survivors have vision and hearing issues.
19Some others experience physical and emotional pains, fatigue and other problems.
20The medical community is negotiating uncharted waters as it tries to measure the scale of this problem that comes on the tail end of the biggest Ebola outbreak in history.
21"If we can find out this kind of information, hopefully we can help other Ebola survivors in the future," Dr. Zan Yeong, an eye specialist involved in a study of health problems in survivors in Liberia, told The Associated Press.
22About 7,500 people will enroll - 1,500 Ebola survivors and 6,000 of their close contacts - and will be monitored over a five-year period in the study launched by Partnership for Research on Ebola Vaccines in Liberia, or PREVAIL.
23Only about 40 percent of those infected have survived Ebola, according to WHO estimates.
24But while the survivors beat the odds, preliminary research shows that many are still suffering.
25Around half those who received post-recovery check-ups have joint pain, said Dr. Daniel Bausch, an Ebola expert and consultant for WHO.
26"We don't have the capacity yet - we wish we did - to follow every survivor," he said.
27Consequently, the percentage of survivors who have complications isn't known, he said.
28He described the joint pain as "very debilitating and a very serious problem that can prevent people from going back to work and providing for their family."
29Some degree of changes in vision has been reported by roughly 25 percent of the survivors who have been seen by medics, he said, including severe inflammation of the eye that if untreated can result in blindness, he said.
30The Ebola virus has been found, in at least a few cases, to linger in the eyes, though experts say it is not transmitted through tears.
31Morris Kallon, 34, a health worker who survived Ebola in a village in Liberia's Grand Cape Mount County, said he had fevers, headaches, lower abdominal pain and red eyes after he returned home.
32"I have been experiencing whole lot of problems within my body system," he said.
33"I still feel pains in my back. It is very difficult for me to swing my arms. ... My vision is always blurred, like dew on my face."
34Lab technician Mohamed SK Sesay was working at a hospital in Kenema, a town in eastern Sierra Leone, testing blood samples for Ebola when he fell sick with the virus.
35About eight members of his team got infected and he was among the few survivors, WHO said.
36After he recovered, he was discharged from an Ebola treatment unit in September.
37He was still weak, and says he was shunned by his community.
38Then his health deteriorated.
39"Sleepless nights. Joint pain. Muscle pain," he said.
40"I started experiencing loss of weight. ... Loss of sight was the worst one that set me off. I used to cry. I couldn't see my computer."
41He was attended to by one of Sierra Leone's few eye doctors and his health improved overall, but he still has bad days.
42"My biggest challenge is now my health," he said.
43He loses vision from time to time.
44Sometimes if people call out to him on the street he can't hear them.
45Eye problems were noted in some survivors of Ebola outbreaks in Congo in 1995, in Uganda's Gulu district in 2000 and in Uganda's Bundibugyo district in 2007.
46But with such small numbers, past outbreaks haven't provided sufficient opportunities for extensive study, Bausch said.
47Now, with thousands of survivors, doctors want to learn why people are experiencing these ailments, how they affect the body, what percentage of survivors has issues and how to treat them.
48Experts also want to learn whether the physical problems are directly caused by the virus, whether they existed before, are side-effects or perhaps autoimmune reactions, Bausch said.
49"It's too early ... to know what the direct effect or link is to Ebola, if at all," Bausch said.
50In early August, WHO gathered experts in Sierra Leone who concluded that more needs to be done to provide better care plans for survivors, and more research and specialist help is needed.
51Post-recovery problems haven't been confined to West African survivors, whose health might not have been strong to begin with considering the poor state of health care in Liberia, Sierra Leone and Guinea - the three impoverished countries most affected by Ebola - even before the epidemic.
52Dr. Ian Crozier, an American who became infected while working in Sierra Leone for WHO, developed an inflammation and high pressure in one eye months after being released from treatment.
53His iris temporarily changed color from blue to green; doctors found his eye contained the Ebola virus.
54He is still recovering, but his vision has improved, according to Emory University Hospital which has been treating him.
55Nancy Writebol, who last year became the second American infected with Ebola, said she suffers joint pain, mostly in her knees.
56She said she had problems with her vision, but they seem to have gone away.
57She assists a weekly survivor clinic in Liberia at ELWA hospital run by Serving In Mission, a North Carolina-based Christian organization.
58She noted that Liberia's health-care system is broken and many survivors lack running water and electricity in their homes, making their recovery more arduous than that of survivors in the West.
59"There are a lot that are having troubles with vision," she said.
60"One of the greatest complaints that we see is joint pain. And you can tell just by the way people are moving that they are suffering."
61Dr. Rick Sacra, an American Ebola survivor who helps at the ELWA Liberia hospital every few months, said when he was in Liberia in June and July, he saw a mixture of depression and post-traumatic stress disorder in the 15 to 40 people that came to survivor clinic appointments each week.
62Physically the main complaints involve the eyes, joints and nerve problems, he said.
63Less common symptoms are rashes, headaches, abdominal discomfort and cough.
64"I know there's likely a large number of survivors who are fine, but then you have smaller subsets who have one or more of these complications," he said.
65Sacra also suffered eye problems that were treated with steroids.
66He told AP he has fully recovered.
67The epidemic, which has claimed nearly 11,300 lives, has significantly slowed, with only three confirmed cases emerging in the last weekly reporting period, according to WHO figures.
68But experts and survivors say the struggle to deal with the residual damage is just starting.
69Dr. Anders Nordstrom, the WHO representative for Sierra Leone, said: "It is increasingly clear that emerging from an ETU (Ebola treatment unit) is just the beginning."
70AP writer Mike Stobbe in New York contributed to this report.
71This story has been corrected to show Crozier developed high pressure, not high blood pressure, in the eye.
