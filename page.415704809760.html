Pop music reviews: Courtney Barnett offers incisive, cutting songs in 'Sometimes I Sit and Think, and Sometimes I Just Sit'

1In 2013, Courtney Barnett's "Lance Jr." popped on the radio and stopped me in my tracks.
2This first full-length studio album from young Australian guitarist, singer and songwriter Barnett lives up to the promise of that deft introduction, which was taken from her debut, "The Double EP: A Sea of Split Peas."
3"Sometimes I Sit and Think, and Sometimes I Just Sit" confirms that the first-blush crush wasn't misguided.
4Incisive, cutting and verbally dexterous, if a little overwhelming in a single sitting, Barnett's best new songs - "Pedestrian at Best," "Depreston" and "Debbie Downer" among them - inject memorable heft into timeless rock terrain formerly explored by Polly Jean Harvey, young and angry Elvis Costello, Courtney Love and Kurt Cobain.
5Take "Nobody Really Cares if You Don't Go to the Party," a striking, hard-strummed jam about lethargy, isolation and night life.
6After a friend pressures her for a night on the town while maracas shake in quick rhythm, Barnett declines, singing, "I'd rather stay in bed with the rain over my head / Than have to pick my brain up off the floor."
7Wordy? A bit.
8With a few rounds of Raymond Carver-style editing, Barnett could cut a quarter of her syllables without sacrificing any ideas.
9The details, though, make "Sometimes I Sit" instantly alluring, and her guitar playing is just as eloquent.
10Whether banging Nirvana-heavy chords while singing about a chance encounter in a swimming pool ("Aqua Profunda!"
11In "Depreston," she offers a snap-shot moment spent in a bungalow.
12Narrated in part by a real estate agent, the song tells of an empty house still dotted with memories of its previous inhabitants.
13In what should be a theme for the HGTV show "House Hunters," Barnett croons, "If you've got a spare half million / You could knock it down and start rebuildin'."
14She then drifts into a high-lonesome bottleneck guitar solo, which moves longingly until the song fades away.
15Laura Marling arguably hit the high point of her career with 2013's "Once I Was an Eagle."
16Everything about that album was big - its thicket of acoustic arrangements, its peals of lyrics about heartbreak, its run time (an hour and change).
17For a follow-up, she's gone smaller, starting with the LP's title of "Short Movie."
18Though it's not the statement-making brass-ring grab that "Eagle" was, it's certainly more approachable, and maybe even more enjoyable.
19"Short Movie" is both lighter and stranger than its predecessor.
20Here, Marling's ever-brilliant guitar playing is surrounded with specters of ambient electronic noise.
21"False Hope" creaks with Tangerine Dream-style washes cut through with strong drumming and a panicked lyrical insomnia.
22The spare "Howl" brings out her inner Nico, but the rockers like "I Feel Your Love" and "Don't Let Me Bring You Down" steal the show with a newfound zest.
23"Short Movie" is no epic, but it's the most replayable LP of Marling's career.
