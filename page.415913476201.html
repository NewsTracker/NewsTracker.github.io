Merck, NewLink Ebola vaccine appears safe, effective in new studies

1Since trials cannot ethically expose volunteers to Ebola, the production of antibodies is a proxy for whether vaccines could prevent or even treat the disease.
2There is currently no vaccine or specific treatment for Ebola, which has killed over 10,000 people in West Africa since last spring, according to the World Health Organization.
3It is the worst Ebola epidemic in history, but finally appears to be abating.
4The trials all tested a vaccine called VSV-ZEBOV, which was developed at the Public Health Agency of Canada and licensed to NewLink Genetics Corp and then to Merck & Co Inc.
5It consists of a cattle virus called rVSV that has been engineered to carry Ebola genes, which produce proteins meant to trigger production of anti-Ebola antibodies.
6According to separate teams of scientists, that is what happened, two papers in the New England Journal of Medicine reported.
7In the U.S. trials, conducted independently but cooperatively at the National Institutes of Health and the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research starting last October, 52 healthy adult volunteers received single injections of saline or either of two doses of vaccine.
8The most common side effects were mild, such as pain at the injection site and short-lived fever.
9All 40 participants who received vaccine produced anti-Ebola antibodies within 28 days, with most responding sooner.
10The higher dose triggered triple the antibody response of the lower dose.
11The "robust" response to a single dose "could be particularly useful in outbreak interventions," said Walter Reed's Col.
12Stephen Thomas, senior author of the U.S. paper.
13The higher dose is being tested in a larger trial in Liberia.
14Partly through that trial, both VSV-ZEBOV and an experimental vaccine from GlaxoSmithKline PLC called cAd3-EBOZ "appear to be safe," NIH announced last week.
15The other studies were similarly encouraging.
16In coordinated trials in Germany, Switzerland, Gabon and Kenya, 158 healthy volunteers received a placebo or any of five dose-levels of VSV-ZEBOV vaccine.
17Although the Geneva study was temporarily halted last year after 11 of 51 participants developed arthritis, overall there were "no serious vaccine-related adverse events," the researchers reported.
18All 150 people who received vaccine developed antibodies to Ebola with higher responses to higher doses.
