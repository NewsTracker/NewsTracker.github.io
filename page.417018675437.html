2 Political Candidates Fatally Shot in Restive Mexico States

1A candidate for mayor was shot to death in western Mexico and a man running for city council was gunned downed in southern Mexico, bringing to at least three the number of candidates killed in the run-up to the June 7 midterm elections.
2All three killings occurred in areas heavily hit by drug cartel violence, but there was no immediate information on the motives in the deaths.
3On Thursday, gunmen in a truck opened fire on a campaign rally being held by Enrique Hernandez, a mayoral candidate in Yurecuaro, in the western state of Michoacan.
4The state prosecutors' office said the gunfire killed Hernandez on the spot and wounded three other people.
5Hernandez was a leader of the town's "self-defense force," one of the vigilante groups that sprang up in 2013 to break the power of the Knights Templar drug cartel in Michoacan.
6State prosecutor Jose Martin Godoy said Hernandez had been accused by some of illegal activity.
7The self-defense forces often detained people or searched properties without warrants.
8"He is a person who was loved by many, but there were also people who accused him of illegal activities," Godoy told the Milenio television network.
9Hernandez was running on the ticket of the leftist Morena party.
10Yurecuaro is near the border with Jalisco state, an area where the violent Jalisco New Generation cartel has been expanding its influence, fighting both authorities and rival gangs.
11Also Thursday, gunmen fatally shot Hector Lopez Cruz, who was running for the city council of Huimanguillo, in the Gulf coast state of Tabasco, on the ticket of the ruling Institutional Revolutionary Party, or PRI.
12Lopez Cruz was gunned down as he returned to his home after a day of campaigning, said Erubiel Alonso, the head of the PRI in Tabasco.
13Alonso described Huimanguillo as "one of the most violent, most dangerous townships in Tabasco," suffering drug gang violence, immigrant trafficking and assaults, robberies and other crimes.
14Alonso said the killing did not threaten the elections, but PRI candidates would campaign only in daylight between now and June 7.
15Earlier this month, another PRI mayoral candidate, Ulises Fabian Quiroz, was shot to death in Chilapa in the southern state of Guerrero.
16Chilapa has been the scene of bloody turf battles between two local drug gangs, including one case in which a pile of 11 decapitated bodies were dumped by a roadside in November.
17Burned, decapitated or dismembered bodies have been turning up since then, and initially the PRI had troubled getting someone to replace Quiroz as candidate.
18A woman who had planned to run for mayor in another Guerrero town, but had not yet been named as a candidate, was murdered in March.
19Aide Nava Gonzalez had announced plans to run as the Democratic Revolution Party's candidate for mayor of the town of Ahuacuotzingo, Guerrero.
20She was kidnapped and later found dead, reportedly decapitated.
21As in past elections, the violence does not seem to have focused on any one political party.
22Earlier this week, a congressional candidate for the leftist Democratic Revolution Party was kidnapped by armed men and held for about a day along with three of her assistants in another part of Guerrero state.
23State prosecutors said they were later freed unharmed.
24Candidates also have been killed in earlier Mexican elections.
25In 2010, gunmen believed to be working for a drug cartel assassinated Rodolfo Torre, the leading candidate for governor in the northern border state of Tamaulipas.
26Jose Antonio Crespo, a political analyst at Mexico's Center for Economic Research and Instruction, said: "This is all very regrettable, but it is not unusual. That's the way Mexican elections are nowadays."
27"In the majority of cases it is connected to drug cartels, organized crime," Crespo said.
28"That just reveals what we already knew, that this problem has gotten beyond the control of the Mexican government."
