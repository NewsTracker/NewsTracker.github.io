Ukraine's president dismisses powerful regional governor

1The confrontation between oil and banking tycoon Ihor Kolomoysky and the authorities has highlighted the struggle Ukraine will face in diluting the influence of super-rich businessmen in public life.
2President Petro Poroshenko's office said in a statement that Kolomoysky had asked overnight to be released from his duties as governor of Dnipropetrovsk.
3The request came only after days of tension fueled by Kolomoysky's heavy-handed attempts to resist a government effort to wrest important energy companies from his control, however.
4Ukraine's President Petro Poroshenko, left, listens to Ihor Kolomoysky, governor of Dnipropetrovsk, in Kiev, Ukraine, Wednesday, March 25, 2015.
5Poroshenko has fired the billionaire governor of an eastern region following a rancorous dispute pitting the tycoon against the government.
6Kolomoysky made no immediate comment Wednesday.
7Last week, parliament overwhelmingly adopted a law requiring only 50 percent of shareholders of joint stock companies to attend company meetings before decisions could be taken.
8The threshold was previously 60 percent.
9That measure had the most immediate ramifications for Kolomoysky, whose Privat Group conglomerate holds a 43-percent stake in oil and gas company Ukrnafta.
10Although the government-owned energy company Naftogaz controls just over 50 percent of Ukrnafta, Privat Group has - until now - been able to obstruct any decisions going against Kolomoysky's interests.
11Kolomoysky is a contentious figure in Ukraine.
12Garrulous, foul-mouthed and expansive, the rotund and bearded 52-year old has never been afraid to use heavy-handed tactics in his business activities, but has garnered substantial support for his unabashed commitment to defending Ukraine's territorial integrity.
13Over the past year, he has tapped into his wealth, which Forbes magazine estimates stands at $1.3 billion, to finance volunteer battalions waging war against separatist militias in the east.
14Those battalions have since been subordinated to central government control, although it is believed Kolomoysky still retains considerable sway over them.
15That and Kolomoysky's deep pockets have aroused anxieties that more violent showdowns could lie ahead.
16Following the passage of what has been dubbed the "Ukrnafta law," Kolomoysky dispatched groups of armed men to swoop in on the offices of two energy companies in the capital, Kiev.
17Last Thursday, men in combat gear burst into the headquarters of Ukrtransnafta - a wholly state-owned pipeline operator unrelated to Ukrnafta.
18That was apparently prompted by a government decision to fire Ukrtransnafta head Oleksandr Lazorko, a figure known for his loyalty to Kolomoysky.
19With much of his commercial interests invested in oil, Kolomoysky is eager to retain a role in running the nation's pipelines.
20When Kolomoysky turned up in person at the Ukrtransnafta offices, he was cornered by journalists, one of whom asked the businessman to explain his presence.
21The question elicited a stream of profanity-laden invective and unsubstantiated claims that Ukrtransnafta was being seized by a "Russian diversionary group."
22Filmed footage of the entire exchange was uploaded to the Internet and drew much criticism for Kolomoysky's aggressive conduct.
23Not content with that, guards suspected to have been hired by Kolomoysky on Sunday erected a metal cordon around the offices of Ukrnafta, precipitating yet another standoff.
24That last effort sent ripples of alarm through Kiev and prompted President Poroshenko to warn that private armies would not be tolerated.
25All armed formations, even those created and funded by Kolomoysky, Poroshenko said, would have to fall in step.
26"No governor will be allowed to have his own pocket Ukrainian army," he said.
27The government has sought to cast its actions in tightening its control over the energy sector as something of a clean-up operation.
28Devin Ackles, an analyst with Kiev-based think tank CASE Ukraine, said the authorities are eager to show international creditors that they are deserving of financial support.
29"This has something to do with the pending audit of (state oil and gas company) Naftogaz and the cleaning up that needs to take place in the state-owned energy sector this year," he said.
30Others see the showdown in more pedestrian terms, as a clash among Ukraine's business elite.
31With a net worth that Forbes estimates stands at around $1.3 billion, Poroshenko is very much a member of that elite.
32"It is not to be ruled out that the background to this conflict is the confrontation between financial, oligarchic clans and the division of goods. And this will open a Pandora's Box that will lead to new conflict," said Nataliya Slobodyan, an energy expert at the International Center for Policy Studies.
33The spectacle of Ukraine's government being compelled to battle a powerful businessman is being watched with glee by Russia and concern by the United States.
34The Kremlin has done little to disguise the contempt with which it regards the government that replaced Moscow-friendly former President Viktor Yanukovych, who was overthrown in a public revolt last February.
35The United States, meanwhile, has staked significant credibility on Ukraine turning itself around and shedding itself of its burdensome legacy of murky business practices.
36Following the events of last week, U.S. ambassador Geoffrey Pyatt met with Kolomoysky.
37While Pyatt declined in a television interview to reveal what the men discussed, his anxiety was clear.
38"There is nobody I know in this country who wants to go back to the days of Yanukovych and the raider attacks, the use of violence to achieve economic objectives and the idea that there were certain groups or certain individuals who are above the law," Pyatt told Hromadske television.
39Ukraine's President Petro Poroshenko gestures while speaking to Ihor Kolomoysky, governor of Dnipropetrovsk, in Kiev, Ukraine, Wednesday, March 25, 2015.
