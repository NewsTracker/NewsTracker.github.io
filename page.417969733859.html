EU extends sanctions against Russia by 6 months

1LUXEMBOURG (AP) -- The European Union on Monday extended economic sanctions against Russia until January to keep pressure on Moscow over the conflict in eastern Ukraine, drawing a rebuke and a warning of retaliation from Russian officials.
2An EU statement said the decision was taken without debate by the bloc's foreign ministers at a meeting in Luxembourg, in response to "Russia's destabilizing role in eastern Ukraine."
3The sanctions, along with U.S. and other Western measures against Russia, have contributed to a softening of the Russian economy at a time when the price of oil that is crucial to its economic output has also fallen.
4The sanctions have also put a pinch on some of Russia's key EU trading partners.
5The sanctions include limits to access on some financial markets, a ban on certain technologies and exchanges between the bloc and Russia in energy and defense sectors.
6Enacted last July and reinforced in September, the sanctions had been due to expire in July.
7They are now expected to expire on Jan. 31.
8In response, Russia is expected to extend its ban on the import of many agricultural products from the EU, which was imposed as a countermeasure to the EU sanctions.
9"Since they were extended, we will act on the principle of reciprocity," said Dmitry Peskov, spokesman for Russian President Vladimir Putin.
10Fighting between Ukrainian forces and Russian-backed rebels has killed more than 6,400 people in eastern Ukraine and persists despite a cease-fire agreed in Minsk, Belarus, in February that calls for the pullback of heavy weapons from the front line.
11The Russian Foreign Ministry said in a statement Monday it was "deeply disappointed that once again in the EU, the view of the Russophobic lobby has prevailed" in the extension of the "illegal restrictions."
12The ministry said it was wrong to hold Russia fully responsible for the implementation of the Ukraine peace agreement and that the key to a settlement was in Kiev's hands.
13The ministry also said "it looks particularly cynical" that the EU made the decision on June 22, the date in 1941 when Nazi Germany invaded the Soviet Union.
14Russia still holds memorial services on this day.
15"We would like to believe that this is a coincidence, not a specially designed step," the ministry statement said.
16German Foreign Minister Frank-Walter Steinmeier said the extension of sanctions was already likely before Monday's gathering.
17"In other words, all countries agreed," he told reporters during a break in the EU talks.
18"Because as long as the Minsk agreement is not fulfilled and also Russia has some responsibility for that, it was agreed that the measures would be extended."
19EU foreign policy chief Federica Mogherini said the move to roll over the sanctions against Russia was "not an easy or a business-as-usual decision."
20NATO Secretary-General Jens Stoltenberg welcomed the EU's decision, and predicted that the array of punitive measures imposed by the West would "change the behavior of Moscow."
21EU sanctions "are a strong signal and a clear message that it has consequences when a country behaves in the way Russia has done in Ukraine," he told a news conference in Brussels.
22He said Western sanctions were having a clear impact on Russia's economy.
23Christoph Noelting in Luxembourg, John-Thor Dahlburg in Brussels, and Lynn Berry in Moscow contributed to this report.
