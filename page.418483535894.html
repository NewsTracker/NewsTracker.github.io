Greece must do more to earn rescue: Eurozone leaders

1Eurozone leaders told near-bankrupt Greece at an emergency summit on Sunday it must enact key reforms this week to restore trust before they will open talks on a financial rescue to keep it in the European currency area.
2Leftist Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras will be required to push legislation through parliament to convince his 18 partners in the euro zone to release immediate funds to avert a state bankruptcy and start negotiations on a third bailout program estimated at up to 86 billion euros ($95.5 billion).
3Six sweeping measures including tax and pension reforms must be enacted by Wednesday night and the entire package endorsed by parliament before talks can start, a draft decision by Eurogroup finance ministers sent to the leaders showed.
4The document included a German proposal to make Greece take a "time-out" from the euro zone if it fails to meet the conditions.
5But not all ministers endorsed the idea, which a senior EU source said was illegal and would not survive in the summit statement.
6Several hardline countries voiced support for the German proposal that Greece take a five-year "time-out" from the euro unless it accepted and implemented swiftly much tougher conditions, notably by locking state assets to be privatized in an independent trust to pay down debt.
7Tsipras said on arrival in Brussels he wanted "another honest compromise" to keep Europe united.
8But German Chancellor Angela Merkel, whose country is the biggest contributor to euro zone bailouts, said the conditions were not yet right to start negotiations, sounding cautious in deference to mounting opposition at home to more aid for Greece.
9"The most important currency has been lost and that is trust," she told reporters.
10"That means that we will have tough discussions and there will be no agreement at any price."
11If Greece meets the conditions, the German parliament would meet on Thursday to mandate Merkel and Finance Minister Wolfgang Schaeuble to open the talks on a new loan.
12Then Eurogroup finance ministers would meet again on Friday or at the weekend to formally launch the negotiations.
13A Greek government official, in a first reaction to the draft, said: "How can they demand all these measures at the last minute without securing a lifeline to see us through till next week?"
14A European official said a Eurogroup meeting on Monday could discuss ways to provide emergency finance to keep Athens afloat.
15The final deadline for Greece to reach a deal to remain in the euro area is here, but that may not be the end of the drawn out saga.
16As the summit dragged into the late evening, officials said the biggest of a series of sticking points was Germany's insistence that Greek assets worth 50 billion euros earmarked for privatization be placed in a trust fund in Luxembourg to be sold with proceeds going directly to pay down debt.
17One diplomat said that was tantamount to turning Greece into a "German protectorate".
18Another diplomat said Merkel had declared the matter a "red line" for Germany.
19Tsipras, for his part, was insisting on a stronger commitment by the creditors to restructuring Greek debt to make it more sustainable in the medium-term.
20Some questioned whether it was feasible to rush the package through the Greek parliament in just three days.
21Tsipras is set to sack ministers who did not support his negotiating position in a vote last Friday and make dissident lawmakers in his Syriza party resign their seats, people close to the government said.
22Earlier, there were angry exchanges at a finance ministers' meeting that ended without a firm recommendation on Greece's application for a three-year loan on the basis of reform proposals submitted by Tsipras.
23Eurogroup chairman Jeroen Dijsselbloem said that while ministers had made good progress, a couple of big issues were left for the leaders to resolve.
24The draft said Greece needed 7 billion euros by July 20 in immediate funding, when it must make a crucial bond redemption to the European Central Bank, and a total of 12 billion euros by mid-August when another ECB payment falls due.
25It did not say how those needs would be met, and EU officials said finance ministers had been unable to agree on emergency finance.
