Islamic State: not just beheadings but taxes and business rules too

1Sanliurfa, Turkey: The Syrian businessman was enjoying a much-needed holiday in Turkey when the phone call came from the tax inspector of the Islamic State.
2His business partner in Raqqa had been arrested, the inspector told him, and he would not be released until he paid the $131,000 ($US100,000) that it owed the "caliphate".
3"They told me that because I have a lot of money, I have to pay my share," said "Ammar", who asked for his real name not to be used.
4"They analyse your income and take a percentage."
5As IS, also known as ISIL or ISIS, establishes its empire, the jihadists have become fastidious bureaucrats - imposing taxes, paying fixed salaries and introducing trading standards laws to try to create a healthy economy that will sustain their autocratic rule.
6Yet despite brutal punishments for those who break the rules, many Syrian businessmen prefer it to the anarchy that prevails in areas controlled by other rebel groups, including the Western-backed Free Syrian Army.
7Ammar, who deals in cars, houses and poultry, is largely secular and privately despises the jihadists, referring to the IS-held "capital" of Raqqa as "the big prison".
8But he admits that he now works almost exclusively in their areas, having had $US150,000 worth of stock stolen by a gang in turf run by another armed faction.
9Likewise, when he traded in areas controlled by the Syrian government, he was detained by a pro-regime militia, who demanded a bribe of $US25,000.
10While IS charges zakat - an Islamic alms payment similar to income tax - from those who can afford it, Ammar says that in return businesses are protected from theft and corruption.
11"When I take chickens that I have bought from farmers in the rural areas to Raqqa, the first thing I have to do is go to the zakat office and pay the 2.5 per cent. They give me a voucher that allows me to sell my produce in the city," he said.
12The model is being applied across IS territory in Syria, and to such a degree that businesses are now choosing to move their industry into IS areas.
13Factories in Sheikh Najjar, on the front line, have moved to IS-controlled Minbaj, residents and business owners said.
14Hassan, an engineer, said: "The majority of people live in a good enough situation but you have to hold your tongue. If you have some money, and can keep your mouth shut, it is OK."
15In part, the attraction of IS-controlled areas is that they are safer from the barrel bombs the regime drops on other rebel factions' strongholds.
16IS areas are subject to strikes by American jet fighters, but they are less indiscriminate.
17In public services, teachers are allowed to continue to work, provided they agree to a new curriculum that bans English and science.
18Doctors and engineers, particularly those managing IS-controlled oilfields, are paid handsomely.
