1On Wednesday, the Escondido City Council delayed a decision on whether to appeal last week's court ruling overturning the city's ban on development of the golf course property.
2Specifically, the court invalidated a 2013 city ordinance that changed the designation of the site from residential to permanent open space.
3That same year, Schlesinger sued the city over the designation, alleging the ordinance violated the law and was an illegal "taking" of his property.
4On Friday, Judge Earl H. Mass sided with the developer on the first claim.
5The Escondido council - after meeting in closed session Wednesday with City Attorney Jeff Epp - issued a statement saying it would "continue studying the legal options which are available following Judge Maas' decision last week."
6Some observers said the council will now wait to see what type of residential housing plan Schlesinger will submit for the defunct Country Club site.
7The density and other characteristics of the project will play a big part in the council's ultimate decision on whether to appeal the court ruling - a decision that would undoubtedly cost the city hundreds of thousands of dollars more in legal fees on top of the nearly $500,000 it has already spent.
8Dick Daniels, a spokesman for Schlesinger's Stuck in the Rough LLC, said Wednesday the proposal will probably be the 270-home compromise the developer recently floated to city leaders.
9That's several hundred homes fewer than Schlesinger had initially wanted to build at the site.
10The city has time to decide.
11The second part of Schlesinger's lawsuit - alleging that the open-space designation amounted to an illegal "taking" of his property - is still ongoing.
12He is seeking millions of dollars in damages.
13Until the legal proceedings are complete, the appeals clock keeps ticking with no immediate deadline, attorneys on both sides have said.
14Lawyers have also agreed in court that losing the ruling last week could be financially beneficial to the city because Schlesinger is no longer barred from submitting development plans.
15Were the city to win the appeal Schlesinger's claims in the lawsuit would be stronger if he were to ultimately win.
16When Schlesinger bought the financially-strapped golf course in late 2012 and shut it down a few months later, he originally suggested building more than 600 homes on the 110-acre golf course.
17Neighbors who live around the course were furious and gathered enough signatures on petitions to place on the ballot an initiative declaring the golf course permanent open space.
18The council instead adopted the initiative as law rather than have the question put to voters.
19Schlesinger then mounted his own campaign and placed on the November ballot an imitative that would have overturned the council's actions and allowed him to build 430 homes on the property.
20Voters turned him down by a large majority, despite Schlesinger having outspent the opposition by better than 10 to 1.
1A boy passes a roof that was blown away, days after Cyclone Pam in Port Vila, capital city of the Pacific island nation of Vanuatu March 19, 2015.
2PORT VILA (Reuters) - Villagers in Vanuatu buried food and fresh water as one of the strongest storms on record bore down on them, fleeing to churches, schools and even coconut drying kilns as 300 kph winds and massive seas tore their flimsy houses to the ground.
3Despite reports of utter devastation six days after Cyclone Pam pummeled the impoverished South Pacific island nation, Vanuatu appears to be providing something of a lesson in how to survive a category 5 storm.
4The United Nations says the official death toll is 11 and Prime Minister Joe Natuman told Reuters it would not rise significantly.
5"The important thing is that the people survived," he said in an interview outside his office overlooking the hard-hit capital of Port Vila.
6"If the people survived, we can rebuild."
7Officials had feared a spike in deaths once news came in from outer islands of the scattered archipelago and the low figure amazed aid workers and those who lived through the storm.
8"It's absolutely unbelievable the death toll is so low," said Richard Barnes, 43, a property valuer from New Zealand who has lived near the capital Port Vila, on Efate island for seven years.
9Two days ago, a helicopter flight over the north of Efate revealed scenes of total devastation with at least one coastal village destroyed and no sign of life.
10When visited a day later, dozens of villagers were back rebuilding with what materials they could find and reporting only one injury, said Barnes, who was on Cayman Island in 2004 when Hurricane Ivan hit.
11"Everyone is just getting on with it, which was different from Cayman where everyone just sat around waiting for something to be done," Barnes said.
12Sebastian Rhodes Stampa, disaster co-ordinator for the U.N.'s humanitarian affairs office said he was impressed by the country's ability to deal with the storm.
13"In very few places that I have worked have I seen such a resilient population," Rhodes Stampa, who has worked in major disaster sites including the Philippines after Typhoon Haiyan, told Reuters in Port Vila.
14Vanuatu, one of the world's poorest nations, is a sprawling cluster of more than 80 islands and 260,000 people, 2,000 km (1,250 miles) northeast of the Australian city of Brisbane.
15Perched on the geologically active "Ring of Fire", it suffers from frequent earthquakes and tsunamis and has several active volcanoes, in addition to threats from storms and rising sea levels.
16Ben Hemingway, a regional adviser for USAid, said aid organizations like his had been working with the Vanuatu government for years on disaster mitigation.
17"It's a testament to the investment the international community has made. If you look at the days before the storm, the message got out on the power of the storm and what to do to protect yourself. People did heed those warnings."
18Many villages are built further back from the shore to avoid storm surges and tsunamis, and most have at least one sturdy building to retreat to.
19Even the spreading roots of banyan trees that have survived centuries of storms are also sometimes used as shelter if houses are destroyed.
20"Hurricanes or cyclones are not a new thing, since when people started living in these islands maybe about 5,000 years ago this type of event occurs every year," said Natuman.
21"I think also we are now more organized in terms of our disaster management."
22Some villagers survived Pam by sheltering in a kiln used to dry coconuts and make copra, one aid official said.
23People in the capital Port Vila prepared by weighing down corrugated tin roofs with cinder blocks, sandbags or logs.
24On small isolated islands, stock piles of coconuts, fruit and water were buried to enable villagers to survive several days.
25Latrines are dug ahead of storms and lined with palm fronds to prevent contamination of water supplies.
26Aid workers are now trying to get aid to isolated islands where airstrips, ports and communications are extensively damaged.
27Two helicopters were onboard a French frigate leaving nearby New Caledonia on Thursday and Australia and New Zealand were also sending vessels, Natuman said.
