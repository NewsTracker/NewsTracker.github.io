Biz Stone on 'the 100-year journey toward mankinds unity'

Republicans seek State Department documents on Clinton emails

1Every March, tens of thousands of people from around the globe flock to Austin, Texas, to learn and connect over panels, parties and tacos at South By Southwest's interactive, film and music conferences.
2America Tonight's SXDiaries Q&A series highlights interesting and inspiring figures at SXSW.
3Biz Stone was one of Twitter's co-founders, earning him a spot among Time magazine's 100 most influential people in 2009.
4After helping develop the 140-character social media giant, Stone is now CEO of smartphone app developers Jelly Industries.
5He describes his new app, Super, as an "evolution in social communication" - an effort to promote empathy in a digital setting.
6In an interview with America Tonight, Stone discussed his successes and failures, and where STEM innovation is heading.
7Questions and answers have been edited for brevity and clarity.
8Your childhood wasn't easy.
9Your mom was on welfare and your dad was mostly absent.
10How can young people who have dreams but are growing up in tough environments overcome those early struggles like you did?
11I read somewhere there's a gene where people [who] are really traumatized in their youth, they either bend or they snap.
12The ones who bend usually come out stronger for it and the ones who snap usually turn to drugs or something like that.
13I must have been the one who bent.
14But I tell people about this theory in my book called the bright spot theory.
15Look, everything around you might be dark.
16It might be terrible but there's got to be something that's good, one tiny little speck of your life that's good.
17Zoom in on it and make that your whole thing.
18That goes for anything.
19Someone stuck in a job they don't like, a middle school student struggling in school.
20What one class do you like?
21Once you find that and hold on to it, you can say, "OK, I have something I like."
22That keeps you hanging in there.
23The same is true for a startup.
24No matter what you do and no matter how many insults you get about how it's stupid and no one is using it, it's water off a duck's back because you are totally emotionally invested in it.
25That's what saved us at Twitter.
26If we had been building Twitter just because we thought it was a good product but not because we used it, we would have quit way early on.
27Everyone said it was the stupidest thing, unilaterally.
28I remember we had 5,000 people for the service.
29I thought this was massively successful.
30"Could you imagine that many people in a room?"
31We were having tons of fun.
32Now that I'm older, I realize it has to be fun, you have to be emotionally engaged and you have to look for the bright spots.
33Otherwise, you're miserable.
34Your most recent product that launched last month, Super, has been described as a change of pace for you - going from disruptive projects to one that's been described as fun.
35What brought about the shift?
36Twitter was fun, too, you have to admit.
37If something is fun, then people are more likely to use it.
38If a lot of people use it, then it's important.
39But you can't start out with important.
40"Use this in case you need to topple a dictator."
41I'm fond of saying: If you want to build a platform capable of helping people topple despotic regimes, it also has to support fart jokes.
42People need to build a daily muscle memory of having fun in using an app.
43And when something happens like a plane lands in the Hudson, they go, "Whoa, I should tweet this."
44So with Super, we said we got to make this really fun so people will use it a lot.
45If a lot of people use it, then it will become important and then we'll figure out what [it's] important for and double down on that.
46This sounds eye-rolling and optimistic, but what we're trying to do at a very philosophical, head-in-the-clouds level is build technology that fosters empathy, because that's the beginning of the 100-year journey toward mankind's unity.
47This is our crack at it.
48People will say, "Oh God, look at this hippie from Silicon Valley, thinking he can create empathy from this stupid app."
49But that's what we're trying to do.
50Your previous project Jelly was called an attempt to fulfill what you believe to be the true promise of a connected society.
51What is that promise and can it still be fulfilled somehow?
52The idea behind Jelly is that the true promise of a connected society is people helping each other.
53I thought, "Mobile phones are hyperlinks of humanity so why not build a search engine out of real people since most of world's knowledge is not on the Web?"
54Even the vice president of Google Search said if you search long enough, most of the knowledge is not on the Web; it's in people's heads.
1Clinton, a likely Democratic presidential contender and Republicans' top target in the run-up to the 2016 election, has come under scrutiny for using a personal email address to conduct government business, as well as a private computer server to store that correspondence.
2Clinton has said she gave copies of all her work-related emails to the State Department.
3But the Republican National Committee is keeping up the pressure on her, submitting two Freedom of Information Act requests related to her email use.
4The Republicans seek "records related to the configuration, vetting and/or encryption" of Clinton's BlackBerry and copies of any "separation statements" signed by Clinton and her top aides.
5Officials leaving government use these statements to vow they have surrendered classified and unclassified documents related to federal business.
6The statement outlines criminal penalties for "falsifying or concealing" any relevant material.
7There is no record of Clinton having signed such a statement, State Department spokeswoman Jen Psaki said on Tuesday.
8She said Clinton's predecessors Colin Powell and Condoleezza Rice also had not signed separation statements.
9Earlier, Republican National Committee Chairman Reince Priebus said Clinton's handling of her government emails violated the spirit of these agreements.
10"The RNC intends to learn whether Clinton violated State Department guidelines in not signing the statement, or if she signed it making a false statement, and as a result, violated the law," Priebus said in a statement.
11Clinton's office last week said she sent and received 62,320 emails during four years at the State Department.
12After a review, 30,490 official emails were handed over and 31,830 were withheld as personal records.
13Republicans say that review was not enough.
14They want Clinton to hand over her entire email server so someone unaffiliated with Clinton can verify that she turned over all documents related to her work at the State Department.
15House of Representatives Speaker John Boehner sidestepped a question about whether lawmakers would subpoena the server, located at Clinton's home in New York state.
16"Some neutral third party is going to have to make some decisions about what documents are quote 'personal' and which ones are public record," Boehner told reporters on Tuesday.
