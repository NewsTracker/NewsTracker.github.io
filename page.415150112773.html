High court rejects California dispute over disclosing donors

Twitter, law enforcement investigate alleged Islamic State threats

1The justices on Monday let stand a lower court ruling against ProtectMarriage.com, the National Organization for Marriage and other supporters of a 2008 ballot initiative that outlawed same-sex marriages in California until the ban was overturned five years later.
2The groups sought to conceal their past and future campaign finance records because they feared harassment of donors.
3The 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals ruled against them in part because the names have been publicly available for five years.
4State law requires political committees to identify those who contribute more than $100 during or after a campaign, along with the donor's address, occupation and employer.
1WASHINGTON (Reuters) - Twitter Inc and law enforcement authorities are investigating alleged threats made by Islamic State militants against the social media network's co-founder and other employees, the company said in reaction to media reports.
2Islamic State supporters, in online posts on Sunday, called for attacks against Twitter and its "interests," including death threats, according to the online news media company Buzzfeed as well as the NBC news network.
3"Our security team is investigating the veracity of these threats with relevant law enforcement officials," a representative for Twitter said in a statement on Monday.
4Buzzfeed first reported the story on Sunday.
5One alleged threat was directed to Twitter co-founder Jack Dorsey, NBC reported.
6Dorsey didn't address or acknowledge the threats in his latest tweets on Sunday.
7The messages were posted on an anonymous file-sharing website called Pastebin, according to the media reports.
8"Your virtual war on us will cause a real war on you," one post by Islamic State supporters said, according to Buzzfeed.
9"We told you from the beginning it's not your war, but you didn't get it and kept closing our accounts on Twitter, but we always come back."
10Islamic State militants have relied heavily on Twitter and other social media networks to coordinate and to communicate, including the publishing of shocking videos of beheadings and other violent acts against its enemies.
11But social media companies, including Twitter, have also removed content and suspended accounts that post gruesome content, such as executions.
12Twitter's user policy prohibits the use of the service for violence and threats, stating on its website: "You may not publish or post direct, specific threats of violence against others."
13The company does not, however, "proactively monitor content," a spokesperson for the network told Reuters last week, adding that such policies were in place for other similar companies.
14Twitter's platform, which allows people to post 140-character messages with pictures and brief video clips, has more than 288 million users across the world, the company says.
