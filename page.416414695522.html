Justice Department probes Baltimore police-custody death

1April 20, 2015: Edward Brown speaks at a protest outside City Hall about Freddie Gray in Baltimore.
2BALTIMORE - The Justice Department said Tuesday it has opened an investigation into the death of a Freddie Gray, a black man who died of spinal injuries he suffered during an arrest that involved being transported in a police van.
3Gray, 25, was taken into custody April 12 after police "made eye contact" with him and another man in an area known for drug activity and the two started running, Baltimore police said.
4Gray was handcuffed and put in a transport van.
5At some point during his roughly 30-minute ride, the van was stopped and Gray's legs were shackled when an officer felt he was becoming "irate" in the back of the van, police said.
6He was rushed by ambulance to a hospital.
7Gray died Sunday - a week after his arrest - of what Deputy Police Commissioner Jerry Rodriguez described as "a significant spinal injury."
8Justice Department spokeswoman Dena Iverson said federal investigators will look for evidence whether an officer willfully violated a person's civil rights by using unreasonable force.
9It's not uncommon for federal investigators to look into allegations of excessive police force.
10Justice Department investigations in the last year include probes into the fatal shooting of an unarmed black 18-year-old in Ferguson, Missouri - a case that resulted in no charges against the officer - and an ongoing review of a police chokehold death of a New York City man.
11There's a high threshold for bringing federal civil rights charges against police officers in such cases.
12Federal investigators must show an officer willfully deprived a person of his or her civil rights by using more force than the law allows, a standard that's challenging in rapidly unfolding confrontations in which snap judgments are made.
13In the Baltimore case, six police officers have been suspended with pay while local authorities look into the death.
14The officers have been on the force anywhere from three years to 18 years.
15According to court documents, Officer Garrett Miller accused Gray of carrying a switchblade, which was discovered in Gray's pocket after he was stopped.
16The lawyer for Gray's family said he believes the police had no reason to stop him.
17"They've made concessions on lack of probable cause," attorney Billy Murphy said.
18"Running while black is not probable cause. Felony running doesn't exist, and you can't arrest someone for looking you in the eye."

23Kim Deachilla, a spokeswoman for the Baltimore Fraternal Order of Police Lodge 3, said a law firm that contracts with the union is representing them.
24The officers' specific roles in the arrest were not released by city officials.
25Bystander video shows officers on bicycles, in patrol cars and outside the transport van.
26Police Commissioner Anthony Batts said the reason for Gray's stop is "a question we have to dig into."
27Gray's death has prompted daily protests and a vigil was planned Tuesday evening at the spot where he was arrested.
28Harold Perry, 73, a retired small businessman who is nearly blind, said he heard the arrest through his bedroom window.
29A young man was screaming "You're hurting me! Get your knee off my back," Perry said.
30He said he also heard the young man say, "I'm an asthmatic."
