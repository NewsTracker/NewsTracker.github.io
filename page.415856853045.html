Germanwings plane crash: 'The pain is almost unbearable': grieving Australian family visit the crash site

1Melbourne Germanwings plan crash victims Carol Friday and son Greig Friday.
2"I now know the true meaning of the words grief stricken," says Mal Coram.
3On Monday, he was to see with his own eyes the mountain that took his beloved sister and nephew away.
4Mal, with his daughters Pippa and Georgie and son-in-law Michael, have travelled to France on behalf of their extended family to say farewell to Carol Friday, 68, and her son Greig, 29, the two Australians among the 150 who died when Germanwings flight 4U9525 came down in the Alps.
5On Monday they were due to finally arrive in those mountains, carrying tributes from family and friends: poems, photos and other mementos of Australia to leave as a memorial in a foreign land.
6They planned to visit Le Vernet, the closest village to the crash site, where a stone marker has been erected for grieving families, in a field overshadowed by the beautiful, stark peaks of the Alpine range hit by the diving plane.
7"I felt the need to come here," Mr Coram said.
8"I have a need to see the crash site and pay my respects to my sister and nephew.
9"My family suffers this loss terribly. My brother-in-law and niece are both too devastated to travel here today."
10Dave, husband to Carol for 34 years, is being looked after by his siblings in Australia as he grieves.
11He helped compose the words that Mr Coram planned to read to the media at the memorial in Le Vernet on Monday.
12Mr Coram was nervous about reading them.
13He worried that he would not be able to get through them without breaking down, a family member told Fairfax.
14It is a sign of the deep bond in this family.
15Most siblings are close.
16Only some are soulmates.
17"She was the linchpin of the entire family," Mr Coram said.
18"My life-long travelling companion and my mate.
19Carol's son Greig inherited those same "beautiful values", he said, always thinking of others.
20Greig was his godson.
21His daughters considered him not just a cousin but a best friend and "soul mate for their whole lives".
22Georgie and Pippa are devastated by the loss of their aunt and cousin.
23"The pain is almost unbearable," Georgie told Fairfax Media.
24Ms Friday, a child and maternal health nurse with the City of Casey, and Mr Friday, a graduate acoustics engineer, were on their way to meet Pippa Coram in Germany, where she lives, when the plane came down.
25They were on holidays together before Mr Friday started his two-year stint in Europe, where he was going to teach English in France.
26The family's thoughts on the man who caused this grief, the flight's copilot, are a mixture of fury and disbelief.
27"My two brothers are back in Australia still in denial," Mr Coram said.
28"We have decided to treat this as an accident at this stage and thus deny the perpetrator his wishes."
29While in the Alps, the family plan to talk to French prosecutors who are investigating the crash.
30The family wanted to thank the people who helped them make the journey around the world: Australian Federal Police, the government, and the "sincere and supportive" captain and crews of the Lufthansa and Qantas flights they have been on in recent days.
31Once in the mountains, their plans are fluid.
32They may revisit the memorial, out of sight of the media, again on Tuesday if they decide to stay in the region overnight.
33Or they may decide to begin their long journey home, having said a painful farewell on foreign soil to their loved, lost, sister, aunt, nephew, cousin, and best friends.
