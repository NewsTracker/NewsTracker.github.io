1Exit polls by Israel's leading television channels showed that prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu's Likud party drew even with Isaac Herzog's Zionist Union in the election on Tuesday, making the formation of the next government a serious challenge for both candidates.
2As polls closed, Israel's Channel 10 and Channel 1 said Likud and the Zionist Union had secured 27 seats each in the 120-member parliament, known as the Knesset.
3However, Channel 2 said Netanyahu had eked out a narrow victory, winning 28 seats to 27 for Herzog.
4Mr Netanyahu took to twitter to declare "a great victory for Likud" adding the result was a "major victory for the people of Israel".
5Final results are not expected until early on Wednesday morning (local time).
6A narrow loss for Mr Netanyahu would still put him in a better position than the opposition Zionist Union to cobble together a coalition.
7A fourth term would put Mr Netanyahu on track to become Israel's longest-serving prime minister.
8No party has ever won an outright majority in Israel's 67-year history - with any number of allegiances possible among the 10 or 11 parties expected to win a place in the parliament.
9The party invited to try to form a government would have up to 42 days to negotiate a coalition.
10It may be mid-May at the earliest before Israel has a new government.
11After a bitterly contested campaign, the election has turned into a referendum on "Bibi" Netanyahu, 65, who has been in power for a total of nine years spread over three terms.
12Mr Netanyahu took extraordinary steps to drum up support from right-wing voters, reversing policy on the eve of the election with an announcement that he would never allow a Palestinian state.
13On election day, he accused left-wing groups of trying to remove him from power by busing Arab Israeli voters to polling stations, a statement that drew a sharp rebuke from Washington.
14A fourth Netanyahu term would probably also prolong his prickly relationship with Israel's main ally, the United States, at least as long as Barack Obama is in the White House.
15Mr Netanyahu has focused on the threat from Iran's nuclear program and militant Islam.

17But many Israelis said they were tiring of the message, and the centre-left's campaign on social and economic issues, especially the high cost of housing and everyday living in Israel, appeared to have won support.
18In a possible sign of edginess, Mr Netanyahu took to Facebook to denounce what he said was an effort by left-wing non-profit groups to get Arab-Israelis out to sway the election against him.
19"The right-wing government is in danger," he wrote.
20"Arab voters are going to vote in droves. Left-wing NGOs are bringing them in buses."
21The Facebook comments drew a rebuke from Washington, where the Obama administration is already angry with Mr Netanyahu for delivering a speech to Congress opposing its nuclear talks with Iran.
22Since there are more parties on the right and far-right, Mr Netanyahu would have the advantage in coalition building if the Zionist Union wins by only a small margin.
23But if the centre-left wins by four or more seats, it should get the nod first to try to form a government.
24Mr Herzog, who has overcome criticism of his slight stature and reedy voice to lead a strong campaign, voted in Tel Aviv, where he emphasised the election was about a new direction.
