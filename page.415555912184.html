Microsoft must face Xbox 360 class action claims

1Microsoft Corp must face class action claims by Xbox 360 owners who say the video game console has a design defect that causes game discs to be gouged, a federal appeals court ruled on Wednesday.
2The 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals in Seattle said a lower court judge misapplied the law in finding that Xbox owners in the United States could not sue for damages as a group.
3It did not decide whether a class action should be certified.
4Class actions can lead to larger damages or broader remedies than individual lawsuits, which can be costly to pursue.
5Microsoft has sold tens of millions of Xbox 360 consoles since their Nov. 2005 launch.
6But owners have claimed that the console's optical disc drive cannot withstand even small vibrations.
7They said this causes game discs to spin out of control and become scratched even under normal playing conditions, rendering them unplayable.
8Microsoft countered that class certification was improper because just 0.4 % of Xbox owners reported disc scratches, and that misuse was the cause.
9In March 2012, U.S. District Judge Ricardo Martinez in Seattle dismissed the class-action claims.
10He relied on a 2009 ruling in a similar case in which another judge said the dearth of complaints ruled out class certification.
11But the 9th Circuit reversed the decision underpinning that ruling in 2010, in a case addressing whether a suspected defect caused premature tire wear in Land Rover vehicles.
12The appeals court said Martinez did not properly take this reversal into account when he deferred to the 2009 ruling.
13"Plaintiffs' breach of express warranty claim presents a common factual question-is there a defect?-and a common mixed question of law and fact-does that defect breach the express warranty?"
14Circuit Judge Johnnie Rawlinson wrote for the appeals court.
15"The district court erred in finding that individual issues of causation predominate over these common questions."
16The 9th Circuit returned the case to Martinez for further proceedings.
17"We've won in the lower court previously and believe the facts are on our side," said a spokesman for Microsoft, which is based in Redmond, Washington.
18Benjamin Gould, a lawyer for the plaintiffs, said his clients are pleased with the decision.
19The case is Baker et al v.
20Microsoft Corp, 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals, No.
2112-35946.
