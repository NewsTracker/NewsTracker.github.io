150 dead: Airbus crashes in French Alps

1LATEST: ALL 150 passengers and crew are dead after a German budget airliner crashed near a ski in the French Alps.
2A distress signal came moments before the Germanwings Airbus's deadly plunge.
3ALL 150 passengers and crew are dead after a German budget airliner crashed near a ski in the French Alps.
4A distress signal in good weather moments before the deadly plunge of the Germanwings A320 airbus Flight 9525 gave no clue to the cause of the tragedy.
5"There are no survivors," said France's transport minister, Alain Vidalies.
6The A320 was just 46 minutes into the flight from Barcelona when its pilot made a distress call at 10.47am local time (8.47pm Melbourne time) as the plane crossed southern France.
7Minutes later, it began falling from the sky.
8It fell 31,200 feet (nearly 10,000 metres) in just 10 minutes.
9Emergency units near the Germanwings crash site.
10"A distress call was registered at 10.47. The distress signal showed the plane was at 5000 feet in an abnormal situation," Mr Vidalies said.
11There were 45 Spaniards among the 144 passengers and six crew.
12Most were thought to be German.
13It is not known if any Australians were on board.
14Distraught family and friends of victims have been gathering at Dusseldorf International Airport, where a holding area had been set up inside the terminal.
15Authorities have also been pictured comforting some relatives in Spain.
16"The circumstances of the accident, that are not yet clear, make us think there are not thought to be any survivors," President Francois Hollande said.
17"It's a tragedy on our soil."
18According to flight data from FlightAware 24, the aircraft was cruising at 38,000 feet at 532mph before it started to lose altitude to 37,975ft with the speed reportedly increasing to 548mph.
19The last reported radar returns had the aircraft descending to 6800 feet at 434mph.
20The aircraft took 10 minutes to drop 31,200 feet, suggesting the pilots may have been battling to control or land the plane before it crashed.
21The cause of the crash was not immediately known, with weather conditions described as good in the region and the airliner flying at an altitude high above the Alps.
22Debris from the airliner, which was carrying 144 passengers and six crew, was found 2000m high in the mountains.
23Search and rescue teams are racing to the crash site, with authorities expecting a long and difficult recovery process due to the remote and mountainous terrain.
24Barcelonette, in the touristic Provence-Alpes-Cote d'Azur region, has a population of less than 3000 people and is close to popular ski resorts.
25French Interior Ministry spokesman Pierre-Henry Brandet said the crash site in the Digne region, about eight hours drive southeast of Paris, was 2000m altitude in the Alps.
26The plane belonged to Germanwings, a low-cost affiliate of Cologne-based Lufthansa, which had previously had no fatal accidents.
27The single-aisle A320 typically seats 150 to 180 people.
28A worker from a Swissport, a service company handling for Germanwings airlines, inside an office in the Barcelona airport in Spain.
29In a statement, airline spokesman Carsten Spohr said: "We do not yet know what has happened to flight 4U 9525. My deepest sympathy goes to the families and friends of our passengers and crew on 4U9529. If our fears are confirmed, this is a dark day for Lufthansa."
30Interior Minister Bernard Cazeneuve immediately headed to the scene while Prime Minister Manuel Valls said he had called an inter-ministerial crisis cell.
31"We don't know the reasons for the crash, we clearly fear that the 142-150 passengers and personnel have been killed considering the circumstances of the crash," said Valls.
32"All is being done to understand what happened and to help the families of the victims," he said.
33A spokesman for Airbus, the European aerospace giant, said it could not make any comment "for the moment".
34"We have no information on the circumstances of the accident," the spokesman said, adding that the company had opened a "crisis cell".

37France's leading air traffic controller union SNCTA called off a strike planned from Wednesday to Friday after news of the crash.
