Baltimore police hand report on Gray death to prosecutor

1Police in riot gear walk past boarded up row homes after a 10 p.m. curfew went into effect Wednesday, April 29, 2015, in Baltimore.
2The curfew was imposed after unrest in Baltimore over the death of Freddie Gray while in police custody.
3BALTIMORE (AP) - Baltimore police have completed their investigation into the death of Freddie Gray and turned over their findings to prosecutors - one day earlier than the department's self-imposed deadline, the commissioner said Thursday morning.
4Authorities have said the state's attorney's office will review the information, consider charges and decide how to move forward in the death of Gray, who was stopped by police April 12.
5He suffered spinal injuries while in custody and died a week later.
6In his announcement at a news conference, Baltimore Police Commissioner Anthony Batts did not give details of the report or take questions.
7He said the department dedicated more than 30 detectives to working on the case and report.
8"I understand the frustration; I understand the sense of urgency. ... That is why we have finished it a day ahead of time," Batts said.
9Batts also said police would continue to work on the case at the direction of the state's attorney.
10At the same news conference, Deputy Commissioner Kevin Davis reviewed the timeline of Gray's time in custody and his death.
11Gray was arrested after he made eye contact with officers and ran.
12After a chase, officers pinned him down and handcuffed him.
13They loaded him into a van and put leg cuffs on him when officers said he became "irate" in the wagon.
14Davis said Thursday that police discovered a new stop the van made with Gray in it, but they did not say what happened.
15Davis said the stop was found through footage from a privately owned camera.
16At a later stop at the tail end of Gray's ride, police put another man in the van.
17He told investigators that Gray was "was still moving around, that he was kicking and making noises" up until the van arrived at the police station.
18Batts said the man also said the driver did not speed, make sudden stops of "drive erratically."
19Somewhere along the way, Gray suffered a fatal spinal injury.
20He was eventually taken to a hospital.
21He died a week later.
22On Wednesday, rumors circulated that some kind of "verdict" will be rendered when police handed their report to prosecutors.
23But Mayor Stephanie Rawlings-Blake and other officials worked to dispel that notion.
24"It became very clear ... that people misunderstood," Rawlings-Blake said.
25Hassan Murphy, a lawyer for Gray's family, underscored their comments, saying, "This family wants justice and they want justice that comes at the right time and not too soon."
26Said Rawlings-Blake: "Whatever time the state's attorney's office needs to make that determination, the family wants to get it right."
27In widespread protests Wednesday night - not only in Baltimore, but in several cities including Boston, New York and Washington, D.C. - it was clear that tensions over the case are far from subsiding.
28While the demonstrations were mostly peaceful, police made numerous arrests, including 16 in Baltimore and at least 60 in New York.
29Gray's death was the latest in a series of high-profile cases around the country in which black men have died as the result of encounters with police.
30Similar protests have erupted over the deaths of Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri, and Eric Garner in New York last year, and the death earlier this month in South Carolina of Walter Scott.
31Scott was fatally shot in the back by a white police officer who has since been charged with murder.
