1Greece has formalised its claim to reparations from Germanhy over the damages from World War II.
2They now say that the figure owed is 279 billion.
3That figure, made up of damages, forced loans and interest and inflation on those sums is of course a pretty sum.
4Enough to provide Greece with a get out of jail free card* from its current debt woes if it were ever paid.
5And of course the obvious point is that it simply isn't going to get paid so it won't act as a get out of jail free card.
6It's entirely possible that there's some moral right to some form of further compensation for the horrors and damages of those years and that occupation.
7But there's no legal case to answer.
8So, there's a vague possibility that it might act as some form of moral suasion upon German public opinion, and we have seen at least one German political party muse that perhaps part of the claim is valid in that sense.
9It also accords with a substantial amount of Greek public opinion which is where its real usefulness might lie.
10But it's simply not going to be a major part of whatever does happen to the Greek debt and the current crisis.
11Greece's deputy finance minister has said that Germany owes it nearly 279bn ( 205bn) in reparations for the Nazi occupation of the country.
12Greek governments and private citizens have pushed for war damages from Germany for decades but the Greek government has never officially quantified its reparation claims.
13A parliamentary panel set up by Alexis Tsipras's government started work last week, seeking to claim German debts, including war reparations, the repayment of a so-called occupation loan that Nazi Germany forced the Bank of Greece Bank of Greece to make and the return of stolen archaeological treasures.
14This is the first time the Greek government quantified its claims, which included seeking war reparations and a so-called occupation loan that Nazi Germany forced the Bank of Greece to make.
15Athens also demanded that Berlin return its stolen archaeological treasures.
16Speaking at parliamentary committee, Deputy Finance Minister Dimitris Mardas said Berlin owed Athens 278.7 billion euros, according to calculations by the country's General Accounting Office.
17The occupation loan amounts to 10.3 billion euros.
18For those interested in the detailed background of now the Nazis plundered the occupied countries in WWII this book, "Hitler's Beneficiaries" by Goetz Aly, is probably the best source.
19A good review of it is here.
20It's by no means all about Greece but Greece is used as a major example to show how national treasuries were looted.
21One tactic was to insist that the German troops were "protecting" the occupied countries and that therefore those countries should pay the costs of the troops stationed there to enforce the occupation.
22This sounds very odd indeed but it's not actually all that out of line with what the general international law allowed at the time.
23The Nazis, of course, went further and started to insist that actually, those troops fighting on the Eastern Front were defending the occupied countries from the Soviets and that they should thus contribute to those costs as well.
24That was beyond what law at the time thought of as generally acceptable.
25(In more detail, Article 49 of the Hague convention which was in force at the time: "Art. 49. If, in addition to the taxes mentioned in the above article, the occupant levies other money contributions in the occupied territory, this shall only be for the needs of the army or of the administration of the territory in question."
26Little of this was specific to Greece though: they were general tactics used by the Nazis.
27Yes, of course they were foul, unfair, enforced by naked military power and so on.
28But that's not quite the issue at hand.
29Which is, is there a legal requirement to pay reparations for them?
30The answer to that is no, almost certainly not.
31The essential problem is that there is, a I've explained before a number of times, no Court of Equity in international law.
32While this will, in detail, horrify lawyers it's a good enough description for us here.
33A court of equity is one where you can go and simply say "Look, it doesn't matter what the law is, this is just infair. We demand justice."
34And the court will then consider matters on the basis of whether it really is fair or not, and propose remedies based upon fairness, not the jot and tittle of what the law says.
35This simply doesn't exist at the international level.
36We simply do not hold sovereign nations to such standards.
37If we did of course then the private sector bondholders would have a claim against Greece itself for the retrospective change in the collective action clauses that enabled the massive haircuts on the debt a few years back.
38What we're left with is simply what the law says.
39Which causes something of a problem for this Greek claim.
40Which comes in two parts: there's reparations for damages during the war as the first part.
41Add interest and inflation and that makes up by far the largest part here.
42But those reparations have already, in international law, been settled.
43In 1960 and then again in 1990 at the time of German unification.
44This might not be fair: but as above the law here isn't about fairness.
45So, while there might be a moral claim there isn't that legal one.
46The second concerns the zero interest forced loan that the Bank of Greece had to make: that's the one that makes up about 10 billion of the total claim.
47This walks into something of a trap.
48If it was a forced loan, one imposed simply by military might and occupation, then it's already covered in those reparations.
49If it's not a forced loan then the terms of it do stand: but it's a zero interest loan.
50Meaning that the sum, after 70 years of inflation, is too small to do anything very much to make a difference.
51To claim interest (which is the same as trying to claim inflation in economic terms) it needs to be shown that the terms were forced: which means that it falls under the reparations which have already been solved.
