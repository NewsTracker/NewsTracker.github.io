Mexico Doing DNA Tests on Girl in Mistaken-Identity Case

1A teenage girl at the center of an international custody case won't be sent to the United States until Mexican authorities confirm her identity through DNA testing.
2After Mexican authorities mistakenly seized another girl with a similar name and sent her across the border in April, Judge Cinthia Elodia Mercado said late Tuesday she would wait for the test results, which she expects Friday.
3Mercado said relatives of 13-year-old Alondra Diaz Garcia agreed that Dorotea Garcia of Houston, Texas, is the girl's mother.
4Alondra is believed to have been taken by her father to Mexico in 2007 without Garcia's consent.
5The girl will be staying with relatives and a child-protection official at a hotel in the city of Los Reyes, in the western state of Michoacan, until the tests results come back and the court hearings resume.
6Garcia and the relatives agreed that no DNA test was needed, but apparently embarrassed by the first mistake, authorities want to be sure now.
7"They (the relatives and the mother) did not want DNA testing carried out, but the prosecutor asked for it, to be certain," Mercado said.
8In April, Mexican officials seized a different girl, 14-year-old Alondra Luna, and Mercado ordered her into Garcia's custody despite protests by the teen's parents and without any DNA confirmation.
9The issue attracted international attention last month when a video was posted online showing Alondra Luna desperately resisting police after the court ruling, prompting protests for her return.
10A DNA test conducted in the United States revealed she wasn't Garcia's daughter and Luna was returned to her family in Mexico.
11Mercado denied there was any contradiction in the way the two girls were handled, saying no one had asked for DNA testing on the first Alondra.
12"The last time, it is important to point out that there was no request for DNA tests," said Mercado, apparently referring to requests from authorities.
13Alondra Luna's parents have said they begged Mercado not to take their daughter and demanded DNA testing, though it was unclear who would have paid for it.
14"There has been no error made here," Mercado said of the first case.
15Garcia traveled to Mexico on Monday and has met with Alondra Diaz, according to the woman's lawyer, Pedro de la Rosa.
16He called the encounter a happy moment and said the girl was choosing to go with her mother.
17Garcia would be willing to withdraw legal complaints against Alondra's father, Reynaldo Diaz, if she regains custody, he said.
18Alicia Diaz, the girl's aunt who accompanied her to the court, said her brother had decided to leave the girl with her grandmother to personally hand her over to Garcia.
19She said she was sure Alondra would be happy to be with Garcia, adding, "We will miss her."
