Joint Viking: Norway buzzes Russian border with biggest military drill since Cold War

Ukraine's military says pro-Russian rebels using truce to amass weapons

1The biggest exercises since Cold War days will last for a week, amid growing tensions between Russia and NATO.
2Joint Viking, Norway's biggest military training exercise in nearly 50 years, is being held in the country's far northeast Finnmark County, above the Arctic Circle.
3The region borders Russia's Kola Peninsula.
4The last time a similar military exercise was conducted in Finnmark was in 1967.
5"Today the army will transport their vehicles and crews to Finnmark and begin exercise Joint Viking. This is an operative exercise with all weapons and branches involved," Norwegian Army spokesperson Lt. Col.
6Aleksander Jankov said.
7"To illustrate the magnitude of this, I can mention that if we put the vehicles one after another on the road it will stretch 6km."
8Apart from the ground troops involved, the exercise (which will last until March 18) has put Norway's Navy submarines and surface vessels off the coast of Finnmark and Air Force fighter jets on alert.
9All types of weapons are promised to be put to use, reports the Barentz Observer.
10The buildup of military activities next to Russian border does not promote restoration of confidence in Euro-Atlantic, acknowledged Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov after meeting with his Spanish counterpart.
11"We believe problems should be resolved through equitable and mutually respecting dialogue," Lavrov said, stressing that Moscow reserves the right to "react adequately" to the drills in Norway.
12The Russian Air Force staged military training of its own last week, when fighter jets from Monchegorsk Air Base on the Kola Peninsula trained intercepting enemy cruise missiles and aircraft.
13Norway is planning a large joint air force exercise for May, in which more than 100 planes from eight NATO member states are going to take part in the Arctic Challenge Exercise in the skies of the Barents Sea region.
14The joint task force will be performing training lights from airfields in Lulea (Sweden), Rovaniemi (Finland) and (Norway).
15Last October Defense Minister Sergey Shoigu said that in 2015 Russia will be ready to "meet unwelcome guests" coming from any direction, after completing a network of radar stations in the Arctic.
16In December 2014, Russia's Northern Fleet was announced as the backbone of the new Arctic Command, with an area of engagement including the high-latitude areas of the Arctic Ocean and the North Pole.
17In February 2015, Shoigu confirmed that Russia may consider protecting its national interests in the Arctic with military means if necessary, pointing to the increasing interest in the region's resources by countries with no direct access to the Arctic.
18"The constant military presence in the Arctic and a possibility to protect the state's interests by the military means are regarded as an integral part of the general policy to guarantee national security," Shoigu said at a Ministry of Defense meeting.
19Just a couple of years ago Norway and Russia, which share a Barents Sea coastline and an almost-200km border, used to hold joint military trainings.
20The last one, named Pomor, was held in 2013 and involved visits by Norwegian battleships to Russia's port of Severomorsk and the Russian Navy calling to the Norway's port of in the north.
21After the escalation of the crisis in Ukraine and reunification of the Crimean Peninsula with Russia, Oslo suspended all military contacts with Moscow, simultaneously promoting closer military cooperation with other Scandinavian capitals and NATO member states.
1Kiev: Ukraine's military says pro-Russian rebels are amassing heavy weapons in depots around separatist-held Donetsk city despite a ceasefire deal.
2Attacks have fallen in the past week, but accusations from both sides of violations show the fragility of the peace deal agreed in Minsk last month, which calls for the withdrawal of heavy weapons from the frontline.
3"[Rebels] are continuing to transport equipment and artillery ammunition intensively via the Debaltseve railway junction and build weapons storages around Donetsk," military spokesman Andriy Lysenko said in a televised briefing.
4A rebel soldier looks in the direction of Ukrainian forces on the outskirts of Donetsk.
5The Organisation for Security and Co-operation in Europe, which monitors the pull-back of weapons, has said it cannot fully confirm withdrawal on both sides as it has not been given access to all the locations where some weapons have been moved.
6Lysenko said Ukrainian positions had come under attack from separatists 31 times on Monday, including five instances of artillery fire, and reported nine servicemen had been wounded in the past 24 hours.
7"The most troubled areas are Shyrokyne and around Donetsk airport ... yesterday there was fighting almost all day in Shyrokyne," Lysenko said, referring to a village on the outskirts of government-held Mariupol, a strategic port city Kiev fears could be the focus of the next rebel offensive.
8Chains of ammunition hang in a room dubbed "the sniper's bedroom" at a pro-Russian rebel base on the outskirts of Donetsk.
9On the rebel side, officials said Ukrainian forces had violated the ceasefire 17 times in the past 24 hours, but reported no casualties, separatist press service DAN said.
10Ukrainian President Petro Poroshenko said earlier that the ceasefire deal was largely holding, but the army is still sustaining losses.
11Sixty-four soldiers have been killed since the peace deal came into effect on February 15, bringing to 1549 the number killed since the war started, he said.
12The separatists rarely announce their own casualty figures.
13Ukraine and the West accuse Russia of providing separatists with troops, training and weaponry.
14Moscow denies this but has remained under strong diplomatic pressure from European capitals and Washington during the peace deal.
15British Foreign Secretary Philip Hammond on Tuesday accused Russian President Vladimir Putin of undermining other former Soviet republics, besides Ukraine.
16"We are now faced with a Russian leader bent not on joining the international rules-based system which keeps the peace between nations, but on subverting it," Hammond told the Royal United Services Institute in London.
17"President Putin's actions - illegally annexing Crimea and now using Russian troops to destabilise eastern Ukraine - fundamentally undermine the security of sovereign nations in Eastern Europe," he said as NATO forces prepared for a major exercise in the Baltic states.
18US military officials said deployment of about 3000 troops had begun for the three-month Operation Atlantic Resolve.
19The exercise will see NATO forces working alongside their allies in Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania.
20The United States also said on Monday it had delivered more than 100 pieces of heavy military equipment to the Baltic states.
