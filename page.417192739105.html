Hillary Clinton got sensitive email about the 2012 Benghazi attack on her unsecured private server

1WASHINGTON - Former Secretary of State Hillary Rodham Clinton received information on her private email server that has now been classified about the deadly attack on U.S. diplomatic facilities in Benghazi.
2The email in question, forwarded to Clinton by her deputy chief of staff Jake Sullivan, relates to reports of arrests in Libya of possible suspects in the attack.
3The information was not classified at the time the email was sent but was upgraded from "unclassified" to "secret" on Friday at the request of the FBI, according to State Department officials.
4They said 23 words of the Nov. 18, 2012, message were redacted from the day's release of 296 emails totalling 896 pages to protect information that could damage foreign relations.
5Because the information was not classified at the time the email was sent, no laws were violated, but Friday's redaction shows that Clinton received sensitive information on her unsecured personal server.
6No other redactions were made to the collection of Benghazi-related emails for classification reasons, the officials said.
7They added that the Justice Department had not raised classification concerns about the now-redacted 1 1/2 lines when the documents were turned over to the special House committee looking into the Benghazi attack in February.
8The committee retains a complete copy of the email, the officials said.
9It is at the end of a chain of communication that originated with Bill Roebuck, the then-director of the Office of Maghreb Affairs, that pointed out that Libyan police had arrested several people who might have connections to the attack.
10The redacted portion appears to relate to who provided the information about the alleged suspects to the Libyans.
11A total of five lines related to the source of the information were affected, but only the 23 words were deleted because the FBI deemed them to be classified.
12Roebuck's email was sent to a number of senior officials, including the former assistant secretary of state for near eastern affairs, Elizabeth Jones, who then sent it to Sullivan with the comment: "This is preliminary, but very interesting. FBI in Tripoli is fully involved."
13Sullivan then forwarded the email to Clinton with the comment: "FYI."
14There was no immediate indication that Clinton herself forwarded the email.
