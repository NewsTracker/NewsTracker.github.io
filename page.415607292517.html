1Odin Camus, 13, has Asperger's syndrome, an autism spectrum disorder characterized by difficulties in social interaction and communication.
2As a result, the Peterborough, Ont., boy struggles to make friends and is bullied, his mother Melissa wrote on Facebook.
3Odin sent out birthday invitations for a party to be held Friday, but not one kid responded to the invite, his mother said.
4She took to Facebook to ask others in her community to send Odin a text message on his birthday.
5"I want his thirteenth birthday to feel special, and like people are about him," Melissa Camus wrote.
6"I'm really struggling with finding a way to make today awesome for him, when it will just be us celebrating."
7The message was posted on the "Peterborough Moms Swap and Buy" Facebook page.
8The women who use the page suggested Melissa host a public event for Odin, and post the details to a separate Facebook page.
9Melissa planned a bowling night in Peterborough, opening the event to anyone who wished to stop by and wish her son a happy birthday.
10Dozens of strangers showed up at the Lakeview Bowl around 8 p.m., bringing gifts, balloons and hugs for Odin.
11"I think it's amazing how so many people actually care because not a lot of people where I used to live cared about my birthday," Odin told CTV Toronto before the party.
12He estimated that he had received around 4,000 text messages Friday, some of them from people living as far away as Japan, India and Russia.
13Odin said he looked forward to his party and is thankful to everyone who made it happen.
14"Thank you to the people who recognize it's above and beyond what I could have dreamed."
15Dozens of strangers who couldn't make it to the event posted messages on the Facebook page, wishing the boy a happy birthday.
16One group of kids made a colourful sign, and filmed a four-second video saying happy birthday.
17Canadian country singer Beverley Mahood also commented on the page, complimenting the teen on his moose-patterned sweater, "Have a great time bowling and have the best birthday. PS you look cute in your moose picture."
18Others asked what toys and games Odin liked, planning to bring a gift to the bowling party.
19His mother wrote that he loves his Xbox 360, anything army or navy related, Minecraft, Nerf guns and paintballing.
20"He's been non-stop smiling and getting all overwhelmed. It's so amazing," Melissa told CTV Toronto.
21Many have also tweeted their birthday messages for Odin with the hashtag #odinbirthday.
22Among the well-wishers: TV personalities, celebrities, firefighters, police officers, Toronto sports teams and even federal politicians.

1It's a monumental, ambitious project: An eco-friendly city the size of Singapore in the desert outside Cairo to serve as a new capital, with skyscrapers and a park more than twice the size of New York's Central Park.
2But urban planners and real estate experts say the plans remain a mystery and are skeptical it can or should be done.
3CAIRO (AP) - The idea is colossal: building an eco-friendly city the size of Singapore in the desert outside Cairo, a sleek new capital fit for the 21st century where a diverse slice of society will live in harmony.
4Of the projects unveiled at the corporate jamboree that was Egypt's economic conference last weekend, "The Capital Cairo" is the biggest and least transparent endeavor, the latest mega-project ordered by President Abdel-Fattah el-Sissi to kick-start an ailing economy.
5There will be leafy mixed-use neighborhoods, housing for up to 7 million people, 2,000 schools, shopping malls, solar energy farms, efficient public transport, skyscrapers, a million new jobs and even a central park more than twice the size of New York City's.
6It's everything missing from Egypt today - with a price tag of up to $80 billion.
7For urban planners, the project - described as "a dream of what could become reality" - is a mystery, hastily conceived with no concrete information on funding.
8Questions stick out over the actual demand for so many units, who will build required infrastructure and how to attract a diverse cross-section of society.
9Is a flashy Dubai-style city feasible in impoverished Egypt?
10"No one's looked at it on any serious scale ... It is more like a scripted media event than a serious proposal, reflecting the current government's obsession with foreign, Gulf investments somehow coming to Egypt's rescue," said Cairo-based urban planner David Sims.
11"Real estate is now considered the engine of Egypt's growth and what better way to stimulate it, in spite of the dismal record of new desert towns to date, than to proclaim a colossal new capital?"
12Crowds flocked to a scale model of the city during the conference in the resort town of Sharm el-Sheikh, where companies and states committed $36 billion in investments and billions more in preliminary agreements.
13In his closing remarks, el-Sissi said he was "in a hurry" to see the new capital built and urged work done faster than the original 10-year timeframe.
14Peopling the desert with new cities has been an obsession of successive presidents in the Arab world's most populous country, but after decades of work and billions spent, occupancy rates are a fraction of original forecasts.
15Drawing Egyptians to new sites has been a major challenge, with high prices, lack of infrastructure, and few jobs in the areas.
16All that will change with the new project, initiators say.
17The new plan falls under the auspices of the Housing Ministry and will be built by an Emirati-led fund called Capital City Partners.
18Founding partner Mohamed Alabbar said clear profit incentives will encourage the private sector to lead the way.
19Alabbar is chairman of Emaar Properties, the Emirati real estate developer behind large-scale projects like the record-breaking Burj Khalifa skyscraper and Downtown Dubai.
20"If you really want growth, if you want a great percentage of return, then this is the place in the Middle East ... For us as businessmen, the numbers make sense," he said in Sharm el-Sheikh.
21"I like to do big things in big countries where I know the economy and the people, and I know how eager the government (is) to move forward."
22"In Egypt, I'll bite any risk any day," he added.
23Others wonder if Egypt, a country with mass illiteracy, crushing poverty and tiny individual wealth compared to the energy-rich Emirates, is ready for the swank Dubai model of "world-class lifestyle" put forward by the man who built the world's first Armani Hotel.
24"Let's say this does happen in seven years as the government wants. That's about 150,000 housing units per year, the same figure of what's currently being built annually in the whole country," urban policy researcher Yahia Shawkat said.
25"So where is the demand going to come from?"
