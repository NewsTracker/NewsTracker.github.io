Ian McKellen works his wizardry as a 93-year-old Sherlock in ‘Mr. Holmes’

1Hattie Morahan and Sir Ian McKellen in "Mr. Holmes."
2Bill Condon's "Mr. Holmes" is a delightful if somewhat minor addition to the supersleuth's ever-growing film and TV canon, providing a smashing role for Ian McKellen as a 93-year-old Sherlock trying to solve his final mystery in post-World War II England.
3What prompted our elderly hero to abandon detecting and exile himself to England's Sussex coast 30 years before?
4That's a mystery to Holmes himself, who hopes to reverse memory loss by consuming royal honey from his own bees - and prickly ash, another possible cure he's procured during a mysterious recent trip to Japan.
5As his time runs down, Sherlock finds an ally in Roger (Milo Parker), the bright 14-year-old son of his gruff, widowed housekeeper (Laura Linney).
6Roger becomes hooked on the puzzle after sneaking a peek at Holmes' incomplete manuscript on his last case, which the great young detective deduces must have ended disastrously.
7In slowly unfolding flashbacks to 30 years ago, we see a 63-year-old Sherlock following the depressed wife (Hattie Morahan) of a client who fears she's fallen under the influence of a spiritualist (Frances de la Tour), who's giving her lessons on playing the glass harmonica.
8That mystery is ultimately less satisfying than the plight of the elderly Sherlock, who's struggling simultaneously with the indignities of old age and the possibility of losing his long-suffering maid to another job.
9His memory may be faulty, but he's still capable of breathtaking deductive leaps when necessary.
10"Mr. Holmes" is very much a companion piece to Condon's 1998 film "Gods and Monsters," in which McKellen played dying director James Whale, whose memories of directing "Frankenstein'' are jogged after meeting a younger man he desires.
11That film won Condon an Oscar for adapted screenplay and netted McKellen an Oscar nomination.
12"Mr. Holmes,'' derived from a novel by Mitch Cullin, isn't quite as deep or as poignant, but amply rewards McKellen and Holmes fans willing to go with its leisurely pace.
